# CEO Dismissal After Natural Disasters: A Board Legitimacy Perspective

## Introduction

Corporate boards are tasked with one of the most consequential decisions in firm governance: whether to retain or dismiss the CEO. The dominant theoretical framework treats this decision as a rational inference problem. Boards observe firm performance, filter out common shocks using industry or market benchmarks, and update their beliefs about the CEO's unobservable ability (Parrino, 1997; Jenter & Kanaan, 2015). Under this relative performance evaluation logic, dismissal follows when the board's posterior estimate of CEO quality falls below a threshold that justifies the costs of replacement. The framework is elegant, internally consistent, and deeply embedded in how the field conceptualizes CEO turnover.

Yet this framework rests on an assumption that is rarely examined: that boards can, in practice, isolate the CEO's contribution to firm outcomes from the noise generated by forces beyond managerial control. In principle, benchmarking against industry peers should strip away common shocks. In practice, however, the decomposition is far more fragile than the theory suggests. Firm performance is the joint product of managerial decisions, competitive dynamics, regulatory shifts, macroeconomic conditions, and stochastic events — and these components do not arrive with labels identifying their origin. Industry benchmarks absorb only the common component of exogenous variation; idiosyncratic shocks that differentially affect individual firms within an industry remain entangled with the CEO's true performance signal. The board thus faces an irreducible identification problem: it cannot observe the counterfactual of what performance would have been under a different CEO exposed to the same realized shocks. What the literature models as a signal-extraction exercise is, in reality, an attribution judgment made under deep uncertainty.

This identification problem has a behavioral consequence that standard models do not accommodate. If boards cannot reliably separate luck from skill, then CEO dismissal cannot be purely a function of updated ability beliefs. Something else must be driving the decision — particularly in cases where the exogenous nature of a performance shock is publicly observable. A large body of evidence confirms the anomaly: CEOs are systematically penalized for events they did not cause and could not have prevented (Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2001; Jenter & Kanaan, 2015). This finding is not a minor empirical wrinkle. It represents a fundamental challenge to the premise that boards evaluate CEO ability in a manner consistent with rational Bayesian updating.

We propose that the missing element is board legitimacy. Corporate boards do not operate in an institutional vacuum. Their credibility as governance bodies is subject to continuous scrutiny by shareholders, proxy advisors, regulators, and the financial press. When a visible negative shock depresses firm performance — regardless of its origin — the board's monitoring function becomes publicly salient. Stakeholders observe the outcome and expect a response. In this setting, CEO dismissal serves not only, and perhaps not primarily, as an act of managerial discipline, but as a signal of governance competence. The board demonstrates that it is attentive, decisive, and willing to act, thereby maintaining its own legitimacy as a fiduciary body. The dismissal is less about what the board has learned about the CEO and more about what the board needs to communicate about itself.

We exploit natural environmental disasters as a setting that allows us to test this legitimacy framework against the standard ability-updating account. Natural disasters provide a uniquely powerful identification opportunity: they are exogenous to prior managerial decisions, their occurrence is publicly observable, and — crucially — their exogeneity is known to all market participants. If boards were engaged in rational ability updating, disasters that visibly lie outside managerial control should carry no informational content about CEO quality and, therefore, should not affect the probability of dismissal. Any systematic effect of disasters on CEO turnover thus constitutes direct evidence that the dismissal decision is driven by forces other than belief updating about ability.

We find that natural disasters that negatively affect firm performance significantly increase the probability of CEO dismissal. More importantly, we document four moderating effects that collectively discriminate between the legitimacy account and competing explanations. First, the dismissal effect is weaker in firms with higher institutional ownership. Sophisticated institutional investors, who possess the expertise and incentive to distinguish luck from skill, serve as legitimacy buffers — their presence reduces the board's need for performative governance actions because the relevant audience is less likely to misattribute the shock to managerial failure. Second, the effect is amplified when the CEO underperformed the industry prior to the disaster. This pattern suggests that the shock serves as a catalyst for dismissals that were already latently motivated: the disaster opens a policy window (Kingdon, 1984) in which accumulated dissatisfaction can be acted upon under the cover of a publicly defensible justification. The board is not learning something new about the CEO; it is exploiting an opportunity to act on what it already suspected. Third, the effect is stronger when the CEO holds a larger termination package. Golden parachutes lower the execution cost of dismissal by enabling a dignified, low-conflict exit. This finding is difficult to reconcile with pure ability-updating models, where the CEO's exit compensation should be irrelevant to the board's assessment of competence — but it follows directly from a legitimacy account in which boards weigh the political and reputational costs of action. Fourth, the effect is amplified in boards with a higher proportion of co-opted directors — those appointed during the focal CEO's tenure. Co-opted directors face a chronic legitimacy deficit as monitors, and when a visible governance event arises, they face asymmetric incentives to overcompensate by acting decisively against the CEO, thereby signaling the independence they are otherwise perceived to lack.

Taken together, these findings paint a coherent picture in which CEO dismissal after exogenous shocks is driven less by updated beliefs about managerial ability and more by the board's need to manage its own institutional legitimacy. The disaster provides the justification, prior underperformance provides the underlying motivation, the golden parachute provides the mechanism, and the board's own governance credibility determines the intensity of the response. Our study contributes to the literatures on CEO turnover, board governance, and institutional legitimacy by demonstrating that the same firm-level shock produces markedly different governance responses depending on the board's pre-existing legitimacy position — a pattern that attribution-based models of CEO evaluation cannot accommodate.
