## Abstract

This paper introduces the CRISP framework to explain rule-following behavior. Through four experiment series involving 14,034 participants, we demonstrate that a substantial majority (55-70%) of people follow arbitrary costly rules even when they are anonymous and face no enforcement. The CRISP framework identifies four key determinants: Conformity as a function of Respect for the rule-maker, Incentives, Social expectations, and intrinsic Preferences for rule-following.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Puzzle of Rule-Following

Rules are ubiquitous in society - from traffic regulations to organizational policies to social norms. A fundamental puzzle in social science is why people follow rules even when:
- Violations are anonymous
- Enforcement is absent
- Following the rule is personally costly

Standard rational choice theory predicts widespread violation under these conditions, yet empirical observation suggests otherwise.

### 1.2 The CRISP Framework

We propose the CRISP framework to organize the determinants of rule-following:

**C = f(R, I, S, P)**

Where:
- **R** = Respect for the rule-maker (legitimacy)
- **I** = Incentives (material consequences of compliance/violation)
- **S** = Social expectations (beliefs about others' behavior)
- **P** = Preferences (intrinsic disposition toward rule-following)

### 1.3 Key Contributions

1. **CRISP Framework:** A unified theoretical framework for rule-following
2. **Empirical Quantification:** Large-scale experimental evidence (N = 14,034)
3. **Unconditional Rule-Followers:** Identification of ~23% who follow rules regardless of context
4. **Peer Effects:** Precise estimation of social influence on compliance

## 2. Experimental Design

### 2.1 Overview of Experiment Series

Four experiment series designed to isolate each CRISP component:

| Series | Focus | Sample Size | Key Manipulation |
|--------|-------|-------------|------------------|
| 1 | Baseline rule-following | ~3,000 | Anonymous costly rules |
| 2 | Respect (R) | ~3,500 | Legitimacy of rule-maker |
| 3 | Social expectations (S) | ~4,000 | Information about peer compliance |
| 4 | Preferences (P) | ~3,500 | Individual heterogeneity measurement |

**Total Sample:** 14,034 participants

### 2.2 Basic Paradigm

- Participants face rules that are:
  - Arbitrary (no inherent moral content)
  - Costly to follow (monetary sacrifice)
  - Anonymous (no identifiable consequences)
  - Unenforced (no punishment for violation)

### 2.3 Key Design Features

- Real monetary stakes
- Complete anonymity guaranteed
- No deception
- Pre-registered hypotheses

## 3. Main Results

### 3.1 Baseline Rule-Following

**Key Finding 1:** 55-70% of participants follow arbitrary costly rules even when anonymous.

This contradicts the prediction of pure self-interest models but is consistent with:
- Social preferences literature (Fehr & Schmidt 1999)
- Intrinsic motivation theories
- Norm-following heuristics

### 3.2 Unconditional Rule-Followers

**Key Finding 2:** Approximately 23% of participants are "unconditional rule-followers."

These individuals:
- Follow rules regardless of R, I, S conditions
- Show stable behavior across contexts
- May represent a distinct behavioral type

### 3.3 Respect Component (R)

Legitimacy of the rule-maker significantly affects compliance:
- Higher respect → Higher compliance
- Effect is robust across contexts
- Magnitude: ~15-20 percentage point difference between high/low legitimacy

### 3.4 Social Expectations Component (S)

**Key Finding 3:** Strong peer effects in rule-following.

**Quantified Effect:** A 1 percentage point increase in observed peer violations leads to a 0.19 percentage point increase in own violations.

This implies:
- Social information is causally important
- Compliance cascades are possible
- "Broken windows" effects are real but moderate

### 3.5 Preferences Component (P)

Substantial individual heterogeneity in intrinsic rule-following preferences:
- Distribution is not bimodal (continuous variation)
- Stable over time within individuals
- Partially correlated with other social preferences

## 4. Theoretical Framework

### 4.1 CRISP Model Specification

Individual i's probability of compliance:

$$P(comply)_i = f(R_i, I_i, S_i, P_i)$$

Where each component enters non-linearly with interaction effects.

### 4.2 Integration with Existing Theory

**Connection to Social Preferences:**
- Fehr & Schmidt (1999): Inequity aversion
- Fehr & Gächter (2000, 2002): Punishment and cooperation
- Our P component captures intrinsic norm-following

**Connection to Punishment Literature:**
- Herrmann et al. (2008): Cross-cultural variation in antisocial punishment
- Rule-following may substitute for costly punishment
- Unconditional rule-followers reduce need for enforcement

### 4.3 Policy Implications

1. **Design:** Rules should maximize R (legitimacy)
2. **Information:** Publicize compliance rates (S component)
3. **Selection:** Identify unconditional rule-followers for key positions
4. **Incentives:** Material incentives (I) may crowd out P

## Appendix: CRISP Components Detail

### A.1 Respect (R)

Sources of legitimacy:
- Democratic selection of rule-maker
- Expertise of rule-maker
- Procedural fairness in rule creation
- Consistency of rule application

### A.2 Incentives (I)

Types of incentive effects:
- Direct material consequences
- Signaling value of compliance
- Crowding out of intrinsic motivation (caution)

### A.3 Social Expectations (S)

Mechanisms:
- Descriptive norms (what others do)
- Injunctive norms (what others approve)
- Conditional compliance (cooperate if others cooperate)
- Social learning

### A.4 Preferences (P)

Components of intrinsic rule-following:
- Moral reasoning
- Habit/automaticity
- Identity (rule-follower self-concept)
- Guilt/shame anticipation
