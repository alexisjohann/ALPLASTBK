## Abstract

This Anomalies column examines the distinction between decision utility (the weight assigned to outcomes in decisions) and experienced utility (the hedonic quality of outcomes). A rational agent's decisions should maximize experienced utility, but systematic errors in predicting future hedonic states—hedonic forecasting errors—cause decision utility to diverge from experienced utility. We review evidence on four categories of errors: (1) effects of current emotional state on predictions, (2) effects of choice context, (3) failures to learn from past experience, and (4) misprediction of adaptation. These findings challenge the assumption that individuals maximize well-being through their choices.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Two Utilities

The concept of utility in economics serves two distinct functions:

**Decision Utility:** The weight an outcome receives in a decision—what economists typically mean by utility, inferred from choices.

**Experienced Utility:** The hedonic quality of an outcome—pleasure, satisfaction, or well-being actually experienced.

### 1.2 The Assumed Identity

Standard economic theory assumes these concepts are identical: people choose what they will enjoy most. This identity requires:
- Accurate prediction of future hedonic states
- Rational integration of predictions into decisions
- Appropriate learning from past experiences

### 1.3 Hedonic Forecasting Errors

When people systematically mispredict their future utility, decision utility diverges from experienced utility. This column reviews evidence that such errors are common, substantial, and predictable.

## 2. Effects of Current Emotional State

### 2.1 Projection Bias

**Definition:** The tendency to project current preferences onto future selves, underestimating how much preferences will change.

**The Hungry Shopper Study (Read & van Leeuwen, 1998):**
- Office workers chose snacks for next week
- Half chose while hungry (late afternoon), half after eating (after lunch)
- When hungry: 78% chose unhealthy snacks
- When satiated: 42% chose unhealthy snacks
- Actual consumption a week later: ~50% regardless of choice state

**Implication:** Current visceral state (hunger) biases predictions about future preferences.

### 2.2 Hot-Cold Empathy Gaps (Loewenstein, 1996)

**Definition:** Failure to appreciate how "hot" emotional states (hunger, anger, pain, sexual arousal) will influence future behavior and preferences.

**Cold-to-Hot Gap:** In cold states, people underestimate the influence of hot states on their future behavior.

**Hot-to-Cold Gap:** In hot states, people fail to anticipate returning to cold states.

**Examples:**
- Heroin addicts in withdrawal overestimate future craving
- Sexual arousal increases predicted willingness to engage in risky behaviors (Ariely & Loewenstein, 2006)

## 3. Effects of Context on Choice

### 3.1 Joint vs. Separate Evaluation (Hsee, 1996)

**Paradigm:** Objects are evaluated either in isolation (separate evaluation) or alongside alternatives (joint evaluation).

**Music Dictionary Example:**
- Dictionary A: 10,000 entries, perfect condition
- Dictionary B: 20,000 entries, torn cover

**Results:**
| Condition | Preferred |
|-----------|-----------|
| Separate evaluation | Dictionary A ($24) > Dictionary B ($20) |
| Joint evaluation | Dictionary B ($27) > Dictionary A ($19) |

**Explanation:** Number of entries is evaluable only in comparison. In separate evaluation, condition (evaluable without comparison) dominates.

### 3.2 Preference Reversals

The joint/separate effect produces genuine preference reversals—different choices lead to different experienced utilities. People in separate evaluation choose the option that provides less experienced utility.

## 4. Failures to Learn from Past Experience

### 4.1 The Peak/End Rule

**Definition:** Retrospective evaluations of episodes are determined primarily by:
1. The most intense moment (peak)
2. The final moment (end)

Duration is largely neglected.

**Colonoscopy Study (Redelmeier & Kahneman, 1996):**
- N = 154 patients
- Pain ratings every 60 seconds during procedure
- Duration: 4 to 69 minutes (no correlation with retrospective evaluation)
- Retrospective evaluation predicted by: (Peak + End) / 2, r = 0.67

### 4.2 Duration Neglect

**Experimental Demonstration:**
- Condition A: Hand in 14°C water for 60 seconds
- Condition B: Hand in 14°C water for 60 seconds + 30 seconds at 15°C

**Result:** Subjects preferred to repeat Condition B (objectively more pain) because of better ending.

### 4.3 Violations of Dominance

**Clinical Application (Redelmeier, Katz & Kahneman, 2003):**
- N = 682 colonoscopy patients
- Treatment group: Procedure extended with colonoscope left in place
- Result: Extended group rated procedure as less aversive
- Follow-up: Extended group more likely to return for screening

**Implication:** Adding pain (objectively worse) improved retrospective evaluation and future behavior. Decision utility based on remembered utility, not total experienced utility.

## 5. Mispredicting Adaptation

### 5.1 The Focusing Illusion

**Definition:** When evaluating a life condition, people focus on it to the exclusion of other factors that affect well-being.

**Midwest vs. California Study (Schkade & Kahneman, 1998):**
- Students predicted life satisfaction for someone like them in California vs. Midwest
- Californians predicted to be happier (focus on weather)
- Actual life satisfaction: No significant difference

**The Focusing Illusion Principle:**
> "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it."

### 5.2 Tenure Decision Study

**Methodology:** Assistant professors predicted happiness following tenure decision.

**Predictions:**
- Tenure: Strong positive effect on happiness
- Denial: Strong negative effect

**Actual Outcomes (Gilbert et al., 1998):**
- Tenure: Small positive effect, quickly adapted
- Denial: Small negative effect, quickly adapted

**Misprediction Magnitude:** Predicted effects were 2-3 times larger than actual effects.

### 5.3 Adaptation Neglect

People systematically underestimate:
- How quickly they will adapt to positive changes (promotion, lottery)
- How quickly they will adapt to negative changes (disability, divorce)

**Consequence:** Overinvestment in changes that produce temporary hedonic effects.

## 6. Implications for Economic Theory

### 6.1 Welfare Analysis

If decision utility diverges from experienced utility:
- Revealed preference may not reveal welfare
- Willingness to pay may not measure value
- Market equilibria may not maximize welfare

### 6.2 When Do Errors Matter?

**Errors are most likely when:**
1. Decisions are infrequent (no learning opportunity)
2. Feedback is ambiguous (difficulty attributing outcomes)
3. Decisions involve novel experiences
4. Current emotional state is intense

**Errors matter less when:**
1. Markets provide arbitrage opportunities
2. Repeated decisions allow learning
3. Stakes justify careful deliberation

### 6.3 Policy Implications

Given systematic hedonic forecasting errors:
- Libertarian paternalism may improve welfare
- Default options can guide toward better outcomes
- Cooling-off periods can reduce hot-state decisions

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **Decision ≠ Experienced Utility:**
   - Choices reveal decision utility
   - Well-being requires experienced utility
   - Gap creates welfare losses

2. **Four Error Categories:**
   - Current state projection (projection bias, empathy gaps)
   - Context effects (joint vs. separate evaluation)
   - Memory biases (peak/end, duration neglect)
   - Adaptation neglect (focusing illusion)

3. **Systematic, Not Random:**
   - Errors are predictable
   - Direction of bias is known
   - Intervention design possible

4. **Learning Failures:**
   - Experience doesn't automatically correct
   - Peak/End rule persists
   - Focusing illusion is robust

5. **Paternalism Implications:**
   - Nudges can improve welfare
   - Default options matter
   - Cooling-off periods help

## Appendix: Taxonomy of Hedonic Forecasting Errors

### A.1 Complete Classification

| Category | Error Type | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Current State** | Projection Bias | Project current preferences to future | Hungry shopping |
| **Current State** | Hot-Cold Gap | Underestimate state influence | Withdrawal predictions |
| **Context** | Evaluability | Attributes need comparison | Dictionary study |
| **Context** | Joint/Separate | Mode affects preference | Preference reversals |
| **Memory** | Peak/End | Representative moments | Colonoscopy |
| **Memory** | Duration Neglect | Time ignored | Cold-pressor |
| **Adaptation** | Focusing Illusion | Overweight focal attribute | California study |
| **Adaptation** | Adaptation Neglect | Underestimate habituation | Tenure decisions |

### A.2 When Errors Compound

Multiple errors can reinforce:
- Hot state + Focusing illusion → Major overprediction
- Duration neglect + Peak/End → Preference for dominated options
- Projection bias + Adaptation neglect → Poor investment decisions
