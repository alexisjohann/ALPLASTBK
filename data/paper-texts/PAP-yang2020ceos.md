# How Do CEOs Make Strategy?

**Authors:** Mu-Jeung Yang (University of Washington and NBER), Michael Christensen (University of Toronto), Nicholas Bloom (Stanford University and NBER), Raffaella Sadun (Harvard Business School, NBER, and CEPR), Jan Rivkin (Harvard Business School)

**Year:** 2020

**Source:** NBER Working Paper No. 27952, October 2020

**JEL Classification:** L2, M1, M2, D22

**Keywords:** Strategy practices, CEO behavior, management practices, strategic planning, regression discontinuity design, MBA education, firm performance

## Abstract

We open the black box of CEO "strategizing" by collecting a novel dataset of over 262 CEO strategy practices. Strategy practices describe how CEOs organize and structure the strategy process within their firm, such as how firms develop, evaluate, and implement their strategies. We measure the overall "Strategy Practices Score" (SPS) using a survey instrument that captures 14 specific strategic practices grouped into three broad areas: Strategy Formalization, Strategy Development, and Strategy Implementation. We find that more structured strategy practices are strongly correlated with higher firm performance. Using a regression discontinuity design based on a 1983 curriculum reform at Harvard Business School, we find that exposure to the case-based strategy curriculum causally increases Strategy Formalization but decreases Strategy Implementation scores. Our findings suggest that MBA education may lead to a particular style of strategizing that emphasizes the formulation of strategy over its implementation.

## 1. Introduction

How do CEOs actually make strategy? Despite the centrality of strategy in the modern corporation, remarkably little systematic evidence exists on the practices that CEOs use to develop, evaluate, and implement strategic decisions. This paper aims to fill this gap by introducing and analyzing a novel survey instrument that captures the "strategy practices" of CEOs: the specific processes and routines they use to "do strategy."

The academic study of strategy has a long and distinguished history, spanning at least from Andrews (1971) and Ansoff (1965) through Porter (1980, 1985, 1996) and beyond. Yet much of this work has focused on the content of strategy -- what strategies firms should pursue -- rather than the process of strategy -- how firms actually go about strategizing. A parallel literature in organization studies, known as "strategy-as-practice," has examined the micro-level activities of strategists (Jarzabkowski, 2004; Jarzabkowski, Balogun and Seidl, 2007; Jarzabkowski and Spee, 2009; Whittington, 2006, 2007; Vaara and Whittington, 2012; Regnr, 2003). However, this literature has relied primarily on qualitative case studies and lacks the large-scale quantitative evidence needed to draw generalizable conclusions about how strategy practices relate to firm outcomes.

Our approach to measuring strategy practices is inspired by the World Management Survey (WMS) developed by Bloom and Van Reenen (2007), which has successfully measured management practices across thousands of firms worldwide. Just as the WMS measures the structured adoption of management practices -- such as lean manufacturing, performance management, and talent development -- we develop a parallel instrument to measure the structured adoption of strategy practices. We define strategy practices as the degree to which firms adopt structured processes for strategy formulation, development, and implementation.

Our survey instrument comprises 14 specific questions grouped into three broad categories. Strategy Formalization (F1-F3) captures whether firms have clear strategy statements, well-defined strategic scope, and articulated sources of competitive advantage. Strategy Development (D1-D6) measures how firms gather information, conduct strategy meetings, consider alternatives, and subject strategies to structured criticism. Strategy Implementation (I1-I5) assesses whether firms create implementation plans, review strategic progress, learn from outcomes, communicate strategy effectively, and manage resistance to change. Each question is scored on a 1-5 scale, where 1 represents the least structured approach and 5 represents the most structured approach.

We collect data from 262 CEOs, drawn primarily from an alumni sample of Harvard Business School (HBS) MBA graduates. The resulting Strategy Practices Score (SPS) exhibits substantial variation across firms, with an overall mean of 3.33 and a standard deviation of 0.52. Interestingly, we find that the three sub-components of strategy practices -- Formalization, Development, and Implementation -- are only weakly correlated with each other, suggesting that these represent genuinely distinct aspects of the strategizing process.

Our main empirical findings are threefold. First, we document a strong and robust positive correlation between strategy practices and firm size. A one-standard-deviation increase in SPS is associated with approximately 50 log points higher employment. This relationship is robust to controlling for industry, CEO characteristics, and noise controls, and is confirmed using administrative data from the U.S. Census Longitudinal Business Database (LBD).

Second, we find that strategy practices are positively correlated with firm growth. Using Census LBD data, we document that firms led by CEOs with higher SPS exhibit significantly faster employment growth and establishment growth over the subsequent five-year period.

Third, we exploit a natural experiment to investigate the causal effect of MBA education on strategy practices. In 1983, Harvard Business School undertook a major curriculum reform that significantly increased the emphasis on competitive strategy and the case method in its MBA program. Using a regression discontinuity design (RDD) centered on the 1983 reform, we find that exposure to the reformed curriculum causally increases Strategy Formalization scores but significantly decreases Strategy Implementation scores. This finding suggests that MBA education may shape strategizing styles in ways that emphasize formulation over execution -- a pattern that has important implications for management education.

This paper contributes to several streams of literature. First, we contribute to the growing empirical literature on management practices (Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al., 2013, 2014; Bloom, Sadun and Van Reenen, 2021; Scur et al., 2021) by extending the measurement approach to the domain of strategy. While the WMS has been extraordinarily productive in measuring operational management practices, it does not capture how firms approach strategic decision-making. Our survey instrument fills this gap.

Second, we contribute to the strategy-as-practice literature (Jarzabkowski, 2004; Whittington, 2006) by providing the first large-scale quantitative evidence on strategy practices and their correlates. While qualitative studies have provided rich descriptions of how individual strategists operate, they have not been able to establish generalizable patterns across large numbers of firms.

Third, we contribute to the literature on the effects of management education. While there is growing evidence on the effects of management training programs (Bloom et al., 2013; Giorcelli, 2019; Bianchi and Giorcelli, 2020), relatively little is known about the effects of elite MBA programs on managerial practice. Our RDD design provides credible causal evidence on how exposure to a strategy-focused curriculum shapes subsequent strategizing behavior.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes our data, including the survey design, data collection process, and descriptive statistics. Section 3 discusses the construction and properties of the Strategy Practices Score. Section 4 presents correlations between strategy practices and firm outcomes. Section 5 describes our identification strategy based on the 1983 HBS curriculum reform. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. Data

### 2.1 Survey Design

Our survey instrument measures strategy practices along 14 specific dimensions, grouped into three broad categories: Strategy Formalization (3 questions), Strategy Development (6 questions), and Strategy Implementation (5 questions). The instrument was developed through an iterative process involving extensive consultation with strategy scholars and practitioners.

The design of our survey instrument follows the general approach of the World Management Survey (WMS), developed by Bloom and Van Reenen (2007). Like the WMS, our instrument uses semi-structured interviews conducted by trained interviewers, with responses scored on a 1-5 scale based on a detailed scoring grid. However, while the WMS focuses on operational management practices (such as lean manufacturing, performance management, and talent management), our instrument focuses specifically on strategy practices -- the processes by which firms formulate, develop, and implement their strategies.

Each of the 14 questions is designed to capture a specific aspect of the strategy process. The questions are presented in Box 1, and the scoring grid is presented in Figure 1.

#### Box 1: The 14 Strategy Practice Questions

**Strategy Formalization**

**F1: Strategy Statement.** A company has a strategy statement if the company has defined its strategy and has clearly documented this strategy. Evidence includes a written strategy document or plan, or a clear verbal description of the company's strategy provided by the CEO during the interview. The best-performing firms have a concise, formalized strategy statement that summarizes the company's goals, scope, and competitive advantage.

**F2: Strategy Scope.** A company's strategic scope describes the boundaries of the company's strategy, including what businesses the company operates in, what markets it serves, and what it chooses not to do. The best-performing firms can clearly define the boundaries of their strategy, including which markets, products, or customer segments they deliberately avoid.

**F3: Strategic Differentiation.** A company's strategic differentiation describes how it differentiates itself from its competitors. The best-performing firms have a clear articulation of their competitive advantage and how they create value for customers in ways that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Strategy Development**

**D1: Proactivity and External Focus.** A company has proactive and externally focused strategy development if the company's leadership proactively monitors the external environment and uses external information to inform strategic decisions. The best-performing firms continuously scan the competitive landscape, monitor technological trends, and track regulatory developments, and they use this information systematically to update their strategies.

**D2: Information for Strategy Selection.** A company uses information for strategy selection if it uses systematic analytical tools and data to evaluate strategic options and make strategic decisions. The best-performing firms use sophisticated analytical frameworks, detailed market analysis, and financial modeling to evaluate strategic alternatives.

**D3: Strategy Meetings: Frequency.** This measures how frequently the company holds dedicated strategy meetings (as opposed to operational meetings). The best-performing firms hold regular, dedicated strategy sessions at least quarterly, with ad hoc sessions as needed to address emerging strategic issues.

**D4: Strategy Meetings: Involvement.** This measures who participates in strategy meetings and discussions. The best-performing firms involve a broad cross-section of the organization in strategy discussions, including senior management, middle management, and relevant functional specialists, to ensure diverse perspectives are represented.

**D5: Consideration of Alternatives.** A company considers alternatives if, when making strategic decisions, it systematically generates and evaluates multiple strategic options before selecting a course of action. The best-performing firms explicitly generate at least three strategic alternatives for major decisions and use structured evaluation criteria to compare them.

**D6: Structured Criticism.** A company uses structured criticism in its strategy process if it has formal mechanisms for challenging and stress-testing strategic proposals before they are adopted. The best-performing firms use techniques such as devil's advocacy, red-team exercises, or formal debate to identify weaknesses in proposed strategies before committing to them.

**Strategy Implementation**

**I1: Implementation Planning.** A company engages in implementation planning if, after a strategic decision is made, it creates a detailed plan for executing the strategy, including specific actions, responsibilities, timelines, and milestones. The best-performing firms create comprehensive implementation plans that translate strategic objectives into specific operational actions with clear accountability.

**I2: Strategy Review and Follow-ups.** A company conducts strategy reviews if it regularly monitors the progress of strategic initiatives and compares actual outcomes to planned milestones. The best-performing firms conduct formal quarterly reviews of strategic progress, with real consequences for deviations from plan.

**I3: Learning from Strategy Outcomes.** A company learns from strategy outcomes if it has a systematic process for evaluating the success or failure of past strategic decisions and incorporating these lessons into future strategic planning. The best-performing firms conduct formal post-mortems of all major strategic initiatives and maintain institutional memory of lessons learned.

**I4: Strategy Communication.** A company communicates its strategy effectively if it has processes to ensure that the strategy is understood and embraced throughout the organization. The best-performing firms use multiple communication channels to ensure that every employee understands the company's strategy and how their role contributes to it.

**I5: Resistance to Change.** This measures how effectively the company manages resistance to strategic change. The best-performing firms proactively identify potential sources of resistance, engage stakeholders early in the change process, and have formal change management processes to address opposition.

#### Figure 1: Scoring Grid

Each of the 14 strategy practice dimensions is scored on a scale from 1 to 5, with detailed descriptions at levels 1, 3, and 5 (scores of 2 and 4 are assigned as intermediate values):

**F1: Strategy Statement**
- Score 1: The interviewee is not able to summarize the company's strategy. The firm does not have a formal strategy document or plan.
- Score 3: The interviewee is able to provide a clear summary of the company's strategy, but the strategy is not formally documented.
- Score 5: The company has a formalized, concise strategy statement that includes goals, scope, and competitive advantage. The statement is written down and widely communicated.

**F2: Strategy Scope**
- Score 1: The company's strategy has no clear boundaries. The CEO cannot articulate what the company deliberately chooses not to do.
- Score 3: The CEO can describe the general boundaries of the company's strategy, but these boundaries are not formally documented or regularly reviewed.
- Score 5: The company has clearly defined strategic boundaries, including explicit decisions about which markets, products, and customer segments to avoid. These boundaries are documented and regularly reassessed.

**F3: Strategic Differentiation**
- Score 1: The CEO cannot clearly articulate what differentiates the company from competitors.
- Score 3: The CEO can describe the company's competitive advantage, but this advantage is not formally documented or systematically validated.
- Score 5: The company has a clear, documented articulation of its competitive advantage that is supported by evidence and regularly tested against competitive developments.

**D1: Proactivity and External Focus**
- Score 1: The company's strategy is primarily reactive, with little systematic monitoring of the external environment.
- Score 3: The company monitors the external environment periodically and uses this information in strategic discussions, but does not have a systematic process for environmental scanning.
- Score 5: The company continuously monitors the competitive landscape, technological trends, and regulatory developments through formal processes, and this information is systematically incorporated into strategic planning.

**D2: Information for Strategy Selection**
- Score 1: Strategic decisions are made primarily based on intuition or CEO judgment, with little formal analysis.
- Score 3: The company uses some analytical tools and data in strategic decision-making, but analysis is not systematic or comprehensive.
- Score 5: The company uses sophisticated analytical frameworks, detailed market analysis, and financial modeling to evaluate all major strategic decisions. Analysis is comprehensive, systematic, and documented.

**D3: Strategy Meetings: Frequency**
- Score 1: The company does not hold dedicated strategy meetings. Strategy is discussed only in the context of operational meetings.
- Score 3: The company holds dedicated strategy meetings approximately annually.
- Score 5: The company holds regular, dedicated strategy sessions at least quarterly, with ad hoc sessions as needed to address emerging strategic issues.

**D4: Strategy Meetings: Involvement**
- Score 1: Strategy is developed exclusively by the CEO or a very small group, with no broader organizational involvement.
- Score 3: Strategy meetings involve the senior management team, but not broader organizational perspectives.
- Score 5: Strategy discussions involve a broad cross-section of the organization, including senior and middle management and relevant functional specialists.

**D5: Consideration of Alternatives**
- Score 1: The company does not systematically consider alternative strategies. Strategic decisions are typically made without generating or evaluating multiple options.
- Score 3: The company considers alternatives for major strategic decisions, but the process for generating and evaluating alternatives is informal.
- Score 5: The company explicitly generates at least three strategic alternatives for major decisions and uses structured evaluation criteria to compare them.

**D6: Structured Criticism**
- Score 1: There is no formal process for challenging or stress-testing strategic proposals. Dissent is discouraged.
- Score 3: Some informal mechanisms exist for questioning strategic proposals, but there is no formal process for structured criticism.
- Score 5: The company uses formal techniques such as devil's advocacy, red-team exercises, or structured debate to challenge all major strategic proposals before adoption.

**I1: Implementation Planning**
- Score 1: After a strategic decision is made, there is no formal plan for how the strategy will be implemented.
- Score 3: The company creates basic implementation plans, but these lack specific milestones, timelines, or accountability mechanisms.
- Score 5: The company creates comprehensive implementation plans with specific actions, responsibilities, timelines, milestones, and accountability mechanisms for every major strategic initiative.

**I2: Strategy Review and Follow-ups**
- Score 1: There is no formal process for reviewing the progress of strategic initiatives.
- Score 3: The company periodically reviews strategic progress, but reviews are infrequent or informal.
- Score 5: The company conducts formal quarterly reviews of all strategic initiatives, comparing actual progress to planned milestones, with real consequences for deviations.

**I3: Learning from Strategy Outcomes**
- Score 1: The company does not systematically evaluate the outcomes of past strategic decisions.
- Score 3: The company evaluates outcomes of some strategic decisions, but the process is informal and lessons are not systematically captured.
- Score 5: The company conducts formal post-mortems of all major strategic initiatives and maintains institutional memory of lessons learned, which are incorporated into future strategy processes.

**I4: Strategy Communication**
- Score 1: Strategy is not communicated beyond the senior leadership team.
- Score 3: Strategy is communicated to the organization, but communication is infrequent or limited to a single channel.
- Score 5: The company uses multiple communication channels to ensure that every employee understands the company's strategy and how their role contributes to strategic objectives.

**I5: Resistance to Change**
- Score 1: The company has no formal process for managing resistance to strategic change. Resistance is dealt with reactively and ad hoc.
- Score 3: The company recognizes the importance of managing resistance and makes some effort to engage stakeholders, but does not have a formal change management process.
- Score 5: The company proactively identifies potential sources of resistance, engages stakeholders early in the change process, and has formal change management processes to address opposition effectively.

### 2.2 Data Collection

The data were collected through semi-structured telephone interviews conducted between 2012 and 2015 with executives serving as CEO or in CEO-equivalent roles. The sample was drawn from a pool of approximately 1,000 HBS alumni from the MBA classes of 1974-2001. Of these, 779 were contacted, and 262 completed interviews, yielding a response rate of approximately 33.6%.

Each interview lasted approximately 45-60 minutes and was conducted by one of a team of trained interviewers. Interviewers followed a detailed interview guide but were allowed to ask probing follow-up questions to elicit detailed descriptions of the executive's strategy practices. Based on the interviewee's responses, the interviewer scored each of the 14 strategy practice dimensions on a 1-5 scale using the scoring grid presented in Figure 1.

To ensure consistency across interviewers, we implemented several quality control measures. First, all interviewers underwent extensive training that included practice interviews and calibration exercises. Second, a subset of interviews was double-scored by a second interviewer, and inter-rater reliability was assessed. Third, the interviews were reviewed by a senior researcher to ensure that scoring was consistent with the scoring grid.

The interview also collected information on firm characteristics (including industry, size, age, ownership structure, and geographic scope), CEO characteristics (including tenure, age, and educational background), and recent strategic decisions (including the type, timing, and perceived success of the three most significant strategic decisions made in the past five years).

### 2.3 Noise Controls

Following the approach of Bloom and Van Reenen (2007), we include a set of "noise controls" in all regressions to account for factors that might influence the measured strategy score but do not reflect actual differences in strategy practices. These noise controls include:

- **Interviewer fixed effects:** Different interviewers may have different scoring tendencies. Including interviewer fixed effects controls for systematic differences across interviewers.
- **Interview duration:** Longer interviews may produce higher scores because the interviewer has more information on which to base the scoring. Controlling for interview duration accounts for this potential bias.
- **Day-of-week and time-of-day fixed effects:** Interview quality and scoring may vary with the time of the interview. These fixed effects control for such temporal variation.
- **Interview reliability indicator:** The interviewer's assessment of the overall reliability and quality of the interview, scored on a 1-5 scale. This controls for variation in the quality of information obtained during the interview.

Including these noise controls is important because they account for measurement error that could bias our estimates. In practice, we find that including noise controls typically has modest effects on the estimated coefficients but reduces the residual variance, thereby increasing the precision of our estimates.

### 2.5 Descriptive Results

Figure 2 presents the distribution of the overall SPS and its three sub-components. The overall SPS distribution is approximately normal, centered around 3.33 with moderate dispersion. The sub-component distributions reveal interesting heterogeneity: Strategy Formalization scores tend to be higher on average (mean 3.48) than Strategy Development (mean 3.26) or Strategy Implementation (mean 3.27).

A notable feature of the data is that the three sub-components are only modestly correlated with each other. The correlation between Formalization and Development is 0.35, between Formalization and Implementation is 0.28, and between Development and Implementation is 0.52. This pattern suggests that strategy practices are multidimensional and that firms that excel at one aspect of strategizing do not necessarily excel at others.

Figure 3 shows the relationship between SPS and firm size (measured as log employment). There is a clear positive relationship: larger firms tend to have higher strategy practice scores. This relationship holds for all three sub-components, although it is strongest for Strategy Formalization and weakest for Strategy Implementation.

## 3. Strategy Practices Scores

In this section, we examine the properties of the Strategy Practices Score in more detail, including its factor structure, internal consistency, and the relationships among its components.

### Factor Analysis and Principal Components

To examine the underlying structure of our 14 strategy practice questions, we conduct both exploratory factor analysis and principal components analysis. The factor analysis reveals three distinct factors that correspond closely to our a priori categorization into Strategy Formalization, Strategy Development, and Strategy Implementation. The first factor loads most heavily on the Formalization questions (F1-F3), the second on the Development questions (D1-D6), and the third on the Implementation questions (I1-I5). This alignment between the empirical factor structure and our a priori classification provides reassurance that the three categories capture genuinely distinct dimensions of the strategy process rather than arbitrary groupings.

Principal components analysis confirms this three-factor structure. The first three principal components account for approximately 55% of the total variance in the 14 questions. The first component has roughly equal loadings across all questions, representing an "overall quality of strategizing" dimension. The second and third components distinguish between the different aspects of strategy practices, with the second component contrasting Formalization against Implementation and the third component isolating Development practices. The scree plot shows a clear elbow at three components, further supporting the three-dimensional structure of the data.

### Construction of the Strategy Practices Score

We construct the overall Strategy Practices Score (SPS) as the unweighted average of the 14 individual question scores. This approach has the advantage of simplicity and transparency. We have verified that alternative weighting schemes -- including weights derived from the principal components analysis and equal weighting within each sub-component -- produce very similar results. The correlation between the unweighted SPS and the first principal component is 0.94, indicating that the simple average captures essentially the same information as the statistically optimal linear combination.

For the three sub-component scores, we similarly take unweighted averages: Strategy Formalization is the average of F1-F3, Strategy Development is the average of D1-D6, and Strategy Implementation is the average of I1-I5.

### Internal Consistency

We assess the internal consistency of each sub-component using Cronbach's alpha. The alphas are 0.72 for Strategy Formalization, 0.71 for Strategy Development, and 0.78 for Strategy Implementation, all of which are above conventional thresholds for acceptable reliability (typically 0.70). The alpha for the overall SPS is 0.82, indicating good internal consistency for the composite measure.

### Relationships Among Sub-components

As noted in Section 2.5, the three sub-components are only modestly correlated with each other. This pattern is consistent with the view that strategy formulation, development, and implementation represent genuinely distinct organizational capabilities. A firm may have a clear, well-articulated strategy (high Formalization) but lack the processes to effectively implement it (low Implementation), or vice versa.

This finding has important implications for both research and practice. From a research perspective, it suggests that aggregate measures of "strategic planning" that combine different aspects of the strategy process may obscure important variation. From a practical perspective, it suggests that improving strategy practices may require distinct interventions targeting different aspects of the process.

### 4.2 Management Practices

An important question is whether strategy practices simply reflect broader organizational quality -- i.e., whether firms with good strategy practices also have good management practices, and whether the two are measuring the same underlying construct. To investigate this, we examine the relationship between our SPS and the World Management Survey (WMS) management practices score for the subset of firms in our sample that were also covered by the WMS.

We find that strategy practices and management practices are positively correlated, with a correlation coefficient of approximately 0.40. However, the correlation is far from perfect, suggesting that the two constructs capture distinct aspects of organizational capability. Moreover, in regressions that include both SPS and WMS scores as predictors of firm size, both remain statistically significant, indicating that strategy practices contain information about firm performance beyond what is captured by management practices alone.

This finding is important because it demonstrates that our strategy practices measure is not simply a repackaging of the management practices measured by the WMS. Rather, it captures a genuinely distinct dimension of organizational capability -- the structured approach to strategic decision-making.

### 4.3 Firm Growth

Table 3 presents results using administrative data from the U.S. Census Bureau's Longitudinal Business Database (LBD). The LBD provides comprehensive data on employment, establishments, and other firm characteristics for the universe of U.S. firms with paid employees. Matching our survey data to the LBD allows us to examine the relationship between strategy practices and firm outcomes using objective, administrative data rather than self-reported survey data.

The matching process yields a sample of approximately 200 firms for which we have both survey data and LBD data. We use this matched sample to examine the relationship between SPS and two measures of firm growth: employment growth and establishment growth, both measured over a five-year period following the survey.

Following Davis, Haltiwanger, and Schuh (1996), we measure growth rates using the symmetric growth rate formula, which bounds growth rates between -2 and 2:

Growth_it = (X_it - X_it-1) / (0.5 * (X_it + X_it-1))

This formula has the advantage of treating expansions and contractions symmetrically and accommodating firm entry and exit.

## 5. Identification Strategy

The correlations documented in Section 4 are suggestive but may reflect reverse causality (larger, more successful firms can afford more structured strategy processes) or omitted variables (unobserved CEO or firm characteristics that drive both strategy practices and outcomes). To address these concerns, we exploit a natural experiment: the 1983 curriculum reform at Harvard Business School.

### 5.1 Background on the 1983 HBS Reform

In 1983, Harvard Business School implemented a major reform of its MBA curriculum. The reform was led by Professor Michael Porter, who at the time was developing his influential frameworks for industry analysis and competitive strategy. The reform significantly increased the emphasis on competitive strategy in the MBA curriculum, introducing new required courses and case studies focused on strategic analysis, competitive positioning, and industry dynamics.

Prior to the reform, the HBS MBA curriculum included strategy content, but it was less prominent and less systematically organized. The reform made competitive strategy a central pillar of the MBA experience, with a dedicated required course in the first year and expanded elective offerings in the second year. The reform also increased the use of the case method for teaching strategy, with new cases developed specifically to illustrate Porter's analytical frameworks.

The timing and nature of the reform provide a useful source of exogenous variation in exposure to formal strategy training. MBA students who graduated in 1983 or later were exposed to the reformed curriculum, while those who graduated before 1983 were not. Because our sample includes HBS MBA alumni from the classes of 1974-2001, we have substantial numbers of observations on both sides of the discontinuity.

Figure 4 shows the distribution of MBA graduation years in our sample. The distribution is roughly symmetric around the mid-1980s, with adequate observations in the years immediately before and after the 1983 reform. This provides the variation we need for the RDD analysis.

### 5.2 RDD Design

We implement the RDD using both non-parametric and parametric approaches. Our baseline specification is:

X_i = f(C_i) + beta_1 * 1{C_i >= 1983} + epsilon_i

where X_i is the strategy practice score (or sub-component), C_i is the MBA graduation year, f(C_i) is a smooth function of the running variable, 1{C_i >= 1983} is an indicator for graduating in 1983 or later, and beta_1 is the parameter of interest -- the causal effect of exposure to the reformed curriculum on strategy practices.

For the non-parametric approach, we use local linear regressions with optimal bandwidth selection following Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012). We report three non-parametric specifications:

1. **IK-NN:** Bandwidth selection using nearest-neighbor variance estimation, implemented by Calonico, Cattaneo and Titiunik (2017).
2. **IK-PI:** Bandwidth selection using analytic heteroscedasticity-robust plug-in variance estimates, as discussed by MacKinnon (2013).
3. **FIK:** An alternative implementation by Nichols (2011) and Fuji, Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2009).

The optimal bandwidth selection results in a bandwidth of approximately 3 years, implying the use of approximately 36 observations in the estimation. For the parametric approach, we use the entire MBA sample (185 observations) with quadratic and cubic specifications for f(C_i).

Table 5 presents the RDD results. The key findings are as follows.

**Panel A: Strategy Formalization.** Exposure to the reformed curriculum significantly increases Strategy Formalization scores. The baseline IK-NN estimate is 0.845 (significant at the 5% level), implying that the reform increased Formalization by approximately 0.85 points on the 1-5 scale. This effect is large, corresponding to approximately 1.2 standard deviations of the Formalization sub-component. The result is robust across all five specifications.

**Panel B: Strategy Development.** The effect on Strategy Development is small and statistically insignificant across all specifications. The baseline IK-NN estimate is -0.025, with a standard error of 0.250. This suggests that the curriculum reform had little effect on how firms conduct the development phase of strategy.

**Panel C: Strategy Implementation.** Exposure to the reformed curriculum significantly decreases Strategy Implementation scores. The baseline IK-NN estimate is -1.165 (significant at the 1% level), implying that the reform decreased Implementation by approximately 1.2 points on the 1-5 scale. This is a large negative effect, corresponding to approximately 1.9 standard deviations of the Implementation sub-component. The result is robust in the non-parametric specifications, although it is weaker in the parametric specifications.

### 5.3 Alternative Explanations

We consider several alternative explanations for our RDD results and provide evidence against them.

**Age effects.** One concern is that the discontinuity at 1983 may capture age effects rather than the effect of the curriculum reform, since CEOs who graduated later are younger. We address this in two ways. First, we include a smooth function of graduation year (the running variable), which by construction controls for any smooth age effects. Second, we show that the discontinuity is sharp at 1983, which is inconsistent with smooth age effects that would produce a gradual change.

**Cohort effects.** A related concern is that the reform coincided with broader changes in the business environment (such as the economic recovery of the mid-1980s) that may have affected all MBA graduates, not just those who attended HBS. We cannot fully rule out this possibility, but we note that our RDD design compares graduates from immediately before and after 1983, minimizing the scope for such confounds.

**Selection effects.** If the reform changed the composition of students admitted to HBS (for example, by attracting students more interested in strategy), this could bias our results. However, the reform was implemented after the entering class of 1981 had already been admitted, providing a clear source of exogeneity. Moreover, admission decisions at HBS are made approximately 18 months before graduation, further limiting the scope for selection effects.

**Heterogeneous treatment effects.** We examine whether the RDD results differ across subgroups of firms. We find that the positive effect on Formalization is strongest for CEOs of larger firms, while the negative effect on Implementation is strongest for CEOs of smaller firms. This pattern is consistent with the interpretation that the reformed curriculum taught a particular style of strategizing that was better suited to large-firm contexts where formal strategy processes are more important.

## 6. Discussion and Conclusion

This paper introduces a novel survey instrument for measuring the strategy practices of CEOs and provides the first large-scale quantitative evidence on how CEOs "do strategy." Our findings can be summarized in three main points.

First, strategy practices vary substantially across firms and are multidimensional. The three sub-components of strategy practices -- Formalization, Development, and Implementation -- are only modestly correlated with each other, suggesting that firms' approaches to different aspects of the strategy process are largely independent. This finding challenges the notion that there is a single dimension of "strategic planning quality" and instead suggests a more nuanced view of how firms approach strategizing.

Second, more structured strategy practices are strongly correlated with better firm outcomes. Firms with higher SPS are significantly larger, grow faster, and make more proactive strategic decisions. While these correlations do not establish causality, they are consistent with the view that structured strategy practices contribute to firm performance. The magnitude of the associations is economically meaningful: a one-standard-deviation increase in SPS is associated with approximately 65% higher employment and 6-7 percentage points faster employment growth.

Third, MBA education causally affects strategy practices, but in a nuanced way. Exposure to the Porter-reformed HBS curriculum significantly increases Strategy Formalization but decreases Strategy Implementation. This finding suggests that MBA education may lead to a particular style of strategizing that emphasizes the articulation and analysis of strategy at the expense of its execution. This pattern resonates with a long-standing criticism of MBA programs -- that they produce graduates who are skilled at analyzing strategic problems but less effective at implementing solutions (Mintzberg, 2004).

Several caveats are in order. First, our sample is drawn primarily from HBS MBA alumni, which limits the external validity of our findings. HBS MBA graduates are likely to lead larger, more successful firms than the average CEO, and their strategy practices may not be representative of the broader population of firms. Second, our survey instrument, while more comprehensive than previous measures, inevitably captures only certain aspects of the complex phenomenon of strategizing. Third, our RDD design, while providing credible causal evidence on the effect of the curriculum reform, is based on relatively small samples in the neighborhood of the discontinuity, which limits statistical precision.

Despite these limitations, we believe that this paper makes several contributions. First, it provides the first large-scale quantitative evidence on the strategy practices of CEOs, demonstrating that these practices can be measured systematically and exhibit meaningful variation across firms. Second, it demonstrates that strategy practices are associated with firm outcomes, even in observational data, and that these associations are confirmed using administrative data from the U.S. Census. Third, it highlights the potential for education interventions to influence strategic management practices, while also suggesting that such interventions may have unintended consequences -- in this case, improving formulation at the expense of implementation.

Future work could extend this analysis in several directions. First, it would be valuable to replicate these findings in different contexts and with different sampling frames, particularly in non-U.S. settings and among smaller firms. Second, longitudinal data on strategy practices would help establish the direction of causality more convincingly and would allow researchers to study how strategy practices evolve over time. Third, a deeper investigation of the mechanisms through which strategy practices affect firm outcomes would be valuable, particularly in terms of the quality of strategic decisions as opposed to the quality of strategic processes. Fourth, the disconnect between Formalization and Implementation practices, and the finding that MBA education may widen this gap, deserves further investigation, as it has important implications for the design of management education curricula.

### Appendix A: Details on the RDD Estimation

Our regression discontinuity approach estimates the effect of exposure to the 1983 HBS curriculum reform on strategy practices. The running variable is the MBA cohort year, centered at 1983. The treatment is defined as graduating in 1983 or later.

#### Non-parametric local regression estimates

For the non-parametric approach, we use local linear regressions with optimal bandwidth selections. We report two main non-parametric specifications:

1. **IK-NN (Imbens-Kalyanaraman with Nearest-Neighbor variance estimation):** This is our baseline specification. It uses bandwidth selection by Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012) using a nearest-neighbor variance estimation, denoted by IK-NN, and is implemented by the code by Calonico, Cattaneo and Titiunik (2017).

2. **IK-PI (Imbens-Kalyanaraman with Plug-In variance estimation):** This uses the same bandwidth selection procedure with analytic heteroscedasticity-robust "plug-in" variance estimates as discussed by MacKinnon (2013) and is implemented by Calonico, Cattaneo and Titiunik (2017).

The optimal bandwidth selections result in a bandwidth of approximately 3 years, implying the use of 36 observations in the estimation.

#### Parametric or "global regression" estimates

This approach uses the entire sample instead of only focusing on a neighborhood around the discontinuity, but also assumes that the functional form for the regression is known. In particular, starting with the following regression:

X_i = f(C_i) + beta_1 * 1_{C_i >= 1983} + epsilon_i

the parametric approach specifies the function f(C_i) as different continuous functions. This approach has the advantage of being more precise, as the whole data is used, but that comes at the cost of increased bias in the estimates, as observations that are far away from the discontinuity can still influence the estimates. Furthermore, the more complex and flexible the functional form of f(C_i) is chosen, the more the noise in the data will influence the estimates, thereby eventually rendering the estimates less precise again and increasing the standard errors.

The last two columns of Appendix Table 3 report the results using the parametric specifications using robust standard errors. Generally, the signs of estimates are consistent with our baseline results, although some of the results are much weaker in the parametric specifications. An exception are the results on Formalization (F2+F3), which tend to be slightly stronger in the parametric specifications.
