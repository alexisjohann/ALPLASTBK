## Abstract

While the significance of narrative thinking has been increasingly recognized by social scientists, very little empirical research has documented its consequences for economically significant outcomes. The current paper addresses this gap in one important domain: valuations. In three experiments, participants were given the opportunity to sell an item they owned (mug in Study 1, hat in studies 2 and 3) using an incentive-compatible procedure (multiple price list). Prior to making selling decisions, participants were randomly assigned to either a narrative treatment, in which they were asked to tell the story of their item, or a list treatment, in which they were asked to list the characteristics of their item. The narrative treatment led to significantly higher selling prices and increased rates of participants refusing all offered prices. We further explore potential mechanisms, and the impact of different types of narratives, by analyzing self-reported classifications of, and employing natural language processing techniques on, participants' narratives.

## I. Introduction

### The Importance of Narratives

A narrative, or a story, places selected events on a timeline and establishes causal links between them (Bruner, 1990; Pennington and Hastie, 1991). A narrative provides "structured higher-order mental representations summarizing causal, temporal, analogical, and valence structure" (Johnson et al., 2023).

From Egyptian Hieroglyphics to newspapers and Tweets, narratives have been central to the creation of religion and culture and have played a key role in shaping popular opinions.

### Why Study Narrative Thinking?

The human inclination to think in terms of narratives has been posited to:
- Promote efficient communication (Dautenhahn, 2002)
- Aid in imagining counterfactuals of potential actions (Abbott, 2000)
- Confer meaning to events by making them part of a whole (Polkinghorne, 1991)
- Increase sense of control by providing explanations for events (Sarbin, 1986)
- Provide an approach to decision making in situations of 'radical uncertainty' (Johnson et al., 2023)

### Research Gap

Despite widespread recognition of their importance, there has been a dearth of systematic research dealing with narratives. Most prior work focused on:
- Impact of narrative thinking on judgments (e.g., juror decisions)
- Financial predictions
- Persuasion effectiveness

**This paper explores the effect of narrative thinking on VALUATIONS - of everyday objects.**

## II. Related Literature

### Psychology: Story Model (Pennington & Hastie)

Jurors reach verdicts by constructing causal stories. Manipulating the ease with which stories can be formed shifts research participants' verdicts in directions consistent with the narratives they received or were encouraged to create.

### Marketing: Narrative Advertising

Narrative evaluations are more likely when an ad portrays a story, as opposed to presenting product attribute information. Ads providing narratives generate:
- More favorable cognitive responses
- Warm feelings
- Positive ad and brand attitudes

### Economics: Recent Work

Recent economic work on narratives includes:
- **B´enabou et al. (2019):** Theoretical models
- **Eliaz & Spiegler (2020):** Narrative competition
- **Shiller (2017):** "Narrative epidemics" in popular discourse
- **Schwartzstein & Sunderam (2021):** Persuasive capabilities of narratives

### Decision Research: Source Dependence

**Loewenstein & Issacharoff (1994):** The value placed on an object depends on the sequence of events that led to its acquisition. Students who thought they received a mug due to their performance specified higher selling prices than those who thought they received it by chance.

**Query Theory (Johnson et al., 2007):** Different types of decisions naturally elicit different "queries" - explicit considerations taken into account by decision-makers.

## III. Potential Mechanisms

### 1. Attention-Based Mechanism

Recounting a narrative may shift sellers' attention:
- **FROM:** Objective characteristics (cost, usage, profit)
- **TO:** Qualitative features and idiosyncratic connections (people, memories, sentiment)

### 2. Value Uncertainty

Holistic considerations are more difficult to quantify, increasing seller's uncertainty about correct valuation. Value uncertainty has been identified as a potential mechanism driving the endowment effect.

### 3. Immediate Emotions

The act of generating a narrative might trigger immediate emotions that produce a change in valuation. However, the authors find this unlikely given that mood was not affected by treatment.

### 4. Anticipated Emotions

Generating a narrative might affect emotions people anticipate feeling contingent upon selling:
- Guilt from parting with an object associated with important people
- Loss of future memory triggers

## IV. Study 1: Significant Mug (Prolific)

### Design

- **N = 469** valid Prolific workers (US census representative)
- **Item:** Personally significant mug (self-selected, photo uploaded)
- **Manipulation:** Narrative vs. List treatment
- **DV:** WTA via Multiple Price List ($1-$100)

### Manipulation

**Narrative:** "Please tell the story of your mug - e.g., how you came to own it"
**List:** "Please list the characteristics of the mug"

### Results

**Extensive Margin (Refusing All Prices):**
| Condition | % Refusing |
|-----------|------------|
| Narrative | 33% |
| List | 22% |
| **Effect** | **+10.6 pp (47.7%)** |

**Intensive Margin (Sellers Only):**
No significant difference in mean WTA among those who agreed to sell.

**Overall Effect (Tobit):**
Narrative effect = **$10.46 (17.1%)** increase in minimal selling prices

## V. Study 2: Hats (MTurk)

### Design

- **N = 914** valid MTurk workers
- **2x2 Design:** Narrative/List × Significant/Miscellaneous hat
- **DV:** WTA via Multiple Price List ($1-$300)

### Results: Miscellaneous Hat

| Measure | List | Narrative | Effect |
|---------|------|-----------|--------|
| % Non-sellers | 5.4% | 15.1% | **+9.7 pp** |
| Mean WTA (sellers) | $35.08 | $51.91 | **+$16.83** |
| Tobit WTA | $51.31 | $95.43 | **+$44.12** |

### Results: Significant Hat

| Measure | List | Narrative | Effect |
|---------|------|-----------|--------|
| % Non-sellers | 13.7% | 26.6% | **+12.9 pp** |
| Mean WTA (sellers) | $74.95 | $85.28 | +$10.33 (ns) |
| Tobit WTA | $114.6 | $162.4 | **+$47.78** |

### Significance × Narrative Interaction

No significant interaction - narrative effect was stable across significant and miscellaneous hats.

## VI. Study 3: Spontaneous Hat (Field)

### Design

- **N = 146** participants recruited on campus (CMU and U. Pittsburgh)
- **3 Conditions:** Narrative, List, Blank (no writing)
- **Item:** Hat they were currently wearing
- **Immediate transaction:** Cash payment, hat taken on the spot

### Results

| Condition | % Non-sellers | Mean WTA (sellers) |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Blank | 16% | $32.71 |
| List | 11% | $39.86 |
| Narrative | 12% | $48.56 |

**Key Finding:** List treatment did NOT suppress prices below Blank - confirming that the narrative effect is driven by the narrative treatment **raising** prices.

## VII. Mechanism Analysis

### Self-Reported Item Classifications (Pooled Studies 1-3)

**Table 4 Results - Factors Increasing WTA:**

| Factor | Effect on WTA (SD) | p |
|--------|-------------------|---|
| Related to another person | +0.461 | < 0.001 |
| Custom-made | +0.206 | < 0.05 |
| Self-made | +0.826 | < 0.01 |
| Ownership years | +0.020/year | < 0.01 |
| Cost proxy | +0.012/$ | < 0.001 |
| Gift | +0.394 | < 0.05 |
| Inherited | +0.475 | < 0.01 |

**Factors NOT affecting WTA:**
- Career-related: -0.097 (ns)
- Used often: +0.014 (ns)

### Mediation Analysis (Study 3)

**Price Considerations Affected by Narrative Treatment:**

| Consideration | Treatment Effect | Effect on WTA | % Mediated |
|---------------|------------------|---------------|------------|
| Monetary profit | -0.312* (reduced) | -$9.75*** | **30%** |
| Looks | -0.300* (reduced) | ns | ~0% |
| Related people | +0.240 (increased) | +$8.92*** | **23%** |
| Sentiment | +0.225 (increased) | +$15.63*** | **32%** |
| Memories | +0.252 (increased) | +$13.59*** | **31%** |

**Grouped Associative Considerations (People + Memories + Sentiment):**
Explains approximately **42%** of the narrative treatment effect.

### What Did NOT Drive the Effect

| Factor | Treatment Effect | Mediates? |
|--------|------------------|-----------|
| Current mood | 0.001 (ns) | No |
| Emotional connection intensity | -0.044 (ns) | No |
| Cost consideration | -0.043 (ns) | No |
| Replacement cost consideration | +0.044 (ns) | No |
| Gut feeling | -0.105 (ns) | No |

## VIII. Natural Language Processing Analysis

### Bag-of-Words Analysis

**Words Predicting HIGHER WTA:**
- People-related: girlfriend, daughter, son, mom, wife, mother, friend
- Sentiment: feel, mean, remember
- Events: passed, away

**Words Predicting LOWER WTA:**
- Practical: wear, work, purchase, shop, make, good

### Text Semantics (BERT Models)

**Emotions and WTA:**

| Emotion | Effect (1 SD) | p |
|---------|---------------|---|
| Sadness | +0.177 SD | < 0.10 |
| Anger | +0.065 SD | < 0.05 |
| Disgust | -0.067 SD | < 0.10 |
| Joy | +0.094 SD | ns |

**Unexpected Finding:** Sadder narratives predicted HIGHER WTA (almost double the effect of joy). This contradicts simple mood-based explanations.

**Sentiment:** No significant effect of positive vs. negative sentiment on WTA.

## IX. Key Findings Summary

### Overall Treatment Effects

| Metric | List | Narrative | Effect |
|--------|------|-----------|--------|
| % Refusing all prices | 13.7% | 23.8% | **+75%** |
| Mean WTA (sellers) | $50.0 | $58.2 | **+16%** |
| Censored WTA | $68.8 | $93.4 | **+36%** |

### Mechanism Conclusions

1. **Attention-based mechanism SUPPORTED:**
   - Narratives shift attention FROM profit/cost TO people/memories/sentiment
   - 42% of effect explained by associative considerations

2. **Mood-based mechanism NOT SUPPORTED:**
   - No treatment effect on current mood
   - No treatment effect on emotional connection intensity
   - Valence of narrative (happy/sad) does not drive effect

3. **Value uncertainty mechanism POSSIBLE:**
   - Narrative effect robust to inclusion of all covariates
   - Suggests baseline price increase regardless of item attributes

## X. Discussion

### Implications for Economic Behavior

1. **Gift-Giving:** Classic theory predicts dead-weight loss from gifts vs. cash. But if being given as a gift provides a more favorable narrative, gifts may generate surplus. (Consistent with Kube et al., 2012)

2. **Context Markups:** Increased WTP for products in souvenir stores, merchandise stands reflects added value from narrative associations with location/event.

3. **Persuasion:** Non-informative ads triggering associative responses can be more effective than informative ads (Bertrand et al., 2010).

### Practical Applications

- **Negotiation:** Sellers can inflate prices by telling stories; buyers can deflate by analytical inspection
- **Marketing:** Trade-off between listing attractive features (analytical) vs. narrative presentation
- **Management:** Design communications considering narrative vs. analytical framing

### Limitations

1. Self-selected items introduce noise
2. Self-generated narratives introduce variance
3. Effect magnitude may be specific to items used (mugs, hats)
4. Unknown if effects generalize to high-stakes items (cars, houses)
