## Abstract

Much behavioral public policy has been directed at encouraging individuals to change their behaviors through "nudge" interventions, which attempt to influence the choices that individuals make (choice architecture), provide individuals with information or decision-aids, or seek to motivate behavioral change through financial or other incentives. While this "individual frame" (i-frame) perspective focuses on the individual, the "systemic frame" (s-frame) draws attention to systems that affect people's behavior. These include institutions, markets, legal structures, physical environment, and social norms. Our thesis is that many of the problems behavioral policy attempts to address are systems problems that require s-frame solutions and, moreover, that i-frame interventions are rarely sufficient to solve such problems and can even be counterproductive. We make this argument across 11 policy areas: climate change, obesity, retirement savings, plastic waste, healthcare, education, discrimination, privacy, misinformation, opioids, and gun violence. We consider possible reasons for the dominance of the i-frame in behavioral policy, which include the influence of large economic actors that benefit from i-frame solutions that leave their own behaviors unaddressed.

## I. Introduction

### 1. The Rise of Behavioral Public Policy

Behavioral economics has transformed our understanding of human decision-making. This transformation has led to a new approach to public policy, sometimes referred to as "behavioral public policy" or "nudge," which attempts to harness insights from behavioral economics to design policies that help people make better decisions.

The rise of behavioral public policy has been dramatic. Nudge units have been established in governments around the world. The approach has been applied to domains including health, finance, energy, education, and beyond.

### 2. The I-Frame and S-Frame Distinction

We distinguish between two frames for thinking about behavioral policy problems:

**The Individual Frame (I-Frame):**
- Focuses on changing individual behavior
- Assumes individuals are the locus of the problem
- Interventions target individual decision-making
- Examples: nudges, information campaigns, financial incentives

**The Systemic Frame (S-Frame):**
- Focuses on changing systems that shape behavior
- Assumes systems create or enable problematic behaviors
- Interventions target institutions, markets, laws, environments
- Examples: regulations, taxes, infrastructure changes, institutional reforms

### 3. Our Central Thesis

We argue that:
1. Many problems that behavioral policy addresses are primarily systems problems
2. I-frame solutions are often insufficient to solve systems problems
3. I-frame solutions can be counterproductive by distracting from s-frame solutions
4. The dominance of i-frame thinking may serve the interests of economic actors who benefit from the status quo

## II. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Why Systems Matter

Human behavior is profoundly shaped by the systems in which it is embedded. These systems include:

**Institutional Systems:**
- Market structures and incentives
- Legal frameworks and regulations
- Organizational policies and procedures
- Educational institutions

**Physical Systems:**
- Built environment and infrastructure
- Transportation systems
- Food systems and supply chains
- Energy systems

**Social Systems:**
- Social norms and expectations
- Cultural practices
- Information ecosystems
- Political structures

### 2.2 The Limits of Individual Behavior Change

Individual behavior change faces several fundamental limitations:

**1. Bounded Rationality:**
Even with perfect information, individuals have limited cognitive capacity to process complex decisions.

**2. Coordination Problems:**
Many social problems require coordinated action that individual choice cannot achieve.

**3. Power Asymmetries:**
Individuals often lack power to change systems even when they recognize problems.

**4. Cumulative Effects:**
Individual actions are often trivial compared to systemic impacts.

### 2.3 How I-Frame Solutions Can Be Counterproductive

I-frame solutions can undermine s-frame solutions through several mechanisms:

**1. Moral Licensing:**
Small individual actions can reduce motivation for larger systemic change.

**2. Attention Crowding:**
Focus on individual solutions diverts attention from systemic problems.

**3. Responsibility Shifting:**
Framing problems as individual makes systemic actors appear less responsible.

**4. Political Opposition:**
I-frame solutions can reduce political support for s-frame policies.

## III. Eleven Case Studies

### Case 1: Climate Change

**The Problem:**
Global greenhouse gas emissions threaten catastrophic climate change.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Individual carbon footprint calculators
- Voluntary carbon offsets
- Energy conservation campaigns
- Green consumerism

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Carbon pricing (taxes or cap-and-trade)
- Renewable energy mandates
- Building codes and efficiency standards
- Infrastructure investment
- International agreements

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Individual emissions are tiny fraction of total
- Even perfect individual behavior cannot solve coordination problem
- Fossil fuel industry profits from i-frame narrative
- Historical responsibility lies with corporations, not individuals

**Evidence:**
- 100 companies responsible for 71% of global emissions
- Individual actions at scale could reduce emissions by ~4%
- Carbon footprint concept was invented by BP

### Case 2: Obesity

**The Problem:**
Rising obesity rates with associated health consequences.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Diet and exercise advice
- Calorie labeling
- Fitness tracking apps
- Financial incentives for weight loss

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Sugar taxes
- Food marketing regulations
- School meal standards
- Agricultural subsidies reform
- Urban planning for walkability
- Portion size regulations

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Food environment is obesogenic by design
- Food industry spends billions on marketing
- Individual willpower depletes; environment is constant
- Socioeconomic gradients in obesity reflect system, not individual, factors

**Evidence:**
- Food industry spending on marketing vs. government health promotion: 10:1 ratio
- Willpower interventions have poor long-term outcomes
- Sugar taxes show population-level effects

### Case 3: Retirement Savings

**The Problem:**
Inadequate retirement savings leading to poverty in old age.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Financial literacy education
- Auto-enrollment in retirement plans
- Commitment devices
- Information about retirement needs

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Mandatory savings programs
- Employer contribution requirements
- Social security reform
- Pension regulation
- Housing policy

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Many workers lack access to employer plans
- Income volatility makes consistent saving difficult
- Financial literacy has limited effects
- Low wages are the fundamental problem

**Evidence:**
- Auto-enrollment increases participation but not adequacy
- Median retirement account balance remains inadequate
- Countries with mandatory systems have better outcomes

### Case 4: Plastic Waste

**The Problem:**
Plastic pollution in oceans and environment.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Recycling campaigns
- Reusable bag programs
- Consumer education
- Individual pledges

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Extended producer responsibility
- Plastic production taxes
- Packaging regulations
- Single-use plastic bans
- Deposit-return systems

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Recycling is largely a myth (only 9% actually recycled)
- Plastic production continues to increase
- Industry-funded recycling campaigns distract from production limits
- Individual choices cannot address upstream production

**Evidence:**
- "Keep America Beautiful" campaign was funded by beverage industry
- Countries with deposit systems achieve 90%+ return rates
- Plastic production projected to triple by 2060 without systemic change

### Case 5: Healthcare

**The Problem:**
High costs, unequal access, poor outcomes in healthcare.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Health behavior nudges
- Price transparency
- Consumer-directed health plans
- Medication adherence programs

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Universal coverage
- Drug price negotiation
- Provider payment reform
- Public health investment
- Social determinants policies

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Health largely determined by social factors
- Information asymmetries in healthcare
- Market failures pervade healthcare
- Individual cost-bearing shifts risk to sick

### Case 6: Education

**The Problem:**
Educational inequalities and inadequate outcomes.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Grit and mindset interventions
- Study skills training
- Character education
- Parental involvement programs

**S-Frame Approach:**
- School funding reform
- Teacher compensation
- Early childhood investment
- Desegregation policies
- Higher education affordability

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Achievement gaps reflect resource gaps
- Mindset interventions have small, contested effects
- Family resources dominate outcomes
- Structural barriers persist

### Case 7: Discrimination

**The Problem:**
Persistent discrimination in employment, housing, criminal justice.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Implicit bias training
- Diversity awareness programs
- Individual prejudice reduction
- Contact interventions

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Audit studies and enforcement
- Affirmative action
- Blind review processes
- Algorithmic accountability
- Structural reform

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Bias training has limited effects
- Structural discrimination operates without individual prejudice
- Institutional practices perpetuate inequality
- Focus on attitudes distracts from outcomes

**Evidence:**
- Meta-analyses show bias training doesn't change behavior
- Audit studies reveal persistent discrimination
- Structural interventions show larger effects

### Case 8: Privacy

**The Problem:**
Erosion of privacy in digital age.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Privacy notices and consent
- Privacy settings education
- Individual data management tools
- Opt-out rights

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Privacy regulation (GDPR)
- Data minimization requirements
- Surveillance restrictions
- Antitrust enforcement
- Platform accountability

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- No meaningful choice given data economy
- Privacy policies incomprehensible
- Collective action problems
- Power asymmetry with platforms

### Case 9: Misinformation

**The Problem:**
Spread of false information undermining democracy and public health.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Media literacy education
- Fact-checking labels
- Critical thinking training
- Individual source evaluation

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Platform regulation
- Algorithmic transparency
- Political advertising rules
- Public broadcasting investment
- Antitrust enforcement

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Cognitive biases make individual correction difficult
- Information environment is polluted by design
- Economic incentives favor sensationalism
- Scale of problem exceeds individual capacity

### Case 10: Opioids

**The Problem:**
Opioid addiction epidemic causing mass casualties.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Addiction treatment
- Drug education (DARE)
- Prescription drug monitoring
- Individual responsibility messaging

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Pharmaceutical regulation and enforcement
- Prescriber accountability
- Treatment system investment
- Harm reduction policies
- Pain management alternatives

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Epidemic was created by pharmaceutical industry
- Individual vulnerability was exploited systematically
- Treatment alone cannot address ongoing supply
- Criminal justice approach failed

**Evidence:**
- Purdue Pharma knew addiction risks, marketed aggressively
- DARE programs actually increased drug use
- Countries with harm reduction have better outcomes

### Case 11: Gun Violence

**The Problem:**
Mass shootings and gun deaths in United States.

**I-Frame Approach:**
- Mental health services
- School safety training
- Responsible gun ownership education
- Background check cooperation

**S-Frame Approach:**
- Assault weapons bans
- Universal background checks
- Red flag laws
- Gun purchase waiting periods
- Manufacturer liability

**Why I-Frame Is Insufficient:**
- Mental illness not predictive of violence
- Gun availability is the key variable
- Other countries have mental illness, not mass shootings
- Individual responsibility cannot address systemic availability

## IV. Why the I-Frame Dominates

### 4.1 Psychological Factors

**Fundamental Attribution Error:**
People systematically attribute behavior to individuals rather than situations.

**Illusion of Control:**
Belief that individuals can control outcomes through effort.

**Just World Beliefs:**
Assumption that outcomes reflect individual choices.

### 4.2 Political Economy

**Corporate Interests:**
Large economic actors benefit from i-frame solutions that leave their behaviors unaddressed.

**Examples:**
- Fossil fuel companies promoting carbon footprints
- Food companies emphasizing exercise over diet
- Beverage companies funding recycling campaigns
- Tech companies promoting digital literacy

**Lobbying and Influence:**
- Industry lobbying against s-frame regulations
- Funding of behavioral research that supports i-frame
- Capture of regulatory agencies

### 4.3 Academic Incentives

**Research Funding:**
- Corporate funding favors i-frame research
- Foundation funding often emphasizes individual solutions
- Government funding follows political priorities

**Publication Incentives:**
- I-frame interventions easier to test experimentally
- RCTs favor individual-level interventions
- Systems research harder to publish in top journals

### 4.4 Political Feasibility

**Path of Least Resistance:**
- I-frame solutions face less political opposition
- S-frame solutions threaten powerful interests
- Regulatory capture limits s-frame options

## V. The Way Forward

### 5.1 Reframing Behavioral Policy

Behavioral policy should:
1. Start with systems analysis
2. Identify systemic causes of problems
3. Design s-frame interventions where possible
4. Use i-frame only as complement to s-frame
5. Avoid i-frame when it crowds out s-frame

### 5.2 Research Priorities

**Systems Analysis:**
- Map systems that shape behavior
- Identify leverage points for intervention
- Understand feedback loops and unintended consequences

**Policy Evaluation:**
- Evaluate s-frame interventions
- Study i-frame/s-frame interactions
- Measure crowding out effects

### 5.3 Institutional Change

**Academic Institutions:**
- Diversify research funding
- Value systems research
- Train researchers in systems thinking

**Government:**
- Resist regulatory capture
- Build capacity for s-frame policy
- Evaluate policies systemically

## VII. Key Findings Summary

### Overall Framework

1. **I-frame and s-frame are distinct approaches:**
   - I-frame: targets individual behavior change
   - S-frame: targets systemic/structural change

2. **Most problems are systems problems:**
   - Created or enabled by systems
   - Cannot be solved by individual action alone

3. **I-frame can be counterproductive:**
   - Moral licensing reduces motivation
   - Attention crowding diverts focus
   - Responsibility shifting protects systemic actors

4. **I-frame dominance serves corporate interests:**
   - Deflects responsibility from corporations
   - Avoids costly regulations
   - Maintains profitable status quo

### Implications for Behavioral Science

1. **Expand scope:** Include systems analysis in behavioral research
2. **Question assumptions:** Recognize limits of individual-level interventions
3. **Diversify methods:** Go beyond RCTs to systems evaluation
4. **Consider unintended consequences:** Including crowding out effects
5. **Address political economy:** Recognize whose interests are served

## Key Equations and Formalizations

### Behavioral Spillover (Crowding Out)

$$\Delta S = f(\Delta I)$$

Where:
- $\Delta S$ = change in support for systemic solutions
- $\Delta I$ = implementation of individual-level solutions
- $f'(I) < 0$ for crowding out effect

### Effectiveness Comparison

$$E_{total} = E_I + E_S + \gamma(I,S)$$

Where:
- $E_I$ = effectiveness of i-frame intervention
- $E_S$ = effectiveness of s-frame intervention
- $\gamma(I,S)$ = interaction effect (often negative)

### Attribution of Responsibility

$$R_{system} + R_{individual} = 1$$

I-frame focus shifts $R_{individual} \uparrow$, therefore $R_{system} \downarrow$
