# Inflation: What we have learned and what we need to know

**Author:** Jan Hatzius (Goldman, Sachs & Co, New York)
**Journal:** Journal of Monetary Economics 148 (2024) 103656
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2024.103656
**Conference:** Spring 2024 NBER conference on "Inflation in the Covid era and beyond"
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

We discuss the lessons that economic forecasters have learned about inflation since the Covid
shock. First, physical shortages—e.g., in the auto sector—can push up goods prices much more
dramatically than most forecasters expected following several decades near price stability.
Second, imbalances in the rental housing market can sharply increase inflation and keep it high,
especially in economies such as the US where rents are used to impute owner-occupied housing
costs. Third, the jobs-workers gap can be a better measure of labor market balance than the
unemployment rate or the employment/population ratio.

## Three Big Forecasting Errors (2020-2023)

1. **Spring 2020:** Consensus underestimated V-shaped recovery potential (GS got right)
2. **2021:** Consensus underestimated inflation surge due to conditioning on prior 30 years of stability (GS got wrong)
3. **2022-2023:** Most predicted recession needed to bring inflation down (GS got right — no recession)

## Three Lessons Learned

### Lesson #1: Physical Shortages Drive Extreme Price Moves
- Auto inventories/sales plunged to record lows (semiconductor disruptions in Asia)
- Used vehicle prices set via auctions surged dramatically
- Impact: 2-3pp for core CPI, ~1pp for core PCE at peak
- Key insight: When individual markets get really disrupted, prices go "really haywire"

### Lesson #2: Rental Housing Market Imbalances
- Rental vacancy rate (especially apartments) plunged to record lows in 2021
- Private sector rent measures (Zillow, Apartment List, Yardi, RealPage) showed surge quickly
- Official CPI/PCE rent measures lagged significantly
- Forecasters failed to use leading rent indicators in 2021

### Lesson #3: Better Labor Market Tightness Measures
- Unemployment rate and E/P ratio missed tightness in 2021
- Jobs-workers gap (job openings minus unemployed workers) showed much tighter market
- Introduced by Goldman Sachs in early 2022 (Briggs & Hatzius 2022)
- Better predictor of wage growth than unemployment rate
- Consistent with Barnichon & Shapiro findings

## Outlook (as of Spring 2024)
- Auto prices declining, auction prices suggest further drops
- Rent inflation declining, leading indicators point to further slowdown
- Labor market strong but not overheated (jobs-workers gap back to early 2020 levels)
- Wage growth falling, expected to reach 3.5% without further softening
- Core PCE expected to fall to 2% with continued solid GDP growth
- "Soft landing" achievable — unlike past disinflationary episodes
