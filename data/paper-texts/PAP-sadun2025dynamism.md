# Management and Firm Dynamism

**Authors:** Bloom, Nicholas; Brynjolfsson, Erik; Foster, Lucia; Jarmin, Ron; Patnaik, Megha; Saporta-Eksten, Itay; Sadun, Raffaella; Van Reenen, John

**Year:** 2025

**Journal:** Working Paper / NBER

**Abstract:** We show that management practices are important for firm dynamism, broadly defined as the reallocation of resources between and within firms. Using a novel combination of the World Management Survey (WMS) matched to accounting panel data, we find that better-managed acquiring firms restructure target firms after acquisition, boosting their performance. Using confidential US Census data from the Management and Organizational Practices Survey (MOPS), we find that better-managed firms open new plants with higher management scores and acquired plants improve their management practices. To rationalize these empirical patterns, we build a multi-plant firm model with heterogeneous management that we estimate using WMS, MOPS and Census of Manufactures data. We find that the firm-level management technology has a strong influence on the entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal of a firm's plants. Simulating a policy that increases management in all firms shows large positive effects on firm dynamism, aggregate productivity, and welfare.

---

## 1. Introduction

A well-functioning economy requires the efficient allocation and reallocation of resources. Reallocation is driven in part by the entry, exit, and restructuring of firms and their plants. A large literature in macroeconomics has addressed the importance of firm-level dynamics and the reallocation of resources among firms and plants for aggregate productivity and growth, using a variety of frameworks and approaches (see, for example, Jovanovic, 1982; Hopenhayn, 1992; Ericson and Pakes, 1995; Davis and Haltiwanger, 1992; Davis et al, 1998; Foster, Haltiwanger and Krizan, 2001; Bartelsman, Haltiwanger and Scarpetta, 2013; Decker et al, 2018). We refer to the churning of resources within and between firms in an economy as "dynamism."

The macro dynamics literature typically attributes firm-level churning to heterogeneity in a "catch all" production function parameter which is commonly referred to as "productivity." This means that the drivers of firm-level heterogeneity are often left unexplained, although there are notable exceptions (e.g. Acemoglu et al, 2018). Recent work, however, has shown that structured management practices are an important driver of firm-level productivity and performance (e.g. Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al, 2013; Bender et al, 2018), and are among the main determinants of measured productivity differences across firms and countries (see Bloom, Sadun and Van Reenen, 2017, for a survey).

The purpose of this paper is to shed light on the links between management and firm dynamism, broadly defined as the reallocation of resources between and within firms. To this end, we combine novel firm-level data and a structural multi-plant firm model in which management is a firm-level technology, governed by firm heterogeneity, that can be allocated to a firm's plants through the processes of entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal.

Our main sources of data are the World Management Survey (WMS) and the Management and Organizational Practices Survey (MOPS). The WMS has been conducted since 2004, collecting scores across 18 management practice questions in an interview-based survey (see Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom, Sadun, and Van Reenen, 2017). We use the WMS sample covering approximately 14,000 medium-sized firms across 34 countries, matched to firm-level accounting panel data from Bureau Van Dijk's Orbis database. Our second source of data comes from the MOPS. This is a large US Census Bureau management survey of approximately 32,000 manufacturing plants in 2010 and 2015, which was initiated in 2010 by Bloom et al. (2013) (see also Bloom et al., 2019, for a description of the survey). Since the Census Bureau collects plant-level data, the MOPS can be matched to the Census of Manufactures (CMF) and the Longitudinal Business Database (LBD). These matched datasets provide comprehensive data on the composition and restructuring activities at the plant level within a firm, enabling us to study how management relates to the reallocation of resources.

We establish three main empirical facts about the relationship between management and firm dynamism.

**First, well-managed acquiring firms restructure target firms after acquisition, boosting their performance.** Using the WMS-Orbis data for international M&A events, we show that targets of well-managed acquiring firms experience an increase in revenue of approximately 15% relative to targets of less-managed firms in the 9 years following the acquisition. The effect is driven primarily by increases in employment but also by productivity improvements, suggesting that well-managed firms expand production at the target firm. Using the larger MOPS data (matched to US Census data), we also find a positive relationship between acquirer management and the change in productivity of the acquired plant.

**Second, well-managed firms open new plants with higher management scores.** Using confidential US Census data linking plants to firms, we show that the management scores of entrant plants are strongly and positively correlated with the management scores of their parent firms. Specifically, we find that a one-standard-deviation increase in a parent firm's management score is associated with a 0.43-standard-deviation increase in the management score of its entrant plants. This relationship is robust to controlling for a range of firm-level characteristics, including industry and geography.

**Third, acquired plants improve their management practices.** We show that plants that change ownership between the two MOPS waves (2010 and 2015) experience an increase in management scores that is positively correlated with the gap between the acquirer's and seller's management scores. Specifically, we find that a one-standard-deviation increase in the acquirer-seller management gap is associated with a 0.31-standard-deviation increase in the acquired plant's management score.

To rationalize and further explore these facts, we develop a quantitative multi-plant firm model in which management is a key firm-level technology. Our model builds on the dynamic investment framework of Ericson and Pakes (1995), where firms make forward-looking decisions about their portfolio of plants. Each firm is endowed with a management technology that influences the productivity of its plants. Firms can adjust their portfolio of plants through four channels: greenfield entry (opening new plants), greenfield exit (closing existing plants), brownfield acquisition (buying plants from other firms), and brownfield disposal (selling plants to other firms).

The model features two key ingredients. First, management is a firm-level technology that partially transfers to the firm's plants. When a firm opens a new plant, the plant's management score is drawn from a distribution that depends on the parent firm's management. Similarly, when a firm acquires a plant, the plant's management converges toward the acquirer's management level. Second, firms face adjustment costs for all four channels of restructuring, which depend on the firm's management level. Better-managed firms face lower costs of restructuring, reflecting the idea that managerial capabilities facilitate organizational change.

We estimate the structural parameters of the model using Simulated Method of Moments (SMM), targeting moments from both the WMS and the MOPS datasets. The estimated model generates predictions that are broadly consistent with the empirical patterns we document. The model replicates the positive relationship between management and entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal rates, as well as the positive correlation between parent firm management and entrant plant management.

We then use the estimated model to conduct counterfactual policy exercises. We simulate a policy that increases management in all firms and find large positive effects on firm dynamism, aggregate productivity, and welfare. Specifically, a one-standard-deviation increase in the management of all firms leads to a 17% increase in aggregate productivity and a significant increase in the entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal rates. These results suggest that policies aimed at improving management practices could have substantial effects on economic performance through their impact on firm dynamism.

Our paper contributes to several literatures. First, we contribute to the growing body of work on the real effects of management practices (e.g., Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al., 2013; Bender et al., 2018; Bloom, Sadun, and Van Reenen, 2017; Syverson, 2011; Giorcelli, 2019). While this literature has established that management is an important determinant of firm-level productivity, our paper is the first to show that management also plays a key role in firm dynamism, by influencing the processes of entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal of plants.

Second, we contribute to the macro dynamics literature on reallocation (e.g., Hopenhayn, 1992; Ericson and Pakes, 1995; Davis and Haltiwanger, 1992; Foster, Haltiwanger, and Krizan, 2001; Bartelsman, Haltiwanger, and Scarpetta, 2013; Decker et al., 2018). While this literature has documented the importance of reallocation for aggregate productivity, the drivers of reallocation are often left unexplored. We show that management is an important driver of reallocation, and that policies aimed at improving management can have substantial effects on aggregate productivity through their impact on dynamism.

Third, we contribute to the literature on mergers and acquisitions (e.g., Maksimovic and Phillips, 2001; Schoar, 2002; Braguinsky et al., 2015; David, 2021; Bao and Barrage, 2024). While this literature has documented the effects of ownership changes on firm performance, the role of management in driving these effects has received less attention. We show that management is a key mechanism through which acquiring firms restructure and improve the performance of target firms.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the data. Section 3 presents the empirical facts. Section 4 develops the model. Section 5 describes the estimation strategy and presents the estimation results. Section 6 conducts counterfactual policy exercises. Section 7 concludes.

## 2. Data

### 2.1 World Management Survey (WMS)

The WMS collects detailed management practices data through an interview-based survey that evaluates firms across 18 management practice questions, each scored from 1 (worst practice) to 5 (best practice). The management score is then the unweighted average of the practice scores, creating a continuous measure of management from 1 to 5. The 18 practices are split into three areas: monitoring management (five questions on tracking, reviewing, and using performance data for continuous improvement), targets management (five questions on setting targets, their interconnection, and time horizon), and incentives management (eight questions on rewarding performance, addressing underperformance, and promoting and retaining employees).

The WMS has been conducted in waves since 2004 (the most recent wave was in 2014) across 35 countries, covering approximately 22,000 interviews with a focus on medium-sized firms (employing between 50 and 5,000 employees). The data collection uses a "double blind" technique, where managers are not told they are being scored and interviewers are not informed about the firm's performance data, thus mitigating potential biases.

We match the WMS to Orbis, Bureau van Dijk's international database of firm-level financial data, providing annual financial data on companies globally. For the WMS firms, we obtain panel data on assets, revenue, employees, cost of goods sold, and capital. Using Orbis, we also identify M&A events, defined as changes in a firm's Global Ultimate Owner (GUO). Together, the matched WMS-Orbis database covers approximately 13,854 firms with financial data in 34 countries, of which roughly 1,253 are involved in M&A events during our sample period.

We note that these M&A events are at the firm level: they capture instances where a WMS target firm experiences a change in its ultimate ownership, matched to the management score of the acquirer from the WMS. This enables us to analyze how acquirer management affects target performance post-acquisition.

### 2.2 Management and Organizational Practices Survey (MOPS)

The MOPS is a mandatory management survey run by the US Census Bureau using the same underlying management framework as the WMS. It was initiated in 2010, with a follow-up wave in 2015. In each wave, approximately 32,000 plants are surveyed. The MOPS collects data on management practices at the plant level using a mail-based questionnaire. As a Census product, the MOPS can be matched to other Census datasets, including the Census of Manufactures (CMF), the Annual Survey of Manufactures (ASM), and the Longitudinal Business Database (LBD). This enables us to track plants over time, identify their parent firms, and observe when plants change ownership (i.e., when a plant's firm identifier changes between the 2010 and 2015 waves).

We use the MOPS to study two aspects of firm dynamism: the management of newly entered plants and the change in management of acquired plants. For newly entered plants, we identify plants that appear in the MOPS in 2015 but not in 2010, and whose parent firm had at least one plant in the MOPS in 2010. This allows us to relate the management score of the entrant plant to the management score of its parent firm. For acquired plants, we identify plants that appear in both the 2010 and 2015 MOPS but have a different firm identifier, indicating a change in ownership.

### 2.3 Census of Manufactures (CMF) and Longitudinal Business Database (LBD)

The CMF is a comprehensive census of all US manufacturing establishments, conducted every five years. We use the CMF for 2007, 2012, and 2017 to measure plant-level outcomes such as revenue, employment, and capital. The LBD is an annual business register that covers the universe of US establishments with paid employees, enabling us to track plant births, deaths, and ownership changes over time.

We merge the MOPS data with the CMF and LBD to construct a comprehensive plant-level panel dataset. This merged dataset allows us to observe management practices alongside detailed financial and operational data for each plant, as well as to track the firm affiliations of plants over time.

### 2.4 Matching and Sample Construction

For the WMS-Orbis analysis, our estimation sample includes approximately 13,854 firms with financial data, of which 1,253 are involved in M&A events. In the WMS data, 10,900 firms have complete financial data for the TFP regression (where TFP is calculated as the residual of a regression of log(sales) on log(capital) and log(labor) plus a full set of 3-digit industry, country, and year dummy controls).

For the MOPS-Census analysis, our estimation sample for the entry analysis includes approximately 1,100 entrant plants with parent firms in the MOPS. For the acquisition analysis, we use two samples: a MOPS-to-MOPS sample of approximately 850 acquired plants (plants in both the 2010 and 2015 MOPS with a change in firm identifier) and a larger CMF sample of approximately 4,400 acquired plants (plants in the CMF in both 2012 and 2017 with a change in firm identifier between 2010 and 2015).

As a benchmark, Figure A1 (in the Appendix) confirms the strong positive relationship between management and firm performance that has been established in the prior literature. Panel A shows that TFP is strongly increasing in management scores, while Panel B shows the same positive relationship between management and firm size (log sales).

## 3. Empirical Facts

We establish three main empirical facts about the relationship between management and firm dynamism: (i) well-managed acquiring firms restructure target firms after acquisition; (ii) well-managed firms open new plants with higher management scores; and (iii) acquired plants improve their management practices when bought by better-managed firms.

### 3.1 Fact 1: Well-managed firms restructure their acquisitions

#### 3.1.1 WMS Evidence

We begin by studying the effects of acquisitions by well-managed firms using the WMS-Orbis data. We estimate an event study specification that exploits variation in the management score of the acquiring firm:

$$y_{it} = \alpha_i + \sum_{\tau=-3}^{9} \left[ \delta_\tau \cdot 1(t = t^* + \tau) + \gamma_\tau \cdot M_a \cdot 1(t = t^* + \tau) \right] + \mathbf{X}_{it}\beta + \epsilon_{it} \quad (1)$$

where $y_{it}$ is a measure of performance (log revenue, log employment, log TFP, or log total assets) for target firm $i$ in year $t$; $t^*$ is the year of acquisition; $M_a$ is the management score of the acquirer; $\alpha_i$ is a firm fixed effect; and $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ includes year fixed effects, acquirer country fixed effects, and industry fixed effects. The coefficients of interest are $\gamma_\tau$, which capture the differential effect of acquirer management on target performance at each time period relative to the acquisition. The base period is the two years prior to the merger ($\tau \in \{-2, -1\}$).

Figure 2 presents the results of this event study. Panel A shows that targets of well-managed firms experience a significant increase in revenue in the years following the acquisition, with the effect growing over time to approximately 15% after 9 years. Panel B shows a similar pattern for employment, with the effect reaching approximately 8% after 9 years. Panel C shows a positive but noisier effect on TFP, and Panel D shows a modest positive effect on total assets.

These results suggest that well-managed acquiring firms expand production at the target firm, primarily through increases in employment. The revenue effects are larger than the employment effects, consistent with productivity improvements as well.

We also estimate a cross-sectional version of the event study:

$$y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Post}_t + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Post}_t \times M_a + \mathbf{X}_{it}\gamma + \epsilon_{it} \quad (2)$$

where Post$_t$ is an indicator for the post-acquisition period. Table 4 presents the results. Column (1) shows that targets experience an average revenue increase of approximately 70% after acquisition, with better-managed acquirers generating an additional 14% increase per unit of management (column 2). The results are robust to controlling for target employment and capital (columns 3-5).

We also examine the effects of acquirer management on target employment and capital. Column (6) shows that better-managed acquirers lead to a 35% increase in target employment, with an additional negative effect on capital of approximately 20%, suggesting that well-managed acquirers invest in labor rather than capital at the target firm. The interaction between post-merger and the acquirer's GUO log employment (column 5) suggests that the management effect is not simply a size effect.

#### 3.1.2 MOPS Evidence

Using the MOPS-Census data, we can examine how acquired plants change their actual management practices after acquisition. For plants that were acquired between 2010 and 2015 (identified by a change in firm identifier), we regress the 5-year change in the plant's management score on the gap between the acquirer's and seller's management scores:

$$\Delta M_{it} = \alpha + \beta \cdot (M_{\text{acquirer}} - M_{\text{seller}}) + \mathbf{X}_{it}\gamma + \epsilon_{it} \quad (3)$$

Table 2 presents the results. The dependent variable is the 5-year change in plant management ($M_t - M_{t-5}$). Column (1) shows that the acquirer-seller management gap is strongly positively correlated with the change in the acquired plant's management score: a one-unit increase in the management gap is associated with a 0.31 increase in the plant's management score. This result is robust to using a leave-out mean for firm management (column 2), and to controlling for industry and state fixed effects (columns 3-5).

In column (6), we decompose the management gap into the acquirer's and seller's management scores separately. We find that the positive coefficient on the gap is primarily driven by the acquirer's management level, with the seller's management having a negative but statistically insignificant effect. This suggests that it is the acquirer's management capability, rather than the seller's, that drives the improvement in the acquired plant's management.

We also examine how acquirer management affects the performance (revenue per worker) of acquired plants. Table 3 presents the results using the CMF data for plants acquired between 2010 and 2015. We find that acquirer management is positively associated with the change in revenue per worker at the acquired plant: a one-standard-deviation increase in acquirer management is associated with a 21-28% increase in revenue per worker over the 5-year period (columns 1-3). In columns 4-6, we use the acquirer-seller management gap and find similar results. In columns 7-9, we include both the acquirer's and seller's management scores separately and find that the acquirer's management has a strong positive effect while the seller's management has a negative effect, consistent with the idea that management improvements at the acquired plant are driven by the acquirer's superior management capabilities.

### 3.2 Fact 2: Well-managed firms open plants with higher management scores

We next examine whether the management scores of newly opened plants are related to the management scores of their parent firms. This analysis leverages the unique plant-level structure of the MOPS data, which allows us to identify plants that were newly established between survey waves and to link them to their parent firms.

Table 1 presents the results of regressing the management score of entrant plants (plants that appear in the MOPS in 2015 but not in 2010) on the management score of their parent firm (measured in 2010). The sample includes approximately 1,100 entrant plants whose parent firms had at least one other plant surveyed in the MOPS in the earlier wave. This allows us to construct a reliable measure of parent firm management that is not mechanically linked to the entrant plant's own management score.

Column (1) shows a strong positive relationship: a one-unit increase in parent firm management is associated with a 0.43-unit increase in the entrant plant's management score. This relationship is robust to controlling for industry fixed effects at the 4-digit NAICS level (column 2), industry and state fixed effects (column 3), and parent firm size measured by log employment (column 4). In column (4), we find that while parent firm size has a positive and significant effect on entrant plant management (coefficient of 0.013), the management coefficient remains large and significant at 0.34, indicating that the management transfer effect is not simply capturing a size effect.

These results are consistent with the idea that management is a firm-level technology that partially transfers to new plants. The magnitude of the coefficient suggests that about one-third to one-half of the parent firm's management advantage is transmitted to its new plants. This is a substantial transfer rate, indicating that firm-level management capabilities are an important determinant of the management practices established at new plants.

The finding has important implications for understanding the dynamics of management dispersion in the economy. If well-managed firms systematically establish well-managed plants, then the cross-sectional distribution of management is reinforced over time through the entry process. This amplifies the competitive advantage of well-managed firms and contributes to the persistence of management heterogeneity across firms.

### 3.3 Fact 3: Well-managed firms have higher entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal rates

Figure 1 presents evidence on the relationship between management and firm dynamism using the MOPS data matched to the Longitudinal Business Database (LBD). For each firm with at least one plant in the MOPS, we compute the entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal rates over a 5-year period and relate them to the firm's management score.

Panel A of Figure 1 shows that better-managed firms have higher greenfield entry rates. The entry rate is defined as the number of plants that enter (are newly created) between period $t$ and $t+5$, divided by the total number of plants at the firm in year $t$. The relationship is positive and approximately linear, with the entry rate increasing from about 2% for firms in the lowest management decile to about 8% for firms in the highest management decile.

Panel B shows that better-managed firms also have higher greenfield exit rates. The exit rate is defined analogously as the number of plants that exit (are closed) between periods. The positive relationship between management and exit rates may seem counterintuitive at first glance. However, it reflects the fact that well-managed firms are more actively restructuring their portfolio of plants, closing underperforming establishments to reallocate resources to more productive uses. This is consistent with the view that management capability facilitates organizational change and adaptation.

Panel C shows that the net entry rate (entry minus exit) is also positively related to management, indicating that well-managed firms grow faster on net through the greenfield channel. The slope is positive but smaller than for gross entry or gross exit alone, reflecting the fact that much of the increased dynamism is churning rather than net expansion.

Panels D, E, and F present analogous results for the brownfield channel (acquisitions and disposals). Panel D shows that better-managed firms have substantially higher acquisition rates, with the acquisition rate increasing from about 3% for the lowest management decile to about 12% for the highest. Panel E shows that better-managed firms also have higher disposal rates, though the relationship is less steep. Panel F shows that the net acquisition rate is strongly positively related to management, indicating that well-managed firms grow significantly through acquisitions.

Together, these patterns reveal that well-managed firms are substantially more dynamic across all four channels of restructuring. They create more new plants, close more existing plants, acquire more plants from other firms, and sell more plants to other firms. On net, they grow faster than less-managed firms through both greenfield and brownfield channels.

### 3.4 Summary of Empirical Facts

Together, these three facts paint a consistent picture: management is a firm-level technology that influences how firms allocate and reallocate resources across their plants. Well-managed firms open better-managed plants, improve the management of acquired plants, and boost the performance of their acquisitions. Moreover, well-managed firms are substantially more dynamic, with higher rates of entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal. These facts motivate our structural model, in which management is a key driver of firm dynamism.

## 4. Model

### 4.1 Setup

We develop a multi-plant firm model in which management is a firm-level technology that influences plant-level productivity and the costs of restructuring. The model builds on the dynamic investment framework of Ericson and Pakes (1995), extended to accommodate multiple plants per firm and a firm-level management technology.

**Plants.** The economy consists of a continuum of plants indexed by $i$. Each plant is owned by a firm $f$. A plant's revenue is determined by its Cobb-Douglas production function:

$$Y_i = \tilde{A}_i K_i^{1-\beta-\gamma} L_i^\beta M_i^\gamma \quad (4)$$

where $K_i$ is capital, $L_i$ is labor, $M_i$ is the plant's management score, and $\tilde{A}_i$ is plant-level TFP. The output elasticities are $1-\beta-\gamma$ for capital, $\beta$ for labor, and $\gamma$ for management.

The revenue function under monopolistic competition with demand elasticity $\rho$ is:

$$(PY)_i = A_i K_i^a L_i^b M_i^c \quad (5)$$

where $a = \alpha(1-1/\rho)$, $b = \beta(1-1/\rho)$, $c = \gamma(1-1/\rho)$, and $A_i = \tilde{A}_i^{1-1/\rho}$.

**Firms.** Each firm $f$ is endowed with a management technology $C_f$, which is drawn from a distribution and remains fixed over the firm's lifetime. The firm's management technology determines the management scores of its plants. When a firm opens a new plant or acquires an existing plant, the plant's management converges toward the firm's management technology $C_f$.

**Timing.** Each period, the sequence of events is as follows:

1. TFP shocks are realized for each plant.
2. Firms observe their TFP shocks and make static input decisions (labor, capital).
3. Firms make dynamic restructuring decisions: entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal of plants.
4. Restructuring is implemented and adjustment costs are paid.

### 4.2 Production

Each plant $i$ in firm $f$ at time $t$ has a TFP level $A_{it}$ that follows an AR(1) process in logs:

$$\ln A_{it} = \rho_A \ln A_{it-1} + \epsilon_{it}^A, \quad \epsilon_{it}^A \sim N(0, \sigma_A^2) \quad (6)$$

Given its TFP, management, and capital stock, each plant chooses labor to maximize static profits:

$$\pi(A_{it}, M_{it}, K_{it}) = \max_{L_{it}} \left\{ A_{it} K_{it}^a L_{it}^b M_{it}^c - w L_{it} \right\} \quad (7)$$

where $w$ is the wage rate. The optimal labor choice yields the profit function:

$$\pi(A_{it}, M_{it}, K_{it}) = \theta A_{it}^{1/(1-b)} K_{it}^{a/(1-b)} M_{it}^{c/(1-b)} \quad (8)$$

where $\theta$ is a constant that depends on $b$ and $w$.

### 4.3 Adjustment Costs

Firms face adjustment costs for changing their management and capital at each plant, as well as for entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal of plants.

**Management and Capital Adjustment Costs.** The adjustment cost for management at plant $i$ is:

$$S_{M}(M_t, M_{t-1}) = \gamma_M M_{t-1} \left(\frac{M_t - M_{t-1}}{M_{t-1}} + \delta_M \right)^2 \quad (9)$$

Similarly, for capital:

$$S_{K}(K_t, K_{t-1}) = \gamma_K K_{t-1} \left(\frac{K_t - K_{t-1}}{K_{t-1}} + \delta_K \right)^2 \quad (10)$$

where $\delta_M$ and $\delta_K$ are the depreciation rates for management and capital, respectively, and $\gamma_M$ and $\gamma_K$ scale the adjustment costs.

**Entry and Acquisition Costs.** The cost of greenfield entry (opening a new plant) and brownfield acquisition (buying an existing plant from another firm) are specified as:

$$\text{Cost}_{\{enter,acquire\}} = a_{\{enter,acquire\}} + b_{\{enter/acquire\}}[\log(C) - \log(C_0)] + d_{\{acquire\}}\varepsilon_i \quad (11)$$

where $C$ is the firm's management technology, $C_0$ is the lowest possible value of firm management, and $\varepsilon_i$ is a noise term for acquisition costs. The slope parameter $b_{\{enter/acquire\}}$ captures how the cost of entry/acquisition varies with the firm's management level.

**Exit and Disposal Costs.** The cost of greenfield exit (closing an existing plant) and brownfield disposal (selling an existing plant to another firm) are specified similarly:

$$\text{Cost}_{\{exit,dispose\}} = a_{\{exit,dispose\}} + b_{\{exit/dispose\}}[\log(C) - \log(C_0)] + d_{\{dispose\}}\varepsilon_i \quad (12)$$

### 4.4 Management Transfer

A key feature of the model is how management transfers from firms to plants.

**Greenfield Entry.** When a firm with management technology $C$ opens a new plant, the plant's initial management score is drawn from a distribution that depends on $C$:

$$M_{\text{new}} = h(C) + \eta, \quad \eta \sim N(0, \sigma_\eta^2) \quad (13)$$

where $h(C)$ is an increasing function of the firm's management technology. This captures the idea that better-managed firms are able to establish better management practices at their new plants, but with some noise.

**Brownfield Acquisition.** When a firm with management technology $C$ acquires a plant with initial management $M_0$, the plant's management converges toward the acquirer's management technology:

$$M_{t+1} = M_t + \lambda(h(C) - M_t) + \eta_t \quad (14)$$

where $\lambda \in (0,1)$ is the speed of convergence and $\eta_t$ is a noise term. This captures the gradual transfer of management practices from the acquiring firm to the acquired plant.

### 4.5 Dynamic Problem

Each firm solves a dynamic programming problem to maximize the expected discounted value of its plants, net of adjustment costs. The firm's state variable is the vector of its plants' characteristics (TFP, management, capital) and the firm's management technology $C$.

The Bellman equation for the firm is:

$$V(\mathbf{s}_f, C) = \max \left\{ \sum_{i \in f} \pi(A_i, M_i, K_i) - \text{Adjustment Costs} + \frac{1}{1+r} E[V(\mathbf{s}_f', C)] \right\} \quad (15)$$

where $\mathbf{s}_f$ is the state vector, $r$ is the discount rate, and the expectation is taken over the evolution of TFP and the realization of management transfer shocks.

The firm's restructuring decision involves choosing which plants to close (exit), which plants to sell (dispose), whether to open new plants (enter), and whether to buy plants from other firms (acquire). These decisions are made simultaneously and take into account the costs and benefits of each action.

### 4.6 Firm Heterogeneity and the Distribution of Management

A central feature of the model is that firms are heterogeneous in their management technology $C_f$. The management technology is drawn from a distribution at the time of firm creation and remains fixed over the firm's lifetime. This fixed heterogeneity generates persistent differences in firm behavior: firms with higher $C_f$ systematically open better plants, improve acquired plants more, and face lower restructuring costs.

The distribution of firm-level management technologies determines the steady-state distribution of plant-level management scores and, through the production function, the distribution of plant-level productivity. In equilibrium, the plant-level distribution of management arises endogenously from the interaction of firm-level management technologies with the entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal decisions of all firms.

The cross-sectional dispersion in management scores observed in the WMS and MOPS data (Figure A8) is thus a result of both the underlying heterogeneity in firm management technologies and the dynamic restructuring decisions that firms make. Countries with wider dispersion in firm management technologies, or with higher barriers to restructuring, will exhibit wider dispersion in plant-level management scores and productivity.

### 4.7 Equilibrium

An equilibrium is defined as a set of decision rules for all firms such that:

1. Each firm's decision rules solve its dynamic programming problem given prices.
2. The labor market clears: the total demand for labor across all plants equals the aggregate labor supply.
3. The market for acquisitions and disposals clears: every plant that is disposed of by one firm is acquired by another firm. The price at which plants are traded is determined by the intersection of the supply of plants (from disposing firms) and the demand for plants (from acquiring firms).

The equilibrium is computed numerically using value function iteration on the discretized state space. The state space for each plant consists of its TFP level, management score, and capital stock. The firm's state is the collection of its plants' states plus the firm's management technology. Given the large dimensionality of the problem, we employ several computational techniques to make the problem tractable, including discretizing the continuous state variables and using interpolation methods.

### 4.7 Model Predictions

The model generates several predictions that are consistent with the empirical facts:

1. **Well-managed firms have higher entry and acquisition rates.** Because better-managed firms face lower adjustment costs and can transfer better management to new and acquired plants, they find it more profitable to expand through entry and acquisition.

2. **Well-managed firms have higher exit and disposal rates.** Better-managed firms also have higher exit and disposal rates because they are more actively restructuring their portfolios. They are more willing to close or sell underperforming plants because their opportunity cost of maintaining them is higher.

3. **Plants born to well-managed firms have higher management scores.** The management transfer mechanism ensures that new plants of better-managed firms start with higher management scores.

4. **Acquired plants improve their management.** The convergence mechanism ensures that plants acquired by better-managed firms experience an increase in their management scores.

5. **Well-managed firms have higher net entry and net acquisition rates.** On balance, better-managed firms grow faster because their entry and acquisition rates exceed their exit and disposal rates by a larger margin.

**MOPS Moments.** From the MOPS data matched to the LBD, we target the following moments:
- Entry rates: the number of plants that enter between period $t+1$ and $t$ divided by the total number of plants at a firm in year $t$. We target both the constant and the coefficient on firm management in a regression of the entry rate on firm management.

## 6. Counterfactual Experiments

### 6.1 Increasing Management

We use the estimated model to conduct counterfactual policy exercises. Our main experiment simulates a policy that increases the management technology of all firms by one standard deviation. This allows us to quantify the effects of management on firm dynamism, aggregate productivity, and welfare.

The results of this experiment show large positive effects across multiple dimensions:

**Dynamism.** The policy leads to significant increases in firm dynamism. Entry rates increase as firms find it more profitable to open new plants. Acquisition rates also increase as firms expand through acquisitions. Exit and disposal rates also increase, reflecting the more active restructuring of firm portfolios. On net, the increases in entry and acquisition exceed the increases in exit and disposal, leading to a net expansion of well-managed firms.

**Aggregate Productivity.** The policy leads to a substantial increase in aggregate productivity. Through the management transfer mechanism, new and acquired plants receive higher management scores, which translates into higher plant-level productivity. Moreover, the increased dynamism improves the allocation of resources, as more productive firms expand at the expense of less productive ones.

**Welfare.** The combined effects on productivity and resource allocation lead to significant welfare gains. The welfare calculation takes into account the costs of increased dynamism (adjustment costs) as well as the benefits (higher productivity and better resource allocation).

### 6.2 Decomposing the Effects

To understand the channels through which management affects outcomes, we decompose the total effect into several components:

1. **Direct productivity effect:** Better management directly increases plant-level productivity through the production function.

2. **Transfer effect:** Better-managed firms transfer higher management to their new and acquired plants, amplifying the direct productivity effect.

3. **Selection effect:** Better-managed firms are more active in entering and acquiring plants, and in exiting and disposing of underperforming plants. This improves the allocation of resources across plants.

4. **Adjustment cost effect:** Better-managed firms face lower restructuring costs, which enables them to be more dynamic. This facilitates the selection effect.

The decomposition reveals that all four channels contribute to the overall effect, with the transfer and selection effects being particularly important. This highlights the role of management not just as a direct input to production, but as a key enabler of efficient resource reallocation.

### 6.3 Role of Different Channels

We also examine the relative importance of the different channels of restructuring (entry, exit, acquisition, disposal) by shutting down each channel in turn. This exercise allows us to quantify the contribution of each channel to the overall effects of management on aggregate outcomes.

- **Acquisitions are the most important channel for management transfer.** When we shut down acquisitions, the management transfer to existing plants is significantly reduced, leading to lower aggregate productivity. This is because acquisitions are the primary mechanism through which well-managed firms can improve the management of underperforming plants. Without acquisitions, plants with low management scores remain stuck at their current level, and the economy loses an important mechanism for management upgrading.

- **Entry is the most important channel for the creation of well-managed plants.** When we shut down entry, the economy loses the ability to create new high-management plants, reducing the long-run management level. Entry is particularly important for aggregate dynamics because it is the only channel through which new, potentially highly productive plants can be introduced into the economy. The management transfer mechanism ensures that new plants of well-managed firms start with above-average management, contributing disproportionately to aggregate productivity.

- **Exit contributes to productivity through selection.** When we shut down exit, underperforming plants remain in the economy, reducing average productivity. Exit serves as a cleansing mechanism, removing the least productive plants from the economy. Better-managed firms are more willing to close underperforming plants because their opportunity cost of maintaining them is higher, and because they are better at identifying which plants to close.

- **Disposal contributes through reallocation.** Disposal allows underperforming plants to be transferred to better-managed firms, which can improve their management and productivity. When disposal is shut down, plants that would benefit from a change in ownership are stuck with their current owners, reducing the efficiency of resource allocation.

- **All channels are complementary.** The effects of shutting down multiple channels simultaneously are larger than the sum of the individual effects, indicating complementarities between the different channels of restructuring. For example, shutting down both entry and acquisitions reduces productivity by more than the sum of shutting down each individually, because the two channels serve as partial substitutes for each other in the baseline: firms that cannot acquire can still enter, and vice versa.

The direct effect of higher management on plant-level productivity accounts for roughly half of the total effect. The remaining half comes from improved resource allocation, as the increased dynamism reallocates resources from less productive to more productive plants and firms.

The welfare implications are also significant. The welfare calculation accounts for the transition dynamics as the economy moves from the baseline to the counterfactual steady state. During the transition, firms incur adjustment costs as they restructure their plant portfolios in response to the higher management levels. However, these costs are more than offset by the gains from higher productivity and better resource allocation, resulting in net welfare gains even in the short run.

These results suggest that the aggregate effects of management on the economy are substantially larger than what would be predicted by a simple model that treats management as just another input to production. The amplification comes from the dynamic effects of management on firm restructuring: better management not only increases productivity directly, but also enables firms to be more dynamic in their entry, exit, acquisition, and disposal decisions, which improves the allocation of resources across the economy.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper has shown that management practices are an important driver of firm dynamism. Using novel combinations of the World Management Survey (WMS) and the Management and Organizational Practices Survey (MOPS), we established three key empirical facts: well-managed acquiring firms restructure and improve their acquisitions, well-managed firms open new plants with higher management scores, and acquired plants improve their management when bought by better-managed firms.

To rationalize these facts, we developed a quantitative multi-plant firm model in which management is a firm-level technology that influences plant-level productivity and the costs of restructuring. The model, estimated using data from the WMS, MOPS, and Census of Manufactures, generates predictions that are broadly consistent with the empirical patterns we document.

Our counterfactual experiments show that policies aimed at improving management practices can have large positive effects on firm dynamism, aggregate productivity, and welfare. These effects operate through multiple channels: direct productivity improvements, management transfer to new and acquired plants, improved resource allocation through selection, and lower adjustment costs that enable more active restructuring.

These findings have important implications for economic policy. While much of the policy discussion around productivity has focused on technology adoption and R&D, our results suggest that management practices deserve equal attention. Policies that improve management, such as training programs, consulting interventions, or the dissemination of best practices, can have large and lasting effects on economic performance through their impact on firm dynamism.

Several important avenues remain for future research. First, understanding the determinants of management heterogeneity across firms is crucial for designing effective policies. Second, extending the model to incorporate the role of workers and their skills in mediating the transfer of management practices would provide a richer picture of the restructuring process. Third, studying the interaction between management and other drivers of firm dynamism, such as technology adoption, financial frictions, and market structure, would help us understand the relative importance of different factors.
