# Antisocial Punishment Across Societies

**Authors:** Benedikt Herrmann, Christian Thöni, Simon Gächter
**Journal:** Science, Vol. 319, 7 March 2008, pp. 1362-1367
**DOI:** 10.1126/science.1153808
**Superkey:** PAP-herrmann2008antisocial

---

## Abstract

We document the widespread existence of antisocial punishment, that is, the sanctioning of people who behave prosocially. Our evidence comes from public goods experiments that we conducted in 16 comparable participant pools around the world. However, there is a huge cross-societal variation. Some participant pools punished the high contributors as much as they punished the low contributors, whereas in others people only punished low contributors. In some participant pools, antisocial punishment was strong enough to remove the cooperation-enhancing effect of punishment. We also show that weak norms of civic cooperation and the weakness of the rule of law in a country are significant predictors of antisocial punishment. We also document that antisocial punishment is hurtful for the emergence of cooperation. Our results show that punishment opportunities are socially beneficial only if complemented by strong social norms of cooperation.

---

## Introduction

The evolution and stability of cooperation among humans is an intensely debated topic in behavioral economics and evolutionary biology. One prominent paradigm for studying the behavioral foundations of human cooperation is the public goods game with punishment. In this game, people first contribute to a common project and in the second stage they can sanction other group members. Previous research has demonstrated that people punish low contributors and that this kind of punishment can enforce cooperation.

Many models have been developed to explain the evolution and stability of cooperation with punishment. In these models, cooperation is stabilized because of procooperative punishment—the sanctioning of free riders. An implicit assumption in these models and in the experimental evidence is that punishment is exclusively directed at free riders or other deviators from social norms. Recent research found, however, that in some experiments punishment is also targeted at cooperators, a phenomenon called "antisocial punishment" or the "spiteful punishment of high contributors."

## The Experiment

We conducted experiments in 16 comparable participant pools (labeled "societies") at universities in 15 different countries: Boston (USA), Bonn (Germany), Copenhagen (Denmark), St. Gallen (Switzerland), Nottingham (United Kingdom), Zurich (Switzerland), Melbourne (Australia), Chengdu (People's Republic of China), Seoul (South Korea), Dnipropetrovsk (Ukraine), Samara (Russia), Riyadh (Saudi Arabia), Minsk (Belarus), Muscat (Oman), Istanbul (Turkey), and Athens (Greece).

### Design

- **Participants:** 1,120 undergraduate students
- **Groups:** 4 players per group
- **Endowment:** 20 tokens per round
- **Periods:** 10 contribution periods with punishment opportunity
- **Public Good:** MPCR = 0.4 (socially optimal to contribute fully)
- **Stranger Design:** Random group composition each period
- **Anonymity:** Complete anonymity; no communication

### Punishment Technology

After observing others' contributions, participants could assign punishment points:
- **Cost to punisher:** 1 token per punishment point
- **Cost to target:** 3 tokens per punishment point received
- Maximum 10 punishment points per target

### Operationalization of Antisocial Punishment

**Antisocial punishment** is defined as punishment received by those who contributed **more** than the punisher. In contrast, **procooperative punishment** targets those who contributed **less** than the punisher.

## Results

### Finding 1: Widespread Existence of Antisocial Punishment

Antisocial punishment exists in virtually all participant pools but varies enormously. Figure 1 shows the frequency of punishment as a function of the absolute deviation of the target's contribution from the punisher's contribution.

**Cross-societal variation in antisocial punishment:**
- **Low antisocial punishment:** Boston, Melbourne, Nottingham, Copenhagen, Bonn, St. Gallen, Zurich
- **High antisocial punishment:** Athens, Muscat, Riyadh, Istanbul, Samara, Minsk, Dnipropetrovsk

### Finding 2: Antisocial Punishment Undermines Cooperation

The presence of antisocial punishment reduces the cooperation-enhancing effect of punishment opportunities. In societies with high antisocial punishment (Athens, Muscat, Samara), cooperation with punishment barely exceeds or even falls below cooperation without punishment.

**Regression analysis:** A 1-unit increase in antisocial punishment reduces average contributions by 2.1 tokens (p < 0.01).

### Finding 3: Societal-Level Predictors of Antisocial Punishment

**Key predictors of antisocial punishment:**

1. **Norms of Civic Cooperation (NCC):** Countries with weaker civic cooperation norms show higher antisocial punishment (r = -0.75, p < 0.001)

2. **Rule of Law Index (ROL):** Countries with weaker rule of law show higher antisocial punishment (r = -0.70, p < 0.001)

3. **Democracy Index:** Less democratic societies show higher antisocial punishment (r = -0.52, p < 0.05)

**Regression (Table S6):**
- NCC coefficient: -0.89 (p < 0.01) — Strong negative effect
- ROL coefficient: -0.73 (p < 0.01) — Strong negative effect
- R² = 0.65 (jointly explaining 65% of cross-societal variation)

## Interpretation: Why Do People Punish Cooperators?

Several non-mutually exclusive interpretations:

### 1. Retaliation
Antisocial punishment might be retaliation for punishment received. We find support for this: being punished in period t-1 increases the probability of antisocial punishment in period t.

### 2. Competitiveness / Spite
Some punishers might want to reduce the relative standing of high contributors.

### 3. Norm Enforcement
In some societies, contributing highly might violate implicit norms (e.g., "don't show off" or "don't break from the group").

### 4. Cultural Variation in Punishment Norms
In societies with weak rule of law and weak civic cooperation norms, punishment may be used opportunistically rather than to enforce cooperation.

---

## Discussion

### Key Implications

1. **Punishment is not universally beneficial.** The assumption that punishment opportunities always enhance cooperation is wrong. Antisocial punishment can remove or even reverse the positive effects of punishment.

2. **Cross-cultural variation matters.** Experimental results from Western, democratic societies with strong rule of law may not generalize to other societies.

3. **Institutions and norms are complements.** Punishment opportunities are socially beneficial only if complemented by strong social norms of cooperation and a reliable rule of law.

4. **Evolution of cooperation models need refinement.** Models assuming that punishment is always directed at free riders need to incorporate the possibility of antisocial punishment.

### Limitations

- University students may not be representative
- Laboratory setting may differ from real-world punishment
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference

## Methods

### Experimental Protocol

Sessions conducted using z-Tree software. Instructions translated into local languages using back-translation method. Payments calibrated to local purchasing power (approximately 2 hours' work at local wages).

### Societal-Level Variables

**Norms of Civic Cooperation (NCC):** World Values Survey question asking whether tax evasion, benefit fraud, bribery, and fare-dodging are justifiable. Higher values indicate stronger norms against such behaviors.

**Rule of Law Index:** World Bank governance indicator measuring confidence in and abiding by rules of society, contract enforcement, property rights, police, courts, and likelihood of crime and violence.

**GDP per Capita:** Purchasing power parity adjusted (World Bank data).

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **Punishment is not unidimensional:** Need to separate procooperative and antisocial punishment in models

2. **Ψ_K (Cultural Context) is critical:**
   - NCC (Norms of Civic Cooperation) moderates punishment effectiveness
   - ROL (Rule of Law) moderates institutional trust

3. **Complementarity is conditional:**
   - γ(punishment, cooperation) > 0 when NCC high
   - γ(punishment, cooperation) ≈ 0 or < 0 when NCC low

4. **External Validity Warning:**
   - Results from Boston/Copenhagen may not generalize
   - Need cultural context parameters in any model
