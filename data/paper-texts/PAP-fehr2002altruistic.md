# Altruistic Punishment in Humans

**Authors:** Ernst Fehr & Simon Gächter
**Journal:** Nature, Vol 415, 10 January 2002, pp. 137-140
**DOI:** 10.1038/415137a
**Superkey:** PAP-fehr2002altruistic

---

## Abstract

Human cooperation is an evolutionary puzzle. Unlike other creatures, people frequently cooperate with genetically unrelated strangers, often in large groups, with people they will never meet again, and when reputation gains are small or absent. These patterns of cooperation cannot be explained by the nepotistic motives associated with the evolutionary theory of kin selection and the ## selfish motives associated with signalling theory or the theory of reciprocal altruism. Here we show experimentally that the altruistic punishment of defectors is a key motive for the explanation of cooperation. Altruistic punishment means that individuals punish, although the punishment is costly for them and yields no material gain. We show that cooperation flourishes if altruistic punishment is possible, and breaks down if it is ruled out. We also show that the existence of altruistic punishment is associated with increased negative emotions towards defectors. Our results suggest that negative emotions towards defectors are the proximate mechanism behind altruistic punishment. These results provide strong evidence for the importance of altruistic punishment in evolutionary models of human cooperation.

## Main Text

Human cooperation is a puzzle from an evolutionary viewpoint because cooperation is costly for the individual, yet the benefits of cooperation accrue to other individuals. Evolutionary biology has developed sophisticated theories—reciprocal altruism, indirect reciprocity and costly signalling theory—to explain how cooperation among non-relatives can evolve. These theories all predict the absence of cooperation in one-shot anonymous interactions among strangers. However, many experiments have shown that there is substantial cooperation in such situations.

Recent theoretical advances offer a way out of this puzzle by relaxing the assumption of selfish preferences that underlies the theories mentioned above. Several authors have shown that if—in addition to many selfish types—a fraction of people have a tendency to punish unfair behaviour, then cooperation can be sustained even in one-shot, anonymous encounters. Such a tendency to punish at cost to oneself and without material benefit has been termed 'altruistic punishment' or 'strong reciprocity'.

### Experimental Design

The key feature of our experiment is that we conduct the same type of public goods experiment with and without a punishment opportunity. In the public goods game subjects are grouped anonymously and each subject decides how much of his/her endowment to contribute to a 'public' project. All contributions to the project are multiplied by a factor greater than one but less than the number of group members. This means that (i) the sum of payoffs is maximized if everybody contributes everything to the project but (ii) whatever the others contribute, each subject maximizes his or her own monetary payoff by contributing nothing. In the **partner condition**, group composition remains constant across ten periods. In the **stranger condition**, groups are randomly re-formed in every period.

After all subjects have made their contribution decisions, they are informed about the contributions of the other members of their group. In the **punishment treatment**, they can then assign punishment points to other group members. Each punishment point costs the punishing subject one monetary unit and reduces the income of the punished subject by three monetary units. In the **no-punishment treatment**, subjects can only observe but not punish other group members.

### Results: Cooperation with and without Punishment

**Figure 1** shows the evolution of average contributions in the partner and stranger conditions. The results are striking:

**Partner condition (Fig. 1a):**
- Without punishment: cooperation starts high (~10 units out of 20) but steadily declines to ~3 units by period 10
- With punishment: cooperation increases from ~11 to ~18 units, approaching full cooperation

**Stranger condition (Fig. 1b):**
- Without punishment: similar decline from ~10 to ~3 units
- With punishment: cooperation increases to ~15 units and remains stable

The Wilcoxon signed rank test shows that average contributions are significantly higher in the punishment treatment (P < 0.05 for all relevant comparisons).

### Who Punishes and Why?

A crucial question is whether punishment is indeed altruistic—that is, costly to the punisher without yielding material benefits. Our experimental design ensures this because:
1. Punishment is costly to the punisher
2. All interactions are one-shot (stranger) or finite (partner)
3. Subjects are anonymous
4. Reputation effects are ruled out

**Figure 2** shows the relationship between negative deviation from others' contributions and received punishment points. The relationship is strongly positive: those who contribute less than others receive substantially more punishment. This relationship holds in both conditions and in all periods.

**Key finding:** Punishment is directed at free-riders. The lower a subject's contribution relative to the group average, the more punishment they receive.

### The Role of Emotions

After the experiment, subjects were asked to indicate how angry they were at each of the other group members on a 7-point scale (1 = not at all, 7 = very much).

**Figure 3** reveals a strong relationship between negative emotions and punishment behaviour:
- Subjects who punished reported significantly higher anger towards free-riders
- The correlation between anger and punishment intensity is positive and significant
- Subjects who free-rode themselves reported less anger towards other free-riders

This suggests that **negative emotions towards defectors are the proximate mechanism behind altruistic punishment**.

### Frequency of Punishment

**Table 1** shows the percentage of subjects who punished at least once:

| Condition | Punishment Rate |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Partner | 84.3% |
| Stranger | 74.2% |

The vast majority of subjects engaged in costly punishment at least once, demonstrating that altruistic punishment is widespread and not limited to a few unusual individuals.

### Payoff Consequences

An important question is whether altruistic punishment is payoff-enhancing. In our experiment:
- In the partner condition, punishment is payoff-enhancing in later periods because it establishes cooperation
- In the stranger condition, punishment never enhances the punisher's payoff because groups change every period
- Yet punishment remains substantial even in the stranger condition

This finding is consistent with the interpretation that punishment is driven by emotions rather than strategic payoff calculations.

## Discussion

Our results demonstrate that altruistic punishment of defectors is a powerful force in sustaining human cooperation. The experimental design rules out all standard explanations based on repeated game effects, reputation, or kin selection. The fact that a large majority of subjects punish defectors, even when this is costly and yields no material benefit, strongly supports theoretical models based on strong reciprocity.

Several features of our results deserve emphasis:

1. **Punishment is widespread:** 84.3% of subjects in the partner condition and 74.2% in the stranger condition punished at least once.

2. **Punishment targets free-riders:** There is a strong negative relationship between contribution level and received punishment.

3. **Punishment sustains cooperation:** With punishment, cooperation approaches full contribution levels; without it, cooperation collapses.

4. **Emotions drive punishment:** Negative emotions towards defectors are strongly correlated with punishment behaviour.

5. **Punishment occurs even without strategic benefit:** In the stranger condition, punishment cannot benefit the punisher in future periods, yet it remains substantial.

These findings have important implications for understanding human cooperation. Standard evolutionary theories cannot explain cooperation in one-shot anonymous encounters. Our results suggest that altruistic punishment—which is costly and yields no material benefit—is a key mechanism that sustains cooperation in such situations.

The existence of altruistic punishment also has implications for the evolution of cooperation. If some individuals have a propensity to punish defectors at personal cost, this can sustain cooperation even among selfish individuals who would otherwise defect. This suggests that 'strong reciprocity'—the combination of altruistic cooperation and altruistic punishment—may be an important component of human evolutionary psychology.

## Methods

### Subjects
- 240 students from various disciplines at the University of Zürich
- No subject participated in more than one session
- Subjects were paid based on their performance

### Procedure
- Sessions conducted in German
- 24 subjects per session, divided into groups of 4
- Computerized interaction using z-Tree software
- All interactions anonymous
- Endowment: 20 tokens per period
- Exchange rate: 1 token = CHF 0.03

### Public Goods Game
- Marginal per capita return (MPCR) = 0.4
- Group size = 4
- Dominant strategy (if selfish): contribute 0
- Social optimum: contribute everything

### Punishment Technology
- Cost to punisher: 1 token per punishment point
- Cost to target: 3 tokens per punishment point received
- Maximum punishment: 10 points per target

### Design
- 2 × 2 factorial: (partner vs. stranger) × (punishment vs. no punishment)
- Within-subject comparison: each subject experienced both punishment and no-punishment
- 10 periods per treatment
- Order counterbalanced across sessions
