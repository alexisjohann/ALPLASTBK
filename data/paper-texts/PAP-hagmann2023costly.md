## Abstract

Policy challenges can typically be addressed both through systemic changes (e.g., taxes and mandates) and by encouraging individual behavior change. In this paper, we propose that, while in principle complementary, systemic and individual perspectives can compete for the limited attention of people and policymakers. Thus, directing policies in one of these two ways can distract the public's attention from the other—an "attentional opportunity cost." In two pre-registered experiments (n = 1,800) covering three high-stakes domains (climate change, retirement savings, and public health), we show that when people learn about policies targeting individual behavior (such as awareness campaigns), they are more likely to themselves propose policies that target individual behavior, and to hold individuals rather than organizational actors responsible for solving the problem, than are people who learned about systemic policies (such as taxes and mandates, Study 1). This shift in attribution of responsibility has behavioral consequences: people exposed to individual interventions are more likely to donate to an organization that educates individuals rather than one seeking to effect systemic reforms (Study 2). Policies targeting individual behavior may, therefore, have the unintended consequence of redirecting attention and attributions of responsibility away from systemic change to individual behavior.

## I. Introduction

The world faces major policy challenges that do not lend themselves to simple solutions. Effectively combating climate change, for example, will require firms to reduce emissions at factories as well as individuals to invest in electric cars. Similarly, poverty in old age cannot be solved solely by individual saving, but requires policies which ensure that employers share in the burden of financially supporting retirees. Increasing the diversity of the workforce may benefit from targeting the unconscious bias of hiring managers via training, but will also depend on public investments that make education accessible to traditionally excluded groups, as well as structural reforms to the hiring process.

### The I-Frame vs S-Frame Distinction

The magnitude of these and other policy challenges calls for a multitude of solutions that address both individual behavior and systemic incentives. Individual-level "nudge" interventions to encourage people to change their behavior tend to have relatively modest effect sizes (DellaVigna & Linos, 2022; Maier et al., 2022; Mertens et al., 2022). By contrast, systemic changes, such as decarbonizing the power grid and mandating low-carbon technologies has already contributed to dramatic falls in carbon emissions in many countries.

### The Attentional Opportunity Cost Hypothesis

In this paper, we present evidence from two preregistered experiments showing that there is an unanticipated cost to a focus on individual-level change. People exposed to policies targeting individual behavior shift their attention away from organizations and toward individuals as both the solution to, and source of, the problem. They subsequently believe it is less important to implement systemic solutions and are more likely to support causes that target individual behavior.

The possibility of such a redirection of attention is raised in Chater and Loewenstein (2022), who are concerned that behavioral scientists may inadvertently have contributed to shifting attention toward individual behavior (what they refer to as i-frame policies) and away from systemic reforms (s-frame policies).

## II. Related Literature

### Moral Licensing

Previous research has examined how individual behavior in one domain can affect behavior in another domain. This research shows, for example, that people who are nudged to conserve water end up using more electricity, increasing total carbon emissions overall (Tiefenbeck et al., 2013). Such "moral licensing" behavior, whereby good actions appear to excuse offsetting behavior elsewhere, appears to be widespread, albeit weak, across domains (Blanken et al., 2015).

### Crowding Out of Support for Systemic Policies

The most direct evidence comes from research which shows that drawing attention to individual behavior, or to policies that target individual behavior, decreases support for more systemic policies:

- **Werfel (2017):** When people are reminded of their energy-saving efforts, they become less likely to support a carbon tax.
- **Hagmann, Ho, & Loewenstein (2019):** In six experiments (including one with policymakers), people become less supportive of systemic reforms when first asked about support for a softer nudge.

### Corporate Strategy Evidence

While we are unaware of previous research demonstrating such attentional opportunity costs, we observe that corporate strategies have been consistent with a belief in this effect:

1. **Coca-Cola and Pepsi:** Have financed extensive health research emphasizing the importance of exercise over caloric consumption
2. **Oil Companies:** Launched recycling campaigns when public became concerned about plastic pollution
3. **Carbon Footprint Calculator:** The concept was introduced by British Petroleum
4. **Sustainable Investing:** Advanced by industries that profit from selling ESG funds and carbon offsets

## III. Study 1

### Design

- **Sample:** n = 1,200 participants from Prolific Academic
- **Design:** 3 (policy domain) × 2 (individual vs systemic prime) between-subjects
- **Domains:** Climate change, obesity, insufficient retirement savings
- **Pre-registration:** AsPredicted (https://aspredicted.org/YRC_74H)

### Manipulation

Participants evaluated three policies tackling their assigned problem:

**Individual Condition Example (Climate):**
> "When looking for vacation destinations, many people choose to fly to far-off destinations. One way people can reduce their carbon emissions is by taking fewer flights and instead traveling more locally."

**Systemic Condition Example (Climate):**
> "Electric cars lead to fewer carbon emissions than gas powered vehicles. To phase-out the number of gas-powered vehicles, the government can ban their sale after a specific date."

### Main Outcome Measures

1. **Self-categorization of proposed policy:** Individual, mix, or systemic
2. **Responsibility attribution:** Government vs. individual responsibility (7-point scales)
3. **Importance of systemic interventions:** Slider from -50 (individual) to +50 (systemic)

### Results

#### Policy Proposals (Figure 1)

| Domain | Systemic Condition | Individual Condition |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Climate | 90% systemic | 79% systemic |
| Finance | 79% systemic | 42% systemic |
| Health | 82% systemic | 75% systemic |

**Main Effect:** Participants are **18 percentage points less likely** to propose a systemic solution when they first evaluated individual policies (p < 0.001).

#### Responsibility Attribution (Figure 2)

| Domain | Systemic: (Gov - Ind) | Individual: (Gov - Ind) |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Climate | +1.08 | +0.83 |
| Finance | -0.02 | -1.37 |
| Health | -1.17 | -1.93 |

**Main Effect:** Exposure to individual interventions shifts responsibility attribution toward individuals (β = -0.786, p < 0.001).

#### Importance of Systemic Policies (Figure 3)

| Domain | Systemic Condition | Individual Condition |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Climate | 18.18 | 14.72 |
| Finance | 11.93 | -2.49 |
| Health | 9.23 | 2.69 |

**Main Effect:** Participants in Individual condition rate systemic interventions as less important (β = -8.135, p < 0.001).

## IV. Study 2

### Design

- **Sample:** n = 600 participants from Prolific Academic
- **Domain:** Retirement savings only (strongest effect in Study 1)
- **Pre-registration:** AsPredicted (https://aspredicted.org/CGV_JS4)

### Key Innovation: Incentivized Behavior

Participants voted for which organization should receive a $200 donation:
1. **Operation HOPE:** "Provides free programs to educate people on how to manage their money and enhance public financial literacy"
2. **American Retirement Association:** "Lobbies for a more comprehensive framework of policy to improve and protect the American retirement system"

### Results

#### Replication of Study 1

- **Systemic Condition:** 78% proposed systemic interventions
- **Individual Condition:** 44% proposed systemic interventions
- **Effect:** t(598) = 9.03, p < 0.001

#### Main Finding: Donation Behavior

| Condition | Vote for Systemic Charity |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Systemic | 44% |
| Individual | 35% |

**Effect:** t(598) = 2.31, p = 0.021

Participants in the Individual condition were **9 percentage points less likely** to vote for the organization seeking systemic reform.

## V. Discussion

### Main Conclusions

1. **Attentional Opportunity Cost:** Exposure to individual-level policies redirects attention away from systemic reforms
2. **Responsibility Shift:** People attribute more responsibility to individuals (vs. government) after exposure to individual interventions
3. **Behavioral Consequences:** This affects real (incentivized) donation behavior
4. **Cross-Domain Effect:** Replicates across climate, health, and finance domains

### Implications for Behavioral Public Policy

The findings suggest that the small gains from interventions targeting individual behavior may reduce the likelihood that systemic reforms are supported and implemented. This "attentional opportunity cost" should be incorporated into cost-benefit analyses of nudge interventions.

### Corporate Strategy Interpretation

The findings are consistent with corporate behavior:
- Coca-Cola/Pepsi funding exercise research (not diet research)
- Oil companies promoting recycling and carbon footprint calculators
- Financial industry advancing ESG investing

These may be strategic attempts to redirect attention from systemic solutions to individual behavior.

### Negative Complementarity (γ < 0)

This paper documents **NEGATIVE COMPLEMENTARITY** between i-frame and s-frame interventions:

$$\gamma(\text{i-frame}, \text{s-frame}) < 0$$

Exposure to i-frame policies **reduces** support for s-frame policies - the opposite of the standard complementarity assumption.

### Policy Implications

1. **Do not assume** i-frame and s-frame are independent
2. **Consider attentional opportunity costs** in cost-benefit analysis
3. **Sequence matters:** Leading with nudges may crowd out systemic reform support
