# People's Understanding of Inflation

**Authors:** Alberto Binetti (Bocconi), Francesco Nuzzi (Harvard), Stefanie Stantcheva (Harvard, NBER, CEPR)
**Journal:** Journal of Monetary Economics 148 (2024) 103652
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2024.103652
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

This paper studies people's understanding of inflation—their perceived causes, consequences,
trade-offs—and the policies supported to mitigate its effects. We design a new, detailed online
survey based on the rich existing literature in economics with two experimental components —
a conjoint experiment and an information experiment — to examine how well public views align
with established economic theories. Our key findings show that the major perceived causes of
inflation include government actions, such as increased foreign aid and war-related expenditures,
alongside rises in production costs attributed to recent events like the COVID-19 pandemic,
oil price fluctuations, and supply chain disruptions. Respondents anticipate many negative
consequences of inflation but the most noted one is the increased complexity and difficulty in
household decision-making. Partisan differences emerge distinctly, with Republicans more likely
to attribute inflation to government policies and foresee broader negative outcomes, whereas
Democrats anticipate greater inequality effects. Inflation is perceived as an unambiguously
negative phenomenon without any potential positive economic correlates. Notably, there is
a widespread belief that managing inflation can be achieved without significant trade-offs,
such as reducing economic activity or increasing unemployment. These perceptions are hard to
move experimentally. In terms of policy responses, there is resistance to monetary tightening,
consistent with the perceived absence of trade-offs and the belief that it is unnecessary to
reduce economic activity to fight inflation. The widespread misconception that inflation rises
following increases in interest rates even leads to support for rate cuts to reduce inflation.
There is a clear preference for policies that are perceived to have other benefits, such as
reducing government debt in progressive ways or increasing corporate taxes, and for support
for vulnerable households, despite potential inflationary effects.

## Key Findings

### 1. Knowledge of Inflation (Section 3.1)
- 57% believe inflation INCREASES after interest rate hike (incorrect)
- Only 13% correctly understand interest rate → inflation relationship
- 44% believe unemployment rises after interest rate increase
- Republicans more likely to see shocks as inflationary

### 2. Perceived Causes (Section 3.2)
- Government spending/debt/taxation: 67% (top cause)
- Production cost increases: 43%
- Federal Reserve actions: 31%
- Politicians/political interests: 22%
- Firms/businesses: 15%
- Labor market changes: 13%
- Household spending: 10%
- Partisan gap: Republicans blame government more, Democrats blame firms more

### 3. Perceived Consequences (Section 3.4)
- Complexity in daily decisions: 85% (important), 35% (MOST important)
- Shoe-leather costs: 80%
- Decreased trust in government: 70%
- Lower GDP growth: 70%
- Uncertainty for households: 70%

### 4. Perceived Distributional Impacts (Section 3.3)
- 84% believe low-income people lose from inflation
- Only 25% believe high-income people lose
- 75% believe small firms lose
- Only 20% believe big firms lose (56% think big firms BENEFIT)

### 5. Perceived Trade-offs (Section 3.5)
- Only 30% believe inflation happens more in booms
- Only 32% see inflation as side-effect of positive development
- Only 10% associate inflation with good economy
- Only 35% believe reducing unemployment policies increase inflation
- Only 30-40% think policymakers need to reduce activity to fight inflation
- 62% think reducing government debt necessary to reduce inflation

### 6. Policy Preferences (Section 4.2)
- 50% support DECREASING interest rates to fight inflation (misconception!)
- <20% support increasing interest rates
- 60% support reducing debt by taxing high incomes
- Strong support for corporate taxes, anti-trust, price freezes
- 55% support taxing high incomes to fund transfers to low incomes
- Strong support for redistributive policies (min wage, vouchers, food stamps)

### 7. Conjoint Experiment (Section 4.1)
- AMCE of inflation > AMCE of unemployment
- Inflation aversion higher than unemployment aversion
- Republicans put more weight on inflation (especially >10%)
- Higher-income and older respondents put more weight on inflation

### 8. Information Experiment (Section 4.3)
- Treatment slightly improved understanding of inflation as by-product of growth
- Did NOT shift perceptions about policy constraints
- Did NOT shift policy preferences
- "Deeply ingrained perception that inflation is inherently negative"

## Sample
- N = 2,264 (from initial 3,055)
- Representative US sample (Lucid platform)
- March-May 2024
- Median survey time: 29 minutes
- Quotas: age, income, gender, race
