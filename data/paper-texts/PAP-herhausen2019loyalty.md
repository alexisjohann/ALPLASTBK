# Loyalty Formation for Different Customer Journey Segments

**Authors:** Dennis Herhausen, Kristina Kleinlercher, Peter C. Verhoef, Oliver Emrich, Thomas Rudolph
**Journal:** Journal of Retailing, Vol. 95, No. 3, pp. 9-29
**Year:** 2019
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jretai.2019.05.001
**Publisher:** Elsevier

---

## Abstract

Retailers use a multitude of touchpoints to interact with their customers. Based on anticipated utility theory and using latent class analyses on large-scale data from two samples of 2,443 and 2,649 journeys, the researchers identified five time-consistent segments that differ considerably in their touchpoint and mobile device usage: (1) store-focused shoppers, (2) pragmatic online shoppers, (3) extensive online shoppers, (4) multiple touchpoint shoppers, and (5) online-to-offline shoppers. Importantly, these segments also differ substantially in how loyalty is formed. Journey satisfaction is most important for multiple touchpoint shoppers, while product satisfaction matters more for store-focused and pragmatic online shoppers. Customer inspiration additionally contributes to loyalty for store-focused shoppers, multiple touchpoint shoppers, and online-to-offline shoppers. The findings further indicate that mobile device users do not form a separate segment but are concentrated in the multiple touchpoint segment. We also find no separate showrooming segment, while online-to-offline behavior, also termed webrooming, is more common. The findings have direct implications for retailers' touchpoint investment, communication, and relationship marketing strategies.

**Keywords:** Customer journey; Customer experience; Touchpoints; Segmentation; Latent class analysis; Loyalty

## Introduction

Retailers use a multitude of touchpoints to interact with their customers. At the same time, customers are using increasing numbers of touchpoints before they purchase. Prior research has used touchpoint-based segmentation to identify segments of customers along their journeys, finding that different journey segments have different levels of customer lifetime value and should therefore be treated differently. However, it remains unclear how these journey segments differ in terms of the effects of satisfaction and other constructs on loyalty and repurchase intentions.

This research offers a comprehensive analysis of different customer journey segments and their loyalty formation processes. Applying latent class analysis, we identify journey segments based on touchpoint usage. We then investigate how loyalty is formed differently for each segment by analyzing the effects of journey satisfaction, product satisfaction, and customer inspiration on store loyalty and repurchase intentions.

## Conceptual Framework

### Anticipated Utility Theory

We employ anticipated utility theory as a theoretical lens through which we develop our predictions. Anticipated utility theory proposes that consumers derive utility not only at the time of consumption, but also from the anticipation of consumption. During customer journeys, customers are engaged in search, anticipation, and decision processes before the actual transaction takes place.

### Touchpoint-Based Segmentation

Based on anticipated utility theory, we expect customer journey segments to emerge that display differential touchpoint usage patterns. We measure touchpoint usage both before (as drivers of journey satisfaction) and at the point of purchase (as drivers of product satisfaction and customer inspiration).

### Loyalty Formation

We predict that customer journey segments not only differ in touchpoint usage but also in how loyalty is formed. The key question is which antecedents drive loyalty for which customer journey segment.

**H1:** Customer journey segments differ in the importance of journey satisfaction for loyalty formation.

**H2:** Customer journey segments differ in the importance of product satisfaction for loyalty formation.

**H3:** Customer journey segments differ in the importance of customer inspiration for loyalty formation.

## Methodology

### Data Collection

We collected data from two independent online panel samples in the DACH region (Germany, Austria, and Switzerland):
- **Sample 1 (2013):** 2,443 customer journeys
- **Sample 2 (2016):** 2,649 customer journeys
- **Total:** 5,092 customer journeys

Respondents were asked about their most recent shopping journey within a specified product category.

### Product Categories (8)

1. Clothing/Fashion
2. Consumer electronics
3. Drugstore products
4. Furniture
5. Groceries
6. Home improvement
7. Sports equipment
8. Toys

### Touchpoint Classification

**Retailer-Owned Touchpoints:**
- Physical store
- Print catalog
- Website
- Online shop
- Mobile app
- Social media presence

**Competitor-Owned Touchpoints:**
- Competitor's physical store
- Competitor's website/online shop
- Competitor's mobile app

**Additional Touchpoints:**
- Search engines
- Price comparison sites
- Product review sites
- Social networks (general)
- Friends/Family recommendations

### Latent Class Analysis (LCA)

We used latent class analysis to identify journey segments based on:
- Touchpoint usage (13 touchpoints)
- Mobile device usage
- Purchase channel (online vs. offline)

Model selection was based on:
- Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)
- Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
- Lo-Mendell-Rubin adjusted likelihood ratio test

## Results

### Five Customer Journey Segments (Table 2)

| Segment | Size | Key Characteristics |
|---------|------|---------------------|
| **Store-focused shoppers** | 22% | Low touchpoint usage overall; prefer physical stores; low mobile usage |
| **Pragmatic online shoppers** | 31% | Efficient online journey; use search engines and retailer website; purchase online |
| **Extensive online shoppers** | 16% | High online information search; comparison sites, reviews; purchase online |
| **Multiple touchpoint shoppers** | 15% | Use many touchpoints across channels; highest mobile usage; channel-agnostic |
| **Online-to-offline shoppers** | 16% | Research online (search, reviews); purchase in physical store; webrooming behavior |

### Segment Stability Over Time

The five-segment solution was replicated in both samples (2013 and 2016), demonstrating time consistency. Segment sizes were relatively stable:

| Segment | Sample 1 (2013) | Sample 2 (2016) |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Store-focused | 24% | 20% |
| Pragmatic online | 30% | 32% |
| Extensive online | 15% | 17% |
| Multiple touchpoint | 14% | 16% |
| Online-to-offline | 17% | 15% |

### Mobile Device Usage

Mobile devices do NOT create a new segment. Instead, mobile users are concentrated in:
- **Multiple touchpoint shoppers:** 47% use mobile devices
- **Other segments:** 15-25% mobile usage

### No Showrooming Segment

Contrary to popular belief, we find NO separate showrooming segment (research offline, buy online). Instead:
- **Webrooming (O2O)** is more prevalent: Research online, buy offline
- The online-to-offline segment represents 16% of journeys

## Summary of Hypotheses

| Hypothesis | Result |
|------------|--------|
| H1: Segments differ in journey satisfaction → loyalty | **Supported** (χ² = 47.82***) |
| H2: Segments differ in product satisfaction → loyalty | **Supported** (χ² = 38.14***) |
| H3: Segments differ in inspiration → loyalty | **Supported** (χ² = 15.67**) |

## Key Managerial Implications

### For Store-Focused Shoppers (22%)
- **Focus:** Product quality and in-store experience
- **Inspiration matters:** Create inspiring store environments
- **Journey less important:** Don't invest heavily in pre-purchase touchpoints

### For Pragmatic Online Shoppers (31%)
- **Focus:** Product quality and efficient online experience
- **Inspiration doesn't matter:** Focus on functionality, not inspiration
- **Streamline:** Reduce friction in online purchase process

### For Extensive Online Shoppers (16%)
- **Focus:** Information quality across touchpoints
- **Journey matters:** Invest in comprehensive product information
- **Reviews critical:** Ensure positive reviews and comparisons

### For Multiple Touchpoint Shoppers (15%)
- **Focus:** Seamless cross-channel experience
- **Journey is EVERYTHING:** β = 0.58*** vs. product β = 0.01 (NS)
- **Inspiration helps:** Create inspiring touchpoints across channels

### For Online-to-Offline Shoppers (16%)
- **Focus:** Bridge online research to offline purchase
- **Inspiration matters most:** β = 0.20*** (highest of all segments)
- **Information online, experience offline:** Support webrooming behavior

---

## COUNTERINTUITIVE FINDINGS

### 1. No Showrooming Segment
Popular belief: Customers research in stores, then buy online cheaper.
**REALITY:** We find NO separate showrooming segment. Webrooming (O2O) is more prevalent.

### 2. Mobile Doesn't Create New Segment
Popular belief: Mobile shoppers are a distinct segment.
**REALITY:** Mobile users are concentrated in the multiple touchpoint segment (47%), not a separate group.

### 3. Product Satisfaction Has ZERO Effect for Multiple Touchpoint Shoppers
Most surprising finding: For multiple touchpoint shoppers, product satisfaction has NO effect on loyalty (β = 0.01, NS), while journey satisfaction has the LARGEST effect (β = 0.58***).

### 4. Journey Satisfaction Has MINIMAL Effect for Store-Focused Shoppers
For store-focused shoppers, journey satisfaction barely matters (β = 0.11*), but product satisfaction is crucial (β = 0.40***).
