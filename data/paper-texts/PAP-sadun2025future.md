# The Future of Work: Hybrid Models and Organizational Design

**Author:** Raffaella Sadun (Harvard Business School)
**Year:** 2025
**Type:** Journal Article
**Archived Date:** 2026-02-24
**Content Level:** L2 (Comprehensive structured summary with S1-S6)

## Abstract

This paper examines how the shift to hybrid work models — combining remote and in-person work — reshapes organizational design. Drawing on organizational economics and management practice evidence, the paper argues that hybrid work is not merely a location choice but a fundamental redesign of how organizations coordinate, communicate, and create value. The analysis shows that successful hybrid models require complementary changes in management practices, technology infrastructure, and organizational culture. Firms that treat hybrid work as a simple flexibility arrangement without redesigning complementary organizational elements underperform relative to those that adopt a systems approach.

---

## 1. Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the largest natural experiment in work organization in modern history. As firms transition from emergency remote work to deliberate hybrid models, fundamental questions about organizational design resurface.

### Research Questions

1. **Design:** How should organizations design hybrid work models that maintain productivity and coordination?
2. **Complementarities:** What complementary organizational changes are required for hybrid work to succeed?
3. **Management:** How do management practices need to evolve for hybrid organizations?
4. **Culture:** What role does organizational culture play in the success or failure of hybrid models?

---

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Hybrid Work as Organizational Design

The paper frames hybrid work not as a benefits/flexibility decision but as an organizational design choice with system-wide implications:

**Coordination mechanisms** change: informal hallway conversations must be replaced by structured communication protocols. This shifts the coordination burden from emergent to designed.

**Monitoring and evaluation** change: output-based evaluation replaces input-based (hours-at-desk) evaluation, requiring new management capabilities.

**Culture transmission** changes: organizational culture, which traditionally spread through physical proximity and observation, must be transmitted through deliberate mechanisms in hybrid settings.

### 2.2 Complementarity Framework

Building on Milgrom and Roberts (1990) and Bloom et al. (2012), the paper identifies key complementarities:

| Element A | Element B | Complementarity |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Remote work | Digital tools | Must co-invest for effectiveness |
| Flexible scheduling | Output metrics | Input monitoring insufficient remotely |
| Hybrid meetings | Communication norms | Clear protocols needed for inclusion |
| Distributed teams | Trust-based culture | Culture enables remote autonomy |

**Key insight:** Partial adoption (e.g., allowing remote work without changing management practices) may actually reduce productivity — the "hybrid productivity trap."

## 3. Evidence on Hybrid Work Performance

### 3.1 Productivity Effects

The evidence on hybrid work productivity is mixed, but the paper argues this is explained by complementarity:
- Firms with high management quality that adopt hybrid work see productivity gains
- Firms with low management quality that adopt hybrid work see productivity losses
- The difference is the management infrastructure that supports remote coordination

### 3.2 The Management Practice Gap

Drawing on World Management Survey data, the paper documents that:
- Well-managed firms were faster to adopt effective hybrid arrangements
- Management practice scores predict hybrid work success better than industry or firm size
- The gap between well-managed and poorly-managed firms has widened post-pandemic

### 3.3 Organizational Culture and Hybrid Work

Culture plays a critical moderating role:
- Strong cultures (high δ_OC in Van den Steen 2024 terminology) enable hybrid work because employees share behavioral expectations even when physically separated
- Weak cultures struggle with hybrid work because coordination depends more on physical proximity
- New employee socialization is particularly challenging in hybrid settings, as culture transmission mechanisms that depend on observation and informal interaction are disrupted

## 4. Design Principles for Hybrid Organizations

### 4.1 Structured Flexibility

Effective hybrid models combine:
- **Core coordination days:** Specific days when teams are physically co-located
- **Flexible focus days:** Days for individual deep work, location-flexible
- **Explicit communication norms:** Clear expectations for response times, meeting formats, documentation

### 4.2 Technology-Management Complementarity

Technology enables but does not substitute for management capability:
- Collaboration tools (Slack, Teams, etc.) are necessary but insufficient
- Management practices around goal-setting, feedback, and evaluation must evolve
- Performance management must shift from input monitoring to output measurement

### 4.3 Culture Investment

The paper argues for increased deliberate investment in culture for hybrid organizations:
- Onboarding programs must be redesigned for hybrid contexts
- Mentoring and informal knowledge transfer require structured facilitation
- Organizational rituals and ceremonies need both physical and virtual components

---

## 5. Implications

### 5.1 For the Complementarity Framework

Hybrid work provides a contemporary example of organizational complementarity:
- Changing one element (work location) without changing complements (management, technology, culture) reduces overall system performance
- This mirrors the classical finding that HRM practice clusters matter more than individual practices (Ichniowski et al. 1997)

### 5.2 For Strategic Management

Hybrid work strategy is not separable from broader organizational strategy:
- Firms choosing innovation-intensive strategies may need more in-person time for serendipitous interaction
- Firms choosing efficiency-focused strategies may benefit more from distributed work
- The optimal hybrid model depends on the firm's overall strategic configuration
