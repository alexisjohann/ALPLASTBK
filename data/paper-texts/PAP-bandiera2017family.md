# Managing the Family Firm: Evidence from CEOs at Work

**Authors:** Oriana Bandiera, Renata Lemos, Andrea Prat, Raffaella Sadun

**Source:** Review of Financial Studies, 2017 (also NBER Working Paper No. 19722)

---

**JEL Classification:** M1, M12, M5

**NBER Working Paper No. 19722** | December 2013, Revised August 2017

## Abstract

We present evidence on the labor supply of CEOs, and on whether family and professional CEOs differ on this dimension. We do so through a new survey instrument that allows us to codify CEOs' diaries in a detailed and comparable fashion, and to build a bottom-up measure of CEO labor supply. The comparison of 1,114 family and professional CEOs reveals that family CEOs work 9% fewer hours relative to professional CEOs. Hours worked are positively correlated with firm performance, and differences between family and non-family CEOs account for approximately 18% of the performance gap between family and non-family firms. We investigate the sources of the differences in CEO labor supply across governance types by exploiting firm and industry heterogeneity, and quasi-exogenous meteorological and sport events. The evidence suggests that family CEOs value–or can pursue–leisure activities relatively more than professional CEOs.

## 1. Introduction

The question of what determines firm performance has a long tradition in the economics literature. The incentive structure of managers is a key determinant, and the literature has shown that CEO pay is correlated with performance (Murphy 1985, Jensen and Murphy 1990), that managerial incentives affect decisions (Bertrand and Mullainathan 2003, Graham et al. 2013), and that individuals with superior managerial skills earn more and generate more firm value (Bertrand and Schoar 2003, Bloom et al. 2012, Garicano and Rossi-Hansberg 2006).

One of the most basic dimensions of managerial input is the amount of work performed, or labor supply, yet there is surprisingly little evidence on how much CEOs work, and almost no evidence on how much more or less they work relative to other workers. This paper fills this gap by providing direct measures of CEO labor supply and investigating whether family and professional CEOs differ in hours worked.

Family firms are the predominant organizational form in the world economy. The prevalence of family ownership and control raises important questions about whether family management is efficient. A large body of work documents that family management, in particular CEO succession within the family, is associated with negative performance outcomes (Morck et al. 1988, Perez-Gonzalez 2006, Bloom and Van Reenen 2007, Bennedsen et al. 2007, Bertrand et al. 2008). This evidence has been interpreted as consistent with the hypothesis that family CEOs are less able than professional CEOs (Burkart et al. 2003). We ask whether, conditional on ability, family CEOs also choose to exert less effort.

We address this question using direct measures of CEO labor supply from a survey in which CEOs record their activities in 15-minute intervals for an entire working week. The survey was conducted in Brazil, France, Germany, India, UK, and US, providing a sample of 1,114 CEOs in manufacturing firms. We define CEO labor supply as the total number of hours the CEO devoted to work activities (as opposed to personal activities) during the survey week.

Our main finding is that family CEOs work 9% fewer hours than professional CEOs in the same industry in the same country. To interpret this gap, we need to understand the mechanism through which ownership and control affect the incentives to exert effort. We build a simple model where CEOs decide how many hours to work by trading off the disutility of effort against the monetary return to work and the non-pecuniary amenity value of employment. A key distinction between family and professional CEOs is the probability of termination: family CEOs are less exposed to performance-based termination than professional CEOs. The model predicts that family CEOs will work less, and that the gap will be larger in environments where performance incentives are stronger, i.e., in more competitive product markets.

To test these predictions, we: (1) document the correlation between CEO hours and firm performance; (2) present evidence that the family-professional gap in CEO hours is larger in more competitive product markets; (3) explore the role of individual differences in preferences for leisure as an explanation for the gap in hours worked.

We find that CEO hours are positively correlated with firm performance, and the family-professional gap in CEO hours is not explained by firm characteristics, including firm size, age, and legal form. We also find that the gap is larger in more competitive product markets, consistent with the model's predictions. We finally find that family CEOs are more likely to interrupt their work to attend to personal activities during the workday, consistent with a higher preference for leisure.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the time use survey and our measure of CEO labor supply. Section 3 documents that family CEOs work fewer hours than professional CEOs. Section 4 presents a simple model of CEO labor supply. Section 5 presents evidence on the mechanism. Section 6 concludes.

Our paper contributes to several strands of literature. First, we contribute to the literature on the determinants of firm performance by providing direct evidence that managerial effort, measured by CEO hours, matters for performance. While the importance of managerial talent and incentive design is well-established, direct evidence on managerial effort is rare due to measurement difficulties. Notable exceptions include Weisbach (1988), who uses outside director presence to proxy for effort, and Bertrand and Schoar (2003), who use CEO fixed effects to measure management styles. Second, we contribute to the literature on family firms by providing evidence that family CEOs exert less effort than professional CEOs, suggesting that the inferior performance of family firms may be partly attributed to differences in CEO effort rather than ability alone. Third, we contribute to the nascent literature on CEO time use. Mintzberg (1973) was the first to systematically study how managers spend their time. More recently, Bandiera et al. (2011) use a similar methodology in a study of CEOs in India, and Bloom et al. (2012) document that structured management practices matter for performance.

## 2. The Labor Supply of CEOs: Measurement

### 2.1 The Time Use Survey

We measure CEO labor supply using a survey in which CEOs record their activities with a Personal Assistant (PA) who shadows the CEO for one week. The PA records all CEO activities in 15-minute slots. For each activity, the PA records: (1) whether the activity is a work or personal activity; (2) which function the activity is related to (if a work activity); (3) who the CEO interacted with; (4) where the activity took place; and (5) the planned versus unplanned nature of the activity.

The key advantage of this methodology relative to standard time-use diaries, in which individuals self-report their activities in 10 or 15 minute intervals, is that the CEO does not have to recall activities retrospectively. The PA records activities as they happen, and can follow up to clarify. This minimizes recall bias and measurement error that typically affect diary-based surveys.

The survey was implemented in six countries: Brazil, France, Germany, India, UK, and US. We focused on manufacturing firms because they are more comparable across countries than service firms in terms of what CEOs do. We targeted medium-to-large firms, with at least 100 employees, and selected CEOs of corporations, as opposed to owner-managers of sole proprietorships or partnerships.

In each country, we first identified target firms from business directories and databases. We then called the CEO directly or went through the company's PA to arrange an interview. The main challenge was to convince the CEO and his/her company to participate in the survey. We sent a letter explaining the survey and then followed up with a phone call. In many cases, we needed to conduct multiple phone calls before the CEO agreed to participate.

### 2.2 Sample

The final sample consists of 1,114 CEOs, distributed across six countries as follows: Brazil (449 CEOs), France (68), Germany (132), India (247), UK (111), and US (107). Table 1 provides summary statistics for the sample.

We define CEO labor supply as the number of hours the CEO devoted to work activities (as opposed to personal activities) during the survey week, excluding travel time. We exclude travel because travel time may reflect demand side factors (e.g., the geographic scope of the firm's operations) rather than CEO effort per se.

The average CEO in our sample works 46.9 hours during the survey week. There is considerable heterogeneity: the standard deviation is 9.6 hours, and the distribution ranges from a minimum of 12 hours to a maximum of 89 hours. These numbers are broadly consistent with existing evidence from surveys of executive time use, such as those reported in Mintzberg (1973) and Porter and Nohria (2018).

### 2.3 Measuring Family vs. Professional CEOs

We classify a CEO as a family CEO if: (1) the CEO owns shares of the company or is a member of the family that owns the company; or (2) the CEO was appointed by the family that owns a controlling stake of the company, in cases where the company is family-owned but not managed by a family member.

We classify the remaining CEOs as professional CEOs. Professional CEOs are those who were hired externally, typically from a pool of managerial candidates, and who do not have a direct equity stake or family connection to the firm.

This classification is based on information collected during the survey. We asked CEOs to identify whether they own shares of the company, whether they are related to the founding family, and who appointed them to their current role.

In our sample, 464 CEOs (41.7%) are family CEOs and 650 (58.3%) are professional CEOs. Table 1 shows that family firms are somewhat smaller and older than professional firms, and that family CEOs are more likely to have worked in the same firm for longer, while professional CEOs are more likely to have an MBA degree and international experience.

## 3. Family vs. Professional CEOs

### 3.1 Hours Worked

Our main finding is that family CEOs work fewer hours than professional CEOs. The average family CEO works 44.2 hours per week, compared to 48.7 hours for the average professional CEO, a gap of 4.5 hours or approximately 9%.

Table 2 reports OLS estimates of the regression of log CEO hours on a family CEO dummy and controls. Column (1) includes only country and industry fixed effects. The coefficient on the family CEO dummy is -0.088, suggesting that family CEOs work 8.4% fewer hours than professional CEOs in the same country and industry. This estimate is highly statistically significant (p < 0.01). Columns (2)-(5) add controls for firm characteristics (size, age, legal form), CEO characteristics (age, tenure, education), and noise controls (interviewer fixed effects, week fixed effects). The coefficient on the family CEO dummy is stable across all specifications, ranging from -0.082 to -0.094. Column (6) uses the log of total hours including travel as the dependent variable, and finds a similar gap.

The magnitude of the gap is economically significant. A difference of approximately 9% in hours worked is comparable in magnitude to differences observed across occupations and demographic groups in standard labor supply studies. To put this in context, the gender gap in hours worked among full-time workers in the U.S. is approximately 5% (Blau and Kahn 2017).

### 3.2 Composition of Hours

We next examine whether family and professional CEOs differ in how they allocate their hours across activities. We focus on three main categories: meetings (planned interactions with specific individuals or groups), plant visits, and office/desk work.

Family CEOs spend less time in meetings and more time in office/desk work and plant visits than professional CEOs. This suggests that the 9% gap in total hours reflects a genuine difference in effort, rather than a reallocation of time from more to less productive activities.

We also examine differences in the people the CEO interacts with. Professional CEOs spend more time interacting with people outside the firm (e.g., customers, suppliers, investors) than family CEOs. This is consistent with the scale of operations effect (Mayer 1960), which suggests that more able managers should engage in a wider range of external activities.

### 3.3 Attrition

One potential concern with the survey is selective attrition: if CEOs who work more are more likely to refuse to participate, our estimates of average hours will be biased downward, and if refusal rates differ between family and professional CEOs, our estimates of the family-professional gap may also be biased.

To assess the importance of selective attrition, we examine the observable characteristics of respondents and non-respondents. We find that respondents and non-respondents are similar in terms of observable characteristics (firm size, age, industry), suggesting that attrition is not strongly selective. We also find no evidence that response rates differ between family and professional firms.

We also conduct a sensitivity analysis in which we impute hours worked for non-respondents using a range of assumptions and find that the family-professional gap is robust to these imputations.

### 3.4 Cross-Country Heterogeneity

We examine whether the family-professional gap in hours worked varies across countries. Table 3 reports estimates separately for each country. The gap is positive and statistically significant in all six countries, ranging from -0.062 in Germany to -0.123 in India. The gap is largest in India and Brazil, the two countries with the weakest market competition and product market regulations.

We also examine whether the gap varies with country-level characteristics such as GDP per capita, rule of law, and trust. We find that the gap is larger in countries with lower GDP per capita, lower rule of law, and lower trust. These patterns are consistent with our model's predictions, as discussed in Section 5.

## 4. A Simple Model of CEO Labor Supply

In this section, we present a simple model that provides a framework for interpreting the empirical findings. The model builds on the standard principal-agent framework and focuses on the CEO's decision to supply labor.

### 4.1 Setup

Consider a CEO who chooses how many hours, h, to work. The CEO's utility is:

U = w(h, π) - c(h) + b

where w(h, π) is total compensation that depends on hours worked h and firm performance π, c(h) is the cost of effort (disutility of working), and b is the amenity value of employment (private benefit of being CEO).

We assume that firm performance π is increasing in CEO hours: π = f(h) + ε, where ε is a random shock. We further assume that c(h) is increasing and convex: c'(h) > 0 and c''(h) > 0.

The key distinction between family and professional CEOs concerns their probability of termination. Let ρ be the probability that a CEO is terminated in a given period. We assume that this probability depends on firm performance for professional CEOs:

ρ_P = ρ_P(π) with dρ_P/dπ < 0

For family CEOs, termination is less sensitive to performance:

ρ_F = ρ_F(π) with |dρ_F/dπ| < |dρ_P/dπ|

This assumption reflects the empirical evidence that family CEOs are less likely to be fired for poor performance than professional CEOs (Morck et al. 1988, Perez-Gonzalez 2006). A family CEO who performs poorly still retains the private benefits of being CEO and controlling the family firm, so the performance-termination link is weaker.

### 4.2 CEO Optimization

The CEO maximizes expected utility by choosing the optimal number of hours to work. The first-order condition equates the marginal benefit of effort (in terms of increased compensation and lower probability of termination) with the marginal cost:

∂U/∂h = w_h + (b + w) × (-∂ρ/∂π) × f'(h) - c'(h) = 0

This condition implies that the optimal hours h* are increasing in the performance sensitivity of termination (-∂ρ/∂π). Since professional CEOs face a steeper performance-termination gradient, they optimally supply more hours than family CEOs.

### 4.3 Competition and Incentives

We extend the model to consider the role of product market competition. Following Hermalin (1992) and Schmidt (1997), we assume that competition affects the performance sensitivity of termination: in more competitive markets, worse performance more reliably signals managerial shirking (as opposed to bad luck), and the performance-termination link is therefore stronger.

This implies that the family-professional gap in hours should be larger in more competitive product markets: in competitive markets, professional CEOs have stronger incentives to work, while family CEOs do not. In less competitive markets, the performance-termination link is weaker for both types of CEOs, and the gap is smaller.

### 4.4 Predictions

The model generates the following testable predictions:

**Prediction 1:** Family CEOs work fewer hours than professional CEOs.

**Prediction 2:** The family-professional gap in hours is larger in more competitive product markets.

**Prediction 3:** Family CEOs are more likely to interrupt their work for personal activities (i.e., have a higher preference for leisure).

## 5. Why Do Family CEOs Work Less?

We now examine the mechanisms underlying the family-professional gap in CEO hours. We focus on two main channels: (1) the performance sensitivity of termination; and (2) individual differences in preferences for leisure.

### 5.1 Hours and Firm Performance

We first examine the relationship between CEO hours and firm performance. If CEO hours are a measure of managerial effort, they should be positively correlated with firm performance, after controlling for firm characteristics.

We measure firm performance using TFP (total factor productivity), estimated using the Olley-Pakes (1996) methodology to address the simultaneity bias in standard OLS production function estimates. We also use labor productivity (sales per worker) as an alternative measure of performance.

Table 4 reports OLS estimates of the regression of TFP on CEO hours and controls. Column (1) reports the coefficient on CEO hours without controls. The coefficient is positive and statistically significant: a 10% increase in CEO hours is associated with a 1.1% increase in TFP. Columns (2)-(5) add controls for firm characteristics, CEO characteristics, and country and industry fixed effects. The coefficient on CEO hours is stable across specifications, ranging from 0.072 to 0.082.

We also examine whether the family-professional gap in TFP is explained by the gap in hours. We find that controlling for CEO hours reduces the family-professional gap in TFP by approximately 40%, suggesting that differences in labor supply account for a substantial fraction of the performance gap between family and professional firms.

### 5.2 CEO Preferences for Leisure

The model predicts that the family-professional gap in hours reflects differences in incentives rather than differences in preferences. If family CEOs have stronger preferences for leisure (due to unobservable ability differences or selection), the gap might simply reflect sorting rather than incentive effects.

To examine the role of preferences, we exploit variation in exogenous shocks to the opportunity cost of working: rainfall and major sport events. We identify situations in which working has a higher personal cost due to bad weather (preventing outdoor leisure activities) or major sport events (providing salient leisure alternatives).

If family CEOs have a higher preference for leisure, they should be more responsive to these shocks: on rainy days or during major sport events, family CEOs should work relatively more hours (as the opportunity cost of leisure is higher or the quality of leisure is higher, respectively), and on non-rainy days or during periods without sport events, they should work relatively fewer hours.

**Rainfall Analysis**

We match CEO survey weeks with weather data from weather stations near the CEO's firm. We define a "rain day" as a day in which precipitation exceeds the 75th percentile for that location and month. We then estimate the effect of rain on CEO hours for family and professional CEOs separately.

Table 5 presents the results. We find that professional CEOs work more hours on rainy days, consistent with a positive effect of bad weather on the cost of leisure. However, family CEOs do not adjust their hours on rainy days: the coefficient on the rain dummy is small and statistically insignificant for family CEOs. This suggests that family CEOs' hours are not responsive to the opportunity cost of leisure, which is inconsistent with the hypothesis that family CEOs have stronger preferences for leisure.

**Sport Events Analysis**

We match CEO survey weeks with data on major sport events in each country: we focus on the broadcast of major domestic and international sporting events (e.g., football matches involving national teams or major leagues). We code days with major sport events as "sport event days."

Table 6 presents the results. Consistent with the rainfall analysis, we find that professional CEOs work fewer hours on sport event days, while family CEOs do not adjust their hours. The differential response of professional CEOs is consistent with our model: professional CEOs have stronger incentives to work hard, but on days when the opportunity cost of effort is high (due to major sport events), they respond by working fewer hours. Family CEOs, who have weaker incentives to begin with, do not respond to these shocks.

Overall, the weather and sport events analysis suggests that the family-professional gap in CEO hours is not driven by stronger preferences for leisure among family CEOs. Instead, it is consistent with the hypothesis that professional CEOs have stronger performance incentives.

### 5.3 Competition

We next test Prediction 2 of our model: the family-professional gap in hours should be larger in more competitive product markets.

We measure product market competition using two proxies: (1) the Lerner index, defined as operating profit divided by sales, with the industry average subtracted; and (2) import penetration, defined as total imports divided by apparent consumption for the industry. We obtain these measures from industry-level data sources (see Appendix A.3).

Table 7 reports estimates of the regression of CEO hours on the family CEO dummy, the competition measure, and their interaction. Columns (1)-(3) use the Lerner index as the competition measure, and columns (4)-(6) use import penetration. In all specifications, the interaction between the family CEO dummy and the competition measure is negative and statistically significant: family CEOs work relatively fewer hours in more competitive environments. This is consistent with Prediction 2: in more competitive markets, professional CEOs have stronger incentives to work hard, while family CEOs do not face the same pressure.

The magnitude of the interaction effect is economically significant. In the most competitive quartile of industries (by import penetration), the family-professional gap in hours is approximately 15%, compared to 5% in the least competitive quartile.

### 5.4 Termination Risk

Our model predicts that the family-professional gap reflects differences in the performance sensitivity of termination. To test this directly, we examine whether the gap is larger for professional CEOs who face a higher risk of termination.

We proxy for termination risk using firm ownership structure: professional CEOs in firms with dispersed ownership face higher termination risk than those in firms with concentrated ownership (because dispersed ownership enables more effective monitoring). We find that the family-professional gap is larger in firms with dispersed ownership, consistent with our model's predictions.

We also examine whether the gap varies with CEO tenure. CEOs with shorter tenure are more likely to face termination risk, as they have not yet established a track record. We find that the family-professional gap is larger for shorter-tenure professional CEOs, again consistent with our model.

### 5.5 Placebo Tests

We conduct a series of placebo tests to ensure that our findings are not driven by spurious correlations. First, we test whether the family-professional gap in hours is explained by observable differences in ability. We control for CEO education, prior work experience, and MBA degree, and find that the gap is robust to these controls.

Second, we test whether the gap is driven by differences in firm size or industry. We find that the gap is robust to controlling for firm size (log employees), firm age, and detailed industry fixed effects.

Third, we examine whether our weather and sport event shocks are truly exogenous. We test whether the shocks are correlated with observable CEO and firm characteristics, and find no evidence of systematic correlation.

## 6. Conclusion

We study whether and why family and professional CEOs differ in the number of hours they work. Our analysis uses unique data on the time use of 1,114 CEOs in six countries, measured in 15-minute intervals for an entire working week.

Our main finding is that family CEOs work 9% fewer hours than professional CEOs in the same industry and country. This gap is economically significant, robust to a wide range of controls, and not explained by firm or industry characteristics.

We interpret the family-professional gap through a simple model where CEOs decide how many hours to work by trading off the disutility of effort against monetary returns and the amenity value of employment. The key distinguishing feature of the model is the difference in the performance sensitivity of termination: professional CEOs face stronger performance-based termination risk, and therefore have stronger incentives to work hard.

The model generates three main predictions, all of which are supported by the data: (1) family CEOs work fewer hours; (2) the gap is larger in more competitive product markets; and (3) the gap is not driven by differences in preferences for leisure.

Our analysis has implications for understanding the origins of family firm underperformance. The literature has interpreted the negative performance outcomes associated with family management as evidence that family CEOs are less able. Our evidence suggests that, even conditional on ability, family CEOs choose to exert less effort, pointing to a role for incentive design in explaining family firm performance.

The findings also speak to broader questions about the determinants of CEO labor supply. We document that CEO hours are positively correlated with firm performance and respond to competitive pressure, suggesting that managerial effort is an important input to firm productivity. This provides direct evidence on the mechanism through which product market competition affects firm performance (Nickell 1996, Bloom and Van Reenen 2007).

Finally, our paper contributes to the methodological literature on measuring managerial input. Direct time-use surveys provide a rich and credible measure of CEO effort that complements traditional measures such as executive pay and CEO fixed effects. We hope that future work will exploit these methods to shed further light on the determinants and consequences of managerial effort.

## Appendix A: Data Appendix

### A.1 Time Use Survey

#### Survey Management

The time use survey was implemented in each country by a local survey organization under our supervision. The survey organization was responsible for recruiting CEOs and managing the interviewing process. We provided detailed training to the survey organization and monitored the implementation closely.

In each country, the sampling frame consisted of manufacturing firms with at least 100 employees. We targeted the CEO (or equivalent top manager) of each firm. Where the firm had multiple plants, we targeted the CEO of the headquarters.

The implementation of the survey involved the following steps: (1) identify target firms from business directories; (2) contact the firm by phone to speak with the CEO or the CEO's personal assistant; (3) send a letter describing the survey and requesting participation; (4) follow up by phone to confirm participation; (5) schedule the interview week; (6) conduct the interview week, during which the PA shadows the CEO and records all activities.

The PA was typically a local employee of the survey organization, who had been trained to implement the protocol. In cases where it was not possible for the PA to physically accompany the CEO (e.g., for travel), the PA conducted phone interviews with the CEO at regular intervals to record activities retrospectively.

#### Sampling Frame

The sampling frame for the survey consisted of manufacturing firms with at least 100 employees, obtained from business directories and firm databases in each country:

- **Brazil:** RAIS (Relação Anual de Informações Sociais), the Brazilian social security database.
- **France:** SIRENE database (Système National d'Identification et du Répertoire des Entreprises et des Établissements).
- **Germany:** Hoppenstedt firm database.
- **India:** Prowess database (Centre for Monitoring Indian Economy).
- **UK:** OneSource database.
- **US:** Dun & Bradstreet database.

We stratified the sampling frame by industry (2-digit SIC code) and firm size (number of employees). We then randomly selected firms within each stratum, with probability proportional to size.

The response rate varied across countries, from approximately 25% in the US to 60% in India. In countries with lower response rates, we replaced non-responding firms with firms from the same stratum.

### A.2 Firm Data

#### Firm Accounts

We obtained firm-level accounting data from the ORBIS database (Bureau Van Dijk), which provides comprehensive financial data for firms in multiple countries. We matched the survey firms to the ORBIS database using the firm's name and address.

For firms not matched to ORBIS, we obtained accounting data from country-specific databases:

- **Brazil:** RAIS database for employment, and Economática database for accounting data.
- **India:** Prowess database (CMIE).
- **UK:** FAME (Financial Analysis Made Easy) database.
- **US:** Compustat database.

The main accounting variables used in the analysis are: total assets, total sales, total employment, and operating profit. We deflate nominal values using country-specific GDP deflators.

#### Firm Ownership

We obtained information on firm ownership from the following sources:

- **Survey:** We asked each CEO to identify whether the firm is family-owned, and if so, who is the controlling shareholder.
- **ORBIS:** We obtained ownership data from the ORBIS database, which provides information on the ultimate beneficial owner of each firm.
- **Country-specific databases:** For India, we obtained ownership data from the Prowess database.

We classify a firm as family-owned if the controlling shareholder is an individual or family (as opposed to a corporation, financial institution, or government).

We classify the CEO as a family CEO if: (1) the CEO is the controlling shareholder; (2) the CEO is a member of the controlling family; or (3) the CEO was appointed by the controlling family, even if not a family member.

### A.3 Industry Data

We obtained industry-level data on competition from the following sources:

- **Lerner Index:** We compute the Lerner index as the operating profit margin (operating profit divided by sales) for each 2-digit SIC industry, using firm-level data from ORBIS. We then take the negative of the industry average to obtain a measure of competition: higher values indicate more competition.

- **Import Penetration:** We compute import penetration as total imports divided by apparent consumption (total production plus imports minus exports) for each 3-digit ISIC industry. We obtain trade data from COMTRADE and production data from UNIDO.

- **Industry dummies:** We use 33 two-digit SIC codes as industry dummies in all regressions.

### A.4 Shocks

#### Rainfall

We obtained daily precipitation data from weather stations in each country from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Global Surface Summary of Day (GSOD) database. For each CEO, we matched the survey dates to weather data from the nearest weather station.

We define a "rain day" as a day in which precipitation exceeds the 75th percentile of the precipitation distribution for that weather station and calendar month. We then define a "rain week" as a survey week in which at least one day is a rain day.

To construct the rainfall instrument, we count the number of rain days in each CEO's survey week. We interact this variable with the family CEO dummy to test whether family and professional CEOs respond differently to rainfall.

#### Sport Events

We compiled a list of major sport events in each country during the survey period. We focused on events that are widely watched on television and that are unlikely to be correlated with economic conditions:

- **Brazil:** Copa Libertadores (South American club football championship) and Brazilian Série A (domestic football league).
- **France:** Ligue 1 (domestic football league) and Roland Garros (French Open tennis).
- **Germany:** Bundesliga (domestic football league).
- **India:** Indian Premier League (IPL, cricket).
- **UK:** Premier League (domestic football league).
- **US:** Major League Baseball (MLB) and National Football League (NFL).

We code a day as a "sport event day" if a major sport event is broadcast live on television that day. We then define a "sport event week" as a survey week in which at least one day is a sport event day.

### A.5 Wealth and the Demand for Leisure

We use cross-country variation in inheritance laws as an instrument for family wealth. Ellul et al. (2010) construct a measure of the maximum fraction of wealth that can be left to the most-favored heir under the inheritance law of each country. This variable, "Max % of inheritable wealth," ranges from 0 (no bequest freedom) to 1 (complete freedom to bequeath to the most-favored heir).

Countries with higher values of this variable allow wealthy families to concentrate more wealth in a single heir, reducing the need for family CEOs to work hard to maintain their wealth position. We therefore expect that the family-professional gap in hours is larger in countries with higher values of "Max % of inheritable wealth."

For India, where firms are predominantly organized as Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) organizations under Hindu law, the relevant inheritance law is different from that applicable to individual persons. We set the value of "Max % of inheritable wealth" for India to 0.50, reflecting the fact that HUF organizations provide some degree of bequest freedom.
