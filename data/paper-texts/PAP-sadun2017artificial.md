**Type:** Journal Article
**Archived Date:** 2026-02-24
**Content Level:** L2 (Comprehensive structured summary with S1-S6)

## Abstract

Taking three recent business books on artificial intelligence (AI) as a starting point, this paper explores the automation and augmentation concepts in the management domain. Whereas automation implies that machines take over a human task, augmentation means that humans collaborate closely with machines to perform a task. Taking a normative stance, the three books advise organizations to prioritize augmentation, which they relate to superior performance. Using a more comprehensive paradox theory perspective, the authors argue that, in the management domain, augmentation cannot be neatly separated from automation. These dual AI applications are interdependent across time and space, creating a paradoxical tension. Overemphasizing either augmentation or automation fuels reinforcing cycles with negative organizational and societal outcomes. However, if organizations adopt a broader perspective comprising both automation and augmentation, they could deal with the tension and achieve complementarities that benefit business and society. Drawing on these insights, the paper concludes that management scholars need to be involved in research on the use of AI in organizations and argues that a substantial change is required in how AI research is currently conducted in order to develop meaningful theory and to provide practice with sound advice.

## 1. Introduction

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has created both excitement and anxiety in the management domain. Three prominent business books — McAfee and Brynjolfsson's "Machine, Platform, Crowd," Davenport and Kirby's "Only Humans Need Apply," and Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb's "Prediction Machines" — offer different perspectives on how organizations should respond to AI capabilities. All three, however, converge on a normative recommendation: organizations should prioritize augmentation (human-machine collaboration) over automation (machine replacement of humans).

### Research Questions

1. **Automation vs. Augmentation:** Can these two approaches to AI in management be cleanly separated, or are they fundamentally interdependent?
2. **Paradox:** How do the tensions between automation and augmentation play out in organizational practice?
3. **Complementarities:** Under what conditions can organizations achieve positive synergies between automation and augmentation?

## 2. The Three Books

### 2.1 Machine, Platform, Crowd (McAfee & Brynjolfsson)

The first book argues that three fundamental shifts are reshaping business: the shift from human minds to machines, from products to platforms, and from the core to the crowd. The authors advocate for augmentation, where humans use AI tools to enhance their decision-making, rather than replacement.

### 2.2 Only Humans Need Apply (Davenport & Kirby)

The second book focuses on five strategies for humans to remain relevant alongside AI: stepping up (moving to higher-level cognitive work), stepping aside (doing work that machines cannot do well), stepping in (monitoring and adjusting AI systems), stepping narrowly (finding narrow specializations), and stepping forward (developing new AI systems). The emphasis is on augmentation through complementary human-machine skills.

### 2.3 Prediction Machines (Agrawal, Gans & Goldfarb)

The third book frames AI fundamentally as a prediction technology — one that reduces the cost of prediction. This economic framing suggests that as prediction becomes cheaper, the value of human judgment (the ability to determine what to do with predictions) increases. This creates a natural augmentation framework where machines predict and humans judge.

## 3. The Automation-Augmentation Paradox

### 3.1 The Interdependence Argument

The paper's central theoretical contribution is arguing that automation and augmentation are not independent strategic choices but are paradoxically intertwined:

**Temporal Interdependence:** Automation in one period creates the conditions for augmentation in the next. As machines take over routine tasks, humans are freed to engage in higher-level collaboration with machines.

**Spatial Interdependence:** Within an organization, automation in one function (e.g., data processing) enables augmentation in another (e.g., strategic decision-making). The two approaches coexist and depend on each other.

**Level Interdependence:** At the task level, automation may dominate; at the job level, augmentation may be more appropriate; at the organizational level, both must be managed simultaneously.

### 3.2 Reinforcing Cycles

The paper identifies negative reinforcing cycles when organizations overemphasize one approach:

**Automation Trap:** Excessive automation leads to deskilling of employees → reduced capacity for augmentation → further automation → organizational brittleness → inability to handle novel situations.

**Augmentation Trap:** Excessive focus on augmentation leads to underinvestment in automation → competitive cost disadvantage → inability to free resources for human-machine collaboration → stagnation.

### 3.3 Complementarity Resolution

The resolution lies in recognizing that automation and augmentation are complements, not substitutes:

$$V(\text{auto}, \text{aug}) > V(\text{auto}, 0) + V(0, \text{aug})$$

When organizations invest in both simultaneously, they can achieve positive synergies: automation handles routine predictions, freeing humans for judgment-intensive augmentation tasks. This complementarity is strongest when:
- The task environment combines routine and novel elements
- The organization has sufficient absorptive capacity for both approaches
- Feedback loops between automated and augmented processes are well-designed

## 4. Implications for Management Research

### 4.1 Need for Management Scholars in AI Research

The paper argues that management scholars bring unique perspectives to AI research:
- Understanding of organizational dynamics and change processes
- Knowledge of human behavior in organizations (motivation, resistance, adaptation)
- Expertise in managing paradoxes and tensions
- Experience with complementarities in organizational design

### 4.2 Required Changes in AI Research

Current AI research is primarily conducted by computer scientists and engineers, with limited attention to organizational and human factors. The paper calls for:
- Interdisciplinary research teams including management scholars
- Longitudinal studies of AI adoption in organizations
- Attention to organizational context (culture, structure, incentives)
- Study of complementarities between human and machine capabilities
