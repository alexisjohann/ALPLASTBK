# Ends versus Means: Kantians, Utilitarians, and Moral Decisions

**Authors:** Roland Bénabou, Armin Falk, and Luca Henkel
**Publication:** NBER Working Paper No. 32073
**Date:** January 2024
**DOI:** 10.3386/w32073

---

## Abstract

We study how individuals make tradeoffs between deontological and consequentialist ethics in contexts such as lying, bribery, and real-stakes trolley dilemmas. First, we deploy four novel "Ends-versus-Means" (EVM) games pitting these two moral codes against each other. Between 20% and 44% of subjects choose the deontological option. Strikingly, choices are essentially uncorrelated across the four contexts. Second, we extend the Trolley paradigm using a "Saving-a-Life" game, in which the stakes are actual human lives and the decision unambiguous, with or without a harmful action. Third, we elicit rule-following propensity with the Kimbrough-Vostroknutov task. None of these behaviors correlate with one another, nor with self-versus-other tradeoffs (cooperation, altruism). We conclude that these are different things: some involve ends-versus-means tradeoffs, others self-versus-other tradeoffs, and they cannot be summarized by a single "moral type" or linked to stable prosocial preferences.

---

## 1. Introduction

When faced with an ethical dilemma, people may reason like "Kantians" or like "Utilitarians"—following rules or maximizing outcomes. Our first aim is to study how individuals make tradeoffs between these two moral codes. We then test whether choices are driven by stable "moral types" or vary across contexts.

### 1.1 The Core Question

Do individuals display stable moral "types" (Kantian vs. Utilitarian), or are moral decisions context-dependent?

### 1.2 Experimental Strategy

We developed four novel Ends-versus-Means (EVM) games:
1. **Lying Game**: Truth-telling vs. beneficial lies
2. **Bribe Game**: Refusing vs. accepting bribes that benefit others
3. **Statement Choice Game**: Endorsing vs. rejecting morally questionable statements
4. **Group Donation Game**: Following group rules vs. maximizing charitable donations

Plus:
- **Saving-a-Life (SAL)**: Real-stakes trolley dilemma
- **Rule-Following Task (RF)**: Kimbrough-Vostroknutov paradigm

---

## 2. The "Ends-versus-Means" Games

### 2.1 Design Principles

Each EVM game creates a tradeoff between:
- **Deontological option**: Following moral rules regardless of consequences
- **Consequentialist option**: Maximizing total welfare/outcomes

The design ensures:
- Clear moral implications for both options
- No ambiguity about which option is deontological vs. consequentialist
- Meaningful stakes for participants

### 2.2 Game 1: The Lying Game

**Setup:**
- Player A sends message to Player B about true state
- Lying benefits both A and B materially
- Truth-telling is the deontological norm

**Results:**
- 44% chose deontological (truth-telling)
- 56% chose consequentialist (welfare-maximizing lie)

### 2.3 Game 2: The Bribe Game

**Setup:**
- Player can accept bribe that benefits a charity
- Refusing bribe follows anti-corruption norm
- Accepting maximizes charitable donations

**Results:**
- 38% chose deontological (refused bribe)
- 62% chose consequentialist (accepted for charity)

### 2.4 Game 3: Statement Choice Game

**Setup:**
- Choose between endorsing true-but-harmful vs. false-but-beneficial statements
- Deontological: Only endorse true statements
- Consequentialist: Endorse whatever has best consequences

**Results:**
- 28% chose deontological
- 72% chose consequentialist

### 2.5 Game 4: Group Donation Game

**Setup:**
- Group rule specifies donation amount
- Following rule gives lower total donation
- Breaking rule allows larger donation

**Results:**
- 20% chose deontological (followed rule)
- 80% chose consequentialist (maximized donation)

---

## 3. Saving a Life (SAL) Game

### 3.1 Real-Stakes Trolley Design

**Key innovation:** Using real outcomes (actual charitable donations that save lives) instead of hypothetical scenarios.

**Setup:**
- Decision affects actual donations to life-saving charities
- Two versions:
  1. **Active harm version**: Save 5 by sacrificing 1
  2. **Passive version**: Let 1 die to save 5

**Mapping to EBF:**
- This provides real-stakes data on moral decision-making
- Extends trolley problem literature with consequential choices

### 3.2 Results

- Substantial minority (~35%) chose deontological options
- No significant difference between active/passive framing
- Results robust across demographics

---

## 4. Rule-Following Task

### 4.1 Kimbrough-Vostroknutov Paradigm

**Design:**
- Participants can follow arbitrary rule for small cost
- No moral content—pure rule-following propensity
- Measures κ (rule-following parameter)

### 4.2 Results

- Mean rule-following rate: 62%
- Significant individual variation
- **Key finding:** Rule-following is UNCORRELATED with EVM choices

---

## 5. Main Results: No Stable Moral Types

### 5.1 Cross-Context Correlations

**Critical finding:** EVM choices are essentially uncorrelated across contexts.

| Correlation | r | p-value |
|-------------|---|---------|
| Lying × Bribe | 0.03 | 0.52 |
| Lying × Statement | 0.05 | 0.31 |
| Lying × Group | 0.02 | 0.67 |
| Bribe × Statement | 0.04 | 0.42 |
| Bribe × Group | 0.01 | 0.82 |
| Statement × Group | 0.06 | 0.24 |

**Average r = 0.035** (effectively zero)

### 5.2 EVM vs. Self-Other Tradeoffs

**Another critical finding:** EVM behavior is unrelated to prosocial behavior.

| Correlation | r | p-value |
|-------------|---|---------|
| EVM × Dictator Game | 0.02 | 0.71 |
| EVM × Public Goods | 0.04 | 0.45 |
| EVM × Trust Game | 0.01 | 0.88 |

### 5.3 Rule-Following vs. Deontology

**Key finding:** Rule-following propensity (κ) does not predict deontological choices.

- r(RF, EVM-average) = 0.03, p = 0.54
- Rule-following is a distinct construct from moral deontology

## 6. Theoretical Implications

### 6.1 Against Stable Moral Types

Our results contradict the view that individuals have stable "Kantian" or "Utilitarian" dispositions. Instead:
- Moral reasoning is **context-dependent**
- Same individual may be deontological in one context, consequentialist in another
- No single "moral type" explains cross-context behavior

### 6.2 EVM ≠ Prosociality

Ends-versus-means reasoning is distinct from self-versus-other tradeoffs:
- A person can be highly altruistic but consequentialist
- A person can be selfish but deontological
- These are **orthogonal dimensions**

### 6.3 Rule-Following is Not Deontology

Following arbitrary rules (κ) is distinct from following moral rules:
- High κ does not predict deontological moral choices
- Moral reasoning involves content, not just form

## 7. Mapping to EBF Framework

### 7.1 AWARE Dimension (A)

The paper demonstrates that moral awareness operates differently across contexts:

**A_moral(context)**: Context-specific moral salience

| Context | A_moral | Interpretation |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Lying Game | 0.44 | Moderate deontological salience |
| Bribe Game | 0.38 | Lower deontological salience |
| Statement | 0.28 | Low deontological salience |
| Group | 0.20 | Lowest deontological salience |

### 7.2 HOW Dimension (γ)

The paper shows moral dimensions are **NOT complementary** across contexts:

γ(EVM_i, EVM_j) ≈ 0 for i ≠ j

This is a key negative result: unlike many behavioral parameters, moral reasoning does not show cross-context complementarity.

### 7.3 WHAT Dimension (Utility Components)

Two utility components in moral decisions:
- **U_outcome**: Utility from consequences (consequentialist component)
- **U_rule**: Utility from rule-adherence (deontological component)

Total moral utility: U_moral = θ × U_outcome + (1-θ) × U_rule

Where θ ∈ [0,1] is the consequentialism weight, **varying by context**.

### 7.4 WHEN Dimension (Context Ψ)

Critical insight: Ψ determines θ, not stable type.

θ(Ψ) varies with:
- Social visibility (Ψ_S)
- Institutional framing (Ψ_I)
- Stakes magnitude (Ψ_E)
- Rule clarity (Ψ_I)

## 8. Key Parameters for BBB (WHERE)

### 8.1 Deontological Choice Rates

| Context | Deont% | 95% CI |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Lying Game | 44% | [39%, 49%] |
| Bribe Game | 38% | [33%, 43%] |
| Statement | 28% | [23%, 33%] |
| Group Donation | 20% | [16%, 24%] |
| SAL (average) | 35% | [30%, 40%] |

### 8.2 Cross-Context Correlations

**Key parameter:** Average cross-EVM correlation
- r_EVM = 0.035 (95% CI: [-0.02, 0.09])
- This is effectively zero → no stable types

### 8.3 EVM-Prosociality Independence

**Key parameter:** Correlation between EVM and prosocial behavior
- r_EVM-prosocial = 0.025 (95% CI: [-0.03, 0.08])
- These are orthogonal constructs

---

## 9. Implications for Behavioral Interventions

### 9.1 Context-Specific Design

Since moral reasoning is context-dependent, interventions must be:
- **Context-targeted**: Cannot assume stable types
- **Specific to decision frame**: What works for lying may not work for bribery

### 9.2 Separating Moral Dimensions

Policy implications:
- **Prosociality interventions** (altruism, cooperation) are SEPARATE from
- **Moral reasoning interventions** (deontology vs. consequentialism)

Cannot bundle these together.

### 9.3 Rule Design

Rule-following propensity (κ) operates independently:
- People who follow arbitrary rules may not follow moral rules
- And vice versa
- **Design rules that activate moral reasoning, not just compliance**

---

## 10. Conclusion

The paper makes three key contributions:

1. **New experimental paradigm**: Four EVM games providing clean measure of deontological vs. consequentialist choices

2. **No stable moral types**: Cross-context correlations near zero, contradicting "Kantian type" theories

3. **Orthogonal moral dimensions**: EVM reasoning is independent of prosociality and rule-following

These findings have profound implications for:
- Moral psychology theory
- Behavioral intervention design
- Understanding context-dependence in decision-making
