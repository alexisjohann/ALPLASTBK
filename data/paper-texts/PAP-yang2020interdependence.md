## Abstract

This article discusses an economics approach to studying business strategy, with particular focus on the relationship between business strategy and economic policy. It begins with a definition of business strategy and a survey of how complementarities provide a mathematical framework for studying strategy. Complementarities describe interactions between strategic choice variables: raising one choice variable increases the returns of raising other choice variables—and so changes in the business environment, including economic policies, will provoke changes in several strategic choice variables simultaneously. Next, the article asks how strategic decisions are made and discusses measurement approaches. It then reviews the literature on key strategic interactions, with a focus on international business strategies and interactions with innovation policies. It concludes with discussions of potential implications for economic policy, including antitrust, industrial policy, and corporate social responsibility.

## I. Introduction

Business strategy has held a core position in economics for many decades. Important contributions from such economists as Penrose (1959), Chandler (1962, 1977), and Porter (1980, 1985) have sought to understand how businesses succeed, and why some fail. When Milgrom and Roberts (1990) demonstrated that complementarities and supermodularity provide a natural framework for strategy, it sparked a large literature on business complementarities and how strategy arises—a literature that has spanned management, industrial organization, organizational economics, and macroeconomics.

There is a fundamental interdependence between a business and its environment. Changes in its environment cause changes in strategic choice variables, and a business' choices have implications for its environment. This interdependence gives rise to the interactions between a business' strategy and the economy. Business strategy is therefore an important concern in designing economic policy.

This article discusses the economics approach to studying business strategy. After it reviews the body of academic work on complementarities, it then turns to measuring strategy. Here, economists distinguish between strategy process (how strategy is determined) and strategy content (what type of activities a business chooses to focus on and how these choices are organized). Finally, after discussing strategic interactions for businesses engaged in international markets and interacting with innovation policies, it concludes with a discussion of the implications for economic policy.

## II. Complementarities as a Framework for Studying Strategy

### II.1 Definition of Business Strategy

What is business strategy? Porter (1996) identifies three components: (i) a unique and valuable position in its market, (ii) trade-offs that distinguish it from competitors, and (iii) a 'fit' or coherence across internal activities.

Michael Porter notes: 'A company can outperform rivals only if it can establish a difference that it can preserve. It must deliver greater value to customers or create comparable value at lower cost, or do both.'

Strategy is more than simply operational effectiveness. Operational effectiveness refers to firms adopting superior methods for operating their business. It yields only temporary competitive advantage, since these practices spread easily and competition will dissipate any competitive gains. True strategic positioning, in contrast, involves performing different activities from rivals' or performing similar activities in different ways (Porter, 1996).

### II.2 Complementarities and Supermodularity

Milgrom and Roberts (1990) demonstrated that complementarities and supermodularity provide a natural framework for studying strategy. Complementarities describe interactions between strategic choice variables: raising one choice variable increases the returns of raising other choice variables.

**Formal Definition:** Two activities are complements if doing more of one activity increases the returns to doing more of the other. Mathematically, for a function f(x,y), activities x and y are complements if:

$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} > 0$$

**Supermodularity:** A function f is supermodular if for all x and y:

$$f(x \vee y) + f(x \wedge y) \geq f(x) + f(y)$$

where ∨ denotes the componentwise maximum and ∧ the componentwise minimum.

### II.3 Implications of Complementarities for Strategy

Milgrom and Roberts (1990, 1995) explain key implications:

1. **System-wide changes:** Complementarities imply that changes in the environment will affect not just one choice variable, but several simultaneously. A modern manufacturing firm that adopts flexible manufacturing will also change its inventory policy, marketing strategy, product variety, and organizational structure.

2. **Coherent strategy:** The returns to any activity increase when other complementary activities are also present. This explains why successful firms exhibit coherent strategies where choices 'fit' together.

3. **Discrete jumps:** If activities are highly complementary, partial changes may actually reduce firm performance. This implies that strategy changes will be discrete jumps from one coherent configuration to another—explaining why strategic change is often radical rather than incremental.

4. **Difficulty of imitation:** Complementarities help explain sustainable competitive advantage. A competitor who imitates only some practices without the full system may actually harm their performance.

### II.4 Mathematical Framework

Building on Topkis (1978), the mathematical framework uses lattice theory:

**Theorem (Topkis):** If f(x,θ) is supermodular in x for each θ, and has increasing differences in (x,θ), then the set of maximizers x*(θ) is increasing in θ.

This means that when parameters shift (θ increases), optimal choices move together in the same direction—exactly the clustering of strategic choices that we observe empirically.

## III. Measuring Strategic Decisions

### III.1 Strategy Process vs. Strategy Content

Economists distinguish between:

- **Strategy process:** How strategy is determined—whether by explicit planning, emergent learning, or spontaneous order
- **Strategy content:** What type of activities a business chooses to focus on and how these choices are organized

### III.2 World Management Survey Approach

Bloom and Van Reenen (2007) pioneered a large-scale survey approach to measuring management practices. They developed:

1. **Structured interviews** with plant managers
2. **Scoring methodology** across 18 management practices
3. **Double-blind evaluation** to reduce measurement error

Key findings:
- Large persistent differences in management practices across firms
- Management practices strongly correlate with productivity
- Differences persist even within industries and countries

### III.3 Measuring Complementarities Empirically

Several approaches have been used:

**Adoption approach:** Examining whether practices are adopted together (Milgrom and Roberts, 1990; Ichniowski et al., 1997)

**Performance approach:** Testing whether the joint adoption of practices leads to performance improvements greater than the sum of individual effects

**Structural approach:** Estimating production functions that allow for complementary inputs (Brynjolfsson and Milgrom, 2012)

## IV. Strategic Interactions: International Business

### IV.1 Multinational Enterprises and Complementarities

For multinational enterprises (MNEs), complementarities arise between:
- Location choice and organizational structure
- Technology transfer and local adaptation
- Headquarters control and subsidiary autonomy

### IV.2 Global vs. Local Strategies

MNEs face a fundamental tension:
- **Global integration:** Exploiting economies of scale and scope
- **Local responsiveness:** Adapting to host country conditions

This trade-off creates complementarities between:
- Product standardization vs. customization
- Centralized vs. decentralized decision-making
- Expatriate vs. local management

### IV.3 Knowledge Transfer

Complementarities exist between:
- R&D investment and absorptive capacity
- Knowledge codification and tacit knowledge transfer
- Internal training and external hiring

## V. Strategic Interactions: Innovation Policy

### V.1 R&D and Complementary Investments

Innovation requires complementary investments in:
- Human capital (scientists, engineers)
- Physical capital (laboratories, equipment)
- Organizational capital (routines, culture)
- External relationships (suppliers, customers, universities)

### V.2 Policy Implications

Government innovation policies interact with firm strategy through:

1. **R&D tax credits:** May induce changes in multiple complementary activities
2. **Patent policy:** Affects both innovation incentives and knowledge diffusion
3. **Education policy:** Affects supply of skilled workers

### V.3 Technology Adoption

Complementarities explain patterns of technology adoption:
- Technologies are often adopted in clusters
- Adoption requires complementary organizational changes
- Spillovers create interdependencies across firms

## VI. Implications for Economic Policy

### VI.1 Antitrust Policy

**Merger analysis:** Complementarities matter for merger review:
- Horizontal mergers may create synergies through complementary assets
- Vertical mergers may enable better coordination of complementary activities
- Efficiency claims require understanding of complementarities

**Exclusionary conduct:** Complementarities can explain both pro-competitive and anti-competitive bundling

### VI.2 Industrial Policy

Complementarities create both opportunities and challenges for industrial policy:

**Opportunities:**
- Coordinating complementary investments (infrastructure, skills, R&D)
- Addressing coordination failures when complementarities create multiple equilibria

**Challenges:**
- Government may lack information about complementarities
- Partial interventions may be counterproductive
- Political economy concerns

### VI.3 Corporate Social Responsibility

CSR activities may be complements or substitutes with other business activities:
- Environmental investments may reduce costs or create market opportunities
- Social initiatives may improve employee morale and productivity
- Governance reforms may improve capital access

Policy design should consider how CSR regulations interact with firms' overall strategies.

### VI.4 Public-Private Partnerships

Complementarities between public and private activities suggest scope for partnerships:
- Public infrastructure complements private investment
- Public R&D complements private innovation
- Public education complements private training

## VII. Conclusions

This article has surveyed the economics approach to business strategy, with particular focus on complementarities. Key conclusions:

1. **Complementarities provide a rigorous framework** for understanding strategy, explaining why successful firms exhibit coherent configurations of choices.

2. **Strategic decisions cluster together,** implying that changes in the business environment—including policy changes—affect multiple choice variables simultaneously.

3. **Measurement remains challenging,** but progress has been made through survey-based approaches and structural estimation.

4. **Policy implications are significant,** affecting antitrust analysis, industrial policy design, CSR regulations, and public-private partnerships.

5. **The interdependence between business and environment** means that policy-makers cannot treat firms' strategic responses as exogenous.

Future research should further develop:
- Empirical methods for measuring complementarities
- Understanding of how complementarities interact with uncertainty and learning
- Analysis of complementarities in digital and platform markets
- International comparisons of business strategy and policy
