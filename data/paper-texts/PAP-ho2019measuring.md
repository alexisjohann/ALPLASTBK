## Abstract

This paper develops the Information Preferences Scale (IPS), a measure of individual differences in preferences for obtaining information. Across three studies with over 2,400 participants, we demonstrate that individuals differ systematically in their desire for information about health, personal finances, and personal characteristics. We find that approximately 32% of responses indicate a preference for not obtaining information, even when it is freely available and potentially useful. The IPS shows acceptable test-retest reliability (r = 0.64) and a four-factor structure corresponding to health information, financial information, personal characteristics information, and a general factor. Information preferences measured by the IPS predict actual information acquisition decisions in laboratory tasks and correlate with related psychological constructs including need for cognition, intolerance of uncertainty, and health anxiety.

## I. Introduction

### The Puzzle of Information Avoidance

Standard economic models assume that rational agents will always prefer more information to less, provided information is free and has no direct negative consequences. Yet abundant evidence suggests that people often avoid information, even when it is costless, could be useful for decision-making, and could not directly affect their outcomes.

### Examples of Information Avoidance

- **Health:** People avoid learning about genetic predispositions, HIV status, cholesterol levels
- **Finance:** Investors avoid checking portfolio performance during market downturns
- **Personal:** People avoid feedback about abilities, appearance, and social standing
- **General:** Students avoid checking grades; employees avoid performance reviews

### Need for a Validated Measure

Despite the prevalence and importance of information avoidance, no validated scale exists to measure individual differences in information preferences. This paper addresses this gap by developing and validating the Information Preferences Scale (IPS).

## II. Theoretical Framework

### Why People Avoid Information

Several mechanisms can explain information avoidance:

1. **Anticipatory Utility:** Information that changes beliefs can affect current well-being through anticipation of future states (Loewenstein, 1987; Caplin & Leahy, 2001)

2. **Regret Aversion:** Learning outcomes of foregone alternatives can cause regret; people may avoid information to preclude this possibility

3. **Cognitive Dissonance:** Information inconsistent with prior beliefs or self-image causes psychological discomfort

4. **Strategic Ignorance:** Remaining ignorant can serve as an excuse for self-interested behavior (Dana et al., 2007)

5. **Belief-Based Utility:** People derive utility directly from holding certain beliefs, independent of their truth value (Brunnermeier & Parker, 2005)

### Information Preferences as a Trait

We propose that information preferences represent a relatively stable individual difference:
- Some individuals are generally "information seekers"
- Others are generally "information avoiders"
- Domain-specific variation exists within this general tendency

## III. Study 1: Scale Development

### Method

**Participants:** N = 802 participants recruited from Amazon Mechanical Turk
- Mean age: 35.4 years
- 48% female
- Diverse educational and income backgrounds

**Item Generation:**
- Initial pool of 45 items across three domains
- Items asked about hypothetical scenarios: "If [information] were freely available, would you want to know?"
- Response scale: 5-point (1 = Definitely would not want to know, 5 = Definitely would want to know)

### Three Domains

**Health Information:**
- Genetic disease risk
- Life expectancy
- Future medical conditions
- Disease progression

**Financial Information:**
- Investment performance
- Net worth compared to peers
- Future income trajectory
- Retirement adequacy

**Personal Characteristics:**
- How others perceive you
- True abilities and talents
- Romantic partners' opinions
- Friends' assessments

### Results: Factor Analysis

**Exploratory Factor Analysis** revealed four factors:

| Factor | Items | Eigenvalue | Variance Explained |
|--------|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Health | 4 | 4.21 | 32.4% |
| Financial | 3 | 2.15 | 16.5% |
| Personal | 4 | 1.43 | 11.0% |
| General | 2 | 1.08 | 8.3% |

**Final Scale:** 13 items (IPS-13)

### Descriptive Statistics

**Mean Information Preference Scores (1-5 scale):**

| Domain | Mean | SD | % Prefer NOT knowing |
|--------|------|-----|---------------------|
| Health | 3.42 | 1.12 | 28% |
| Financial | 3.68 | 0.98 | 22% |
| Personal | 2.89 | 1.24 | 41% |
| Overall | 3.33 | 0.89 | 32% |

**Key Finding:** Approximately 32% of all responses indicated a preference for NOT obtaining information.

## IV. Study 2: Reliability and Validity

### Method

**Participants:** N = 1,012 participants (Prolific Academic)
- 51% female
- Mean age: 38.2 years
- Subsample (n = 245) completed scale twice (2-week interval)

### Test-Retest Reliability

| Measure | r (2-week) |
|---------|------------|
| IPS Total | 0.64 |
| Health subscale | 0.61 |
| Financial subscale | 0.58 |
| Personal subscale | 0.67 |

### Convergent Validity

Correlations with related constructs:

| Construct | r with IPS | Interpretation |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Need for Cognition | 0.34*** | Higher NFC → more information seeking |
| Intolerance of Uncertainty | -0.28*** | Higher IU → more information avoidance |
| Health Anxiety | -0.19*** | Higher HA → health info avoidance |
| Optimism (LOT-R) | 0.22*** | More optimistic → more information seeking |
| Neuroticism | -0.31*** | Higher N → more information avoidance |

### Discriminant Validity

The IPS showed weaker correlations (r < 0.15) with:
- Intelligence measures
- Socioeconomic status
- Age (slight positive)
- Gender (negligible)

### Confirmatory Factor Analysis

The four-factor model showed good fit:
- CFI = 0.94
- RMSEA = 0.058
- SRMR = 0.042

## V. Study 3: Predictive Validity

### Method

**Participants:** N = 604 participants (MTurk)
- Mean age: 36.1 years
- 50% female

### Behavioral Measure: Information Acquisition Task

Participants completed a computer task with opportunities to obtain free information:

1. **Health scenario:** Option to learn result of (hypothetical) genetic test
2. **Financial scenario:** Option to see how investment performed vs. alternatives
3. **Personal scenario:** Option to see evaluations from previous study partners

**Design:** Information was:
- Free to obtain
- Clearly relevant to decisions
- Could not affect actual outcomes (hypothetical or past)

### Results: Predicting Actual Information Acquisition

| IPS Subscale | β (Acquisition) | p |
|--------------|-----------------|-----|
| Health IPS | 0.41 | < 0.001 |
| Financial IPS | 0.38 | < 0.001 |
| Personal IPS | 0.33 | < 0.001 |
| Overall IPS | 0.44 | < 0.001 |

**Interpretation:** A one SD increase in IPS score corresponds to approximately 15 percentage point increase in probability of acquiring information.

### Domain Specificity

Cross-domain predictions were weaker:
- Health IPS predicted health information acquisition (β = 0.41) but not financial (β = 0.12)
- Financial IPS predicted financial information acquisition (β = 0.38) but not health (β = 0.09)

## VI. The Information Preferences Scale (IPS-13)

### Final Scale Items

**Health Domain (4 items):**
1. If you could learn whether you carry a gene that significantly increases your risk of developing Alzheimer's disease, would you want to know?
2. If you could learn exactly how many years you have left to live, would you want to know?
3. If you could learn whether you will develop a serious chronic disease in the next 10 years, would you want to know?
4. If you could learn whether a medical treatment will be successful for you, would you want to know?

**Financial Domain (3 items):**
5. If you could learn exactly how your investment returns compare to what you could have earned with different investments, would you want to know?
6. If you could learn precisely how your lifetime earnings will compare to people similar to you, would you want to know?
7. If you could learn whether your retirement savings will be adequate, would you want to know?

**Personal Characteristics Domain (4 items):**
8. If you could learn exactly what your friends really think of you, would you want to know?
9. If you could learn precisely how attractive others find you, would you want to know?
10. If you could learn exactly how intelligent you are compared to others, would you want to know?
11. If you could learn your romantic partner's honest assessment of your relationship, would you want to know?

**General Domain (2 items):**
12. In general, I prefer to have as much information as possible, even if it might be unpleasant.
13. When facing an uncertain situation, I would rather know the truth, even if it's bad.

### Scoring

- Items are summed (range: 13-65)
- Higher scores indicate greater preference for information
- Subscale scores can be computed separately
- Reverse scoring not required (all items in same direction)

## VII. Discussion

### Key Contributions

1. **First Validated Scale:** The IPS is the first validated measure of information preferences as an individual difference

2. **Prevalence of Information Avoidance:** ~32% of responses indicate preference for not knowing, demonstrating that information avoidance is common even when information is free

3. **Trait with Domain Specificity:** Information preferences are relatively stable but show meaningful domain-specific variation

4. **Behavioral Prediction:** The IPS predicts actual information acquisition decisions, not just stated preferences

### Theoretical Implications

**For Economics:**
- Challenges assumption of universal information seeking
- Suggests heterogeneity in information preferences should be incorporated into models
- Relevant for information disclosure policies

**For Psychology:**
- Links to broader literature on defensive processing
- Connects to research on motivated reasoning and belief-based utility
- Relevant for health behavior and financial decision-making research

### Practical Applications

**Health Communication:**
- Identify patients who may avoid genetic testing results
- Tailor information delivery to preferences
- Understand non-adherence to screening recommendations

**Financial Services:**
- Design personalized information presentation
- Understand investor behavior during market volatility
- Improve financial planning interventions

**Organizations:**
- Understand employee responses to performance feedback
- Design effective evaluation and feedback systems
- Tailor communication strategies

### Limitations

1. **Self-Report:** Scale relies on stated preferences, though Study 3 shows behavioral prediction
2. **WEIRD Samples:** Primarily US participants; cross-cultural validation needed
3. **Hypothetical Scenarios:** Scale asks about hypothetical information
4. **Two-Week Retest:** Longer retest intervals would strengthen stability claims
