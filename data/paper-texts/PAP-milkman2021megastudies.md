## Abstract

Massive field experiments, or megastudies, enable efficient comparisons of the impacts of different interventions on a common outcome. Here we report the results of a megastudy involving 61,293 gym members of 24 Hour Fitness and testing 54 interventions designed to increase gym visits. We found that 45% of our tested interventions significantly increased gym visits over a 4-week period, with the top interventions producing a 9% to 27% increase in average weekly visits. The best-performing intervention offered small rewards for returning to the gym after a missed workout. Twenty-two per cent of our interventions that led to significant behaviour change were also significant 10 weeks after the end of the intervention. Our trial was also designed to assess how well experts and lay people predicted intervention effectiveness, and we found that forecasters failed to anticipate the ranking of our top interventions. Our megastudy experimental paradigm provides an efficient approach for mass testing of the effectiveness of behavioural interventions in naturalistic settings.

## I. Introduction

### The Challenge of Behavioural Intervention Research

Behavioural interventions targeting consequential outcomes such as savings, vaccine uptake and gym attendance are increasingly popular, but their effects are often small and studies frequently focus on only one or a few interventions. This limits our ability to identify the most effective approaches.

### The Megastudy Paradigm

We introduce the concept of a **megastudy**—a massive field experiment in which many treatments are tested synchronously—to help researchers efficiently assess which interventions work best. A megastudy provides a single-study comparison of the effects of many interventions on a common outcome, measured in a consistent manner.

### Advantages of Megastudies

1. **Efficiency**: Direct comparison of many interventions in one study
2. **Statistical power**: Large sample sizes enable detection of small effects
3. **Standardization**: Common outcome measure, setting, and population
4. **Forecasting assessment**: Can evaluate experts' predictive accuracy
5. **Rapid knowledge accumulation**: Years of research compressed into one study

## II. Methods

### Experimental Design

**Setting:** 24 Hour Fitness gyms across the United States
**Sample:** 61,293 members who agreed to participate
**Period:** September-December 2018
**Randomization:** Stratified by historical gym visits

### Interventions Tested

54 different interventions designed to increase gym visits, grouped into categories:

| Category | N Interventions | Description |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Planning | 8 | Planning prompts, commitment devices |
| Reminders | 6 | Text message reminders of various types |
| Social | 7 | Social norms, accountability partners |
| Incentives | 9 | Financial rewards, points, gamification |
| Fresh Start | 4 | Framing new beginnings |
| Mindset | 6 | Growth mindset, self-efficacy |
| Identity | 5 | Identity-based interventions |
| Microrewards | 5 | Small rewards for specific behaviours |
| Other | 4 | Miscellaneous interventions |

### Intervention Duration

- **Active intervention period:** 4 weeks
- **Post-intervention monitoring:** 10 weeks
- **Total observation period:** 14 weeks

### Outcome Measures

**Primary outcome:** Average weekly gym visits during 4-week intervention period

**Secondary outcomes:**
- Gym visits during post-intervention period (weeks 5-14)
- Persistence of effects 10 weeks post-intervention

### Forecasting Study

Three groups of forecasters predicted intervention effectiveness:
1. **Lay people** (n = 495)
2. **Behavioural scientists** (n = 98)
3. **Practitioners** (n = 97)

Forecasters ranked interventions and predicted effect sizes.

## V. Theoretical Implications

### 1. The Recovery Hypothesis

The most effective intervention rewarded returning after a missed workout, not consistent attendance. This suggests:

- **Setbacks are critical moments** for behaviour change
- **Self-forgiveness and recovery** may be more important than prevention
- **Behavioral interventions should target vulnerable moments**

### 2. The Flexibility Principle

Rigid plans and commitments underperformed flexible approaches:

- **Adaptation > commitment** in long-term behaviour change
- **Life circumstances** interfere with best intentions
- **Rescheduling should be easy**, not penalized

### 3. The Forecasting Paradox

Experts and laypeople equally failed to predict what works:

- **Intuition is unreliable** for behavioural interventions
- **Theory does not reliably predict effect sizes**
- **Empirical testing is essential** before scaling

### 4. The Decay Problem

Only 8% of effective interventions persisted after ending:

- **Most behavioural interventions are temporary**
- **Habit formation** may require longer interventions
- **Maintenance interventions** may be needed indefinitely

## VII. Methodological Contributions

### The Megastudy Design

**Definition:** A megastudy is a massive field experiment that tests many interventions simultaneously against a common control condition.

**Key Features:**

1. **Scale**: Thousands to tens of thousands of participants
2. **Breadth**: 50+ interventions tested simultaneously
3. **Standardization**: Common outcome, setting, population
4. **Efficiency**: One study replaces dozens of separate experiments
5. **Comparison**: Direct head-to-head intervention ranking

### Advantages Over Traditional RCTs

| Dimension | Traditional RCT | Megastudy |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Interventions tested | 1-3 | 50+ |
| Sample size | 100s-1000s | 10,000s-100,000s |
| Time to results | Years | Months |
| Cross-intervention comparison | Indirect (meta-analysis) | Direct |
| Effect heterogeneity | Hard to detect | Easy to detect |

### Limitations

1. **Outcome homogeneity**: All interventions must target same outcome
2. **Setting specificity**: Results may not generalize to other contexts
3. **Intervention complexity**: Hard to test multi-component interventions
4. **Long-term effects**: Follow-up period limited by cost

## VIII. Policy Implications

### For Practitioners

1. **Test before scaling**: Intuition is unreliable; run pilots
2. **Focus on recovery**: Help people bounce back from lapses
3. **Build in flexibility**: Rigid commitments may backfire
4. **Expect decay**: Plan for maintenance after initial intervention

### For Researchers

1. **Adopt megastudy methods**: More efficient knowledge generation
2. **Report effect sizes**: Not just significance
3. **Track long-term outcomes**: Short-term effects may not persist
4. **Pre-register predictions**: Build calibration literature

### For Organizations

1. **Invest in testing infrastructure**: Enable rapid experimentation
2. **Partner with researchers**: Access to cutting-edge methods
3. **Accept uncertainty**: Even best interventions have variable effects

## IX. Study Details

### Randomization

Participants were stratified by:
- Historical gym visit frequency (low/medium/high)
- Gym location
- Membership tenure

### Intervention Delivery

- **Channel**: Text messages + app notifications
- **Frequency**: 3-5 contacts per week
- **Duration**: 4 weeks

### Outcome Measurement

- **Source**: 24 Hour Fitness gym check-in data
- **Measure**: Number of check-ins per week
- **Baseline period**: 4 weeks pre-randomization

### Statistical Analysis

- **Primary analysis**: Intent-to-treat
- **Model**: Linear regression with clustered standard errors
- **Multiple comparisons**: Benjamini-Hochberg correction
- **Pre-registration**: Yes (AsPredicted #14218)
