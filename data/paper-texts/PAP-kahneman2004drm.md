# A Survey Method for Characterizing Daily Life Experience: The Day Reconstruction Method

**Authors:** Daniel Kahneman, Alan B. Krueger, David A. Schkade, Norbert Schwarz, Arthur A. Stone
**Journal:** Science
**Volume:** 306(5702)
**Pages:** 1776-1780
**Year:** 2004
**DOI:** 10.1126/science.1103572
**Superkey:** PAP-kahneman2004drm

## Abstract

The Day Reconstruction Method (DRM) assesses how people spend their time and how they experience the various activities and settings of their lives, combining features of time-budget measurement and experience sampling. Participants systematically reconstruct their activities and experiences of the preceding day with procedures designed to reduce recall biases. The DRM's utility is shown by documenting close correspondences between the DRM reports of 909 employed women and established results from experience sampling. An analysis of the hedonic treadmill shows the DRM's potential for well-being research.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem

How do people experience the settings and activities of their lives? How do they use their time? These questions are usefully considered together, but there is no generally accepted method for collecting the relevant data.

**Applications:**
- Medical researchers: Assessing burden of illness
- Epidemiologists: Social and environmental stressors
- Economists: Evaluating policies and valuing nonmarket activities
- Well-being measurement: Experienced utility over time

### 1.2 Limitations of Existing Methods

**Time-Budget Studies:**
- Track time allocation
- Rarely include satisfaction measures
- Miss the experiential dimension

**Subjective Well-Being Surveys:**
- Global reports of happiness/satisfaction
- Subject to memory biases and focusing effects
- Don't capture moment-to-moment experience

**Experience Sampling Method (ESM):**
- Gold standard for real-time affect
- Expensive, high participant burden
- Provides limited coverage of rare events

### 1.3 The DRM Solution

The Day Reconstruction Method combines:
1. Time-use measurement
2. Affective experience recovery

**Key Innovation:** Participants reconstruct yesterday as a sequence of episodes, then rate affect for each episode—recovering specific recent memories to reduce recall biases.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 Sample

- **N = 1,018** employed women (convenience sample)
- **N = 909** who had worked on reference day (main analysis)
- Completion time: 45-75 minutes

**Demographics:**
- 49% White (non-Hispanic)
- 24% African American
- 22% Hispanic
- 5% Other
- Mean age: 38 years
- Mean household income: $54,700

### 2.2 DRM Protocol

**Step 1: Diary Construction**

Participants instructed:
> "Think of your day as a continuous series of scenes or episodes in a film. Give each episode a brief name that will help you remember it (for example, 'commuting to work', or 'at lunch with B_'). Write down the approximate times at which each episode began and ended."

**Episode Statistics:**
- Mean number of episodes: 14.1 (SD = 4.8)
- Mean episode duration: 61 minutes

**Step 2: Episode Description**

For each episode, participants reported:
- Start and end times
- What they were doing (16 activity categories)
- Where they were
- Whom they were interacting with
- How they felt (12 affect descriptors)

### 2.3 Affect Measures

**Scale:** 0 (not at all) to 6 (very much)

**Positive Affect:**
- Happy
- Warm/friendly
- Enjoying myself

**Negative Affect:**
- Frustrated/annoyed
- Depressed/blue
- Hassled/pushed around
- Angry/hostile
- Worried/anxious
- Criticized/put down

**Additional Items:**
- Competent
- Impatient
- Tired

### 3.3 DRM vs. Generic Judgments

**Key Finding:** DRM results differ from generic enjoyment ratings:

| Activity | Generic Judgment Rank | DRM Rank |
|----------|----------------------|----------|
| Taking care of children | 1 (highest) | 12 |
| Going on trips | 2 | Not measured |
| Being with friends | 3 | 2 |
| Working at job | 4 | 15 |

**Explanation:** Generic judgments reflect prototypical instances and social desirability ("I enjoy my kids"), whereas DRM captures actual experience ("but they were a pain last night").

## 4. Validation: DRM vs. ESM

### 4.1 Diurnal Patterns

**Finding:** DRM and ESM produce remarkably similar diurnal patterns for:
- Tiredness (V-shaped pattern throughout day)
- Negative affect (highest in morning, declining)

**Significance:** The V-shape of tiredness differs from naive expectation that tiredness increases steadily. DRM replicates this non-obvious pattern from ESM.

### 4.2 Sleep and Age Effects

**Sleep Duration:**
- Those sleeping ≤6 hours: More tired at each hour
- Both groups exhibit V-shape pattern

**Age Effects:**
- Young (<30): Start day much more tired
- Converge to same tiredness by midday
- V-shape most pronounced for young

**Implication:** Respondents are recovering actual experience, not consulting general beliefs.

## 5. Effect Sizes: The Enjoyment Scale

### 5.1 Scale Construction

To facilitate interpretation, an enjoyment scale was constructed using activities as anchors:

**Scale Anchors (0-6 enjoyment scale):**
- "Commuting to work": 2.86 (low anchor)
- "Relaxing with friends": 4.92 (high anchor)

**Intermediate Points:**
- "Cooking alone"
- "Shopping w/ spouse/SO"
- "Watching TV alone"
- "Lunch w/ co-workers"
- "With boss at work"

### 5.2 Job Features Effects

**Time Pressure at Work:**
- With pressure: Mean enjoyment = 2.88
- Without pressure: Mean enjoyment = 3.96
- Effect size: 52% of scale range (commuting → relaxing with friends)

**Job Security (at risk of layoff):**
- Much smaller effect than time pressure

**Specialized Training Required:**
- Much smaller effect than time pressure

**Key Finding:** Time pressure is particularly important determinant of enjoyment at work.

### 5.3 Personal Characteristics Effects

**Sleep Quality:**
- Very bad vs. Very good
- Effect on home enjoyment: 56% of scale range

**Depression Indicator ("trouble maintaining enthusiasm"):**
- Effect: 46% of scale range

**Household Income (<$30K vs. >$90K):**
- Effect: 19% of scale range
- Shrinks under controls

**Religion Importance:**
- Effect: 19% of scale range

**Marital Status (divorced vs. married):**
- Smaller effect, slightly favors divorced

## 6. The Hedonic Treadmill Analysis

### 6.1 Three Hypotheses

**Hedonic Treadmill Hypothesis:**
Life satisfaction and affective experience are equally susceptible to adaptation.

**Aspiration Treadmill Hypothesis:**
Treadmill effects in satisfaction are due to changing standards. Rich experience more pleasure but require more to be equally satisfied.

**Focusing Hypothesis:**
Effects of life circumstances on satisfaction overstate effects on experience. Circumstances become salient when answering survey questions, not in daily life.

### 6.2 Empirical Test

**Life Satisfaction - Net Affect Correlation:** r = 0.38

**Income Effects:**
| Measure | Correlation with log(income) |
|---------|------------------------------|
| Life satisfaction | r = 0.20 |
| Net affect | r = 0.05 |
| Difference | P < 0.001 |

**Marital Status (married vs. divorced):**
| Measure | Correlation |
|---------|-------------|
| Life satisfaction | r = 0.10 (favors married) |
| Net affect | r = -0.08 (favors divorced) |
| Difference | P < 0.001 |

### 6.3 Conclusion: Focusing Hypothesis Supported

**Three Patterns Identified:**

1. **Personal characteristics** (depression, sleep): Pervasive influence on affect. Correlate at least as highly with affect as with satisfaction.

2. **Local features** (interaction partners, time pressure): Powerful influence on affect. Always relevant when present.

3. **Life circumstances** (income, marital status): Small effects on affect unless locally salient. More impact on satisfaction judgments than on actual experience.

**Mechanism:** Life circumstances become salient when answering well-being questions, causing people to evaluate how (un)fortunate they are. This produces larger effects on satisfaction than on actual momentary experience.

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **DRM as Validated Method:**
   - Replicates ESM patterns (gold standard)
   - More practical for population surveys
   - Reduces recall biases through episode reconstruction

2. **Affect vs. Satisfaction Distinction:**
   - Life circumstances affect satisfaction more than actual experience
   - Supporting the focusing hypothesis
   - Implications for welfare measurement

3. **Three Influence Patterns:**
   - Personal characteristics (pervasive effect on affect)
   - Local features (powerful, situation-specific)
   - Life circumstances (small effect unless salient)

4. **Time Allocation Matters:**
   - Large variations in affect across activities
   - Optimizing time allocation can improve well-being
   - Subject to time and income constraints

5. **Activity Rankings:**
   - Intimate relations and socializing: Most positive
   - Work and commuting: Most negative
   - DRM rankings differ from generic beliefs
