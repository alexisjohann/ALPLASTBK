A B S T R A C T
What makes leadership and management theories practical? Theories are practical to the extent that practitioners
can enact proposed cause (X)− effect (Y) relationships. Accordingly, I distinguish three types of practical the-
ories. Manipulate(X) theories give practitioners levers for action. These theories have causal constructs, whose
operationalizations’ levels practitioners can set by themselves. For example, in a theory on charismatic leader
signals, practitioners can use fewer or more signals. In select(X) theories, practitioners cannot themselves vary a
construct’s levels but select the desired level. An example are trait theories of job performance. They inform
practitioners at what trait level to select employees. Lastly, in observe(X) theories, practitioners can only measure
levels of a causal construct. For example, managers can measure employee trust, but they cannot fix this trust at a
certain level. I focus on manipulate(X) theories because they are actionable and rigorous. I discuss criteria for
constructs in such theories (e.g., construct unity) and three flaws undermining the development of manipulate(X)
theories: (1) the simplification fallacy involves the abstraction of complex phenomena like culture into single
constructs, (2) the endogenous-cause problem, when endogenous constructs are treated as exogenous, and (3)
construct conflation, the lumping of several constructs under one label.
“A businessman once stated that ‘there is nothing as practical as a
good theory’” (Lewin, 1943, p. 118). This well-known aphorism ought to
be particularly prominent in applied domains like leadership and man-
agement. The word practical comes from the Greek praktikos, which
means “concerned with action,” derived from prattein,
“to do, to act”
(Oxford Learner’s Dictionary, 2025). Thus, beyond merely explaining, a
practical theory provides a framework for guiding managerial action
towards desired outcomes (Argyris, 2005; Rousseau, 2024).
Yet, most leadership and management theories fall short on guiding
practice (e.g., Antonakis et al., 2024; Argyris, 2005; Pfeffer & Sutton,
2006; Ployhart & Bartunek, 2019). Consider the example of research on
empowering leadership. Ahearne, Mathieu, and Rapp (2005) explicitly
referred to empowering leader behaviors as those that enhance
employee autonomy. Yet, this research offers little guidance for practice
because these behaviors are at least partially defined by their outcomes
(Fischer & Sitkin, 2023). If employees feel autonomous and empowered,
their managers have used empowering behaviors. Which concrete ac-
tions managers would have to undertake remains unclear. In fact, the
same concrete leader behavior, such as delegating a task, could make
some employees feel empowered and others feel helpless. Thus, theo-
retical models of empowering leadership may have a practical aura but
are not actionable.
The example of empowering leadership is emblematic of a broader
challenge in leadership and management theories. Poor conceptualiza-
tion does not only undermine the quality of theories, but also renders
them unactionable (e.g., Mitchell, 1985; Antonakis, 2017). Yet, there
have been repeated calls for actionable theories (e.g., Berg, 2014, Col-
quitt & George, 2011; Nkomo, Bell, Roberts, Joshi, & Thatcher, 2019;
Thomas & Tymon, 1982), which can aid in solving real-world mana-
gerial problems. This manuscript heeds these calls addressing what it
takes to develop actionable theories. In particular, it zooms in on con-
structs as building blocks of theories.
The manuscript unfolds as follows: The first part delineates the
intended contribution and introduces key terms. The second part dis-
cusses the notion of causal constructs. The third part, building on earlier
work (e.g., Podsakoff, MacKenzie, & Podsakoff, 2016; Suddaby, 2010)
elaborates on criteria for constructs. The fourth part then identifies three
flaws in developing constructs, using examples from leadership and
management research. The manuscript concludes with recommenda-
tions for developing actionable theories.

Yet theories cannot optimize on all strengths. For example, actionable
theories are rarely general theories that seek to cover a broad range of
phenomena. If theories can optimize on only selected strengths, then
optimizing on different strengths requires building theories in different
ways. Thus, unlike past work on theories (e.g., Bacharach, 1989) and

theories are typically theories that explain phenomena through cause-

Second, rather than just presenting theory-level arguments, the
manuscript emphasizes the interplay of theory and empirics in concep-
tualization. In addition to defining constructs and proposing linkages
among them, actionable theories must address the operationalization of
constructs, specifically how managers and other actors can determine
and set their levels, for example, to what extent to engage in ethical
leader signaling (Banks et al., 2023). Attention to operationalization, in
turn, improves conceptualization because operationalization is a check
for conceptual clarity.
Third, the manuscript shows that actionability and scientific rigor of
a theory go hand in hand. Theorizing with manipulable causal con-
structs does not only make a theory actionable. It is also an antidote to
“endogenous theorizing” (Antonakis, 2017; Lonati & Antonakis, 2023),
the mixing up of causes and outcomes. For example, managers cannot
directly fix levels of employees’ fairness perceptions. Fairness percep-
tions result from many sources. Thus, a theory that proposes fairness
perceptions as a cause of job performance overlooks that these fairness
perceptions are themselves outcomes. Such a theory is not a cause-
outcome theory, but an outcome-outcome one that might merely cap-
ture a spurious relationship.
In addition to avoiding endogenous theorizing, actionable theories
allow for stronger tests of causal mechanisms. A test of a theory about
effects of fairness perceptions on job performance, which ignores causes
of these perceptions, is limited in ruling out alternative mechanisms
such as demand and halo effects. When, however, a manipulable causal
construct (e.g., a compensation scheme) is included in the theory and its
test, it is more plausible to infer a fairness-related mechanism (Sajons,
2020). Whereas others have addressed endogenous theorizing through a
more methodological lens (Antonakis, 2017; Lonati & Antonakis, 2023),
the manuscript tackles the conceptual roots of this issue, such as over-
simplified, inherently endogenous, or conflated constructs.
It is important to clarify that this manuscript is not a debate of the
practical usefulness of philosophies of science, such as positivism or
constructivism, and theory types, such as cause-effect theories or the-
ories, which seek to uncover the meaning of phenomena. The manu-
script also does not question the value of studying phenomena when
actionable causes are not specified. However, the manuscript’s focus is
on conceptualization and theorizing that can guide practice by allowing
the enactment of cause-effect relationships. Examples include goal-
setting theory (e.g., Locke & Latham, 1990), theory on defaults and
nudging (e.g., Thaler & Sunstein, 2008), and charismatic signaling
theory (Antonakis, Bastardoz, Jacquart, & Shamir, 2016). Having
delineated the contribution, I now briefly review the notion of
explaining theories.
Explaining theories
A theory is“a statement of relationships among concepts within a
boundary set of assumptions and constraints” (Bacharach (1989, p.

416). There are different types of theories, but in leadership and man-
agement research, most theories fall into the category of explaining
theories. These theories clarify how and why phenomena occur by
specifying causal relationships (Sandberg & Alvesson, 2021). In doing
so, explaining theories often include mediating constructs, which point
to the mechanisms by which a cause affects an outcome (Cornelissen &
Werner, in press). Causality in such theories means that changes in the
levels of one construct lead, under specific conditions, to changes in
levels of another construct. These conditions are: (1) the cause has to
precede the outcome, (2) the cause and the outcome must be reliably
associated, and (3) there are no other variables that cause both the cause
and the outcome (e.g., Shadish, Cook, & Campbell, 2002). Given the
emphasis on causality in theorizing (Cornelissen, H¨ ollerer, & Seidl,
2021), Cornelissen and Durand (2014, p. 997) refined Bacharach’s
above definition of theory, stating that theory is“an analytic structure or
system of causal associations that attempts to account for, explain, and
predict empirical phenomena.” In studying phenomena and underlying
causal relations, explaining theories assume a connection to reality,
even if phenomena involve human perception (Sandberg & Alvesson,
2021). Researchers can observe these phenomena, and they treat causal
relationships as consistent, stable, and inferable from observable data.
In terms of content, an explaining theory addresses the questions of
what, how, why, who, where, and when (Whetten, 1989). A theory
defines the constructs that capture the phenomenon (what) and de-
scribes their relationships (how). Furthermore, a theory explains the
causal mechanisms or dynamics (e.g., psychological, economic, or so-
cial) behind these relationships (why). In addition, a theory specifies
contextual boundary conditions, that is, to whom, where, and when the
proposed relationships apply. Explaining theories that address these
questions can guide practice, when they provide levers for action in
addition to specifying cause-effect relationships.
An example of a theoretically rich and actionable theory is cognitive
evaluation theory (e.g., Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999). This theory has
been instrumental in explaining motivation, particularly the crowding-
out effect, where external rewards (e.g., bonuses, incentives) can un-
dermine intrinsic motivation. The theory helps practitioners determine
when to use extrinsic motivators. This example illustrates how
explaining theories can bridge scientific insight with real-world
actionability.
Constructs
Constructs are the building blocks of theories. A construct is an
abstraction that researchers develop to represent a phenomenon based
on a set of observations (e.g., Kerlinger, 1986). Schwab (1999)
emphasized that constructs are abstractions of variable characteristics of
objects or events. Consider the example of the construct of commitment,
which Klein, Molloy, and Brinsfield (2012) defined as a perceived
“volitional psychological bond reflecting dedication to and re-
sponsibility for a particular target” (p. 137). Klein et al. viewed
commitment as a distinguishable type of psychological bond, which can
vary in its strength, and embedded it in a process model. As a second
example, for their model of trust, Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman (1995)
developed the constructs of perceived trustworthiness as a cause of trust,
trust itself, and risk-taking in relationships. The definition of trust is“the
willingness of a party to be vulnerable to the actions of another party
based on the expectation that the other will perform a particular action
important to the trustor, irrespective of the ability to monitor or control
that other party” (1995, p. 712).
The terms constructs and concepts are often used interchangeably,
which is justified if both are considered“theoretical entities” (Podsakoff
et al., 2016). Kerlinger (1986), for instance, viewed a scientific construct
as a type of concept. Podsakoff et al. (2016, p. 161) defined concepts as

Types of practical theories
The practicality of a theory hinges on two key criteria: actionability
and relevance. First, actionability is a function of the extent to which
practitioners can vary the levels of a causal construct’s operationaliza-
tion. Referring to the causal construct as X, I distinguish between
manipulate(X) theories, select(X) theories, and observe(X) theories (see
Table 1).
Manipulate(X) Theories. Manipulate(X) theories are actionable
theories where practitioners can independently determine the levels of
the operationalization of the causal construct in order to obtain the
theorized effect. For example, an actor could set these levels to“low” or
“high”
. Although only the operationalization not the construct itself is
manipulable, henceforth I use the terms manipulable construct or
actionable construct instead of actionably operationalizable construct
for ease of language. In short, a manipulate(X) theory gives practitioners
levers for action (e.g., Berg, 2014, Schauerte, Becker, Imschloss, Wich-
mann, & Reinartz, 2023, Carton, 2025).
Manipulability implies discretionary, observable action. This view of
actionable theories as having manipulable causal constructs does not
mean that constructs must themselves be actions or behaviors. For
example, goal difficulty or an attendance policy are not per se behav-
ioral constructs, but actors can set levels of these constructs in a quasi-
deterministic fashion. For instance, managers may prescribe that em-
ployees can work two or three days per week from home.
Select(X) Theories. Theories with non-manipulable causal con-
structs can also be actionable, when actors can at least select levels of
these constructs. For example, in personality-based theories of job per-
formance (e.g., Tett, Toich, & Ozkum, 2021) traits are non-manipulable
causal constructs. Managers cannot make employees have desired levels
of personality traits, but managers may be able to select employees on
these traits. However, when actors can only select levels of the causal
construct, the risk exists that this construct co-varies with other con-
structs, which may also affect the theorized outcome. For example, when
selecting employees on conscientiousness, its correlation with agree-
ableness (Van der Linden, te Nijenhuis, & Bakker, 2010) has to be taken
into account.
Both manipulate(X) and select(X) theories address the cause-effect
relationships into which actors can intervene. These theories allow
practitioners to change base rates of stimuli (to change the world; cf.
Dhami, Hertwig, & Hoffrage, 2004). In addition, there are theories
where practitioners can neither directly set nor select the levels of the
causal construct. They can, however, measure the causal construct and,
thus, observe existing base rates. I refer to these theories as observe(X)
theories.
Observe(X) Theories. This type of theory comes in at least two
variations. One variation refers to cause-effect theories with exogenous
causal constructs, whose operationalizations actors can neither vary nor
select. For example, inflation can be seen as an exogenous state variable
for marketing and sales managers, when these managers seek to model
individual households’ demands, purchase timing, or saving decisions.
These managers cannot determine macroeconomic conditions, but the-
ory on how inflation affects buyer behavior can nonetheless help man-
agers to predict sales and to adjust production and pricing accordingly.

A second variation of observe(X) theories contains endogenous
causal constructs, that is, constructs that themselves have causes, but the
theory does not specify these causes. Such theories do not identify
constructs that practitioners can directly manipulate. The practical
value of such theories may rather lie in pointing to mechanisms that
underlie causal effects. For example, according to the happy worker–
productive worker hypothesis (Cropanzano & Wright, 2001; Bellet, De
Neve, & Ward, 2024), employee affect is causally linked to job perfor-
mance. Bellet et al. (2024), using an instrumental variable approach,
provided empirical support for this hypothesis. However, employee
affect is an endogenous construct. Managers cannot manipulate levels of
employee affect by setting them to“positive” or “negative.” To say it
drastically, if one wanted to change another person’s psychological state
such as their affect, doing so would require intervening on brains with
implants or so.
Thus, knowing about the affect-performance relationship becomes
actionable only if managers know manipulable causes of employee
affect, that is, levers or practices that lead to better moods. An actionable
theory about the happy worker– productive worker relationship would
include such practices. Then, one can speak of a do(Z)-with-the-intent-
to-change(X)-then-observe(X) theory. These practices, such as rede-
signing the work environment, might increase employee affect and ul-
timately job performance. Note, however, that without such an
actionable theory of employee affect, assumptions about the effective-
ness and mechanisms of mood-enhancing interventions remain specu-
lative. For instance, as Bellet et al. (2024) noted, the mechanisms behind
redesigning the work environment might not only be mood-based, but
could also be reciprocity-based, such that employees react favorably to
favorable treatment.
Relevance. Lastly, it is important to distinguish actionability from
relevance, which Schauerte et al. (2023) referred to as appeal due to
interestingness or importance. Relevance mostly results from practi-
tioners’ interest in the outcome variables. For example, for team leaders,
theories that explain team cohesion can be relevant, even if they do not
contain actionable levers. Actionability is an inherent attribute of a
theory, which results from including manipulable causal constructs. In
contrast, relevance pertains to the subject matter and varies depending
on the stakeholder. An actionable theory can hypothetically be irrele-
vant to managers, if it addresses a phenomenon that does not matter to
them. The opposite is also possible: a theory may address an important
topic while not being actionable. In sum, theory and constructs can be
practical in different ways, but I zoom in on manipulate(X) theories, in
particular their causal constructs.
Causal constructs
Actionability and causality are linked. A managerial lever is only
useful if it causes the intended effect. Thus, actionable theories are a
subset of causal theories. Although causal theorizing is complex, its
specificities are rarely discussed (for exceptions, see Cornelissen,
Mitchell & James, 2001, Cornelissen & Werner, in press; Durand &
Vaara, 2009). In terms of Whetten’s (1989) earlier mentioned questions,
causal theories require at least a causal“what” (a causal construct), an
outcome“what” (an outcome construct), a causal“how” that describes
the relationship between the causal and outcome constructs, and a
causal“why”
, which explains the mechanism (see Fig. 1).

is their positioning in a theory, that is, when they function as anteced-
ents to outcome constructs. The same construct that serves as a cause in
one theory may function as an outcome construct in another theory. For

Causal agents, producing causes, and explanatory causes
“A causal agent [italics added] is a person or phenomenon which can
be considered to have caused something….
” (Nuopponen, 2008, p. 2).
In actionable leadership and management theories, the causal agent is a
human actor, such as a policy maker, manager, or employee, who
purposefully manipulates a cause to bring about a change in an outcome.
Causal agents have a “causator relation” with effects.
A producing cause refers to actions or events that make an outcome
happen.2 Producing causes have a “causation relation” (Nuopponen,
2008, p. 2) with effects and point to the causal“what” in theories. If a
human actor can manipulate the levels of producing causes, the theory is
actionable. For example, when a manager as a causal agent switches
behavior from only discussing work with employees to also engaging in
private conversations, this switch can become a producing cause of
positive manager-employee relationships. In this example, the causal
construct may be labeled“managerial communication” or “private-life
content in managerial communication” depending on the theory’s pur-
pose and level of abstraction.

the causal“what”
, explanatory causes typically refer to attributes (“state,
condition, or property”) and point to the causal“why”

2008, p. 2). Returning to the example of the manager who starts to talk

Importance of distinguishing producing and explanatory causes
Distinguishing producing and explanatory causes in theorizing is
important to ensure that a theory speaks to both the“what” and the
“why” questions. It is evident that an actionable theory must inform
about the causal“what” or producing causes so as to point practitioners
to the levers for action. Theoretical propositions, where the“why” is also
the“what”
, such as the affect-performance hypothesis (e.g., Bellet et al.,
2024) or a model linking trustworthiness, trust, and relationship be-
haviors (Mayer et al., 1995), point to causal mechanisms. However,
theories with such propositions can only be observe(X) theories. In
contrast, theories with constructs that capture producing causes and
whose levels causal agents can vary are manipulate(X) theories.
Distinguishing between producing and explanatory causes also
sharpens causal theorizing by revealing potential circular reasoning.
Consider a theory about the relationship between trust and risk taking.
The answers to the questions of“what causes risk taking in relation-
ships?” and“why is there risk taking in relationships?” are identical.
That is, trust is both the cause and the mechanism. Circular reasoning is
normally seen as indicating absence of an explanation (the“why”).
However, it can also indicate absence of a “what”
, that is, of a producing
cause. Thus, from the point of view of a practitioner who seeks to enact a
theory, cause-effect theories that contain only causal constructs repre-
senting explanatory causes but not constructs representing producing
causes are incomplete mediation models. Such theories propose medi-
ating constructs and outcome constructs while lacking actionable causal
constructs. Stated differently, such theories address the mechanism of a
cause-effect relationship without specifying to which causal construct
the mechanism applies.
Producing versus explanatory causes: leader-member-exchange as an
example
The theory of leader-member exchange (LMX theory, e.g., Dulebohn,
Bommer, Liden, Brouer, & Ferris, 2012; for a critique, see Gottfredson,
Wright, & Heaphy, 2020) proposes a chain from antecedents via LMX to
leadership outcomes. LMX can be defined as followers’ perceptions of
the quality of the relationship between a leader and a follower (e.g.,
Martin, Guillaume, Thomas, Lee, & Epitropaki, 2016, see Table 3 for
critiques of LMX definitions). LMX as an attribute is an explanatory
cause. Producing causes of LMX include leaders’ contingent reward
behaviors and transformational leadership, if conceptualized as behav-
iors (Stock, Banks, Voss, Tonidandel, & Woznyj, 2023). Lastly, LMX is
proposed to cause a range of attitudinal, behavioral, and perceptual
outcomes.
Hypothetically, LMX theory can guide practice. Managers can adjust
contingent reward behaviors like praising employee performance (an

actionable producing cause). However, the theory overlooks that man-
agers’ behaviors may also affect leadership outcomes directly, making
the effects of LMX at least partially spurious. In addition, the theory
ignores reverse causal effects by work outcomes on LMX as well as
threats of tautologies due to conceptual overlaps of LMX and leadership
outcomes (House & Aditya, 1997). Even if managers only wanted to
bring about change in their relationships to followers (and not also in
other outcomes), LMX theory and the LMX construct are of limited
usefulness.
What is worse is that research frequently uses a curtailed version of
LMX theory that positions LMX as a producing cause and exogenous
construct as if it did not have antecedents (Fischer, Dietz, & Antonakis,
2017; Gottfredson et al., 2020). This theory-in-use is neither actionable
nor causally interpretable. Managers could not directly set their fol-
lowers’ LMX at higher or lower levels. It is not plausible to assume that
leaders can change levels of LMX by, for example, telling their em-
ployees that they have excellent leader–follower relationships. Manip-
ulate(X) is not possible with LMX because it is an endogenous
explanatory cause. Conversely, observe(X) (measuring LMX) is possible
but it does not allow causal inference and is not actionable.
Summary on causal constructs
Two criteria are key for assessing the actionability of causal con-
structs so that practitioners can enact cause-effect theories: (1) whether
constructs represent producing or explanatory causes, and (2) whether
constructs are manipulable or not. Regarding the former criterion, the-
ories with only explanatory causal constructs can inform practitioners
about why an effect occurs and can be helpful for understanding and
predicting a phenomenon. Such theories are observe(X) theories, but
they are not actionable.
Unlike theories with only explanatory causal constructs, theories
with constructs capturing producing causes can guide action, when
these causes point to levers whose levels practitioners can set. This latter
clause points to the second criterion of manipulable versus non-
manipulable constructs. Manipulable constructs allow practitioners to
vary levels of the construct in order to produce an outcome. Theories
with such constructs are manipulate(X) theories.
Distinguishing producing and explanatory causes as well as manip-
ulable and non-manipulable constructs does not only help in evaluating
the actionability of a theory but also improves causal theorizing. If the
initial link in a causal chain is an explanatory cause, theorists should ask
whether that cause itself has producing causes. Questions such as “which
action can I undertake to affect this explanatory cause (e.g., a psycho-
logical state)?” are in line with another quote attributed to Kurt Lewin:
“If you want truly to understand something, try to change it” (Tolman,
1996). Adding a producing cause results in more fully specified models
and helps identify potentially spurious relationships. For example, in
studying the link between trust and risk taking in relationships, one
might propose frequency of interactions as a producing cause of trust.
Yet, frequency of interactions, in addition through a mediated effect via
trust, might also affect risk taking in relationships directly. In short,
producing causes are a cure against endogenous theorizing and a recipe
for scientific rigor because omitted causal constructs are identified,
which makes the theory a more accurate representation of the phe-
nomenon. Having introduced causal constructs, I now turn to the third
part on properties of such constructs.
Properties of constructs in explaining theories
Numerous authors have elaborated on properties of constructs (e.g.,
Bacharach, 1989; Kerlinger, 1986; Goertz & Mahoney, 2012; Suddaby,
2010; Whetten, 1989) and their definitions (e.g., Podsakoff et al., 2016;
Lambert & Newman, 2023). Four previously established properties are:
(1) distinctiveness, (2) nomological embeddedness, (3) operationaliz-
ability, and (4) definitional clarity. To this set of properties, I add three

recognizing their interplay with theories is critical. Kaplan (1964, p.

“proper concepts are needed to formulate good theory, but we need a
good theory to arrive at proper concepts.” The implication is that
properties of constructs have to be evaluated in light of the purpose of
the theory, in which they are embedded.
Distinctiveness
As Suddaby (2010, p. 346) noted,“clear constructs are simply robust
categories that distill phenomena into sharp distinctions that are
comprehensible to a community of researchers—that is, animal, min-
eral, or vegetable; gas, liquid, or solid.” These categories are not the
same as the corresponding observations but rather abstractions, which
are embedded in a theory. For example, in personality psychology the
categories“trait” as a stable property and“state” as a situationally
shaped property can be elements of a theory that explains the impact of
traits and states on behaviors. Yet, for a theory of trait effects on job
performance, the distinction between traits and states is taken for
granted, and scientists would distinguish categories within traits, such as
the traits of extraversion and conscientiousness. Distinctiveness is, thus,
also a function of theoretical purpose.
Conceptual and empirical distinctiveness do not necessarily go hand
in hand. For instance, organizational commitment and job satisfaction
are conceptually distinguishable, but their measures are highly corre-
lated (Le, Schmidt, Harter, & Lauver, 2010). In addition, measures of the
two constructs have similar relationships with measures of other con-
structs, such as positive and negative affectivity. These correlations may
stem from either the imprecise measurement of distinct constructs or a
misalignment between researchers’ conceptual distinctions and those of
research participants. Importantly, constructs in explaining theories must
be both conceptually and empirically distinct. Otherwise, empirically
isolating which construct drives an outcome becomes impossible, as it
would be unclear which construct a given measure represents. For
example, does a measure indicate job satisfaction or organizational
commitment? As a result, identifying producing causes as antecedents
becomes very difficult. For example, should these antecedent constructs
be job facets for raising satisfaction or socialization events for building
loyalty to the organization?

Operationalizability
Whereas distinctiveness and nomological embeddedness are at the
conceptual level, operationalizability is about the interplay of theory
and empirics (Kerlinger, 1986). Operationalizability refers to whether a
construct can be linked to observations. It involves specifying observ-
able proxies that indicate the construct. Only once a construct is oper-
ationalizable, it can be measured. Measurability means that variation in
the levels of constructs can be assessed, be it, for example, low or high
for continuous constructs, such as job satisfaction, or women or men for
the categorical construct sex. In short, constructs in explaining theories
must be operationalizable, that is, must be linked to observations.
Definitional clarity
Definitions of constructs are important for conceptual clarity (e.g.,
Podsakoff et al., 2016). By defining, researchers prescribe (sometimes
describe) what meaning (i.e., the definiens) they attach to a term (i.e.,
the definiendum), which they use to label a construct. As obvious as the
importance of construct definitions is, MacKenzie (2003, see also Pod-
sakoff et al., 2016, and Antonakis, 2017) lamented that they are often
missing. Moreover, if definitions are provided, their weaknesses often
undermine the purpose of clarifying constructs (cf. Suddaby, 2010). Yet
others pointed to cases where a construct was defined but used differ-
ently in different research (e.g., Gottfredson et al., 2020; Hulleman,
Schrager, Bodmann, & Harackiewicz, 2010; Klein, Molloy, & Cooper,
2009).
Definitions of constructs succinctly outline the nature or essence of
the construct, using comprehensible language (Podsakoff et al., 2016). A
starting point is specifying the construct type (e.g., attitude or behavior),
its attributes, and the level of abstraction (e.g., a theory about traits and
states versus a theory about different traits). In doing so, definitions
address the aforementioned properties of distinctiveness, nomological
embeddedness, and operationalizability. Moreover, for causal con-
structs, definitions speak to the additional criteria of unity, manipula-
bility, and having an explicit mechanism, which are presented below.
Furthermore, the definition mentions the dimensionality (e.g., unidi-
mensional versus multidimensional), scaling (e.g., ordinal or nominal),
level (e.g., individual versus group), stability (e.g., stable versus spo-
radic), breadth (e.g., broad versus narrow) and, if needed, actors (e.g.,
leader versus follower) and targets (e.g., climate for service versus
climate for safety). In summary, definitions of causal constructs in
explaining theories capture the essence of the construct and detail the prop-
erties of distinctiveness, nomological embeddedness, operationalizability,
construct unity, manipulability, and having an explicit mechanism while
using comprehensible language.

Manipulability
Manipulability is a form of operationalizability that specifically re-
fers to causal constructs. It means that human actors must be able to
deliberately set the levels of a construct’s operationalization. For
actionable theories in management, for example, Hrebiniak and Joyce
(2001) and Toffel (2016) advocated that research ought to include
constructs whose levels managers could manipulate. Manipulability is
not only a prerequisite for making theories actionable but also helps
ensure causality by enabling the establishment of factual and counter-
factual conditions for causal testing. In summary, causal constructs in
actionable explaining theories are manipulable; that is, researchers and
practitioners can set the levels of a construct’s operationalization.
Explicit causal mechanism
Whereas manipulability of causal constructs makes theories action-
able, understanding the mechanism, which explains why a construct has
an effect, enables fine-tuning manipulations. For example, knowing that
the performance-enhancing effects of difficult and specific goals in part
operate through a motivational mechanism of increased employee effort
aids in avoiding setting over-taxing goals. In addition, mechanisms of
causal effects can point to those contexts in which a causal construct has
an effect (Cornelissen & Werner, in press). Hence, although practitioners
would not necessarily need to know the mechanisms behind a causal
effect (Antonakis, 2017), having explanations for why causal effects
occur is useful. In short, causal constructs in explaining theories have
specified mechanisms for their effects (or specify that mechanisms are not yet
known). Having elaborated on criteria for constructs, I now turn to the
fourth part on conceptual flaws.
Conceptual flaws
These flaws are (1) the simplification fallacy, (2) the endogenous-
cause problem, and (3) construct conflation (see Table 3). Whereas
previous research has highlighted other conceptual flaws, such as
concept proliferation (e.g., Banks, Gooty, Ross, Williams, & Harrington,
2018) and concept overlap (Le et al., 2010), these three flaws are
particularly harmful to developing actionable theories. These flaws can
co-occur, and they fail to meet one or more of the afore-discussed
criteria for constructs, undermining a theory’s ability to guide policy
making and managerial action.
The simplification fallacy
The Flaw. The simplification fallacy occurs when a single construct
represents a complex phenomenon and is treated as a causal factor. The
construct hides numerous constructs under one umbrella such that it is
not evident which constructs are concerned. It does not satisfy the
criteria of construct unity and of manipulability. If this construct serves
as a cause, one could not know which part of the construct had an effect.
Moreover, because the construct is not manipulable, theories using it in
a causal position are not actionable.
Representative constructs are organizational culture, organizational
climate, and LMX. Management research treats culture and climate
predominantly as organizational properties (see Chatman & O’Reilly,
2016; Denison, Nieminen, & Kotrba, 2014; Schneider, Ehrhart, &
Macey, 2013), while LMX is a construct that describes one-to-one re-
lationships between leaders and followers (e.g., Scandura & Meuser,
2022). Both organizations and relationships are complex phenomena.
Consequently, definitions of the constructs vary (see Table 4, for com-
ments about definitions in review articles). For organizational culture,
Denison et al. (2014, p. 146) noted“theoretical diversity” in the content
of culture, whereas Chatman and O’Reilly (2016, p. 200) lamented that
“debates about how to define and study culture have ceased to be
generative and instead, are constraining our ability to accumulate and
advance an integrative and comprehensive theory of culture.”
If definitions of a construct vary greatly even after decades of
research, this may indicate that the phenomenon, of which the construct
is an abstraction, is too complex to be represented by a single scientific
construct. Such a construct limits uncovering causal relationships in
three ways. First, relationships of constituent constructs are untestable
because these relationships are defined away. Second, supposed effects
on outcome constructs are causally indeterminate because these effects
might stem from any of the constituent constructs. Third, unless ante-
cedents to broad constructs like culture are modeled, in addition to
causal indeterminacy, spuriousness can also occur.

The endogenous-cause problem
The Flaw. The problem arises when causal constructs are inherently
endogenous, that is by definition influenced by another construct. Ex-
amples are attitudinal constructs like job satisfaction which presume an
attitude object like a job as another construct. This latter third construct
(or constructs) can affect both the endogenous causal construct and any
outcome construct. Although an endogenous construct could have an
independent causal effect, a theory that does not include this third
construct is underspecified, thus not meeting the criterion of nomolog-
ical embeddedness.
As Table 5 shows, endogenous causal constructs include evaluations
of behavior (e.g., abusive supervision), perceptions of the work envi-
ronment (e.g., perceptions of participation), moral or ethical percep-
tions or evaluations (e.g., justice perceptions), and job attitudes and
beliefs (e.g., trust). These constructs appear in conceptual and theory-
testing research, where the tested theory is at times a curtailed version
of the original theory. For example, whereas the original theory of
perceived organizational support (POS; Eisenberger, Huntington,
Hutchison, & Sowa, 1986) proposes antecedents, Riggle, Edmondson,
and Hansen (2009) theorized POS as an exogenous construct and
ignored its antecedents, thus raising severe doubts about any causal
inferences.
Importantly, an endogenous causal construct is not in itself a flawed
construct unlike over-simplified or conflated constructs. In leadership
and management research, endogenous constructs are typically psy-
chological reactions to stimuli, and these reactions can be properly
defined. Thus, using such constructs as outcomes is possible. So is use as
a causal construct, assuming that the theory addresses the independent
causal effect beyond that of antecedents to the causal construct (see, for
example, the earlier mentioned research on the affect-performance
relationship by Bellet et al, 2024).
Otherwise, a theory that does not model the antecedents of endog-
enous causal constructs makes three often unrealistic assumptions. First,
these antecedents do not affect the outcome construct directly. Second,
indirect effects of these antecedents operate only through the endoge-
nous causal construct. These assumptions do not hold when, for
example, managerial behavior is not modeled as an antecedent in a
theory of justice perceptions and employee behaviors. A third assump-
tion is the absence of reverse causality. If either of these three assump-
tions is not met, causality cannot be inferred. Even if causal
identification is possible, theories with endogenous causal constructs are
typically not actionable. Thus, these theories are of the observe(X) type.

Example. Much leadership research has considered positive leader-
ship styles as behavioral constructs (e.g., Lemoine, Hartnell, & Leroy,
2019; Liden, Wang, & Wang, 2025), which affect a range of leadership
outcomes. For example, reviewing ethical, authentic, and servant lead-
ership styles, Lemoine et al. (2019, p. 177) concluded that“moral
leadership behaviors positively impact a host of desirable organiza-
tionally relevant outcomes.”
Yet, positive leadership styles are conflated, mixing leader behaviors
with the evaluation of their underlying intentions, quality of execution,
or behavioral effects (Fischer & Sitkin, 2023). Fischer, Dietz, and
Antonakis (2024) traced this conflation back to confusion about the
meaning of styles. Rather than representing sets of behaviors, styles are
evaluated sets of behaviors. Observers see and then evaluate behaviors to
represent a style (e.g., an authentic style). Styles as evaluations are
outcome constructs that are affected by leader behaviors but also by
other leader properties, contextual factors, and observer properties.
In four experiments, Fischer et al. (2024) found support for the
notion of styles as evaluated sets of behaviors. Ratings on positive
leadership styles predicted outcomes, such as giving donations to a
leader’s charity, even when leader behavior did not vary at all. Hence,
positive leadership style ratings had to be indicative of other constructs
than just leader behaviors. An additional finding pointed to evaluations
as such a construct. Positive leadership style measures and a purely
evaluative leadership measure correlated strongly with each other and
had highly similar patterns of relationships to leadership outcomes.
Taken together, Fischer et al.’s (2024) arguments and findings suggest
that positive leadership styles are amalgams of behaviors and evalua-
tions rather than pure behavioral constructs. Due to this lack of
construct unity, leadership style theories cannot point to specific be-
haviors for leadership interventions, which renders these theories
unactionable and causally unspecified.
Summary. Conflated constructs result in causal indeterminacy
because effects might stem from any of the conflated components.
Moreover, these constructs cannot guide practice because they cannot
clearly point to actionable levers. Even as outcome constructs, conflated
constructs undermine causal and actionable theorizing.
Discussion
When does a theory become actionable enough to guide practice?
The answer developed in this manuscript seems simple: A theory can
guide action when its causal constructs represent producing causes
whose levels practitioners can manipulate. Then it is a manipulate(X)
theory. If the answer is so straightforward, why do so few leadership and
management theories contain such constructs? One reason lies in aca-
demic priorities: while novel theoretical insights are valued, practical
utility often receives mere “lip service” (Corley & Gioia, 2011, p. 18).
Observe(X) theories may satisfy the scientific appetite for supposed
answers to the“why” question often by pointing to causal mechanisms
via psychological states. However, observe(X) theories leave practi-
tioners without levers. Yet, manipulate(X) theories are not only
actionable, but also bring scientific rigor and can lead to new theoretical
insights. Before outlining recommendations to scientists for building
actionable cause-effect theories, I start with a short note to practitioners.
For practitioners: enacting manipulate(X) theories
By identifying the type of practical theory [manipulate(X), select(X),
or observe(X)], managers know what the theory can offer. Only
manipulate(X) theories allow enacting the proposed cause-effect re-
lationships. For a theory to move from being actionable to being enac-
ted, apart from local feasibility challenges, four considerations are
important. First, if an actionable theory ought to be enacted also de-
pends on its boundary conditions, that is, to whom, when, and where the
theory applies. Second, has the theory received empirical support?
Often, the local effectiveness of a manipulate(X) theory cannot be
conclusively determined from past evidence. Then, practitioners might
consider producing evidence themselves, for example, with a small-scale
test (Dietz et al., 2014). Third, theories often focus on a single outcome,
such as job performance. For the sake of focus, other outcomes might not
be considered and eventually ignored. As Francis Bacon (1605/1884, p.
171) noted:“if you put the light in one corner, you darken the rest.”
However, because other outcomes can be as relevant to practitioners as
the target outcome, they must consider and measure these other out-
comes too. Fourth, actionable theories are tools which practitioners can
use to ethical or unethical ends. Thus, the toolbox of actionable theories
must also come with a moral compass (e.g. Thompson, 2010). Having
briefly discussed implications of manipulate(X) theories for practice, I
now turn to recapitulating these theories’ benefits for theoreticians.

For scientists: developing manipulate(X) theories
Manipulate(X) theories offer several benefits to scientists: Avoiding
underspecified theories, discovering causal mechanisms, and enhancing
consistency between conceptualization and operationalization.
Avoiding Underspecified Theories. Including manipulable causal
constructs can reduce the risk of underspecified theories that omit
critical constructs. For example, a leadership researcher might theorize
that follower performance is caused by followers’ perceptions of the
leader as visionary (perceived charisma). Such a theory would overlook
that perceived charisma is an explanatory cause rather than a producing
one. We would have a theory in which perceptions of charisma consti-
tute both the cause of performance (what?) and the explanation for this
causal link (why?). Including a manipulable cause of charisma percep-
tions, such as the use of rhetoric tactics, would solve this problem.
Moreover, including such a manipulable construct allows for a stronger
test of the theory. For the above example, Meslec, Curseu, Fodor, and
Kenda (2020) tested propositions about effects of perceived charisma
and rewards on performance. They found the theorized effect of
charisma perceptions only after including rhetoric tactics as an action-
able construct in their model.
Discovering Causal Mechanisms. Including manipulable causal
constructs strengthens inferences about causal mechanisms in two ways.
First, for the above charisma example, including such constructs reduces
the plausibility of alternative causal mechanisms due to just measuring a
supposed causal construct. Such alternative mechanisms could be fol-
lowers’ implicit theories about leadership and their motivation for being
consistent in questionnaire responses and performance.
Second, manipulable causal constructs can be used as a means to
discover and isolate causal mechanisms. On one hand, practitioners’
interventions can have causal effects through several mechanisms. The
multiple-mechanism argument has been made, for example, for chang-
ing the work environment (Bellet et al., 2024), for setting specific and
difficult goals (Locke & Latham, 2002), and for compensation schemes
(Sajons, 2020). On the other hand, once multiple mechanisms have been
discovered, the scientific challenge becomes to identify manipulable
causal constructs that only operate through one mechanism. Sajons
(2020), for example, did so in studying the effects of compensation
changes on task performance. She initially considered fairness percep-
tions and outcome favorability as two alternative mechanisms. How-
ever, by studying the effects of different wage cut regimes, she could
argue that outcome favorability could not be a plausible mechanism.
Enhancing Consistency of Conceptualization and Operationaliza-
tion. Actionable theories have to clarify how practitioners can affect
construct levels. The inclusion of manipulable causal constructs calls
attention to the consistency of conceptualization and operationalization.
If, for example, a theory proposes behaviors as causal constructs, then
the proposed behaviors should be operationalized with behavioral in-
dicators and not with evaluative questionnaires (Banks, Woznyi, &
Mansfield, 2023; Fischer, 2023).
In addition to ensuring actionability and testability, considering
operationalization early in theory development enhances construct
quality. If a construct’s definition is ambiguous, overly broad, or
incomplete, designing valid manipulations and measurements becomes
very difficult (Podsakoff et al., 2016). Operationalization is, thus, a test
for construct quality and enhances theoretical rigor. Having addressed
the benefits of actionable theories for scientists, I now present recom-
mendations for building such theories.
Develop constructs that practitioners can manipulate
Researchers can address the endogenous-cause problem by including
manipulable antecedents in their theories. In addition, checks for
ensuring construct unity (and, thus, avoiding construct conflation) are
also possible. Lastly, paying to attention to operationalization in the
process of conceptualization can also help in developing manipulable

constructs.
Theorize Manipulable Antecedents of Explanatory Causes. Most
leadership and management theories focus on explaining why things
happen. Answering“why” is considered“the most fruitful but also the
most difficult avenue of theory development” (Whetten, 1989, p. 493).
Hence, theories often use explanatory causes, including psychological
states, such as attitudes, perceptions, preferences, and feelings (Sajons,
2020). However, psychological states are endogenous constructs that
depend on external stimuli. Generally, neither researchers nor practi-
tioners can directly set the levels of psychological states, in particular,
those of others.
Nonetheless, maybe due to overly wanting to explain the“why” or
due to overemphasizing the importance of perceiving stimuli, such as
leader behaviors, relative to that of the stimuli themselves in explaining
outcomes, scientists have at times purposefully ignored external stimuli.
For example, Konovsky (2000, p. 492) noted that“while the link be-
tween objective justice and subjective justice remains largely unexam-
ined…., I will assume that objective PJ [procedural justice, italics added]
leads to subjective justice perceptions.” Colquitt, Conlon, Wesson,
Porter, and Ng (2001, p. 425) explained: “… in the organizational sci-
ences, justice is considered to be socially constructed. That is, an act is
defined as just if most individuals perceive it to be so on the basis of
empirical research…. Thus, ‘what is fair’ is derived from past research
linking objective facets of decision making to subjective perceptions of
fairness.” Both Konovsky (2000) and Colquitt et al. (2001) were aware
that justice perceptions had antecedents but dismissed them. Even if, for
example, rewards were antecedents to both justice perceptions and
employee behaviors, rewards would not figure in their models of justice.
Overcoming the endogeneity of explanatory causes, such as justice
perceptions and other psychological states requires theorizing their
antecedents, as Greenberg (1990) did for perceptions of justice. Consider
as another case in point charisma research. Earlier theories (Conger &
Kanungo, 1987) defined charisma as a follower attribution, which is a
psychological state. Current charisma theory distinguishes between
charisma as behavior and perceptions of charisma as a mediating
construct or an outcome (Antonakis et al., 2016). Reconceptualization as
a behavior makes charisma actionable: leaders use rhetoric tactics to
affect leadership outcomes. One mechanism behind this effect is
perceived charisma (Wilms et al., 2025). This example shows that psy-
chological states have explanatory value. However, theories about
psychological states are more rigorous and actionable if they include
manipulable antecedents.
Ensure Construct Unity. Doing so is both a conceptual and an
empirical task. For a conceptual check, the labeling of constructs offers a
starting point. Labels of behavioral constructs that contain valenced
adjectives (e.g. positive and authentic) or point to outcomes (e.g.,
counterproductive workplace behavior and organizational citizenship)
and attributes (e.g., styles) are red flags concerning construct unity. The
definiens can also reveal a lack of construct unity, when it subsumes
several constructs without justifying combining these constructs into a
new one. An example is the construct of transformational leadership
with its four dimensions, such as intellectual stimulation and individu-
alized consideration (Van Knippenberg & Sitkin, 2013).
Along with conceptual reasoning, empirical testing can reveal non-
unitary constructs. Fischer et al.’s (2024) above-mentioned research
offers guidance in this regard. If a seemingly behavioral construct pre-
dicts an outcome after keeping the behavior constant, then the construct
captures more than behavior alone. Moreover, if a seemingly behavioral
construct, such as authentic leadership, shares a nomological network
with a non-behavioral construct, such as leader evaluations, it is likely
not purely behavioral.
Non-unitary constructs can be fixed by deconflating them (Fischer &
Sitkin, 2023). For example, Stock et al. (2023) did so for trans-
formational leadership. Recognizing the conflation in the trans-
formational leadership style construct, Stock et al. separated
transformational leadership behaviors from their evaluation and defined

In the Theory, Address the Operationalization of Constructs. By
specifying the operationalization, actionable theories facilitate the
testing and application of theories. Whereas the causal construct itself
identifies what lever practitioners can use, the operationalization spec-
ifies how that lever is used. For example, in Antonakis et al.’s (2016)
charisma theory, charismatic signals are the causal construct, speaking
to the“what” and rhetoric tactics are the operationalization, speaking to
the“how.” It is the“how” that makes the construct actionable.
In theorizing, focus on actionable producing causes
While the recommendations above concern the content of actionable
theories, the recommendations below address the process of theorizing.
Thought Experiments. Thought experiments are the cognitive
counterpart to empirical experiments, which are considered the gold
standard for testing causality. Underlying experiments is the notion of
counterfactuals. The effects of a factual and a counterfactual are isolated
and then compared, such as the effects of an adequate versus an inad-
equate explanation in Greenberg’s (1990) earlier mentioned study.
Thought experiments reproduce the counterfactual logic as the imagi-
nation of alternative hypothetical“what if” scenarios (e.g., Weick, 1989;
Folger & Turillo, 1999; Cornelissen & Durand, 2014). For actionable
theorizing, counterfactual reasoning needs to target manipulable pro-
ducing causes (“What if I gave this type of explanation or that type of
explanation?”) and guard the conditions of causality, such as ruling out
alternative explanations. As Folger and Turillo (1999, p. 753) noted:
“Thought experiments isolate manipulated variables, operationalize
them, hold others constant… and disregard those considered extra-
neous. The experimental design process becomes theory input, rather
than a means to obtain data for the theory-output sake of empirically
tested hypotheses.” In this way, so express Folger and Turillo, designing
experiments informs theorizing by pointing to levers for action, which
they refer to as “on–off switches”
, while guarding the conditions for
causality.
Move from Relevant Problems to Actionable Solutions. The example
of trust shows that a theory can address a relevant problem without
being actionable. To build practical theories, a shift in focus from
outcome constructs (the problem) to actionable antecedent constructs
(the solution) is key. This can be achieved by framing“research ques-
tions in terms of managerially controllable variables” (Shrivastava &
Mitroff, 1984, p. 23; see also Toffel, 2016). Such questions align with
managerial interests in“what works” (Bansal, Bertels, Ewart, MacCon-
nachie, & O’Brien, 2012, p. 80) as an intervention, while allowing re-
searchers to pursue the explanation of why constructs have causal
effects.
For identifying manipulable constructs, past research has taken three
avenues. First, the discovery of goals as drivers of performance and of
nudges and defaults as shapers of behavior resulted largely from the
observation of real-world phenomena (Locke & Latham, 2019; Thaler,
2015). Thaler, for example, kept a list of real-life examples of seemingly
irrational behavior and searched for commonalities across examples.
Second, the identification of charismatic signals, transformational
leadership behaviors, and ethical leader signals ensued from advancing
past research on managerially relevant, but causally deficient theories
(Antonakis et al., 2016; Banks et al., 2023; Stock et al., 2023). Antonakis
et al., Banks et al., and Stock et al. recognized the conflation in behav-
ioral leadership constructs and proceeded by isolating the behavioral
components of these constructs. A third avenue for the discovery of
manipulable constructs is the earlier mentioned identification of
behavioral antecedents of psychological states. For example, in research
on teams, the distribution of speaking time might emerge as an ante-
cedent of cohesion (O’Bryan et al., 2024).
Iterate between Theory, Data, and Practice. For discovering
manipulable constructs, an iterative approach of shuttling back and

forth between theory and data can be helpful (Chatman and Flynn,
2005). As the above example of nudges and defaults shows, interesting
facts, whether observed in real-world phenomena or empirical studies,
can stimulate theorizing. In a deductive process, these facts motivate
inquiry and eventually theorizing, which then is tested. Transparency is
critical in this process to clarify that inductive insights from data are
post-hoc explanations and not a-priory hypotheses (Shepherd & Sud-
daby, 2017). These post-hoc explanations become the foundation for
deductive reasoning and subsequent testing. Iteration between theory
and data is especially helpful for building actionable theory when it
involves field data. Latham, for example, first encountered goal setting
by observing pulpwood crews in the 1960s, and it took two decades of
empirical work before Locke and Latham (1990) formalized goal-setting
theory (Locke & Latham, 2019).
Recognize the Breadth of Narrow Constructs. Despite their promise
for causally rigorous and managerially practical research, scholars
might be disenchanted with manipulable causal constructs. Such con-
structs tend to be narrow, that is, be abstractions of small sets of ob-
servations and at a low level of abstraction. However, as already
mentioned, manipulable causal constructs may operate through multi-
ple mechanisms, which makes them interesting. For example, for the
effects of transformational leadership behaviors, knowledge on mecha-
nisms behind their effects is still at a nascent stage (Stock et al. (2023).
Similarly, Lemken (2024) noted the need for identifying mechanisms for
the effects of nudges and defaults. In addition, narrow constructs do not
mean narrow applicability. For example, goals, defaults, and other
nudges have been applied across work, education, and health domains
as well as many types of tasks and decisions (Beshears, Choi, Laibson, &
Madrian, 2021; Locke & Latham, 2019).
Communicate the Type of Practical Theory. Kieser and Leiner
(2009) lamented the uselessness of practical-implications sections in
academic articles. The categorization of practical theories in Table 1 is a
means to give these sections a more practitioner-oriented footing that is
genuine in reflecting what a theory can offer. For example, observe(X)
theories do not pinpoint action levers for practitioners, but they may
help in predicting relevant outcomes. Manipulate(X) theories, however,
are actionable. Whatever type of practical theory, communicating it
clarifies the theory’s utility to practitioners.
Conclusion
While theories can be practical in several ways, manipulate(X) the-
ories are actionable theories with causal constructs whose levels prac-
titioners can set themselves. Beyond meeting the criteria of
distinctiveness, nomological embeddedness, operationalizability, and
definitional clarity, these constructs must also be unitary, manipulable,
and their mechanism should be explained. Focusing on manipulable
causal constructs enhances not just the actionability of theory but also its
scientific rigor. It helps researchers avoid pitfalls, such as the simplifi-
cation fallacy, the endogenous-cause problem, and construct conflation.
In short, actionable theories are cause-effect theories that can be falsi-
fied and that point to causal mechanisms. Thus, developing actionable
theories does not only mean to build practical theories, but also scien-
tifically sound ones.
