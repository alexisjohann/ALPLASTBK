## Abstract

In this chapter we examine the relationship between Human Resource Management (HRM) and productivity. HRM includes incentive pay (individual and group) as well as many non-pay aspects of the employment relationship such as matching (hiring and firing) and work organization (e.g. teams, autonomy). We place HRM more generally within the literature on management practices and productivity. We start with some facts on levels and trends of both HRM and productivity and the main528 528528 528patterns of the exposed across firms. We then discuss some of the potential causal mechanisms relating HRM to productivity focusing on the five main economic channels: (i) the standard selection effect, (ii) information to help monitor and assign workers more effectively, (iii) the gift exchange channel, (iv) multi-tasking/gaming and (v) complementarities. A key focus is the debate on modern HR and more traditional approaches, especially in terms of firm performance.

## I. Introduction

Economists and practitioners have long been concerned about what determines productivity. An impressive literature has amassed recently exploring why there is so much heterogeneity of productivity across firms, even within narrowly defined industries. An influential view is that it is differences in "management practices" across firms which account for a substantial portion of productivity heterogeneity. This chapter addresses the question of whether Human Resource Management (HRM) contributes to productivity.

HRM includes incentive pay (individual and group) as well as many non-pay aspects of the employment relationship such as matching (hiring and firing) and work organization (e.g. teams, autonomy). We also place HRM more generally within the literature on management practices and productivity.

## II. Basic Facts

### 2.1 Dispersion of Productivity Across Firms

There is widespread dispersion of firm-level productivity even within narrowly defined industries. Syverson (2004a) finds that within US 4-digit industries, plants in the 90th percentile of TFP are almost twice as productive as plants in the 10th percentile.

**Key Findings:**
- 90/10 TFP ratio ≈ 1.92 (US manufacturing)
- Dispersion is even larger in developing countries (3-5x)
- Productivity dispersion is persistent over time
- Selection and reallocation drive aggregate productivity growth

### 2.2 What Explains Productivity Dispersion?

Potential explanations include:
1. **Technology/R&D** - but explains small fraction
2. **Human Capital** - worker quality differences
3. **Management Practices** - growing evidence this matters
4. **ICT adoption** - complementary with skills and organization
5. **Competition** - more competitive environments have better practices

## III. HRM and Productivity: Theory

### 3.1 Five Main Economic Channels

#### Channel 1: Standard Selection Effect
Performance pay attracts and retains higher ability workers through self-selection:
- High-ability workers prefer performance pay (maximize earnings)
- Low-ability workers prefer fixed wages (insurance)
- Lazear (2000): Safelite Auto Glass case - half of productivity gain from selection

#### Channel 2: Information/Monitoring
Performance pay generates information about worker productivity:
- Better matching of workers to tasks
- Identification of training needs
- More effective promotion decisions

#### Channel 3: Gift Exchange/Efficiency Wages
Workers reciprocate "good treatment" with higher effort:
- Akerlof (1982): gift exchange model
- Fehr et al. (experimental evidence)
- Can substitute for explicit performance pay

#### Channel 4: Multi-tasking and Gaming
Potential negative effects when tasks are multi-dimensional:
- Holmstrom and Milgrom (1991): multi-task principal-agent
- Workers focus on measured dimensions, neglect unmeasured
- Gaming: manipulating metrics rather than improving performance

#### Channel 5: Complementarities
HRM practices work together as systems:
- Milgrom and Roberts (1990, 1995): supermodularity
- Ichniowski et al. (1997): "systems" vs. individual practices
- ICT and HRM are complements (Bresnahan et al., 2002)

### 3.2 The Debate: High-Performance Work Systems vs. Taylorism

**High-Performance Work Systems (HPWS):**
- Team-based production
- Worker autonomy and discretion
- Broad job definitions
- Employee involvement in decisions
- Performance-related pay
- Extensive training

**Traditional/Taylorist:**
- Narrow job definitions
- Close supervision
- Piece rates
- Limited worker discretion
- Minimal training beyond job-specific

**Key Question:** Is there "one best way" or do different systems suit different contexts?

## IV. Empirical Evidence

### 4.1 Performance Pay and Productivity

**Individual Performance Pay:**
- Lazear (2000): 44% productivity increase at Safelite (windshield installers)
  - About half from incentive effect, half from selection
- Paarsch and Shearer (2000): 20% increase for tree planters
- Bandiera et al. (2005, 2007): 20% increase for fruit pickers

**Group-Based Pay:**
- Knez and Simester (2001): Continental Airlines on-time bonus
- Hamilton et al. (2003): garment workers - 14% gain from teams
- Kleiner and Helper (2003): auto supplier study

### 4.2 HRM Systems and Productivity

**Ichniowski, Shaw and Prennushi (1997):**
- 36 steel finishing lines, detailed HRM data
- "Innovative" HRM systems: 6.7% higher productivity
- Individual practices have little effect alone
- Strong complementarities between practices

**Table 3.1: HRM and Productivity - Key Studies**

| Study | Industry | Main Finding | Effect Size |
|-------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Lazear (2000) | Auto glass | Individual perf. pay | +44% productivity |
| Ichniowski et al. (1997) | Steel | HRM systems | +6.7% productivity |
| Black & Lynch (2001) | Manufacturing | Participation + ICT | Significant positive |
| Bloom et al. (2010) | Multi-country | Management overall | R² = 0.25-0.30 |

### 4.3 Complementarities: ICT and HRM

**Bresnahan, Brynjolfsson and Hitt (2002):**
- IT, workplace organization, and human capital are complements
- Firms with all three see largest productivity gains
- "Skill-biased organizational change"

**Caroli and Van Reenen (2001):**
- New work practices reduce demand for unskilled labor
- Complementarity between organizational change and skills

### 4.4 Management Practices and Productivity

**Bloom and Van Reenen (2007) - Key Paper:**
- First systematic cross-country management measurement
- 732 medium-sized manufacturing firms (4 countries)
- Strong correlation: management scores → TFP, profitability, growth
- 1 point increase in management score → 3.2% higher TFP

**Bloom et al. (2010) - Extended Dataset:**
- 6,000+ firms across 17 countries
- Management explains ~25-30% of productivity dispersion
- Results robust to extensive controls

## V. What Determines HRM Practices?

### 5.1 Competition

**Key Finding:** More competitive environments associated with better HRM.
- Bloom and Van Reenen (2007): competition → better management
- Schmitz (2005): productivity gains in competitive markets
- Syverson (2004b): competition reduces productivity dispersion

**Mechanisms:**
1. Selection: poorly managed firms exit
2. Incentives: managers work harder under competition
3. Information: competition reveals best practices

### 5.2 Ownership

**Family Management:**
- Bloom and Van Reenen (2007): family-managed firms have 0.3 lower management scores
- Bandiera et al. (2009): nepotism in hiring reduces productivity
- Effect strongest for eldest sons (primogeniture)

**Multinationals:**
- Higher management scores than domestic firms
- Transfer best practices across borders
- Bloom et al. (2012): US multinationals spread management

**Private Equity:**
- Davis et al. (2008): productivity gains after PE buyouts
- More aggressive restructuring and performance incentives

### 5.3 Regulation

**Labor Regulation:**
- Strict employment protection → lower use of HRM (tenure based pay)
- Botero et al. (2004): labor regulation and employment

**Product Market Regulation:**
- Entry barriers reduce competitive pressure
- Less incentive to adopt best practices

### 5.4 Trust and Social Capital

**Bloom et al. (2009):**
- Regional trust → more decentralization
- Trust reduces agency costs of delegation
- Cultural differences explain organizational differences

## VI. Decentralization

### 6.1 Theory of Decentralization

Trade-off between:
- **Information:** local managers have better information
- **Agency:** headquarters can better align incentives

**Factors favoring decentralization:**
- Heterogeneous environments (local knowledge valuable)
- High trust (less agency cost)
- Educated workforce (can handle delegation)
- Competition (need fast responses)

### 6.2 Empirical Evidence

**Bloom et al. (2010) - Decentralization:**
- First systematic measurement of decentralization across countries
- US firms most decentralized
- Scandinavian countries also high
- Southern Europe and Asia more centralized

**Correlates of Decentralization:**
| Factor | Effect on Decentralization |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Trust | Positive |
| Distance to HQ | Positive |
| Plant manager education | Positive |
| Heterogeneity of products | Positive |
| Number of plants | Positive |
| Employment protection | Negative |

## VIII. Conclusions

### Main Conclusions

1. **HRM affects productivity** - Both performance pay and work organization matter
2. **Complementarities are crucial** - Practices work together as systems
3. **Management explains dispersion** - Accounts for 25-30% of productivity heterogeneity
4. **Competition improves HRM** - Through selection and incentive effects
5. **Ownership matters** - Family management associated with worse practices
6. **Decentralization varies** - Trust and culture drive organizational choices

### Future Research Directions

1. Better identification of causal effects
2. Understanding adoption barriers
3. Role of ICT and AI
4. Services sector (beyond manufacturing)
5. Developing country contexts
6. Within-firm variation

## Key Equations and Formalizations

### Production Function with Management

$$Y = A \cdot f(K, L, M)$$

where M represents management quality/HRM practices.

### Complementarity Condition

For practices $x_1$ and $x_2$ to be complements:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \Pi}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} > 0$$

The profit function exhibits supermodularity in HRM practices.

### Performance Pay Model (Principal-Agent)

Worker utility:
$$U = w + \beta \cdot (output - \bar{q}) - c(e)$$

where β is the piece rate, $\bar{q}$ is the quota, and c(e) is effort cost.

Optimal effort under performance pay:
$$c'(e^*) = \beta$$

### Decentralization Index

$$D_i = \sum_{j} w_j \cdot d_{ij}$$

where $d_{ij}$ is decision authority on dimension j and $w_j$ are weights.
