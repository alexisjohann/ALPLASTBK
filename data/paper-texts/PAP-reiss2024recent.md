# Recent inflation developments in Austria – an analysis based on different decomposition frameworks

**Authors:** Lukas Reiss, Martin Schneider (OeNB, Business Cycle Analysis Section)
**Journal:** Monetary Policy & the Economy (OeNB)
**JEL Codes:** E31, D33
**Keywords:** inflation, profit share
**Type:** Central Bank Publication
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

This article compares different frameworks for decomposing recent inflation developments in
Austria. The value-added deflator, GDP deflator, total supply deflator and HICP are analyzed
using input-output table-based decomposition methods. The key finding is that GDP deflator
decompositions are MISLEADING for consumer price inflation analysis because they ignore
import costs (34.4% of total supply costs) and overweight wages (54.3% vs. 26.9% in HICP).
Wages contribute only 0.27pp to HICP per 1% wage increase (not 0.54pp as the value-added
deflator decomposition suggests). Profits soared in 2022 (27% of HICP inflation) but cratered
in 2023-2024 — cumulative excess profits since 2020 are NEGATIVE.

## Methodology

### Four Decomposition Frameworks Compared
1. **Value-added deflator decomposition**: Standard national accounts approach — decomposes into
   wages, profits, taxes on production. Covers 54.3% wages, 38.3% profits, 7.4% taxes.
   PROBLEM: Ignores import prices entirely.

2. **GDP deflator decomposition**: Extends value-added with terms of trade. Adds import/export
   prices. Covers domestic production only.
   PROBLEM: Weights reflect GDP composition, not consumer expenditure composition.

3. **Total supply deflator decomposition**: Value-added + imports = total supply. Better coverage
   of import channel.
   PROBLEM: Still not consumer-price-relevant weighting.

4. **HICP decomposition (Schneider 2024)**: Input-output table based method specifically designed
   to decompose consumer price inflation into cost components at the granularity of consumer
   goods categories. Uses CPA-74 → COICOP-45 mapping via Leontief inverse.
   THIS IS THE CORRECT FRAMEWORK for consumer price analysis.

### HICP Decomposition Method (Schneider 2024)
- **Step 1**: Map CPA-74 domestic production categories to COICOP-45 consumption categories
  using input-output tables (2019 base year)
- **Step 2**: Calculate Leontief inverse for multiplier effects (intermediate inputs)
- **Step 3**: Decompose total supply costs per COICOP category into:
  - Wages (compensation of employees)
  - Profits (gross operating surplus)
  - Taxes less subsidies on production
  - Import costs (energy + non-energy)
  - Taxes less subsidies on products
- **Step 4**: Combine with quarterly national accounts data for time variation
- **Step 5**: Weight by HICP consumption basket composition

### Key Structural Insight: Cost Shares Differ Dramatically

| Cost Component | Value-Added Deflator | HICP Decomposition | Difference |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------------|------------|
| Wages | 54.3% | 26.9% | -27.4pp |
| Profits | 38.3% | 17.3% | -21.0pp |
| Taxes (production) | 7.4% | 3.4% | -4.0pp |
| Import costs | 0% | 34.4% | +34.4pp |
| Taxes (products) | 0% | 18.0% | +18.0pp |

→ Using value-added deflator decomposition DOUBLES the apparent wage contribution to inflation.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: GDP Deflator Decomposition Is Misleading for Consumer Prices
- Value-added deflator has 0% import cost share → cannot capture import price shocks
- Import prices = 34.4% of total supply costs but invisible in GDP-based decomposition
- Wages appear as 54.3% of costs in value-added but only 26.9% in HICP
- GDP deflator and total supply deflator show 2022 as mainly profit-driven ("greedflation")
  but HICP decomposition reveals import prices as the PRIMARY initial driver

### Finding 2: Import Prices Were the Primary Initial Driver (2021-2022)
- Import prices drove the initial HICP surge starting Q2 2021
- Energy import prices peaked in 2022 with massive contribution to headline inflation
- Non-energy import prices also contributed significantly (supply chain disruptions)
- This channel is INVISIBLE in value-added deflator decomposition
- Import price contribution reversed sharply in 2023 (base effects + energy price decline)

### Finding 3: Wage-Price Spiral Potential Is Much Smaller Than Commonly Believed
- 1% wage increase → 0.27pp HICP increase (HICP decomposition)
- 1% wage increase → 0.54pp value-added deflator increase
- The value-added deflator OVERSTATES wage pass-through by 2x
- Implication: Fears of wage-price spiral based on GDP/value-added analysis are exaggerated
- Consistent with Köppl-Turyna (2023) VAR finding: 1% wage → 0.59% CPI after 4 quarters
  (but note: VAR captures dynamic effects including second-round, so higher than static 0.27)

### Finding 4: Profits Soared in 2022 but Cratered 2023-2024
- 2022: Profits accounted for approximately 27% of HICP inflation
- 2023: Profit contribution turned sharply negative
- 2024: Profit contribution continued falling
- CUMULATIVE excess profits since 2020: NEGATIVE
- Key insight: "Greedflation" narrative holds for 2022 snapshot but NOT cumulatively
- Companies absorbed part of input cost increases in 2023-2024 through margin compression

### Finding 5: Food Sector — No Evidence of Profit Gouging
- Austrian Federal Competition Authority (Bundeswettbewerbsbehörde, BWB) investigation
- Result: No systematic evidence of food sector profit margin increases
- Food price inflation driven by energy costs (production, transport) + agricultural commodity prices
- Profit margins in food retail were compressed, not expanded

### Finding 6: Energy Sector Profits Rose with Lag, Then Fell
- Energy sector profits rose 2022-2023 (lagged pass-through of wholesale prices)
- Austrian energy market structure: regulated prices + contract structures delayed pass-through
- Energy sector profits fell in 2024 as wholesale prices normalized
- Consistent with Köppl-Turyna (2023): energy price shock arrived later but persisted longer in AT

### Finding 7: Services Inflation Highest in 2024
- Services inflation became the dominant component by 2024
- Primary drivers: wage costs (collective bargaining increases) + some sectoral profits
- Restaurants/Hotels: both wage pressure and some profit margin recovery
- Real estate services: above-average profit growth
- Housing services: rent adjustments (lagged, as predicted by Pallotti et al. 2024)

### Finding 8: Taxes on Products — Significant but Often Ignored
- Taxes on products (VAT, excise duties) = 18.0% of HICP costs
- This component is entirely absent from value-added deflator analysis
- Tax changes (temporary VAT reductions, energy levy changes) had measurable inflation effects
- Fiscal response visible in HICP decomposition but invisible in GDP-based analysis

## Note
This is a central bank analytical publication from the OeNB Business Cycle Analysis Section.
Not peer-reviewed but methodologically rigorous (applies Schneider 2024 input-output decomposition).
Key contribution: Demonstrates that GDP deflator-based inflation decompositions systematically
overstate wage contribution (2x) and understate import price contribution (invisible), leading
to misleading policy conclusions about wage-price spiral risk. The finding that cumulative excess
profits since 2020 are negative directly challenges the persistent "greedflation" narrative.
Directly relevant for Austrian fiscal and wage policy.
