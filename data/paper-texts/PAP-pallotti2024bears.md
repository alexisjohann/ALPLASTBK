# Who bears the costs of inflation? Euro area households and the 2021–2023 shock

**Authors:** Filippo Pallotti (UCL & Lombard Odier), Gonzalo Paz-Pardo (ECB), Jiri Slacalek (ECB), Oreste Tristani (ECB & CEPR), Giovanni L. Violante (Princeton, CEPR, IFS, IZA, NBER)
**Journal:** Journal of Monetary Economics 148 (2024) 103671
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2024.103671
**Conference:** Spring 2024 NBER conference on "Inflation in the Covid era and beyond"
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

We measure the welfare costs of the 2021–2023 inflation surge for Euro Area households. We
develop a sufficient-statistics framework that decomposes the welfare effects of an inflation shock
into four channels: a direct (consumption) channel, an unconventional fiscal policy (UFP) channel,
an indirect (macroeconomic adjustment) channel, and a long-run adjustment channel. Our
framework accounts for consumption and income heterogeneity at a granular level, the role of
housing, and the fiscal response to the inflation shock. We apply the framework to the four largest
Euro Area economies (Germany, France, Italy, Spain). Our key findings are: (1) The aggregate
welfare costs of the inflation shock range from around 3% of one-year income in France and Spain
to 9% in Italy, in a decomposition that reflects primarily the direct channel. (2) Inflation acted as a
regressive tax: retirees lost up to 14% of their income, whereas roughly half of 25–44 year-olds
were net winners. (3) Around 25% of Euro Area households were net winners. (4) Net Nominal
Positions (NNP) are the key driver of heterogeneity. (5) Government was a net winner in France,
Italy, and Spain due to devaluation of outstanding debt exceeding fiscal support costs.

## Methodology

### Sufficient-Statistics Framework (Envelope Theorem)
- Welfare effect = first-order approximation using envelope theorem
- Total welfare change decomposed into 4 channels:
  1. **Direct channel**: unexpected price changes × consumption basket composition
  2. **UFP channel**: unconventional fiscal policy (energy subsidies, tax cuts, price caps)
  3. **Indirect channel**: macroeconomic adjustment (labor income, asset prices, interest rates)
  4. **Long-run adjustment channel**: effect of persistent inflation on nominal contracts

### Welfare Decomposition (Eq. 1-5)
- W_total = W_direct + W_UFP + W_indirect + W_longrun
- W_direct = -Σ_j (ω_j × Δp_j/p_j) where ω_j = consumption share of good j
- W_UFP = fiscal transfers - tax changes (energy-specific)
- W_indirect = Δ(labor income) + Δ(asset values) + Δ(interest payments)
- W_longrun = NNP × cumulative unexpected inflation (Fisher channel)

### Net Nominal Position (NNP) — Key Variable
- NNP = nominal assets - nominal liabilities
- Positive NNP → inflation hurts (creditors lose)
- Negative NNP → inflation helps (debtors gain)
- NNP for government: large negative (outstanding debt) → government wins from inflation

### Data Sources
- **HFCS (2017)**: Household Finance and Consumption Survey — wealth, assets, liabilities
- **HBS (2015)**: Household Budget Survey — detailed consumption baskets
- **HICP disaggregated**: Harmonized Index of Consumer Prices (detailed categories)
- **Bruegel energy crisis dataset**: Government fiscal responses to energy crisis
- **Event studies**: REIT prices, stock indices, bond prices for asset price changes
- **ECB lending surveys**: Mortgage rate changes

### Sample
- 4 largest Euro Area economies: Germany, France, Italy, Spain
- 15 household groups: 3 age groups (25-44, 45-64, 65+) × 5 consumption quintiles
- Period: 2021-2023 (cumulative triennial inflation shock)

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Aggregate Welfare Costs Vary Enormously Across Countries
- France: ~3% of one-year income
- Spain: ~3% of one-year income
- Germany: ~7% of one-year income
- Italy: ~9% of one-year income
- Decomposition: Direct channel dominates (>80% of total in most countries)
- France lower because of extensive fiscal energy support (3% GDP in price-suppressing measures)

### Finding 2: Inflation as Regressive Tax — Age-Dependent
- Retirees (65+): lose up to 14% of income (largest consumption shares in energy/food, fixed pensions)
- Young (25-44): roughly half are NET WINNERS (mortgage debtors benefit from inflation)
- Middle-aged (45-64): mixed, depends on NNP and consumption quintile
- Key mechanism: older households have higher NNP (more nominal savings, no mortgage debt)

### Finding 3: ~25% of Euro Area Households Are Net Winners
- Winners: young mortgage holders, households with high debt/income ratios
- Losers: retirees, high-NNP savers, renters with no debt
- Distribution roughly symmetric around zero for young cohorts
- Strongly negative (net losers) for elderly cohorts

### Finding 4: NNP Is the Key Driver of Heterogeneity
- NNP = nominal assets - nominal liabilities
- NNP channel (Fisher effect) accounts for largest share of cross-household variation
- Even within same age-consumption group, NNP position determines winner/loser status
- Renters: rigid rents serve as inflation HEDGE (rent adjusts slowly)
- Homeowners with mortgage: debt devaluation = net gain

### Finding 5: Government as Net Winner
- Government NNP is LARGE NEGATIVE (outstanding sovereign debt)
- Inflation devalues outstanding debt → fiscal windfall
- In France, Italy, Spain: debt devaluation > fiscal energy support costs
- Government net gain: substantial (exact figures vary by country)
- Implication: government COULD redistribute windfall to compensate households
- If redistributed proportionally: would substantially reduce average household losses

### Finding 6: Consumption Heterogeneity Matters Less Than Expected
- Poor and rich households have similar welfare losses from direct channel
- Reason: poor spend more on food/energy (inflationary) BUT also more on RENT (inflation hedge)
- Rent rigidity offsets higher food/energy exposure for low-income households
- Key insight: rental market institutions mediate inflation's distributional effects

### Finding 7: Unconventional Fiscal Policy (UFP) Channel
- Energy subsidies, tax cuts, price caps reduce direct channel losses
- France: most generous (3% GDP) → lowest welfare costs
- Germany: least generous → highest welfare costs among "rich" countries
- UFP is progressive: benefits low-consumption households more (energy = larger share)

## Complementary Findings

### vs. Dao et al. (2024)
- Both find energy prices as primary driver
- This paper adds DISTRIBUTIONAL dimension: who specifically is hurt/helped
- Dao focuses on inflation dynamics; this paper focuses on welfare costs

### vs. Binetti, Nuzzi & Stantcheva (2024)
- Binetti: perceived costs much larger than measured costs
- This paper: measured costs are 3-9% of income — substantial but lower than perceptions
- Perception gap may be partly explained by salience of food/energy prices

### vs. Romer & Romer (2024)
- Romer: commitment to disinflation matters for success
- This paper: disinflation also has distributional consequences (creditors vs. debtors)

## Policy Implications
1. **Fiscal energy measures work**: France vs. Germany comparison demonstrates effectiveness
2. **Government windfall exists**: Inflation devalues sovereign debt — fiscal space for compensation
3. **Rent regulation matters**: Rigid rents serve as automatic inflation hedge for tenants
4. **Redistribution feasible**: Government gains could compensate worst-affected households
5. **Age-targeted support**: Retirees bear disproportionate burden — pension indexation crucial
