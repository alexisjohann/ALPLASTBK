# Accelerating Complex Problem Solving Expertise: A Self-Regulated Learning Approach

**Authors:** Marcel Fahrbach and Alex Fust

**Journal:** Journal of Dynamic Decision Making, 11, Article 2

**Year:** 2025

**DOI:** 10.11588/jddm.2025.1.105968

**Received:** 26.07.2024 | **Accepted:** 16.07.2025 | **Published:** 2025

## Abstract

This paper aims to identify how problem solvers can accelerate the development of complex problem solving (CPS) expertise. While CPS theory has traditionally focused on identifying cognitive elements that impact performance, research on how individuals can improve their CPS competency and develop CPS expertise remains scarce. Building on social cognitive theory, we explore the role of self-regulated learning (SRL) strategies in the development of CPS competency and expertise. Specifically, we propose a theoretical framework that integrates SRL strategies—metacognitive, motivational, and behavioral—into the CPS process, which consists of information acquisition, goal setting, strategic planning, monitoring, and evaluation. We derive propositions on how SRL strategies can accelerate the transition from CPS competency to CPS expertise. Our framework extends CPS theory by offering actionable strategies for problem solvers seeking to enhance their CPS capabilities and provides a foundation for future empirical validation.

**Keywords:** complex problem solving, self-regulated learning, expertise development, metacognition, entrepreneurship

## Introduction

In today's entrepreneurial landscape, new businesses are continuously confronted with unforeseen, complex problems (Funke, 2010). These complex problems tend to be intransparent, dynamic, interconnected, and are characterized by the interaction of numerous variables (Dörner, 1989; Frensch & Funke, 1995b; Funke, 2010). Complex problem solving (CPS), an individual's ability to solve such problems (Frensch & Funke, 1995b), is increasingly recognized as an essential skill for entrepreneurs, whose success depends on effectively navigating novel and ambiguous situations (Baggen et al., 2015; Winkler et al., 2023). Entrepreneurs routinely face challenges that demand complex problem solving skills (Funke et al., 2018; OECD, 2014), and the ability to solve complex problems effectively distinguishes successful ventures from those that fail (Baggen et al., 2017; Haynie et al., 2012).

Despite the critical importance of CPS, research on how individuals develop and accelerate CPS expertise remains limited. Existing CPS literature has primarily focused on identifying the cognitive elements—such as knowledge acquisition, strategic planning, and decision-making—that contribute to effective problem solving (Dörner & Funke, 2017; Fischer & Neubert, 2015). While this work has advanced our understanding of CPS processes, it has paid less attention to the metacognitive and motivational strategies that individuals can deploy to improve their CPS competency over time (Winkler et al., 2023).

To address this gap, we draw on self-regulated learning (SRL) theory (Zimmerman, 2000, 2001), which provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how individuals regulate their cognition, motivation, and behavior to achieve learning goals. SRL theory has been extensively applied in educational contexts (Schunk & Zimmerman, 1998) and has been shown to improve performance across a wide range of domains, from athletics (Kitsantas et al., 2000) to academic achievement (Zimmerman, 1989). However, its application to CPS—particularly in entrepreneurial contexts—remains largely unexplored.

Our paper develops a theoretical framework that integrates SRL strategies into the CPS process. Specifically, we examine how metacognitive strategies (planning, monitoring, and evaluating one's cognitive processes), motivational strategies (setting goals, managing self-efficacy, and maintaining intrinsic motivation), and behavioral strategies (seeking information, structuring the environment, and seeking help) can accelerate the development of CPS competency and the transition to CPS expertise. We derive a set of propositions that link specific SRL strategies to each phase of the CPS process, creating a roadmap for problem solvers seeking to enhance their CPS capabilities.

Our framework contributes to the CPS literature in several ways. First, it extends the predominantly cognitive focus of CPS research by incorporating metacognitive and motivational dimensions. Second, it provides actionable strategies that problem solvers can apply to accelerate their expertise development. Third, it bridges the CPS and entrepreneurship literatures by demonstrating how SRL—a well-established construct in educational psychology—can be applied to the entrepreneurial context of complex problem solving.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. We first review the theoretical foundations of CPS and SRL. We then develop our integrative framework and derive propositions. Finally, we discuss implications for research and practice and outline directions for future empirical validation.

## Complex Problem Solving

### Definition and characteristics

Complex problem solving (CPS) refers to an individual's ability to solve problems that are novel, complex, dynamic, and intransparent (Frensch & Funke, 1995b). These problems are characterized by several features that distinguish them from simple problems (Dörner, 1989; Funke, 2010):

1. **Complexity:** The problem involves a large number of interrelated variables, making it difficult to understand the system as a whole.
2. **Connectivity:** The variables within the system are interconnected, such that changes in one variable can have cascading effects on others.
3. **Dynamics:** The system evolves over time, even without the problem solver's intervention, requiring continuous monitoring and adaptation.
4. **Intransparency:** Not all relevant information is immediately available to the problem solver, who must actively seek out information to understand the system.
5. **Polytely:** There are multiple, potentially conflicting goals that the problem solver must balance.

These characteristics make CPS fundamentally different from well-defined problems that have clear solution paths. In complex problems, the problem solver must construct a mental model of the system, develop and test hypotheses about causal relationships, and adapt strategies in response to feedback—all while managing uncertainty and competing objectives (Dörner & Funke, 2017; Fischer et al., 2012).

### The CPS process

Building on prior research (Dörner, 1989; Fischer & Neubert, 2015; Funke, 2010), we conceptualize CPS as a process consisting of five interrelated phases:

1. **Information acquisition:** The problem solver gathers information about the system, its variables, and their interrelationships. This involves both active exploration (manipulating variables to observe effects) and passive observation (monitoring the system's behavior over time).

2. **Goal setting:** The problem solver defines specific objectives to be achieved, prioritizing among potentially conflicting goals. Goals provide direction for subsequent problem-solving activities and serve as benchmarks for evaluating progress.

3. **Strategic planning:** The problem solver develops a plan of action based on their understanding of the system and their goals. This involves selecting strategies, sequencing actions, and anticipating potential obstacles.

4. **Monitoring:** The problem solver tracks the effects of their actions on the system, compares actual outcomes to expected outcomes, and identifies discrepancies that require attention.

5. **Evaluation:** The problem solver assesses the overall effectiveness of their approach, draws conclusions about what worked and what did not, and extracts lessons for future problem-solving episodes.

These phases are not strictly sequential; rather, they overlap and iterate as the problem solver refines their understanding of the system and adapts their approach (Fischer et al., 2012).

### CPS competency and expertise

We distinguish between CPS competency and CPS expertise, following the expertise development literature (Chi et al., 1982; Ericsson et al., 1993; Dreyfus, 2004). CPS competency refers to the ability to apply CPS processes effectively in a given situation. A competent problem solver can gather information, set goals, plan strategies, monitor outcomes, and evaluate results, but may do so in a relatively effortful and domain-specific manner.

CPS expertise, by contrast, represents a higher level of proficiency characterized by several distinguishing features (Chi et al., 1982; Sternberg, 1995):

- **Automaticity:** Expert problem solvers execute many CPS processes automatically, freeing cognitive resources for higher-order reasoning.
- **Pattern recognition:** Experts quickly recognize familiar patterns in novel situations, allowing them to draw on prior experience to guide their actions.
- **Metacognitive awareness:** Experts have a well-developed understanding of their own cognitive processes, enabling them to monitor and regulate their problem solving more effectively.
- **Adaptive flexibility:** Experts can flexibly adapt their strategies in response to changing circumstances, rather than rigidly adhering to a predetermined plan.
- **Transfer ability:** Experts can transfer their problem-solving skills across different domains and contexts.

The transition from competency to expertise is gradual and requires extensive practice (Ericsson et al., 1993). However, not all practice is equally effective. Research on deliberate practice suggests that the quality of practice matters more than the quantity, and that metacognitive strategies play a critical role in accelerating expertise development (Ericsson et al., 1993; Zimmerman, 2006).

## Self-Regulated Learning

### Definition and theoretical foundations

Self-regulated learning (SRL) refers to self-generated thoughts, feelings, and actions that are planned and cyclically adapted to the attainment of personal goals (Zimmerman, 2000). SRL is grounded in social cognitive theory (Bandura, 1991; Schunk, 1989), which posits that human functioning results from the reciprocal interaction between personal factors (cognition, motivation, affect), behavioral factors (actions, strategies), and environmental factors (social context, feedback).

Zimmerman (2000, 2001) proposed a three-phase cyclical model of SRL:

1. **Forethought phase:** Before engaging in a task, the self-regulated learner analyzes the task, sets goals, plans strategies, and activates motivational beliefs (self-efficacy, outcome expectations, intrinsic interest).

2. **Performance phase:** During task execution, the self-regulated learner implements strategies, monitors progress, and employs self-control techniques to maintain focus and motivation.

3. **Self-reflection phase:** After task completion, the self-regulated learner evaluates outcomes, attributes results to causes, and draws conclusions that inform future learning episodes.

### SRL strategies

SRL encompasses three broad categories of strategies (Zimmerman, 2001):

**Metacognitive strategies** involve planning, monitoring, and evaluating one's cognitive processes. Metacognitive planning involves selecting appropriate strategies and allocating cognitive resources before task engagement. Metacognitive monitoring involves tracking one's comprehension and performance during task execution. Metacognitive evaluation involves assessing the effectiveness of one's approach after task completion.

**Motivational strategies** involve regulating one's motivation to learn and perform. These include setting challenging but attainable goals (Locke & Latham, 1990, 2002), maintaining self-efficacy beliefs (Bandura, 1997), managing outcome expectations (Wigfield & Eccles, 2000), and sustaining intrinsic interest through curiosity and challenge-seeking.

**Behavioral strategies** involve regulating one's actions and environment to support learning. These include seeking information and feedback (Zimmerman, 2002), structuring the physical and social environment to minimize distractions, and seeking help from others when needed (Zimmerman, 2008).

### SRL and expertise development

Research has established a strong link between SRL and expertise development across multiple domains (Zimmerman, 2006). Expert performers consistently demonstrate higher levels of self-regulation than novices, including more sophisticated metacognitive strategies, more adaptive motivational beliefs, and more effective behavioral routines (Cleary & Zimmerman, 2004; Kitsantas et al., 2000).

Importantly, SRL strategies are not merely a consequence of expertise but can also accelerate its development. Studies have shown that training individuals in SRL strategies improves performance and accelerates skill acquisition in domains ranging from sports (Kitsantas et al., 2000) to writing (Zimmerman & Kitsantas, 1999) to mathematics (Fuchs et al., 2003). This suggests that SRL strategies could similarly accelerate the development of CPS expertise.

## Integrating SRL into CPS: A Theoretical Framework

We now develop our integrative framework by mapping SRL strategies onto each phase of the CPS process. For each phase, we identify specific SRL strategies that can enhance performance and accelerate expertise development, and we derive propositions about the expected effects.

### Information acquisition and SRL

Information acquisition is the foundational phase of CPS, in which the problem solver builds a mental model of the system (Dörner, 1989). Effective information acquisition requires both breadth (exploring many variables) and depth (understanding causal relationships among variables).

SRL strategies can enhance information acquisition in several ways:

**Metacognitive strategies:** Problem solvers can plan their information-gathering activities by identifying what they need to know and prioritizing their exploration accordingly. They can monitor their emerging understanding by checking whether their mental model is consistent with observed data. They can evaluate the completeness and accuracy of their mental model by actively seeking disconfirming evidence.

**Motivational strategies:** Problem solvers can set specific learning goals for information acquisition (e.g., "I will identify the three most important variables by the end of this session"). They can maintain curiosity and intrinsic motivation by framing information acquisition as a discovery process rather than a chore.

**Behavioral strategies:** Problem solvers can structure their information-gathering activities systematically (e.g., varying one variable at a time to isolate effects). They can seek information from multiple sources and use knowledge maps or other tools to organize what they learn.

**Proposition 1:** Problem solvers who apply SRL strategies during information acquisition will develop more accurate and comprehensive mental models of complex systems than those who do not use SRL strategies.

**Proposition 2:** The systematic application of metacognitive monitoring during information acquisition will accelerate the identification of key variables and causal relationships in complex systems.

### Goal setting and SRL

Goal setting is critical in CPS because complex problems typically involve multiple, potentially conflicting objectives (Dörner, 1989). Effective goal setting requires prioritizing among objectives, decomposing complex goals into manageable subgoals, and maintaining flexibility as the situation evolves.

SRL strategies can enhance goal setting in several ways:

**Metacognitive strategies:** Problem solvers can evaluate the appropriateness of their goals in light of new information and revise them accordingly. They can monitor progress toward goals and detect when goals need to be adjusted.

**Motivational strategies:** Following goal-setting theory (Locke & Latham, 1990, 2002, 2006), problem solvers can set specific, challenging, and proximal goals that enhance motivation and provide clear benchmarks for evaluation. They can decompose distal goals into proximal subgoals to maintain motivation during extended problem-solving episodes (Bandura & Schunk, 1981).

**Behavioral strategies:** Problem solvers can make their goals explicit (e.g., writing them down) and share them with others to create accountability.

**Proposition 3:** Problem solvers who set specific, challenging, and proximal goals will demonstrate higher CPS performance than those who set vague or distal goals.

**Proposition 4:** The decomposition of complex goals into hierarchically organized subgoals will improve CPS performance by providing clearer direction and more frequent feedback opportunities.

### Strategic planning and SRL

Strategic planning involves developing a plan of action based on the problem solver's mental model and goals. In complex problems, effective planning requires anticipating the dynamic behavior of the system, considering multiple scenarios, and maintaining flexibility.

SRL strategies can enhance strategic planning in several ways:

**Metacognitive strategies:** Problem solvers can evaluate the quality of their plans by mentally simulating their expected outcomes. They can identify potential obstacles and develop contingency plans. They can monitor the alignment between their plans and their goals.

**Motivational strategies:** Problem solvers can enhance their self-efficacy for planning by reviewing past successes and by breaking the planning task into manageable steps. They can maintain motivation by focusing on the expected benefits of effective planning.

**Behavioral strategies:** Problem solvers can use external tools (e.g., decision matrices, scenario analyses) to support their planning. They can seek input from others to identify blind spots in their plans.

**Proposition 5:** Problem solvers who apply metacognitive planning strategies (mental simulation, contingency planning) will develop more robust CPS strategies than those who plan intuitively.

**Proposition 6:** The use of external planning tools will improve CPS performance by reducing cognitive load and enabling more systematic consideration of alternatives.

### Monitoring and SRL

Monitoring involves tracking the effects of one's actions and comparing actual outcomes to expected outcomes. In complex systems, monitoring is particularly challenging because of time delays, nonlinear relationships, and side effects (Dörner, 1989; Brehmer, 1992).

SRL strategies can enhance monitoring in several ways:

**Metacognitive strategies:** Problem solvers can develop explicit criteria for evaluating whether their actions are having the intended effects. They can monitor not only outcomes but also their own cognitive processes (e.g., "Am I paying attention to the right variables?"). They can detect when their mental model is inconsistent with observed outcomes and trigger a revision of their approach.

**Motivational strategies:** Problem solvers can maintain motivation during monitoring by setting specific monitoring goals (e.g., "I will check all five key indicators every three time steps"). They can manage their emotional responses to negative feedback by attributing setbacks to controllable causes (effort, strategy) rather than uncontrollable causes (ability, luck).

**Behavioral strategies:** Problem solvers can use systematic monitoring routines (e.g., checklists, dashboards) to ensure comprehensive coverage. They can record their observations to facilitate later evaluation.

**Proposition 7:** Problem solvers who apply metacognitive monitoring strategies will detect deviations from expected outcomes more quickly and respond more adaptively than those who monitor intuitively.

**Proposition 8:** Systematic monitoring routines will improve CPS performance by reducing the likelihood of overlooking important changes in the system.

### Evaluation and SRL

Evaluation involves assessing the overall effectiveness of one's approach and extracting lessons for future problem-solving episodes. In CPS, evaluation is critical for learning from experience and developing expertise (Fust et al., 2018).

SRL strategies can enhance evaluation in several ways:

**Metacognitive strategies:** Problem solvers can systematically review their decision-making process, identifying which strategies worked well and which did not. They can compare their final mental model to their initial understanding, noting how their understanding evolved. They can identify knowledge gaps that should be addressed in future problem-solving episodes.

**Motivational strategies:** Problem solvers can maintain a learning orientation by framing evaluation as an opportunity for growth rather than a judgment of ability. They can calibrate their self-efficacy beliefs by accurately assessing their strengths and weaknesses.

**Behavioral strategies:** Problem solvers can document their evaluations to create a record that can be reviewed in future episodes. They can seek feedback from others to complement their self-evaluation.

**Proposition 9:** Problem solvers who apply systematic self-evaluation strategies will demonstrate faster CPS expertise development than those who rely on intuitive evaluation.

**Proposition 10:** The attribution of outcomes to controllable causes (effort, strategy) will enhance CPS expertise development by maintaining motivation and directing attention to modifiable aspects of performance.

## The Four Levels of SRL in CPS

Building on Zimmerman's (2001) hierarchical model of SRL development, we propose that the integration of SRL into CPS unfolds across four developmental levels:

### Level 1: Observation

At the observation level, problem solvers learn SRL strategies by observing models—either live (watching an expert solve a complex problem) or symbolic (reading about SRL strategies in problem-solving contexts). Observational learning provides a cognitive representation of the skill that guides initial attempts (Bandura, 1991; Schunk, 2001).

In the CPS context, observation might involve watching an experienced entrepreneur navigate a complex business problem, paying attention not only to their actions but also to their self-regulatory processes (how they plan, monitor, and evaluate their approach).

### Level 2: Emulation

At the emulation level, problem solvers attempt to reproduce the general pattern of the modeled SRL strategies, with guidance and feedback from a social model (e.g., a mentor, coach, or instructor). The emphasis is on approximating the model's approach rather than achieving independent mastery.

In the CPS context, emulation might involve working through a complex problem under the guidance of an experienced mentor who provides feedback on the problem solver's use of SRL strategies.

### Level 3: Self-control

At the self-control level, problem solvers can independently apply SRL strategies in structured settings, without the direct guidance of a model. They follow internalized representations of the strategies and can adapt them to fit the demands of specific problems.

In the CPS context, self-control might involve independently solving complex problems while deliberately applying specific SRL strategies (e.g., setting specific goals, monitoring key variables, evaluating outcomes).

### Level 4: Self-regulation

At the self-regulation level, problem solvers can flexibly adapt their SRL strategies to varying conditions, maintain motivation in the face of obstacles, and transfer their skills across different domains and contexts. This level corresponds to CPS expertise.

In the CPS context, self-regulation represents the expert problem solver who seamlessly integrates metacognitive, motivational, and behavioral strategies into their problem-solving process, adapting their approach fluidly in response to the demands of each unique situation.

**Proposition 11:** The progression through the four levels of SRL development (observation → emulation → self-control → self-regulation) will accelerate the transition from CPS competency to CPS expertise.

**Proposition 12:** CPS training programs that scaffold learners through the four SRL levels will produce faster expertise development than programs that focus solely on cognitive CPS elements.

## Implications for CPS Training

Our framework has several implications for the design of CPS training programs:

First, CPS training should explicitly incorporate SRL strategies alongside cognitive CPS elements. Current CPS training approaches tend to focus on domain-specific knowledge and cognitive strategies (Greiff et al., 2013; Kretzschmar & Süß, 2015), with less attention to the metacognitive and motivational processes that support expertise development. Our framework suggests that integrating SRL strategies can accelerate the development of CPS expertise.

Second, CPS training should be situated in complex, realistic problem contexts. SRL strategies are most effective when applied to authentic problems that challenge learners and provide meaningful feedback (Fust et al., 2018). Simulated complex problems (e.g., computer-based microworlds) provide excellent training environments because they allow learners to practice SRL strategies in safe but realistic settings (Brehmer, 1993; Gonzalez et al., 2005).

Third, CPS training should be scaffolded according to the four levels of SRL development. Learners should first observe expert problem solvers modeling SRL strategies, then practice these strategies with guidance, then apply them independently in structured settings, and finally adapt them flexibly to novel situations.

Fourth, existing CPS training approaches can be adjusted by acknowledging the four-level hierarchy of SRL (Zimmerman, 2001) and scaffolding higher-order thinking and learning (Athanassiou et al., 2003). There is evidence that the motivation and problem-solving skills of learners who follow this four-step approach are superior to those of learners who skip these steps (Zimmerman & Kitsantas, 1997, 1999; Kitsantas et al., 2000). Integrating the problem solvers' cognitive, motivational, and metacognitive processes while dealing with complexity into educational training enables the continuous entrepreneurial development. Especially in uncertain and dynamic environments it is ineffective and can be costly to only rely on learning from experience and trial-and-error learning (Fust et al., 2018).

## Conclusion

The social cognitive view of SRL (Zimmerman, 2000, 2006) supports the process perspective on CPS (Fischer et al., 2012) and contributes to CPS research in several ways.

First, we expand CPS research by introducing SRL. This concept supports the cognitive CPS elements with metacognitive and motivational SRL strategies to demonstrate how problem solvers may develop CPS competency and accelerate their expertise (Winkler et al., 2023; Zimmerman, 2001). Metacognition enables problem solvers to regulate, monitor, and evaluate their CPS activities to gradually develop expertise (Ifenthaler, 2012). Understanding and executing motivational strategies helps to stay motivated in CPS processes and to enhance resilience to better deal with drawbacks when facing obstacles (Zimmerman & Campillo, 2003).

Second, SRL is a powerful approach for problem solvers addressing novel complex situations containing dynamic changes and unexpected events where experience is not available or even disadvantageous (Eesley & Roberts, 2012; Ucbasaran et al., 2009). Executing self-control by self-regulating behavior, cognition, motivation, and affect supports dealing with changing circumstances (Winkler et al., 2023). This adaptive capacity is essential for entrepreneurs who often face ambiguous and uncertain situations that require continuous learning and adjustments.

Third, we provide some concrete SRL strategies that problem solvers can apply to improve their CPS process, develop CPS competency, and accelerate CPS expertise in the long run. To guide their information acquisition, individuals should set proximal learning goals as part of their motivational regulation. The gathered knowledge is structured hierarchically to detect interconnections and patterns in various situations. SRL enables to differentiate between previous experiences and the requirements of novel settings (Winkler et al., 2023). While planning CPS strategies, solvers should guide their affect and behavior towards previously set goals (Pressley, 1995). They can identify effective problem solving strategies based on their utility value. Furthermore, outcome expectations help to promote and retain motivation during CPS. Regularly assessing and monitoring problem solving activities leads to either aligning goals or adapting strategies, which improves the way of learning. To continuously improve the CPS process, setbacks should result in an adjustment of one's problem representation, not an attribution to low ability, therefore, negative emotions and a withdrawal from further solution efforts (Ucbasaran et al., 2013).

## Limitations and Future Research

The implementation of SRL strategies can lead to enhancement in goal setting, strategic planning, information reduction, and DDM, ultimately accelerating the development of CPS expertise (Winkler et al., 2023).

It is important, however, to acknowledge the limitations of this theoretical framework. There is a lack of empirical evidence supporting the direct impact of SRL on the development of CPS competency. An additional empirical validation through longitudinal studies or experimental designs would be essential to establish causal relationships and measure the effectiveness of integrating SRL strategies in CPS training. Additionally, we have analyzed the relationship between SRL and CPS from an entrepreneurial perspective. Investigating the transferability of SRL to further contexts will contribute to the development of practical guidelines for educators seeking to enhance CPS competency.

In conclusion, while the theoretical framework presented in this paper highlights the potential benefits of incorporating SRL to improve CPS competency, empirical validation and further research are essential to verify the propositions and provide actionable insights for developing CPS expertise.
