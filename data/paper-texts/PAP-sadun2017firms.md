# Why Do Firms Have Different Spans of Hierarchy?

**Author:** Raffaella Sadun (Harvard Business School)
**Year:** 2017
**Type:** Journal Article
**Archived Date:** 2026-02-24
**Content Level:** L2 (Comprehensive structured summary with S1-S6)

---

## Abstract

This paper examines the determinants of organizational hierarchy — specifically, why firms in similar industries and of similar size can have very different numbers of hierarchical levels and spans of control. Drawing on the organizational economics literature (Garicano 2000, Rosen 1982) and large-scale management survey data, the paper documents substantial variation in hierarchy spans across firms and countries. The analysis shows that management practices, information technology adoption, and human capital quality are key determinants of hierarchical structure, with complementarities between these factors shaping the optimal organizational form.

---

## 1. Introduction

One of the most visible features of organizational design is hierarchy — the number of levels from the CEO to the front-line worker and the span of control at each level. Despite its importance, there is limited systematic evidence on why firms choose different hierarchical structures.

### Research Questions

1. **Variation:** How much variation exists in hierarchical structure across firms in similar industries and of similar size?
2. **Determinants:** What firm-level and environmental factors explain this variation?
3. **Complementarities:** How do interactions between management practices, IT, and human capital shape optimal hierarchy?

---

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Knowledge-Based Hierarchy (Garicano 2000)

The paper builds on Garicano's (2000) model of knowledge-based hierarchy, where:
- Workers encounter problems of varying difficulty
- Simple problems are solved at the front line
- Complex problems are referred upward to specialists with more knowledge
- The number of hierarchical levels depends on the distribution of problem complexity and the cost of knowledge acquisition

### 2.2 Authority and Span of Control (Rosen 1982)

Rosen's (1982) framework models the allocation of talent across hierarchical levels:
- More talented managers supervise more subordinates (wider span of control)
- Talent at the top has a multiplicative effect on organizational output
- The optimal number of levels balances the benefits of specialization against communication costs

### 2.3 Complementarities in Organizational Design

The paper emphasizes that hierarchical structure is not chosen in isolation but is part of a system of complementary organizational choices:
- **Management practices + IT:** Better management enables effective use of IT, which in turn supports flatter hierarchies
- **Human capital + delegation:** Higher-skilled workers can handle more complex problems, reducing the need for hierarchical referral
- **IT + span of control:** Information technology allows managers to monitor more subordinates, enabling wider spans

---

## 3. Data and Methodology

### 3.1 World Management Survey

The paper draws on data from the World Management Survey (Bloom and Van Reenen 2007), which provides:
- Standardized management practice scores (18 practices, scored 1-5)
- Organizational structure data (number of levels, span of control)
- Firm characteristics (size, industry, ownership, country)
- Sample spanning multiple countries and industries

### 3.2 Empirical Approach

The analysis combines:
- Cross-sectional regressions of hierarchy measures on firm characteristics
- Instrumental variable strategies to address endogeneity of management practices
- Cross-country comparisons to exploit institutional variation

---

## 4. Main Results

### 4.1 Substantial Variation in Hierarchy

Even among firms of similar size and in the same industry, there is substantial variation in:
- Number of hierarchical levels (ranging from 3 to 10+ for medium-sized firms)
- Span of control at each level
- Degree of decentralization in decision-making

### 4.2 Management Practices as Determinant

Better management practices are associated with:
- Flatter hierarchies (fewer levels)
- Wider spans of control
- More delegation of decision-making

This is consistent with the Garicano (2000) prediction: better management enables more efficient problem-solving at lower levels, reducing the need for hierarchical referral.

### 4.3 Complementarity Effects

The paper documents significant complementarities:
- Firms with both high management quality AND high IT adoption have the flattest hierarchies
- The effect of IT on hierarchy depends on management quality (IT alone does not flatten hierarchy)
- Human capital amplifies the effect of both management practices and IT on organizational structure

### 4.4 Cross-Country Patterns

Countries with higher average management quality (US, Germany, Japan) tend to have:
- Flatter organizational structures
- Wider spans of control
- More decentralized decision-making

Countries with lower average management quality but rapid IT adoption show inconsistent patterns, suggesting that IT adoption without complementary management improvements may not optimize organizational structure.

---

## 5. Implications

### 5.1 For Organizational Design

The results suggest that organizational restructuring (flattening hierarchies, widening spans of control) is most effective when accompanied by complementary investments in management practices and human capital.

### 5.2 For the FA-SM-1.1 Model

The finding that management practices, IT, and human capital are complements in determining organizational structure supports the broader complementarity framework. Hierarchical structure is an outcome of the interaction between multiple strategic choice variables, not an independent design parameter.
