## Abstract

This chapter introduces National Time Accounting (NTA)—a systematic approach to measuring how people spend their time and how they experience the activities that fill their days. We propose the U-index (the proportion of time an individual spends in an unpleasant state) as the key measure for tracking population well-being over time and across groups. Drawing on the Day Reconstruction Method (DRM) and the Princeton Affect and Time Survey (PATS), we provide extensive empirical evidence on the distribution of time use and emotional experience across activities, demographic groups, and nations. The approach focuses on experienced utility rather than life satisfaction, providing a more objective basis for welfare comparisons.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem with National Accounts

National Income Accounts (GDP, etc.) measure economic production but not well-being. A full accounting of societal progress requires measuring how people actually experience their lives.

**Key Insight:** Time use data combined with affective experience ratings provide a systematic framework for measuring experienced well-being at the population level.

### 1.2 National Time Accounting Framework

**Definition:** National Time Accounting (NTA) systematically tracks:
1. How people allocate time across activities
2. How they experience those activities emotionally
3. Changes in experienced well-being over time and across groups

**Analogy to National Income Accounts:**
- Just as GDP tracks the value of goods and services
- NTA tracks the experienced quality of time

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1 Process Benefits (Juster, 1985)

**Definition:** The intrinsic satisfaction derived from engaging in an activity, independent of the products produced.

**Key Distinction:**
- **Product outputs:** Goods and services (measured by National Income Accounts)
- **Process benefits:** The experience of engaging in the activity itself

**Example:**
- Cooking dinner: Product = meal, Process benefit = enjoyment (or stress) of cooking
- Commuting: Product = transportation, Process benefit = frustration or relaxation

### 2.2 Process Benefits vs. Income

**Juster's Finding:** Process benefits are only weakly related to income:
- Correlation between process benefits and family income: r ≈ 0.1
- Many high-income activities have low process benefits (e.g., work meetings)
- Many low-income activities have high process benefits (e.g., socializing)

### 2.3 Four Approaches to Measuring Process Benefits

| Approach | Measure | Strength | Weakness |
|----------|---------|----------|----------|
| **Revealed preference** | Time allocation | Behavioral | Ignores constraints |
| **Life satisfaction** | Global retrospective | Easy to collect | Memory biases |
| **Experience sampling** | Real-time affect | Gold standard | Intrusive, expensive |
| **Day reconstruction** | Systematic recall | Practical, detailed | Still involves memory |

## 3. Psychology of Well-Being

### 3.1 Two Distinct Constructs

**Experienced Happiness (Affective Well-Being):**
- Moment-to-moment emotional states
- Measured through ESM, DRM, or real-time tracking
- Related to hedonic experiences

**Life Satisfaction (Evaluative Well-Being):**
- Global judgment about one's life
- Measured through survey questions ("How satisfied are you with your life?")
- Related to achievement and social comparison

### 3.2 Empirical Distinction

**Key Finding:** These constructs are related but distinct:
- Correlation between life satisfaction and net affect: r = 0.38 (PATS national sample)
- Some groups score high on one but low on the other

**Example:**
- Parents with young children: Lower momentary happiness, higher life satisfaction
- Retirees: Higher momentary happiness, variable life satisfaction

### 3.3 The Focusing Illusion

> "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it." — Kahneman

**Implication:** Life satisfaction judgments are heavily influenced by whatever is salient at the moment of assessment, introducing systematic biases.

## 4. The U-Index

### 4.1 Definition

**U-index:** The proportion of time during which the most intense feeling is a negative emotion.

**Calculation:**
For each episode, identify the dominant emotion (highest rating). If that emotion is negative (e.g., stressed, sad, annoyed), the episode contributes to U-time.

$$U = \frac{\sum (\text{Time when max emotion is negative})}{\sum (\text{Total time})}$$

### 4.2 Advantages of U-Index

1. **Ordinal measurement:** Only requires ranking emotions, not cardinal comparisons
2. **Interpersonal comparability:** Avoids scale use differences across people
3. **Policy relevance:** Directly tracks "misery time"
4. **Robust:** Less sensitive to response styles than mean affect ratings

### 4.3 Activity U-Index Rankings

| Activity | U-Index (%) | Time/Day |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Intimate relations | 4.7 | 12 min |
| Socializing (friends) | 9.0 | 65 min |
| Relaxing | 10.8 | 127 min |
| Watching TV | 12.1 | 137 min |
| Eating | 12.6 | 78 min |
| Exercising | 12.8 | 16 min |
| Praying/worship | 11.5 | 10 min |
| Shopping | 15.2 | 24 min |

| Food preparation | 16.0 | 39 min |

| Commuting | 27.1 | 38 min |

**Key Insight:** Work and commuting have the highest U-index (most unpleasant), while intimate relations and socializing have the lowest.

## 5. Survey Methods

### 5.1 Experience Sampling Method (ESM)

**Protocol:**
- Participants carry electronic devices
- Random signals throughout the day
- Report current activity and feelings

**Advantages:**
- Real-time measurement
- Avoids memory biases

**Disadvantages:**
- Intrusive
- Expensive
- Reactive (changes behavior)

### 5.2 Day Reconstruction Method (DRM)

**Protocol:**
1. Participants complete diary the day after target day
2. Reconstruct day as series of episodes
3. For each episode: activity, duration, companions, location
4. Rate emotional experience during each episode

**Affect Dimensions (0-6 scale):**
- Positive: Happy, Warm/friendly, Enjoying myself
- Negative: Frustrated/annoyed, Depressed/blue, Hassled/pushed, Angry/hostile, Worried/anxious, Criticized/put down

**Advantages:**
- Detailed time use data
- Practical for population surveys
- Good agreement with ESM

### 5.3 Princeton Affect and Time Survey (PATS)

**Design:**
- Telephone survey + mailed DRM diary
- N = 3,982 nationally representative US sample
- Extensive validation studies

**Key Variables:**
- Time use across 22 activity categories
- Affect ratings for each episode
- Demographics and life circumstances

## 6. Validation Evidence

### 6.1 DRM-ESM Comparison

**Finding:** DRM and ESM produce highly similar results:
- Correlation between mean affect ratings: r > 0.90
- Activity rankings nearly identical
- Episode-level correlations: r ≈ 0.60

### 6.2 Affect-Behavior Correlations

**Net Affect and Time Allocation:**
- Activities with higher net affect receive more discretionary time
- Correlation between activity enjoyment and time spent: r = 0.35

**Net Affect and Activity Choice:**
- People who enjoy an activity more spend more time on it
- Individual-level correlations support validity

### 6.3 Cortisol Validation

**Finding:** Self-reported stress correlates with salivary cortisol:
- Higher reported stress → Higher cortisol levels
- Validates biological basis of self-reports

### 6.4 Life Satisfaction Correlations

**Net Affect and Life Satisfaction:**
- Texas DRM: r = 0.44
- PATS national: r = 0.35

**Interpretation:** Related but distinct constructs, supporting two-track model of well-being.

## 7. Results: Well-Being Across Groups

### 7.1 Demographic Patterns

**Age:**
- U-index declines with age (older people report less unpleasant time)
- Young people (15-24): Higher U-index
- Elderly (65+): Lower U-index

**Gender:**
- Women: Slightly higher U-index than men
- But women also report higher positive affect
- Greater emotional amplitude

**Income:**
- Higher income → Lower U-index
- But effect is modest (diminishing returns)
- Income matters more for activities (what you do) than for experience during activities

**Education:**
- Higher education → Lower U-index
- But weaker effect than might be expected

**Marital Status:**
- Married: Lower U-index than divorced/separated
- Widowed: Higher U-index

### 7.2 Activity Patterns

**Lowest U-Index Activities:**
1. Intimate relations (4.7%)
2. Socializing with friends (9.0%)
3. Relaxing (10.8%)
4. Playing with children (11.0%)

**Highest U-Index Activities:**
1. Commuting (27.1%)
2. Working (24.8%)
3. Computer/email (19.5%)
4. Housework (16.8%)

**Key Insight:** Social activities dominate the positive end; work-related activities dominate the negative end.

## 8. Cluster Analysis of Activities

### 8.1 Six Activity Clusters

Based on emotional profiles, activities cluster into six groups:

| Cluster | Activities | Emotional Profile |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **1. Work** | Working, commuting | Low positive, high negative |
| **2. Obligatory home** | Housework, childcare, shopping | Moderate on all |
| **3. Passive leisure** | TV, resting, computer | Moderate positive, low engagement |
| **4. Active leisure** | Exercise, hobbies, worship | High positive, high engagement |
| **5. Social** | Socializing, intimate relations | High positive, low negative |
| **6. Eating** | Eating meals | Moderate positive, low negative |

### 8.2 Dimensional Structure

Factor analysis reveals two primary dimensions:
1. **Pleasantness:** Degree of positive affect
2. **Engagement:** Level of absorption/flow

**Circumplex Pattern:** Activities map onto Russell's circumplex model of affect (valence × arousal).

## 9. Historical Trends (1965-2005)

### 9.1 Changes in Time Allocation

Using data from American Time Use Surveys (1965, 1975, 1985, 1993, 2003-2005):

**Increased Time:**
- Television: +58 minutes/day
- Computer/internet: +42 minutes/day (new category)

**Decreased Time:**
- Market work (women): -84 minutes/day
- Housework (women): -96 minutes/day

### 9.2 Computed U-Index Trends

**Method:** Apply current U-index values to historical time allocations.

**Results:**
- Women: U-index declined from 21.7% (1965) to 18.8% (2005)
- Men: U-index relatively stable (18-19%)
- Gender gap narrowed substantially

**Interpretation:** Women's shift away from housework toward leisure improved their experienced well-being.

## 10. International Comparison: US vs. France

### 10.1 Data Sources

- US: PATS (N = 3,982)
- France: Columbus/Rennes DRM (N = 820)

### 10.2 Key Findings

**Overall U-Index:**
- United States: 18.8%
- France: 16.0%

**Activity-Specific Patterns:**
- Americans spend more time in high-U activities (work, commuting)
- French spend more time in low-U activities (meals, socializing)

**Time Allocation Differences:**
| Activity | US (min/day) | France (min/day) |
|----------|--------------|------------------|
| Work | 328 | 280 |
| Commuting | 38 | 28 |
| Meals | 65 | 135 |
| Socializing | 65 | 82 |

### 10.3 Decomposition Analysis

**Question:** Is the US-France U-index difference due to:
1. Different activities (what people do)?
2. Different experiences (how activities feel)?

**Finding:** 90% of the difference is due to time allocation (Americans do more unpleasant activities), not to within-activity experience differences.

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **Experienced ≠ Evaluative Well-Being:**
   - Net affect and life satisfaction are distinct (r = 0.35-0.44)
   - Both matter for welfare; neither is sufficient alone
   - Policy implications differ for each construct

2. **U-Index as Ordinal Measure:**
   - Avoids cardinal utility assumptions
   - Enables interpersonal comparisons
   - Robust to response style differences

3. **Process Benefits Framework:**
   - Activities have intrinsic experiential value
   - Not captured by market transactions
   - Time allocation reveals constraints + preferences

4. **Time as Currency:**
   - Finite resource (24 hours/day)
   - Zero-sum tradeoffs between activities
   - Changes in allocation affect aggregate well-being

5. **Cross-Cultural Differences:**
   - Same activities feel similar across countries
   - Differences driven by what people do, not how it feels
   - Time allocation is the policy lever

## Appendix: DRM Affect Items

### Positive Affect (PA)
- Happy
- Warm/friendly
- Enjoying myself

### Negative Affect (NA)
- Frustrated/annoyed
- Depressed/blue
- Hassled/pushed around
- Angry/hostile
- Worried/anxious
- Criticized/put down

### Net Affect Calculation
$$\text{Net Affect} = \frac{\sum PA - \sum NA}{6}$$

Scaled 0-6 for each dimension, averaged across episodes.

### U-Index Calculation
For each episode:
1. Identify maximum affect rating across all emotions
2. If maximum is a negative emotion → Episode is "unpleasant"
3. Weight by duration

$$U = \frac{\sum_{i} t_i \cdot \mathbf{1}[\max(\text{affect}_i) \in \text{negative}]}{\sum_{i} t_i}$$
