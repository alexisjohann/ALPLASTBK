# One Year of America First 2.0: Assessing the Effects of Trump's Trade Policies

**Author:** Harald Oberhofer (Vienna University of Economics and Business; Austrian Institute of Economic Research / WIFO)
**Publication:** FIW Policy Brief No. 72, January 2026
**Publisher:** FIW - Research Centre International Economics
**Type:** Policy Brief
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

This policy brief evaluates the trade policy of Donald Trump during the first year of his second
presidential term. The administration pursued a strongly protectionist agenda, implementing new
tariffs on a wide range of products and trading partners, including long-standing allies (EU, Canada,
Mexico) and strategic competitors (China). The brief documents a substantial increase in U.S.
economic policy uncertainty (EPU), reaching historical highs in 2025. Short-run economic effects
include USD depreciation (~12% vs EUR), reduced import volumes, and modest consumer price
pass-through adding approximately +0.7 percentage points to U.S. inflation. For Austria, effective
U.S. import tariffs of ~12.8% caused a sharp decline in exports to the U.S. from April 2025 onwards.

## Key Findings

### 1. Evolution of Trump's Tariffs (Two Legal Pillars)

**Pillar 1 — IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act 1977):**
- Country-specific tariffs justified by declared "national emergency" (trade deficits)
- Legally controversial — U.S. Supreme Court review pending
- Used against Canada, Mexico, EU, and others

**Pillar 2 — Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act 1962):**
- Industry-specific tariffs on "national security" grounds
- Pharmaceuticals: 100% tariff
- Steel and aluminum: 50%
- Automobiles and auto parts: 25% (later reduced to 15% for EU deal)
- Lumber and furniture: 10-25%

**Timeline of effective tariff rates:**
- Jan 2025: ~2% (stable baseline)
- March 2025: ~6% (first executive orders — China, Canada, Mexico)
- April 2 "Liberation Day": ~18% (reciprocal tariffs for all partners, 10% universal minimum)
- April 9: Pause on tariffs above 10% (except China → 145%)
- May: US-China 90-day truce (combined 30% on China)
- June-July: Below 14% (USMCA exemptions + UK deal)
- Late July: EU (15%), Japan (15%), South Korea (15%) deals
- November: Stabilized at ~15% (after Busan Agreement reduced China by 7.5pp)

### 2. Economic Policy Uncertainty — Historical Highs

**EPU Index (Baker, Bloom & Davis 2016):**
- January 2025: Elevated (post-election appreciation phase)
- April 2025: ALL-TIME HIGH of 725 (Liberation Day)
  - Higher than COVID peak (~500) and Global Financial Crisis
- November 2025: Declined to ~325 after trade deals
  - STILL higher than almost all months since 1995

**Trade Policy Uncertainty (TPU):**
- Mirrors EPU pattern — historic highs in April 2025
- Despite decline, remains above all pre-Trump 2.0 levels
- Persistent uncertainty due to: legally non-binding deals + Supreme Court review pending

**Effects of uncertainty:**
- Baker et al. (2016): negative association with firm investment and employment
- Kumar et al. (2023): causal negative effects on firms' real economic activity
- Sebbesen & Oberhofer (2024): firm beliefs transmitted through international I-O linkages
- Gréus et al. (2025): survey of 700 economists → expected cumulative GDP effect:
  - EU: -0.6% over 5 years
  - U.S.: -0.8% over 5 years
  - Firm investment: -4.7% (EU), -6.7% (U.S.)

### 3. U.S. Dollar Depreciation

**January → November 2025 exchange rate movements:**
- EUR: -12% (Dollar depreciated)
- CHF: -12%
- GBP: -7%
- CNY: -2.7%
- JPY: -1.2%

**Theoretical puzzle:**
- Standard theory: tariffs → trade balance improvement → currency APPRECIATION
- Reality: DEPRECIATION — driven by:
  1. Higher production costs for import-dependent firms
  2. Uncertainty → capital flight to safe havens (CHF, JPY)
  3. Retaliatory tariffs worsening trade balance
  4. Market expectations of inflation + reduced foreign investment

**EUR impact on Austrian exports:**
- Glocker et al. (2025): 10% real appreciation → Austrian exports -2.7% (CPI-deflated) to -4.6% (PPI-deflated)
- Ertl & Wende (2025): Austrian export exposure to exchange rate risk relatively limited

### 4. U.S. Import Effects

**China:**
- Imports dropped to <50% of January 2021 level by June 2025
- Partial recovery but remained below 2/3 of January 2025 level

**World/EU/Austria:**
- March 2025: one-time surge (frontloading/stockpiling)
  - EU imports: +80% spike
  - Austrian exports to U.S.: +35% spike
- April-August 2025: imports fell below January 2025 levels
- Austrian exports to U.S. by August 2025: back to September 2021 levels

### 5. U.S. Inflation Effect

**Cavallo et al. (2025) — NBER Working Paper:**
- High-frequency retail microdata linked to tariff rates and country of origin
- Retail tariff pass-through: ~20%
- Aggregate CPI effect: **+0.7 percentage points by September 2025**
- Without tariffs: CPI would have been ~2.3% (near Fed target)
- With tariffs: CPI was ~3.0% in September 2025

**Goods vs. Services (OECD 2025):**
- Share of goods with CPI >2%: rose from ~25% to ~35% in U.S. (2025)
- Share of services with CPI >2%: stable, U.S. consistently below Euro area
- Key insight: tariffed GOODS drove inflation, not non-tariffed services

### 6. Austrian Export Exposure

**Effective U.S. tariff rates on Austrian exports: ~12.8%**
- Higher than Germany (11.7%) — composition effect
- Germany benefits from reduced auto tariffs (25% → 15%)
- Austrian car parts: indirect benefit through German supply chain
- Comparable to Spain (12.4%) and Italy (12.3%)

**GDP effects (Wolfmayr et al. 2025):**
- 10% export decline to U.S. → Austrian GDP: -0.4%
- ~1/3 from indirect effects (global value chain integration)
- Ireland most affected: -1.6% GDP

**Austrian export shares to U.S.:**
- 2024: 8.5% (historic high — U.S. overtook Italy as #2 destination)
- Jan-Sep 2025: 6.7% (sharp decline after tariffs)
- Italy likely re-emerges as #2 after Germany for 2025

**Sectoral vulnerability (Schneider & Sellner 2025):**
- Pharmaceuticals: strongest GDP effects
- Automotive (cars + parts): second strongest
- Heterogeneity across manufacturing industries

### 7. Policy Recommendations

**Trade policy instruments:**
1. Deeper plurilateral cooperation with like-minded economies (Felbermayr et al. 2026)
   - Could FULLY offset EU losses from U.S. tariffs if ambitious enough
2. Reduce non-tariff barriers within WTO framework (Baur & Flach 2025)
   - U.S. = only 13% of global merchandise trade → 87% remains rules-based
3. Avoid multilateralizing protectionism through safeguard tariffs

**Competitiveness instruments:**
4. Implement Draghi (2024) + Letta (2024) recommendations for EU Single Market
5. Strengthen domestic resilience to reduce vulnerability to external shocks

**Historical perspective (Känzig & den Besten 2025):**
- 185 years of data: after unexpected tariffs →
  - Imports fall sharply (observed in 2025 ✓)
  - Exports decline WITH A LAG (expected for 2026)
  - Output and manufacturing production persistently reduced
- U.S. effective tariff (~14%) may be near "unilateral optimal" rate (~19%, Ignatenko et al. 2025)
  - enabled by trade partners NOT retaliating
