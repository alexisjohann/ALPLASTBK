# Identifikation der Inflationstreiber in Österreich im Vergleich zum Euroraum

**Authors:** Monika Köppl-Turyna, Wolfgang Schwarzbauer (EcoAustria)
**Commissioned by:** Bundesministerium für Finanzen (BMF), Österreich
**Scientific Assistance:** Emilie Höslinger
**Date:** Juni 2023, Update Juli 2023
**Type:** Institutional Policy Study (Auftragsstudie)
**URL:** www.ecoaustria.ac.at
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Executive Summary

End of 2022 and beginning of 2023 saw a strong inflation increase in Austria, reaching levels
last seen in June 1974. Austria's inflation rate was in the upper midfield of the Euro area. An
emerging inflation differential to Germany and the Euro area average raised concerns about
competitiveness losses. This study analyzes causes of the strong increase and reasons for the
inflation differential to Germany and the Euro area.

## Methodology

### Part 1: HVPI Component Analysis
- Decomposition of HVPI (Harmonized Index of Consumer Prices) into COICOP categories
- Cross-country comparison: Austria vs. Germany, France, Spain, Euro area
- Analysis at 1-digit, 3-digit, and 4-digit COICOP level
- HVPI weight differences across countries (Inlands-Konzept vs. Inländer-Konzept)
- Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to identify structural inflation dynamics

### Part 2: Supply-Demand Decomposition (Shapiro Method)
- Two variants applied:
  1. **Shapiro (2022b) — Dynamic labelling**: Rolling regression residuals identify supply/demand
     each quarter. Uses SUR estimator with 24-quarter rolling window.
  2. **Shapiro (2022a) — Corona shock identification**: Uses COVID lockdowns as exogenous shock
     to classify COICOP categories as supply- or demand-sensitive (static assignment).
- Data: Monthly HICP prices + quarterly turnover data (Eurostat business statistics)
- 23 of 38 COICOP 2-digit categories could be matched to turnover data
- Comparison with Schiman-Vukan (2023) and Gonçalves & Koester (2022)

### Part 3: VAR Model (Wages-Inflation-GDP)
- 3-variable VAR: GDP growth, CPI inflation, collective wage index (Tariflohnindex)
- Exogenous variables: world trade volume (CPB), HWWI commodity price index, ECB main
  refinancing rate
- Dummy variables: Financial crisis 2009, COVID 2020:Q2-2021:Q4, Russia-Ukraine 2022:Q2-Q4
- Cholesky identification: wage shock → contemporaneous effect on inflation and GDP
- Sample: 2001:Q1 - 2022:Q4 (quarterly data)
- Optimal lag length: 5 quarters (BIC/Hannan-Quinn criteria)

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Austria's Inflation Systematically Exceeds Euro Area
- Austria exceeded Euro area inflation by 0.6pp average (2011-2019)
- In 2020: differential was 1.1pp (even during COVID deflation elsewhere)
- The current differential is NOT new — structural factors persist
- Categories with high pre-COVID price levels also showed strongest post-COVID price growth

### Finding 2: Restaurants/Hotels and Housing/Energy Drive Austrian Differential
- Restaurants & Hotels: HVPI weight 5pp above Euro area average (tourism economy)
- Housing & Energy: pronounced price growth vs. Euro area
- Food prices: actually BELOW Euro area contribution (4pp lower weight)
- Key subcategories:
  - Electricity/Gas: up to 3pp inflation contribution (1/3 of total)
  - Fuel: persistent 2pp contribution (more persistent in Austria than elsewhere)
  - Catering (restaurants, cafés): 1.6pp contribution (already 0.4pp in 2020 = 1/3 of total)

### Finding 3: Energy Price Shock Arrived Later in Austria but Persisted Longer
- Spain: energy price increases already in early 2021
- Germany, Austria: major impact only after Ukraine invasion (February 2022)
- Austria: delayed transmission due to contract structures (Befristungen)
- Austria: more persistent elevated energy prices than other Euro area countries
- Gas and district heating: additional price increase in January 2023

### Finding 4: HVPI Weights Explain Part but Not All of Differential
- Austria: Restaurants/Hotels weight +5pp vs. Euro area (tourism)
- Austria: Food weight -4pp vs. Euro area
- HVPI uses "Inlands-Konzept" (all domestic consumption incl. tourists)
- VPI uses "Inländer-Konzept" (resident consumption only)
- Fixed 2019 weights → only marginally lower inflation (not explanatory)
- Weight differences reflect genuine consumer preference differences

### Finding 5: Supply-Demand Decomposition (Shapiro Method)
- **Dynamic labelling (Shapiro 2022b):**
  - Euro area: supply shock earlier and stronger than Austria
  - Austria: supply shock arrived post-Ukraine invasion, demand-driven before
  - Core inflation: largely demand-driven in both Austria and Euro area
  - Austria Jan/Feb 2023: slower inflation decline due to (a) centralized wage bargaining
    in January and (b) delayed gas/district heating price increases

- **Corona shock identification (Shapiro 2022a):**
  - Pre-Ukraine (March 2022): demand component = 70% Euro area, >50% Austria
  - Through 2022: supply component increased, demand share fell to ~50% both regions
  - Austria Jan/Feb 2023: demand contribution 7pp higher than Euro area
  - Hotels classification ambiguity: demand-classified in Euro area but "unclear" in Austria
    (prices did not fall during COVID despite lockdowns — key anomaly)

- **Schiman-Vukan (2023) comparison:**
  - Confirms results qualitatively and quantitatively
  - Demand pressure peaked October 2022 at ~4pp for both Austria and Euro area
  - Supply shock in Austria: later onset but longer persistence (energy market specificities)
  - Current differential: ~50% supply, ~50% demand, plus January wage shock

### Finding 6: PCA Analysis — Four Distinct Inflation Regimes
- Pre-COVID (2018:1-2020:3): stable, moderate inflation
- COVID (2020:4-2021:1): deflationary shock
- Post-COVID (2021:2-2022:2): recovery-driven inflation
- Russia-Ukraine (2022:3-2023:4): distinct from all prior periods
- Austria: more overlap between periods (structural inflation persistence)
- Spain: least overlap (most distinct phase transitions)

### Finding 7: Wage-Inflation Nexus (VAR Results)
- **Wage shock → Inflation:**
  - 1% wage increase → 0.59% price increase after 4 quarters
  - 1% wage increase → 0.50% price increase after 8 quarters
  - 1% wage increase → 0.30% price increase after 12 quarters
  - Higher than Schneider & Stiglbauer (2022) estimate of 0.3%

- **Wage shock → GDP:**
  - Initial positive GDP effect (+0.37pp from consumption demand)
  - Turns negative after ~2 quarters (cost pressure + competitiveness loss)
  - After 8 quarters: cumulative GDP effect = -0.4pp

- **Scenario comparison (€1bn):**
  - Transfers: GDP +0.2pp in year 1, +0.12pp in year 2; inflation effect negligible
  - Wages: GDP +0.08pp in year 1, NEGATIVE in year 2; inflation +0.24pp in year 1
  - Transfers are superior to wage increases for compensating inflation

- **Wage scenarios for 2024 inflation:**
  - 10% nominal wages → VPI inflation 4.7% (vs. 3.8% baseline)
  - 8.2% nominal wages (WIFO forecast) → 3.8% (baseline)
  - 6% nominal wages → ~2.7% inflation (significant dampening)

### Finding 8: Centralized Wage Bargaining Creates January Shock
- Austrian collective bargaining (Kollektivvertrag) concentrated in January
- Creates demand impulse concentrated in Q1
- Other Euro area countries: more dispersed wage adjustments
- Explains part of Austria's persistent Q1 inflation differential
