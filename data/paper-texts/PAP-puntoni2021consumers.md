## Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) helps companies offer important benefits to consumers,
such as health monitoring with wearable devices, advice with recommender systems,
peace of mind with smart household products, and convenience with voice-activated
virtual assistants. However, although AI can be seen as a neutral tool to be evaluated
on efficiency and accuracy, this approach does not consider the social and individual
challenges that can occur when AI is deployed. This research aims to bridge these two
perspectives: on one side, the authors acknowledge the value that embedding AI technology
into products and services can provide to consumers. On the other side, the authors build
on and integrate sociological and psychological scholarship to examine some of the costs
consumers experience in their interactions with AI. In doing so, the authors identify four
types of consumer experiences with AI: (1) data capture, (2) classification, (3) delegation,
and (4) social. This approach allows the authors to discuss policy and managerial avenues
to address the ways in which consumers may fail to experience value in organizations'
investments into AI and to lay out an agenda for future research.

## The Framework

### Four AI Capabilities → Four Consumer Experiences

| AI Capability | Consumer Experience | Benefits | Costs |
|---------------|--------------------| ---------|-------|
| **Listening** | Data Capture | Served (customized services) | Exploited (loss of control) |
| **Predicting** | Classification | Understood (relevant predictions) | Misunderstood (discrimination) |
| **Producing** | Delegation | Empowered (time savings) | Replaced (skill atrophy) |
| **Communicating** | Social | Connected (natural interaction) | Alienated (failed interaction) |

## Experience 1: Data Capture (Listening)

### Psychological Tension: Served ↔ Exploited

**Benefits (Served):**
- Customized services
- Convenience
- Health monitoring
- Personalization

**Costs (Exploited):**
- Loss of data ownership
- Loss of control
- Negative affect
- Moral outrage
- Psychological reactance

### Key Mechanisms
- **Personal control:** Data capture reduces perceived control
- **Psychological reactance:** Freedom threat → resistance
- **Negative affect:** Privacy invasion triggers negative emotions

### Case Example: Leila (Facebook PYMK)
Sex worker whose clients appeared in Facebook's "People You May Know" feature.
Privacy invasion can be life-threatening for vulnerable populations.

### Case Example: Danielle (Alexa)
Consumer whose Alexa recorded private conversation and sent to contact.
Reactance leads to product abandonment.

## Experience 2: Classification (Predicting)

### Psychological Tension: Understood ↔ Misunderstood

**Benefits (Understood):**
- Identity affirmation
- Relevant predictions
- Accurate recommendations
- Feeling "known"

**Costs (Misunderstood):**
- Incorrect classification
- Discriminatory use
- Stereotyping
- Identity threat

### Key Mechanisms
- **Identity-based marketing:** Classifications affect identity utility
- **Algorithmic discrimination:** Bias perpetuates inequality
- **Uniqueness vs. belonging:** Classification must balance

### Case Example: Spotify
Consumer frustrated by recommendations based on outdated preferences.
AI fails to capture multifaceted, evolving self.

### Case Example: Apple Card
David Heinemeier Hansson's wife received 20x lower credit limit despite
joint finances. Algorithmic discrimination triggers moral outrage.

### Case Example: Amazon Rekognition
Incorrectly matched 28 Congress members with mugshots.
Facial recognition disproportionately fails for people of color.

## Experience 3: Delegation (Producing)

### Psychological Tension: Empowered ↔ Replaced

**Benefits (Empowered):**
- Time savings
- Focus on meaningful tasks
- Efficiency gains
- Convenience

**Costs (Replaced):**
- Loss of self-efficacy
- Skill atrophy
- Reduced internal attribution
- Identity threat (for identity-relevant tasks)

### Key Mechanisms
- **Self-efficacy:** Delegation may reduce sense of competence
- **Identity-relevant consumption:** Some tasks tied to self-concept
- **Internal attribution:** AI assistance reduces credit to self

### Case Example: Smart Compose (John Seabrook)
Journalist accepting AI writing suggestion "proud" instead of "pleased."
Concerns about skill atrophy and authentic self-expression.

---

## Experience 4: Social (Communicating)

### Psychological Tension: Connected ↔ Alienated

**Benefits (Connected):**
- Natural interaction
- Conversational commerce
- 24/7 availability
- Anthropomorphic engagement

**Costs (Alienated):**
- Failed interactions
- Stereotype reinforcement
- Objectification
- Exclusion of vulnerable populations

### Key Mechanisms
- **Anthropomorphism:** Treating AI as human
- **Gender bias in AI:** Female-voiced assistants as servants
- **Objectification:** AI reinforces problematic norms

### Case Example: Danny (Universal Credit)
Man with learning disabilities struggling with automated benefit system.
AI can exclude vulnerable populations from essential services.

### Case Example: Siri
Responding to "you're a slut" with "I'd blush if I could."
AI can reinforce gender stereotypes and normalize harassment.

## Research Agenda (40+ Questions)

### Data Capture (10 questions)
- RQA1: How does socioeconomic status influence feeling exploited?
- RQA4: How does AI diffusion affect exploitation feelings over time?
- RQA10: When will consumers sabotage data collection?

### Classification (6 questions)
- RQB1: How do individual differences in discrimination awareness affect misunderstanding?
- RQB5: How do uniqueness vs belonging motives affect misunderstanding?

### Delegation (8 questions)
- RQC1: How do feelings of replacement depend on perceived humanness of activity?
- RQC6: How do instrumental vs symbolic motives determine replacement perceptions?

### Social (7 questions)
- RQD1: How do antibias beliefs affect alienation in social experiences?
- RQD5: How does timing of AI disclosure influence alienation?

### Interrelationships (6 questions)
- RQE1: How do data capture experiences influence classification experiences?
- RQE4: Do changes in control lead to parallel shifts in data capture and delegation?

### Uncharted Territories (6 questions)
- RQF1: How does learner-AI interaction shape learning experiences?
- RQF3: What motivates AI-enabled companionship experiences?

## Policy and Managerial Implications

### For Companies
1. Consider experiential costs, not just efficiency gains
2. Design for vulnerable populations
3. Maintain human alternatives for essential services
4. Be transparent about AI use and data practices

### For Policy Makers
1. Regulate data capture with privacy protections
2. Audit AI systems for discrimination
3. Require accessibility standards for AI interfaces
4. Mandate disclosure of AI in consumer interactions
