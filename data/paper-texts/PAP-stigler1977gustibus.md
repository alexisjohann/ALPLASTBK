## Abstract

The venerable admonition not to quarrel over tastes is commonly interpreted as advice to terminate a dispute when it has been resolved into a difference of tastes, presumably because there is no further room for rational persuasion. This paper proposes a different interpretation: that tastes neither change capriciously nor differ importantly between people. On this interpretation, one does not argue over tastes for the same reason that one does not argue over the Rocky Mountains—both are there, will be there next year, too, and are the same to all men. The paper demonstrates that this approach, combined with household production theory, can explain addiction, custom and tradition, advertising effects, and fashion without assuming that tastes change.

## I. Introduction

The venerable admonition not to quarrel over tastes is commonly interpreted as advice to terminate a dispute when it has been resolved into a difference of tastes, presumably because there is no further room for rational persuasion. Tastes are the unchallengeable axioms of a man's behavior: he may properly (usefully) be criticized for inefficiency in satisfying his desires, but the desires themselves are data. Deplorable tastes—say, for arson—may be countered by coercive and punitive action, but these deplorable tastes, at least when held by an adult, are not capable of being changed by persuasion.

Our title seems to us to be capable of another and preferable interpretation: that tastes neither change capriciously nor differ importantly between people. On this interpretation one does not argue over tastes for the same reason that one does not argue over the Rocky Mountains—both are there, will be there next year, too, and are the same to all men.

The difference between these two viewpoints of tastes is fundamental. On the traditional view, an explanation of economic phenomena that reaches a difference in tastes between people or times is the terminus of the argument: the problem is abandoned at this point to whoever studies and explains tastes (psychologists? anthropologists? phrenologists? sociobiologists?). On our preferred interpretation, one never reaches this impasse: the economist continues to search for differences in prices or incomes to explain any differences or changes in behavior.

The choice between these two views of the role of tastes in economic theory must ultimately be made on the basis of their comparative analytical productivities. On the conventional view of inscrutable, often capricious tastes, one drops the discussion as soon as the behavior of tastes becomes important—and turns his energies to other problems. On our view, one searches, often long and frustratingly, for the subtle forms that prices and incomes take in explaining differences among men and periods. If the latter approach yields more useful results, it is the proper choice.

**The Central Thesis:**
We are proposing the hypothesis that widespread and/or persistent human behavior can be explained by a generalized calculus of utility-maximizing behavior, without introducing the qualification "tastes remaining the same."

## II. The New Theory of Consumer Choice

The power of stable preferences and utility maximization in explaining a wide range of behavior has been significantly enhanced by a recent reformulation of consumer theory. This reformulation transforms the family from a passive maximizer of the utility from market purchases into an active maximizer also engaged in extensive production and investment activities.

### The Household Production Function

In the traditional theory, households maximize a utility function of the goods and services bought in the marketplace, whereas in the reformulation they maximize a utility function of objects of choice, called commodities, that they produce with market goods, their own time, their skills, training and other human capital, and other inputs.

**Formally, a household seeks to maximize:**

$$U = U(Z_1, \ldots, Z_m)$$

with

$$Z_i = f_i(X_{1i}, \ldots, X_{ki}, t_{1i}, \ldots, t_{ei}, S_1, \ldots, S_j, Y_i)$$

where:
- $Z_i$ are the commodity objects of choice entering the utility function
- $f_i$ is the production function for the ith commodity
- $X_{ji}$ is the quantity of the jth market good or service used in the production of the ith commodity
- $t_{ji}$ is the jth person's own time input
- $S_j$ is the jth person's human capital
- $Y_i$ represents all other inputs

### Shadow Prices

The $Z_i$ have no market prices since they are not purchased or sold, but do have "shadow" prices determined by their costs of production. If $f_i$ were homogeneous of the first degree in the $X_{ji}$ and $t_{ji}$, marginal and average costs would be the same and the shadow price of $Z_i$ would be:

$$\pi_i = \sum_j a_{ji}(w, S, Y_i)p_j + \sum_j \beta_{ji}(w, S, Y_i)w_j$$

where $p_j$ is the cost of $X_j$, $w_j$ is the cost of $t_j$, and $a_{ji}$ and $\beta_{ji}$ are input-output coefficients that depend on the (relative) set of p and w, S, and $Y_i$.

**The numerous and varied determinants of these shadow prices give concrete expression to our earlier statement about the subtle forms that prices take in explaining differences among men and periods.**

## III. Stability of Tastes and "Addiction"

Tastes are frequently said to change as a result of consuming certain "addictive" goods. For example, smoking of cigarettes, drinking of alcohol, injection of heroin, or close contact with some persons over an appreciable period of time, often increases the desire (creates a craving) for these goods or persons, and thereby cause their consumption to grow over time.

### Marshall on Good Music

Alfred Marshall stated this clearly:

> "There is however an implicit condition in this law [of diminishing marginal utility] which should be made clear. It is that we do not suppose time to be allowed for any alteration in the character or tastes of the man himself. It is therefore no exception to the law that the more good music a man hears, the stronger is his taste for it likely to become..."

### Our Explanation: Consumption Capital

We believe that the phenomenon Marshall is trying to explain can be explained with some gain in insight by assuming constant tastes. The essence of our explanation lies in the accumulation of what might be termed "consumption capital" by the consumer.

**Beneficial Addiction:**

Consider an unchanging utility function:

$$U = U(M, Z)$$

where M measures the amount of music "appreciation" produced and consumed, and Z the production and consumption of other commodities.

Music appreciation is produced by:

$$M = M_m(t_m, S_m)$$

where $t_m$ is time allocated to music and $S_m$ is music human capital. Music capital is produced through "learning by doing":

$$S_{mj} = h(M_{j-1}, M_{j-2}, \ldots, E_j)$$

The addiction is **beneficial** if $\partial S_{mj}/\partial M_{j-v} > 0$ for all v.

**Key Result:** The shadow price of music appreciation tends to **decline** with age (at least at younger ages) because consumption capital grows with exposure. Thus, consumption of music appreciation rises with exposure not because tastes shift in favor of music, but because its shadow price falls as skill and experience are acquired.

### Harmful Addiction

For harmful addiction (heroin, alcohol), the derivatives are negative:

$$\partial S_{hj}/\partial H_{j-v} < 0$$

An increase in consumption reduces the stock of consumption capital available subsequently, **raising** the shadow price at all ages. The shadow price rises with age and exposure, inducing consumption to fall.

**Critical Insight:** If the demand curve for the commodity is sufficiently **inelastic**, the use of the good (heroin) would **grow** with exposure at the same time that the commodity (euphoria) **fell**.

> "The use of heroin would grow with exposure at the same time that the amount of euphoria fell, if the demand curve for euphoria and thus for heroin were sufficiently inelastic. That is, addiction to heroin—a growth in use with exposure—is the result of an inelastic demand for heroin, not, as commonly argued, the cause of an inelastic demand."

## IV. Stability of Tastes and Custom and Tradition

A "traditional" qualification to the scope of economic theory is the alleged powerful hold over human behavior of custom and tradition.

### John Stuart Mill's View

Mill wrote:
> "It is not true that the actions even of average rulers are wholly, or anything approaching to wholly, determined by their personal interest... The character and course of their actions is largely influenced (independently of personal calculations) by the habitual sentiments and feelings, the general modes of thinking and acting, which prevail throughout the community..."

### Our Explanation: Information Capital

Mill's "habits and modes of thought," or his "maxims and traditions which have descended," in our analysis result from **investment of time and other resources in the accumulation of knowledge about the environment, and of skills with which to cope with it**.

### The Economics of Search

The making of decisions is costly. In order to make a decision one requires information, and the information must be analyzed. The costs of searching for information and of applying the information to a new situation are such that habit is often a more efficient way to deal with moderate or temporary changes in the environment than would be a full, apparently utility-maximizing decision.

**Optimal Search Frequency:**

$$r^* = \sqrt{\frac{2C}{\delta \bar{p}}}$$

where r is purchases between searches, C is cost of search, δ is rate of price appreciation, and $\bar{p}$ is average price.

### Why Young and Old Respond Differently

When a permanent change in the environment occurs, young and old persons respond differently, even if they have the same preferences and motivation:

- **Older persons** have to either disinvest their capital attuned to the old environment, or invest in capital attuned to the new environment. Their incentive to do so may be weak because relatively few years remain to collect returns.

- **Young persons** are not encumbered by accumulations of capital attuned to the old environment. They have greater incentive to invest in knowledge and skills attuned to the new environment.

## V. Stability of Tastes and Advertising

Perhaps the most important class of cases in which "change of tastes" is invoked is advertising.

### Galbraith's View

John Kenneth Galbraith argued:
> "These [institutions of modern advertising and salesmanship] cannot be reconciled with the notion of independently determined desires for their central function is to create desires—to bring into being wants that previously did not exist."

### Our Explanation: Information Affects Shadow Prices

We argue that it is neither necessary nor useful to attribute to advertising the function of changing tastes. A consumer may indirectly receive utility from a market good, yet the utility depends not only on the quantity of the good but also the consumer's knowledge of its true or alleged properties.

**The Commodity Production Function:**

$$Z = g(A, E, y) \cdot x$$

where A is advertising, E is human capital, y are other variables, and x is the firm's output. With $\partial g/\partial A > 0$, advertising increases the commodity output from a given input of the product.

**The Shadow Price:**

$$\pi_z = \frac{p_x}{g}$$

An increase in advertising **lowers** the commodity price to the household (by raising g), thereby increasing demand for the commodity and the firm's output.

**Key Result:** Advertising affects consumption not by changing tastes, but by changing prices. A movement along a stable demand curve for commodities generates apparently unstable demand curves for market goods.

### Advertising and Competition

**Surprising result:** The marginal revenue from advertising is **directly related** to the elasticity of the commodity demand curve. Firms in perfectly competitive commodity markets may have **greater** incentive to advertise than firms with market power.

## VI. Fashions and Fads

The existence of fashions and fads seems an especially striking contradiction of our thesis of the stability of tastes.

### The Commodity: Social Distinction

The commodity apparently produced by fashion goods is **social distinction**: the demonstration of alert leadership, or at least not lethargy, in recognizing and adopting that which will in due time be widely approved.

**Formalization:**

$$R_i = D_i + h_i$$

where $R_i$ is i's social distinction, $D_i$ is the contribution of his social environment, and $h_i$ is his own contribution (fashion, charitable giving, prestigious occupations, etc.).

### Key Results

1. **High income elasticity:** Even an average income elasticity of demand for distinction implies a high income elasticity of demand for fashion goods.

2. **Conformity pressure:** When some gain distinction by paying attention to new fashions, they lower the social environment of others. The latter are induced to increase their own efforts, including demand for new fashions.

3. **Wealthy countries:** An increase in all incomes induces an even greater increase in distinction-seeking than an increase in one's own income alone. Hence wealthy countries pay more attention to fashion than poor countries, even with the same tastes.

## VII. Conclusion

We have surveyed four classes of phenomena widely believed to be inconsistent with the stability of tastes: addiction, habitual behavior, advertising, and fashions, and in each case offered an alternative explanation. That alternative explanation did not simply reconcile the phenomena with stability of tastes, but also sought to show that the hypothesis of stable tastes yielded more useful predictions about observable behavior.

**The Methodological Principle:**

> "We also claim, however, that no significant behavior has been illuminated by assumptions of differences in tastes. Instead, they, along with assumptions of unstable tastes, have been a convenient crutch to lean on when the analysis has bogged down. They give the appearance of considered judgement, yet really have only been ad hoc arguments that disguise analytical failures."

**The Great Advantage:**

> "The great advantage of relying only on changes in the arguments entering household production functions is that all changes in behavior are explained by changes in prices and incomes, precisely the variables that organize and give power to economic analysis."

**The Challenge:**

> "Our hypothesis is trivial, for it merely asserts that we should apply standard economic logic as extensively as possible. But the self-same hypothesis is also a demanding challenge, for it urges us not to abandon opaque and complicated problems with the easy suggestion that the further explanation will perhaps someday be produced by one of our sister behavioral sciences."
