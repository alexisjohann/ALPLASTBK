## Abstract

We review different streams of social science literature on privacy with the goal of understanding consumer privacy decision making and deriving implications for policy. We focus on psychological and economic factors influencing both consumers' desire and consumers' ability to protect their privacy, either through individual action or through the implementation of regulations applying to firms. Contrary to depictions of online sharing behaviors as careless, we show how consumers fundamentally care about online privacy, and present evidence of numerous actions they take to protect it. However, we also document how prohibitively difficult it is to attain desired, or even desirable, levels of privacy through individual action alone. The remaining instrument for privacy protection is policy intervention. However, again for both psychological and economic reasons, the collective impetus for adequate intervention is often countervailed by powerful interests that oppose it.

## I. Introduction

### The Cambridge Analytica Problem

In the summer of 2016, a Facebook app featuring a personality test funneled millions of consumers' personal data to political operatives for use in targeted ads intended to influence voting in the upcoming US presidential elections. When this scheme was revealed, outrage ensued. Many users who had filled out the test had not realized whom their data could be shared with or for what purpose. Yet, the threatened mass exodus from, and regulation of, social media never came. Four years later, little seems to have changed.

The Cambridge Analytica scandal encapsulates the modern privacy problem:
- Seemingly carefree online sharing behaviors
- Concerns with misuses of data, quickly forgotten
- Staggering—and yet so hard to demonstrate or quantify—individual and societal consequences

## II. Do Consumers Care About Privacy?

### The Claim That Privacy Is Dead

An often-repeated claim is that, under the assault of novel information technologies, privacy is dead—both in the sense that it is no longer achievable and that concern about privacy is fading.

**The authors disagree.** They show that consumers do not just say they care about privacy, but in fact often take action to protect it.

### 2.1 The Evidence for Privacy-Seeking Behavior

#### Altman's Framework

Altman (1975) argued that privacy behaviors are so ubiquitous and common that they occur with little conscious awareness. Writing decades before the Internet age, he noted that protection of personal space is instinctive and universal, across time and geography.

**Offline privacy behaviors (automatic, unnoticed):**
- Lowering voice during intimate conversations
- Leaving a group of people to take a personal call
- Tilting a document to protect from prying eyes
- Drawing curtains to ensure privacy in bedrooms

**Online privacy behaviors (equally ubiquitous):**
- Alternating between different email accounts
- Picking privacy settings for social media posts
- Replying privately to group messages
- Using passwords for online accounts
- Setting "I am busy!" notices on instant messaging
- Turning camera/audio on and off on conference calls

### 2.1.1 Survey Evidence

| Study | Finding | Sample |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Pew (2013) | 86% of US adults took steps to remove or mask digital footprints | US adults |
| Pew (2012) | 58% of social network site users had restricted access to profiles | Social media users |
| Pew (2015) | 34% changed online communication after hearing of surveillance | Those aware of surveillance |
| CISCO (2019) | 33% switched companies over data policies | 2,600 adults in 12 economies |
| Instamotor (2017) | 89% had taken at least one step to protect data | 1,500 Americans |
| DuckDuckGo (2017) | 46% had used "private browsing" at least once | 5,710 US participants |
| Kang et al. (2015) | 77% of non-technical participants took action to hide digital traces | Nontechnical users |
| KFF (2020) | 53% unwilling to install COVID contact tracing app | US population |

### 2.1.2 Field Studies

**Do Not Call Registry:**
- By 2007, 72% of Americans had registered on the list

**Wikipedia Surveillance Effect:**
- Following Snowden revelations (2013), US consumers became less likely to read Wikipedia articles perceived as privacy sensitive (Penney, 2016)

**Private Browsing Adoption:**
- 41% of participants in an academic study had used "private browsing" at least once (Habib et al., 2018)

**GDPR Consent Rates:**
- Opt-in consent to intrusive data collection (e.g., location) was as low as 5.5% (Godinho de Matos & Adjerid, 2019)

**Facebook Privacy Evolution:**
- 86% of CMU Facebook users publicly shared date of birth in 2005
- Only 22% doing so by 2009 (Stutzman et al., 2013)
- Only 7% of Facebook users had not changed default privacy settings by 2017 (Fiesler et al., 2017)

### 2.1.3 Experimental Evidence

| Study | Design | Finding |
|-------|--------|---------|
| Acquisti et al. (2013) | Gift card exchange | 50%+ refused to exchange $10 anonymous for $12 trackable card |
| Tsai et al. (2011) | Lab purchase | ~4% premium to buy from privacy-oriented sellers |
| Marreiros et al. (2017) | Survey experiment | Making privacy policies salient reduced disclosure |
| Jentzsch et al. (2012) | Online shopping | ~€1 WTP to keep mobile phone number private |
| Svirsky (2019) | Online experiment | Majority unwilling to share Facebook profile for $2.50 |
| Savage & Waldman (2015) | Choice experiment | $1-$4 WTP to prevent app access to browsing history, contacts, location |

### 2.1.4 Cross-Cultural Evidence

Privacy-seeking behaviors across cultures and historical periods (Moore, 1984; Murphy, 1964):
- Secret paths in the woods by some tribes in Brazil
- Javanese people hiding emotions and speaking softly
- Pygmies of Zaire rearranging huts in response to new people
- Tuareg of Northern Africa covering almost their entire face

**Altman's conclusion:** Privacy is simultaneously culturally specific and culturally universal.

### 2.2 Reconciling the Evidence

Evidence of disclosure-seeking behavior does not contradict the argument that consumers care for privacy:

**Altman's Dialectic Process:**
Privacy is not a static condition of withdrawal or concealment. Privacy regulation encompasses both the opening and the closing of the self to others. By balancing and alternating the two, individuals manage interpersonal boundaries to achieve desired levels of privacy.

**Key Insight:** The seemingly contrasting examples illustrate that while we manage privacy all the time, we do not continuously protect our data. It would be undesirable (and unfeasible) for any individual to do so.

**The Deeper Issue:** Privacy is extraordinarily difficult to manage in the Internet age. Claims concerning privacy being "dead" too often confuse wants with opportunities—what people want and what they can actually achieve.

## III. Psychological Factors Affecting Privacy Decision Making

### Summary Table of Psychological Factors

| Factor | Description | Consequence | Firms' Response |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Information asymmetries** | Unaware of how data is collected/used | Cannot respond to unknown risks | Write opaque policies |
| **Bounded rationality** | Cannot process complexity | Few read privacy policies | Use sophisticated terms |
| **Present bias** | Overweight immediate benefits | Incur long-term costs for small immediate benefits | Offer small benefits for data |
| **Intangibility** | Underweight diffuse outcomes | Ignore privacy violations | Obscure connections |
| **Constructed preferences** | Rely on crude heuristics | Stick with default settings | Set favorable defaults |
| **Illusory control** | Feeling of control increases risk-taking | Share more when given granular controls | Provide more controls to encourage disclosure |
| **Herding** | Imitate others' behavior | Share more when others share more | Design feeds to show others' sharing |
| **Adaptation** | Get used to gradual risks | Accept ever-increasing violations | Change practices gradually |
| **Drive to share** | Powerful motive to share information | Share highly private information | Work behavioral levers to elicit sharing |

### 3.1.1 Information Asymmetries and Bounded Rationality

Most Americans are unaware of the data practices of firms, and very few are capable of making sense of the ubiquitous privacy policies.

**The Cost of Reading Privacy Policies:**
- Annual national opportunity cost of Americans actually reading privacy policies: Over **$750 billion** (McDonald & Cranor, 2008)

**Misunderstanding of Protection:**
- Many Americans believe privacy policies provide protection, when the reverse is true—they provide firms with uninformed consent to use and sell information (Hoofnagle & Urban, 2014)
- Even privacy-savvy consumers who use tools such as private browsing overestimate the degree of protection they obtain (Habib et al., 2018)

### 3.1.2 Present Bias and Intangibility

**Present Bias:** Consumers respond disproportionately to costs and benefits that are immediate.

**Privacy as "Perfect Storm":**
- Benefits of privacy-challenging activities (e.g., online discounts) are typically immediate and tangible
- Consequences are typically delayed, and have either:
  - Low likelihood (e.g., catastrophic identity theft)
  - High likelihood but small and intangible (e.g., website loading delays due to trackers)

### 3.1.3 Constructed Preferences

**The Unprofessional Website Paradox (John et al., 2011):**
- Students asked sensitive questions (e.g., if they had driven while under the influence)
- Two interfaces: official-looking university interface vs. amateur interface
- **Result:** Students more likely to admit behaviors on unprofessional website
- **Interpretation:** Formal website reminded them of potential hazards of disclosure

**Device Effects:**
- People disclose more intimate thoughts on smartphones than PCs (Melumad & Meyer, 2020)

### 3.1.4 Illusory Control ("Control Paradox")

**Brandimarte et al. (2013) Experiment:**
- Students asked sensitive questions (e.g., if they had ever stolen anything)
- Four conditions varying degree of control over publication:
  1. All answers automatically published
  2. Check one box to allow publication of all
  3. Check box for each question separately
  4. Same as #2 but also provide demographic information

**Results:**
- Greater (illusory) control led to greater admission of sensitive behaviors
- Collecting demographic information (enabling reidentification) had no impact

**The Flip Side - Digital Resignation:**
- Feeling of no control leads to loss of vigilance
- "Digital resignation" (Draper & Turow, 2019): People realize privacy is outside their capabilities, so they give up

### 3.1.5 Herding

**Acquisti, John, & Loewenstein (2012) Experiment:**
- New York Times readers asked sensitive questions
- After each, given ostensible distribution of others' answers
- Feedback manipulated to show most people gave one of three responses

**Result:** Over time, individuals' responses trended toward echoing those of other people.

### 3.1.6 Adaptation

**Adjerid, Pe'er, & Acquisti (2018) Experiment:**
- Participants asked demographic questions and two sets of sensitive questions
- Privacy assurances varied between rounds (low/high combinations)

**Results:**
- Information revelation similar in low-low and high-high conditions (no independent idea of appropriate protection)
- Much greater revelation in low-high condition than high-low condition
- **Interpretation:** Subjects adapted to initial privacy level; became concerned when assurances were reduced

### 3.1.7 The Drive to Share

**The Powerful Motive:**
- Information sharing is inherently pleasurable, particularly when relating to one's own thoughts and feelings (Tamir & Mitchell, 2012 - fMRI study)
- Health, psychological, and social benefits from interpersonal disclosure (Pennebaker, 1997)

**Why People Are "Dying to Share" (Carbone & Loewenstein, 2020):**
Diverse motivations include:
- Emotional release
- Seeking advice
- Social connection
- Impression management
- Entertainment value
- Duty/obligation
- Self-expression

## IV. The Supply Side of Privacy

### 4.1 Privacy Under-Supply

**Supply exists but is limited:**
- VPN services
- User-friendly encrypted emails
- Non-tracking search engines and maps
- Anonymous browsers
- Secure messaging platforms
- Privacy controls (opt-outs, visibility settings)

**But surveillance is ubiquitous:**
- Surveillance cameras
- Face-recognition technologies
- Rental cars with GPS tracking by default
- Apps sharing personal data with opaque ecosystem
- Trackers predicting browsing behavior
- Statistical tools matching data across domains

**Economic drivers:**
- Reduction in costs of surveillance and data storage
- Increasing value of (and success in monetizing) personal data
- Network effects leading to quasi-monopoly incumbents
- Lock-in effects

### Privacy Externality

**Definition:** Other people's usage of privacy-intrusive services increases the cost for privacy-conscious consumers not to use them.

**At the extreme:** Consumers' costs to choose protective options become impractically large. Once no privacy becomes the default, or the social norm, privacy options risk disappearing from the market altogether.

### The Marginal Cost Problem

**Key Insight:** Consumers' marginal costs of protection increase rapidly with degree of protection sought.

| Action | Cost Level | Effectiveness |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| Not publishing SSN on Facebook | Low | High |
| Using a VPN | Moderate | Moderate |
| Hiding most digital footprints | Very High | Limited (constantly evolving surveillance) |

### 4.1.1 Interaction of Economics and Psychology

Firms exploit consumers' psychological characteristics:

| Psychological Factor | Firm Strategy |
|---------------------|---------------|
| Defaults | Set privacy defaults in liberal fashion |
| Control illusion | Provide granular controls to nudge disclosure |
| Adaptation | Change data practices gradually |
| Herding | Design social feeds showing others' sharing |
| Present bias | Offer small immediate benefits for data |

**The Arms Race:**
1. Users adopted cookie managers
2. Industry developed "flash" cookies
3. Users acquired tools against flash cookies
4. Industry switched to device fingerprinting, deep packet inspection

**The consumer is a modern Sisyphus constantly forced to learn new strategies, to little avail.**

### 4.2 Desired vs. Desirable Privacy

#### 4.2.1 Individual and Aggregate Trade-offs

**Rational Privacy Regulation:**
It would be entirely logical for a consumer to:
- Share product interests with marketers (to receive targeted offers)
- Hide reservation price for those products (to avoid price discrimination)

**The Problem:** Under current market conditions, there is no practical way for consumers to meaningfully regulate the use of their information.

**Modeling and Empirical Work Finding:**
- Expansions in data collection are not necessarily welfare-increasing
- Limitations are not necessarily welfare-decreasing

#### 4.2.2 Who Benefits from Consumer Data Collection?

**The Industry Claim:** Targeted advertising creates economic win-wins for all parties.

**Reality:**
- Opting out of behavioral targeting costs publishers and ad exchanges ~$8.50 per consumer (Johnson et al., 2020)
- BUT: Digiday 2019 poll found:
  - 45% of publishers said behavioral targeting produced "no notable benefit"
  - 23% said it caused ad revenues to decline

**Missing Evidence:** What about consumers themselves?
- Do search costs go down because of targeted ads?
- Are prices for advertised products higher or lower?
- What about product quality?

#### 4.2.3 Costs of Privacy Protection

**Regulation Effects:**

| Direction | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Negative | Loss of advertising effectiveness (Goldfarb & Tucker, 2011) |
| Negative | Impaired technology adoption (Miller & Tucker, 2009) |
| Positive | Reduction in identity theft (Romanosky et al., 2011) |
| Positive | Increased technology adoption (Adjerid, Acquisti, et al., 2016) |

**Key Insight:** The decisive factor is not existence of regulation, but specifics of intervention. Well-designed interventions can protect privacy AND stimulate growth.

**Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs):**
- Differential privacy
- Homomorphic encryption
- Allow extracting utility from data while protecting privacy
- Demonstrate that intrusive practices are not always essential

#### 4.2.4 Non-Economic Ramifications: Privacy "Dark Matter"

**The Aggregation Problem:**
Privacy costs are:
- Very common
- Highly diverse in form
- Heterogeneous in likelihood
- Varying in magnitude

**Range of costs:**
- Identity theft
- Price discrimination
- Attention and time waste
- Psychological harm
- Discrimination in targeted advertising
- Filter bubbles
- Stigma
- Rare but catastrophic personal consequences

**Privacy "Dark Matter" - Difficult to Quantify:**
- Collective value of privacy (Regan, 1995)
- Role in preserving individual subjectivity (Cohen, 2010)
- Role in protecting freedom (Westin, 1967)
- Role in protecting dignity (Schoeman, 1984)
- Role in protecting fairness (Jagadish, 2016)
- Integrity of social life (Nissenbaum, 2009)

**If market outcomes respond primarily to economic incentives, market equilibria may not account for these critical implications of data protection.**

## V. The Privacy "Paradox"

### Definition

An apparent gap between people's self-reported mental states (attitudes, concerns, desires) regarding privacy and their actual behaviors.

### Evidence of the Paradox

**Barth et al. (2019) - Mobile Apps:**
- Participants were concerned about app monitoring
- Yet nearly 1/3 downloaded the most intrusive app
- 49% downloaded an app analyzed as intrusive
- Despite being given extra funds to buy a non-intrusive app

**Acquisti & Gross (2006) - Facebook:**
- 16% who expressed highest concern for scenario (stranger knowing schedule + address) actually provided both pieces publicly
- 22% of high-concern subjects provided their address
- 40% provided their schedule

**Madejski et al. (2012) - Social Media:**
- Every single participant exhibited some mismatch between believed and actual privacy settings

**Norberg et al. (2007) - Intentions vs. Behavior:**
- Asked intentions to disclose to marketers
- Weeks later, asked to actually provide information
- Self-reported intentions to disclose were significantly lower than actual disclosures

### Why the Disagreement About Whether a Paradox Exists?

**Factor 1: Different Definitions**
- Different studies focus on different scenarios
- Different pairwise comparisons (attitudes vs. intentions, intentions vs. behaviors, etc.)
- Contextual nature of privacy choice

**Factor 2: Different Interpretations of "Paradox"**
- Some scholars: Focused on discovering seemingly contradictory gap → called it "paradox"
- Other scholars: Focused on explanations that resolved the gap → concluded no paradox

**Key Point:** The fact that misalignments can be explained does not imply that the misalignments themselves do not exist.

### Policy Implications of the Paradox

1. Mismatches between behaviors and preferences do NOT imply:
   - Privacy behaviors never match preferences
   - Self-reported preferences should never be trusted
   - Persistent demand for privacy in surveys should not be taken seriously

2. Evidence of attitudes predicting behavior does NOT imply:
   - Consumers always able to match desires with behaviors
   - No correcting public policy intervention is needed

3. There is no single explanation for the paradox:
   - Explanations include nearly any psychological and economic factor

**Central Lesson:** Consumers may well care for privacy, but psychological and economic hurdles may make desired privacy unattainable without systemic, fundamental change.

## VI. What Should Be Done?

### 6.1 Privacy Nudges

**Potential:** Can counter or ameliorate psychological hurdles to align privacy outcomes with ex ante preferences.

**Examples:**
- Changing social media default visibility settings
- Making consequences of privacy choices more salient

**Limitation:** Platform providers remain in control of choice architecture. Localized behavioral interventions alone cannot correct the enormous imbalance.

### 6.2 Data as Property

**Proposal:** Personal data markets where consumers trade rights with organizations (Laudon, 1996).

**Hurdles:**
1. Can consumers assign fair valuations to their data given informational and behavioral hurdles?
2. Risk of exacerbating inequality (only affluent can afford privacy)
3. Ethical concerns about commodifying privacy (like selling organs)
4. Economic challenge: Most valuable data is contextual and created in interactions with incumbents

### 6.3 Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs)

**Potential:** Allow both data protection and data analytics.

**Barriers:**
- Unawareness of existence
- Distrust
- Perceived (and actual) costs
- Opportunity costs (loss of features when deployed)

**Limitation:** In absence of regulation making privacy the default, a patchwork of localized solutions cannot address systemic problem.

### 6.4 Privacy Regulation (and Its Hurdles)

**Recent Efforts:**
- GDPR (Europe)
- California Consumer Privacy Act (United States)

**Gap Between Principle and Practice:**
Despite intentions to promote "privacy by design" and "privacy by default," consumer tracking remains ubiquitous (Sanchez-Rola et al., 2019).

#### Psychological Hurdles to Regulation

**Why no uprising?**
- Lack of immediacy and tangibility of negative consequences
- Poorly formed notion of negative consequences
- Adaptation to gradually worsening situation
- Feeling of powerlessness

**Comparison to Climate Change:** Another great issue where immediate, tangible consequences are lacking.

#### Economic Hurdles to Regulation

**Regulatory Capture:**
Industries play central role in crafting regulations that apply to them, resulting in regulations that favor regulated industries over consumers.

**Olson's Observation (1965):**
Concentrated economic interests trump diffuse, atomistic interests.
- Huge firms have monumental resources and incentives to lobby
- Individual citizens have limited ability and motivation to influence policies

## VII. Conclusion

### Summary of Findings

| Section | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| 2 | People care about privacy and act to manage it |
| 3 | Psychological hurdles make desired privacy unattainable |
| 4 | Economic hurdles make desirable privacy unattainable |
| 5 | The "paradox" reflects real misalignments with real explanations |
| 6 | Market-based approaches have failed; regulation faces its own hurdles |

### The Inescapable Conclusion

Approaches to privacy management that rely purely on market forces and consumer responsibilization have failed. Comprehensive policy intervention is needed.

### Research Directions Needed

1. **Overcome regulatory hurdles:** How to overcome psychological and economic impediments to enacting regulation
2. **Nuanced cost-benefit analysis:** Examine both costs and benefits of different regulatory approaches, including trade-offs difficult to quantify
3. **Benefit allocation:** Assess how benefits of consumer data collection are allocated to different stakeholders
4. **Effective PETs:** Identify which privacy-protecting technologies would be most effective at minimum cost
5. **Challenge premise:** Scrutinize the premise that loss of privacy is unavoidable to enjoy online services

### A Glimmer of Hope

**Key Observation:** Across history, individuals have revealed remarkable tenacity in attempts to carve out private spaces:
- East Germans under Stasi
- Teenagers on social media using "social steganography"

**If privacy is both culturally specific and culturally universal, chances are that people's quest for privacy will not dissipate.**

Technologies, interfaces, and market forces can all influence human behavior. But probably, and hopefully, they cannot alter human nature.
