## Preface

A word is worth a thousand pictures. Did this sentence make you stop and wonder?

Our language is a lens on the world. It shapes what we perceive and consider important, which is often the very same thing. We look at facts and interpret them through words. Of all the concepts that my colleagues and I regularly use, the most useful is stunningly simple: whenever I read the newspaper or consider a business, I find myself asking the question, "How does this create value?"

Take the toy industry during the coronavirus lockdown. Sales skyrocketed, right? With kids stuck at home, moms and dads were buying them toys to keep them busy. And rising sales are a sure sign of value creation. No? Actually, no. Rising demand for toys is neither necessary nor sufficient to conclude that toy companies created value. Demand is neither necessary nor sufficient to conclude that value changed at all. I will explain this seemingly puzzling claim throughout this book.

I once asked strategy guru Michael Porter what he considered his most important contribution to thinking about business. What concept would most help managers, he wondered? Was it the five forces? The value chain? Generic strategies? He started with generic strategies but then rethought his choice and picked the value chain.

It is a little hard to argue with Michael Porter's assessment of the value of his own ideas. Turns out, however, that people in business and the students I teach believe the most important concept is something else. When I ask this question in my programs at Harvard Business School, one idea comes out first every time: strategy is about creating value. My colleague Jan Rivkin even coined a term for the principle. He calls it "the prime directive of strategy" (referring, of course, to the overarching imperative of nonintervention in Star Trek).

This book is a guide for managers who want to make strategy simpler and more effective. Our understanding of value creation has developed significantly over the past twenty years or so. Thinking about strategy in terms of value will give you a fresh perspective on your business, one that will help you spot opportunities for improving your company's performance.

In practice, our insight into the nature of value is often more intuitive than conscious. We will surface that implicit knowledge. Understanding the structure of a great strategy will bring your own intuition into plain view. Once we have a shared vocabulary, once you know what questions to ask, strategy will seem simpler and more powerful. That simple word—value—will help you see the full picture.

## Part One: Exceptional Performance

### Chapter 1: "Strategy Is Simple"

Managers who have attended my strategy programs sometimes use the phrase "strategy is simple." I always find this slightly ironic because it can take a fair amount of work to recognize when a strategy is simple and when it isn't.

What is so simple about strategy?

Most managers believe strategy is about finding the right market position. Will my company be a broad or a focused player? Will I compete on differentiation or cost? The notion goes back to Michael Porter, who developed the idea of generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation, or focus. Within each of these three generic strategies, companies vie for market share against their rivals, each competing for the wallets of customers.

But this view of competition is too narrow. As you will see throughout this book, companies create value for four groups: the customers they serve, the employees they hire, the suppliers they work with, and the owners of the firm. Managers who focus on market share fail to fully appreciate the opportunities they have for value creation.

Here is an example. Imagine a retail company that wants to gain market share in small towns. The firm decides to compete by undercutting the competition on price. To offer lower prices, it squeezes suppliers for lower costs. Is this a strategy that creates value? Maybe, maybe not. If the company merely reallocates value from suppliers to customers, it simply shifts value around. The zero-sum game does not create value for the economy as a whole. And while customers might applaud lower prices, for how long will the company be able to maintain its strategy if suppliers struggle to survive?

Value-based strategy puts the focus on finding new ways to create value. Can we raise customers' willingness to pay (WTP)? Can we lower suppliers' opportunity cost? If we achieve either objective, we create value. Competing for market share is not value creation; it is the slicing of a given pie. Value-based strategy is about growing the pie.

To understand value-based strategy, you need to understand value. In a nutshell, you create value whenever you raise customers' WTP or lower suppliers' and employees' willingness to sell (WTS). As a manager, you make choices about what to produce and how to produce it. Those choices affect WTP and WTS. Value-based strategy focuses on these two central drivers of company performance.

The value stick is a wonderfully simple device to understand value creation (see figure 1-1). The top of the stick is the willingness to pay, the maximum amount a customer is ever willing to pay for a product or service. If a customer's willingness to pay for a particular product is $100, she will consider purchasing as long as the price does not exceed $100. Anything above $100 is out of the question. A price of $100 makes this customer indifferent between buying and not buying. Once the price falls below $100, a purchase becomes attractive. The difference between WTP and price is often called consumer surplus. Here, I will refer to it as customer delight.

Thinking in terms of WTP and customer delight puts the focus squarely on the needs of our customers. Do we make products that customers value highly? What's the value of the services we provide? Thinking about customer delight—more specifically, thinking about the difference between WTP and price—puts the spotlight on value creation, not on revenue extraction.

The bottom of the value stick is the willingness to sell, the minimum price at which your suppliers will agree to sell their products, raw materials, or components to you. If a supplier's WTS for a particular raw material is $50, she will consider selling as long as the price is at least $50. Anything below $50 is out of the question. A cost of $50 makes this supplier indifferent between selling and not selling. Once price rises above $50, a sale becomes attractive to the supplier. The difference between cost and WTS is often called producer surplus. Because this part of the value stick typically accrues to suppliers and employees, here I refer to it as supplier surplus or employee satisfaction.

Why do we care about the WTS of suppliers and employees? Because whenever we lower the opportunity cost of working with us, we create value. Anything we do to make the job of our suppliers easier will lower their WTS. Anything we do to provide a better work experience will lower our employees' WTS. From your company's perspective, lower WTS translates into lower cost or happier suppliers and employees, or some combination of the two.

Price minus cost is the firm's margin. Here is where profits come from. But don't let the dollar signs distract you. The focus of this book is value creation—growing the pie. Margin is about value capture, the size of the slice that accrues to the company. Value creation has to come first. Only if we create value can we then worry about how to capture a slice of the pie for ourselves.

The entire value created in a transaction equals WTP minus WTS. This value is split among three groups:

- Customers receive value as customer delight (WTP minus price).
- The firm receives value as margin (price minus cost).
- Suppliers and employees receive value as supplier surplus or employee satisfaction (cost minus WTS).

The beauty of the value stick is that it immediately highlights what it takes to achieve superior performance. Your company outperforms others if it creates more value than its competitors or if it captures a larger share of the value it creates. No more, no less. That's what superior strategy is all about.

Strategy is simple, at least in principle.

### Chapter 2: What Is a Good Strategy?

As a longtime strategy professor, I've developed a robust sense of déjà vu. Conversations with executives often circle back to a few fundamental questions, like "What kind of strategic choices do you think make the most difference?" or "How would you know if you're making progress, strategically speaking?" These are fair questions, and in principle, the answers are simple.

In business, there are only two ways to excel. You can raise your customers' WTP or lower your employees' and suppliers' WTS. A great strategy has to have one of these two objectives. Of course, accomplishing these goals often requires extensive effort and considerable ingenuity. The simplicity lies in knowing what you're trying to do. If your strategic initiatives don't ultimately translate into higher WTP or lower WTS, don't bother with them.

But wait. Isn't good strategy all about execution? In a recent survey of top executives, senior leaders ranked execution as "the number-one challenge facing corporate leaders in Asia, Europe, and the United States, heading a list of some eighty issues, including innovation, geopolitical instability, and top-line growth." Has the importance of strategy waned?

I don't think so. More precisely, I believe the results of this survey show something different than what we might think at first blush. Execution worries are the right concern if the strategy is set. If we know which objectives to pursue, then execution is indeed the key challenge. But having a great execution of a mediocre strategy won't help. Executing fast is no good if we're going in the wrong direction.

And here is the thing. When it comes to strategy, we need to be confident that our choices will result in better performance. That's where value-based thinking helps. It provides a clear framework for evaluating strategic choices. Consider a few examples:

- Does this new product feature raise WTP? By how much? Is the investment worth it?
- Will this merger increase our ability to attract talent by lowering employee WTS?
- Does outsourcing production lower WTS or merely cut cost by shifting value away from suppliers?

These are the kinds of questions value-based thinking trains us to ask. With a clear focus on WTP and WTS, strategy becomes simpler and more powerful.

### Chapter 3: Comparative Advantage

One of the most enduring ideas in economics is that of comparative advantage. David Ricardo introduced the concept in his 1817 work, On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation. In the context of international trade, Ricardo showed that it can make sense for countries to specialize even if one country is more productive in all goods.

The same principle applies to companies. Even if your company is better at everything, it does not make sense to do everything. To see why, consider again our value stick. Value is created when WTP exceeds WTS. Comparative advantage says focus on activities where the gap between WTP and WTS is largest.

Here is an example. Imagine two companies, Alfa and Beta, that can each make two products, A and B. The table below shows the WTP and WTS for each company and each product:

| Company | Product | WTP | WTS | Value Created |
|---------|---------|-----|-----|---------------|
| Alfa | A | 100 | 40 | 60 |
| Alfa | B | 80 | 50 | 30 |
| Beta | A | 90 | 50 | 40 |
| Beta | B | 60 | 40 | 20 |

Alfa is better at making both products. It creates more value than Beta for both product A (60 > 40) and product B (30 > 20). Should Alfa make both?

No. Alfa should specialize in product A, where its advantage is greatest. By making A instead of B, Alfa forgoes 30 in value to gain 60. By contrast, if Alfa makes B instead of A, it forgoes 60 to gain 30. The opportunity cost of making B is too high.

This principle has profound implications for strategy. It tells us that focus is essential. Even if we could do more, we should specialize in activities where our value creation is highest relative to alternatives. And it warns against vertical integration into activities where our comparative advantage is low.

## Part Two: Value for Customers

### Chapter 4: The Customer's Perspective

The second part of this book focuses on how companies can raise WTP. We start with the customer's perspective, because everything begins with understanding what customers value.

In the fall of 1995, I visited a friend who had just started his MBA at the Kellogg School of Management in Chicago. He proudly showed me his newly acquired laptop, a ThinkPad notebook computer. Those were the early days of the internet, and he demonstrated how, with the help of a modem, he could connect to a thing called the World Wide Web.

Within minutes, we had found Amazon.com, which had launched just a few months earlier. We browsed the virtual bookstore and put a couple of books in the shopping cart. I don't recall if we actually completed the purchase or eventually got cold feet (shipping to Switzerland was expensive). But I do remember being fascinated by how cleverly Amazon presented its products. Beyond the standard product descriptions, Amazon showed what other customers had bought after viewing the same item. It also featured customer reviews.

These features raised WTP. Before Amazon, buying a book was a hit-or-miss affair. You might rely on recommendations from friends or critics, but once in a bookstore, you were largely on your own. Amazon's recommendation engine and customer reviews reduced uncertainty and helped customers find books they would truly enjoy.

Jeff Bezos famously said, "We're not competitor obsessed, we're customer obsessed. We start with what the customer needs and we work backwards." In terms of the value stick, Bezos focuses on WTP. What do customers value? How can we increase what they're willing to pay? Amazon has consistently invested in features that raise WTP: one-click purchasing, Prime membership with free shipping, and an ever-expanding product selection.

Understanding WTP requires taking the customer's perspective. What problems do customers face? What do they value? What are their alternatives? These questions should guide strategic decision-making. Companies that focus on WTP often find themselves in a virtuous cycle: higher WTP leads to stronger customer loyalty, which leads to better data about customer preferences, which leads to even higher WTP.

### Chapter 5: Willingness to Pay

To measure value creation, we need to assess WTP. This is not always easy. Customers don't always know their own WTP, and even if they do, they may not reveal it truthfully. Still, there are several ways to estimate WTP.

The most direct approach is to ask customers. Surveys can elicit WTP in various ways. Contingent valuation asks customers directly: "What is the maximum you would pay for this product?" Choice experiments present customers with different product configurations at different prices and infer WTP from their choices. Conjoint analysis breaks products into attributes and measures how much customers are willing to pay for each attribute.

Each method has its strengths and weaknesses. Direct questions are easy to administer but may yield biased answers if customers have an incentive to misrepresent their true WTP. Choice experiments are more realistic but require careful design. Conjoint analysis provides detailed insights into customer preferences but assumes customers can evaluate attributes independently.

Another approach is to observe behavior. Revealed preference uses actual purchase data to infer WTP. If a customer bought a product at a price of $50 but not at $60, we can infer that WTP is somewhere between $50 and $60. Auctions are a particularly useful setting for revealed preference analysis because they encourage customers to bid their true WTP.

A third approach is to use proxies. Prices of similar products can provide benchmarks for WTP. If customers are willing to pay $100 for a competitor's product, your product's WTP is likely in the same ballpark, adjusted for differences in features and quality.

Whatever method you choose, remember that WTP is not fixed. It varies across customers, across situations, and over time. A customer's WTP for a cup of coffee might be $3 in the morning but only $1 in the afternoon. Understanding these variations is essential for value-based strategy.

### Chapter 6: Drivers of Willingness to Pay

What drives WTP? In principle, anything that makes a product or service more valuable to customers raises WTP. Let me highlight a few important drivers.

**Quality.** The most obvious driver of WTP is quality. Better products command higher prices because customers value them more. Apple's iPhones consistently rank among the highest-priced smartphones, and customers are willing to pay a premium because they perceive Apple products as higher quality.

**Convenience.** Time is valuable. Products or services that save customers time raise WTP. Amazon Prime's free two-day shipping is a prime example (pun intended). Customers are willing to pay the annual membership fee because the time saved on shipping is worth more to them.

**Brand.** Brands affect WTP in several ways. Strong brands signal quality, reducing the risk of a bad purchase. They provide emotional benefits, like status or identity. And they create trust, which is particularly important for experience goods whose quality cannot be assessed before purchase.

**Customization.** Products tailored to individual customer needs often command higher WTP. Nike By You allows customers to design their own sneakers, and customers are willing to pay a premium for the personalized experience.

**Network effects.** Some products become more valuable as more people use them. Social media platforms, for example, are more valuable when your friends are on them. Network effects can dramatically increase WTP as the user base grows.

**Complements.** Products often become more valuable when used together with other products. A razor is worth more when blades are readily available. A printer is worth more when ink is affordable. We'll discuss complements in detail in a later chapter.

### Chapter 7: Creating Customer Delight

Customer delight is the difference between WTP and price. Companies can increase customer delight by raising WTP, lowering price, or both. The interesting strategic question is how to increase customer delight profitably.

Consider Southwest Airlines. Southwest consistently ranks among the most profitable airlines in the United States. How does it create customer delight? Not by raising WTP through luxurious amenities—Southwest's cabins are famously no-frills. Instead, Southwest increases customer delight by lowering prices while maintaining adequate WTP.

Southwest's strategy is built on operational efficiency. By flying only one type of aircraft (Boeing 737s), Southwest reduces maintenance and training costs. By turning around planes quickly at the gate, Southwest increases aircraft utilization. By not assigning seats, Southwest speeds up boarding. These efficiencies lower costs, which allows Southwest to lower prices while remaining profitable.

The key insight is that customer delight is not just about WTP. It's about the gap between WTP and price. Southwest creates customer delight not by maximizing WTP but by maximizing the value customers receive for the price they pay.

### Chapter 8: Complements

One of the most powerful ways to raise WTP is to think about complements. A complement is a product or service that increases the value of your core offering. Hot dogs and mustard are complements. Cars and gasoline are complements. Smartphones and apps are complements.

The logic is straightforward: if you can make complements more available, more affordable, or higher quality, you raise WTP for your core product. This insight opens up a world of strategic possibilities.

Consider video game consoles. The value of a PlayStation depends on the quality and availability of games. Sony understood this early and invested heavily in attracting game developers. Exclusive titles like the Uncharted series and The Last of Us significantly raised WTP for PlayStation consoles.

The complement strategy can also work in reverse. By making the complement abundant and cheap, you can charge more for the core product. This is the classic "razor and blades" model. Gillette sells razors at low prices to create a large installed base, then profits from selling replacement blades at high margins.

Digital platforms have mastered the complement strategy. Apple's App Store makes millions of apps available for the iPhone, dramatically raising WTP for the device. Amazon's Marketplace allows third-party sellers to offer products alongside Amazon's own inventory, increasing the selection and raising WTP for Amazon Prime membership.

### Chapter 9: Network Effects and Increasing Returns

Network effects occur when the value of a product or service increases as more people use it. The telephone is the classic example: the more people who have telephones, the more valuable it is to have one yourself.

Network effects can dramatically raise WTP. Facebook's value to users increased exponentially as more of their friends joined the platform. By 2021, Facebook had nearly 3 billion monthly active users, making it one of the most valuable services in the world.

Network effects create powerful competitive dynamics. Because the value of the product increases with the number of users, markets with network effects often tip toward a single dominant player. This is why we have one dominant social network (Facebook), one dominant search engine (Google), and one dominant ride-sharing app in most cities (Uber or Lyft).

For companies seeking to build network effects, the key challenge is reaching critical mass. Below a certain threshold of users, the network is not valuable enough to attract new users. Above that threshold, growth becomes self-reinforcing. This is why many platform companies initially subsidize one side of the market—Uber subsidized drivers, PayPal paid users to sign up—to reach critical mass quickly.

Network effects can also create increasing returns to scale. Unlike traditional businesses where larger scale eventually encounters diminishing returns, businesses with network effects may see increasing returns: the bigger they get, the faster they grow. This is why tech companies with strong network effects—Google, Facebook, Amazon—have achieved unprecedented scale and market dominance.

## Part Three: Value for Talent and Suppliers

### Chapter 10: Willingness to Sell

The bottom of the value stick is willingness to sell (WTS), the minimum amount at which employees are willing to work for you or suppliers are willing to do business with you. Anything you can do to lower WTS creates value.

Why would an employee's WTS be lower at one company than another? Several factors come into play:

**Compensation structure.** Beyond base salary, employees value job security, benefits, flexibility, and growth opportunities. Companies that offer attractive packages along these dimensions can lower employee WTS.

**Work environment.** A pleasant workplace, supportive colleagues, and good management all lower WTS. Employees are often willing to accept lower pay in exchange for a better work environment.

**Mission and meaning.** Many employees are willing to accept lower pay to work for a company whose mission they believe in. Nonprofit organizations, for example, often pay less than for-profit companies but attract talented employees who value the organization's social impact.

**Skills development.** Jobs that offer valuable training and experience lower employee WTS. Consulting firms like McKinsey and BCG are known for developing talent, which allows them to attract top graduates even when other industries offer higher starting salaries.

**Reputation and prestige.** Working for a prestigious company can enhance an employee's career prospects. This reputational benefit lowers WTS, which is why elite companies like Google and Goldman Sachs can attract talent despite demanding work environments.

### Chapter 11: Creating Value for Employees

The most direct way to create value for employees is to lower their WTS. This sounds paradoxical—aren't we supposed to pay employees more, not less?—but the logic is clear once you understand the value stick.

Consider two scenarios:

1. Company A pays $70,000 and employee WTS is $50,000. Employee satisfaction = $20,000.
2. Company B pays $60,000 and employee WTS is $35,000. Employee satisfaction = $25,000.

Even though Company A pays more, Company B creates more value for employees because the gap between compensation and WTS is larger. Company B might achieve this lower WTS through a better work environment, more meaningful work, or stronger skills development.

The companies that create the most value for employees are often those that invest in lowering WTS rather than merely increasing compensation. Patagonia, for example, offers lower salaries than many competitors but attracts dedicated employees who value the company's environmental mission. The outdoor clothing company creates employee value through meaning, not money.

This insight has important implications for talent strategy. Competing for talent on compensation alone is expensive and ultimately unsustainable—there's always someone willing to pay more. Competing on WTS is more powerful because it creates genuine value rather than merely redistributing it.

### Chapter 12: Supplier Relationships

The same logic applies to suppliers. Lower supplier WTS creates value that can be shared between your company and your suppliers. How can you lower supplier WTS?

**Stable relationships.** Suppliers value predictability. Long-term contracts and stable order volumes lower supplier WTS by reducing uncertainty.

**Simplified processes.** Making it easy to do business with your company lowers supplier WTS. Clear specifications, efficient ordering systems, and prompt payment all help.

**Knowledge sharing.** Helping suppliers improve their operations lowers their costs, which lowers their WTS. Toyota is famous for working closely with suppliers to improve quality and efficiency.

**Volume.** Larger orders allow suppliers to achieve economies of scale, lowering their costs and WTS.

The trap many companies fall into is treating supplier relationships as zero-sum. If we squeeze suppliers on price, we capture more value, right? This is true in the short run, but it can be counterproductive in the long run. Suppliers who are squeezed too hard may cut corners on quality, become financially unstable, or simply refuse to work with you. Value creation through lower WTS is more sustainable than value capture through lower prices.

## Part Four: Productivity

### Chapter 13: Operational Effectiveness

The middle of the value stick—the gap between price and cost—is the firm's margin. Improving margin requires either raising prices or lowering costs. Raising prices is difficult in competitive markets, so most companies focus on lowering costs through operational effectiveness.

Operational effectiveness means performing similar activities better than rivals. It includes any practices that allow a company to better utilize its inputs: adopting new technologies, improving processes, training employees, and so forth.

The importance of operational effectiveness cannot be overstated. It is the foundation on which competitive advantage is built. Companies that are operationally inefficient will struggle to compete, regardless of their strategy.

But operational effectiveness is not strategy. This is a crucial distinction that Michael Porter emphasized in his influential 1996 Harvard Business Review article, "What Is Strategy?" The problem with competing on operational effectiveness alone is that best practices spread quickly. If Company A discovers a more efficient way of doing something, Company B will soon copy it. Operational effectiveness leads to convergence, not differentiation.

Strategy, in Porter's view, is about being different. It's about choosing to perform different activities, or to perform similar activities in different ways. Southwest Airlines doesn't just do the same things as other airlines more efficiently—it does different things. It doesn't assign seats, doesn't serve meals, doesn't fly hub-and-spoke routes. These choices make Southwest fundamentally different from competitors, and differences are harder to copy than efficiencies.

### Chapter 14: Productivity and Value Creation

From a value perspective, operational effectiveness creates value by lowering WTS. When a company becomes more productive, it can produce the same output with fewer resources. This means lower WTS for employees (the same work requires less effort) and lower WTS for suppliers (the same output requires fewer inputs).

Consider an example. A factory implements a new production system that allows workers to produce the same output in fewer hours. This creates value in several ways:

- Workers can earn the same pay while working less, increasing their satisfaction.
- The company can produce more output with the same workforce, lowering per-unit costs.
- Suppliers can deliver smaller orders, reducing their inventory and transportation costs.

Productivity improvements create value that can be shared among employees, the company, and suppliers. How this value is divided depends on negotiating power and market conditions. But the key point is that productivity creates value rather than merely redistributing it.

### Chapter 15: Dual Advantages

The most powerful strategic positions combine high WTP with low WTS. I call these dual advantages. Companies with dual advantages create more value than competitors and capture more of it.

Consider IKEA. The Swedish furniture retailer raises WTP through distinctive Scandinavian design, a fun shopping experience, and immediate product availability. At the same time, IKEA lowers WTS through flat-pack shipping (which reduces transportation costs), customer self-service (which reduces labor costs), and high volume (which reduces supplier costs).

Dual advantages are rare because they require excellence in multiple dimensions. Most companies face trade-offs: raising WTP often requires higher costs, and lowering WTS often compromises WTP. IKEA's genius is finding ways to do both simultaneously.

How do companies achieve dual advantages? Often through innovative business models that change the rules of competition. IKEA's flat-pack shipping is not just a cost-saving measure—it's a fundamentally different way of doing business that creates value on both sides of the value stick.

Another path to dual advantages is through complements. By investing in complements, companies can raise WTP for their core product while also lowering the cost of delivering value. Apple's investment in the App Store raised WTP for the iPhone (more apps make the phone more valuable) while also lowering Apple's costs (third-party developers create the apps, not Apple).

## Part Five: Implementation

### Chapter 16: Strategy Maps

A strategy map is a visual representation of a company's strategy. It shows the cause-and-effect relationships between strategic objectives and helps align the organization around a common understanding of the strategy.

The value-based strategy map has three levels:

1. **Value drivers:** At the top are WTP and WTS, the fundamental drivers of value creation.

2. **Strategic themes:** The middle level shows the key strategic initiatives that affect WTP and WTS. For example, "improve product quality" raises WTP, while "streamline operations" lowers WTS.

3. **Operational objectives:** The bottom level translates strategic themes into concrete actions and metrics. "Improve product quality" might translate into "reduce defect rate to below 1%" or "increase customer satisfaction score to 4.5/5."

Strategy maps help in several ways. They clarify what the strategy is and isn't. They identify the key cause-and-effect relationships that need to hold for the strategy to work. And they provide a basis for measuring progress and holding people accountable.

### Chapter 17: Resource Allocation

Strategy without resource allocation is just wishful thinking. If you want to raise WTP or lower WTS, you need to invest money, time, and attention in making it happen.

The challenge is that most companies spread their resources too thin. They try to improve everything a little rather than improve the most important things a lot. The result is mediocrity across the board.

Value-based thinking helps prioritize resource allocation. Ask: Which investments will have the greatest impact on WTP and WTS? Focus resources there. Anything that doesn't ultimately translate into higher WTP or lower WTS should be questioned.

This sounds obvious, but in practice, resource allocation often gets disconnected from strategy. Budgets are negotiated politically, with each department fighting for its share. Historical patterns persist long after they've stopped making strategic sense. And pet projects survive even when they contribute nothing to value creation.

The solution is to tie resource allocation explicitly to value creation. Every investment should be evaluated based on its expected impact on WTP and WTS. This creates a common currency for comparing disparate projects and ensures that resources flow to where they'll create the most value.

### Chapter 18: Organization Design

Organization design determines how work gets divided and coordinated within a company. Good organization design supports the strategy; bad organization design undermines it.

From a value perspective, organization design affects both WTP and WTS. Organizational structures that bring together the right people and information can raise WTP by improving products and services. Organizational structures that reduce coordination costs can lower WTS by making operations more efficient.

Consider a company pursuing a dual advantage strategy. Such a company needs organizational structures that support both WTP improvement and WTS reduction. This might mean cross-functional teams that bring together design and operations, or dual career tracks that allow people to specialize in either customer value or operational efficiency.

The key insight is that organization design should follow strategy, not the other way around. Too often, companies design organizations based on convention or historical accident, then try to execute strategy within those constraints. Value-based thinking suggests reversing the order: first, define the strategic objectives (raise WTP, lower WTS), then design the organization to achieve them.

## Part Six: Value

### Chapter 19: Value and Values

Business creates value—for customers, employees, suppliers, and shareholders. But the way companies create value also matters. The values a company embodies affect its ability to create value in the long run.

Consider trust. Companies that build trust with stakeholders find it easier to create value. Customers are more willing to try new products from companies they trust. Employees are more willing to go the extra mile for companies they trust. Suppliers are more willing to make relationship-specific investments for companies they trust.

Trust is valuable because it reduces transaction costs. Without trust, every interaction requires extensive contracting and monitoring. With trust, parties can collaborate flexibly and adapt to changing circumstances. This is why high-trust societies tend to be more economically prosperous—trust lubricates economic exchange.

Values also affect a company's ability to attract talent. Younger workers, in particular, care about working for companies whose values align with their own. Companies with strong values can attract talented employees at lower WTS, creating value through meaning rather than money.

The relationship between value and values goes both ways. Companies with strong values tend to create more value in the long run. But companies that create value also have more resources to invest in living their values. Value creation and values are mutually reinforcing.

### Chapter 20: Value Capture and Stakeholder Strategy

Creating value is necessary but not sufficient for business success. Companies also need to capture enough value to sustain themselves. This brings us back to the middle of the value stick—the firm's margin.

How much value should companies capture? There's no single right answer, but value-based thinking provides some guidance.

First, companies need to capture enough value to sustain their investments in value creation. If value capture falls too low, the company won't have resources to invest in future WTP improvement or WTS reduction. Short-term value capture supports long-term value creation.

Second, companies should consider how value capture affects stakeholder relationships. Capturing too much value from customers (through high prices) may drive them to competitors. Capturing too much value from employees (through low wages) may lead to turnover. Capturing too much value from suppliers (through squeezed margins) may compromise quality. Sustainable value capture requires balance.

Third, companies should think about value capture over time. Short-term value capture (e.g., through price increases) may come at the cost of long-term value creation (e.g., through customer defection). The best strategies balance short-term and long-term considerations.

Value-based strategy puts value creation first. Only if we create value can we worry about how to capture it. Companies that focus obsessively on value capture—on maximizing their slice of the pie—often find that the pie shrinks over time as stakeholder relationships deteriorate. Companies that focus on value creation—on growing the pie—often find that they capture plenty of value even without aggressive tactics.

This is the essence of value-based strategy. Focus on creating value—raising WTP and lowering WTS—and value capture will follow.

```
       │ (WTP - Price)│
       ├──────────────┤
       │    Price     │
       ├──────────────┤
       │    Firm      │
       │   Margin     │
       │(Price - Cost)│
       ├──────────────┤
       │    Cost      │
       ├──────────────┤
       │  Employee    │
       │ Satisfaction │
       │ (Cost - WTS) │
       ├──────────────┤
       │     WTS      │  ← Willingness to Sell
       └──────────────┘
```

The value stick represents the total value created in a transaction. The top is willingness to pay (WTP), the maximum a customer will pay. The bottom is willingness to sell (WTS), the minimum a supplier or employee will accept. Total value created equals WTP minus WTS. This value is split three ways: customer delight (WTP - Price), firm margin (Price - Cost), and employee/supplier satisfaction (Cost - WTS).
