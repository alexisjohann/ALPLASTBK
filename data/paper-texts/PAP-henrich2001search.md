**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.91.2.73

## Abstract

Recent investigations have uncovered large, consistent deviations from the predictions of the textbook representation of Homo economicus (Roth et al., 1991; Fehr and Gächter, 2000; Camerer, 2001). One problem appears to lie in economists' canonical assumption that individuals are entirely self-interested: in addition to their own material payoffs, many experimental subjects appear to care about fairness and reciprocity, are willing to change the distribution of material outcomes at personal cost, and are willing to reward those who act in a cooperative manner while punishing those who do not even when these actions are costly to the individual.

---

## Introduction

These deviations from what we will term the canonical model have important consequences for a wide range of economic phenomena, including the optimal design of institutions and contracts, the allocation of property rights, the conditions for successful collective action, the analysis of incomplete contracts, and the persistence of noncompetitive wage premia.

**Fundamental questions remain unanswered:**
- Are the deviations from the canonical model evidence of universal patterns of behavior, or do the individual's economic and social environments shape behavior?
- If the latter, which economic and social conditions are involved?
- Is reciprocal behavior better explained statistically by individuals' attributes such as their sex, age, or relative wealth, or by the attributes of the group to which the individuals belong?
- Are there cultures that approximate the canonical account of self-regarding behavior?

Existing research cannot answer such questions because virtually all subjects have been university students, and while there are cultural differences among student populations throughout the world, these differences are small compared to the range of all social and cultural environments.

**Our Study:** We and our collaborators undertook a large cross-cultural study of behavior in ultimatum, public good, and dictator games. Twelve experienced field researchers, working in 12 countries on five continents, recruited subjects from 15 small-scale societies exhibiting a wide variety of economic and cultural conditions.

**Sample composition:**
- 3 foraging societies
- 6 slash-and-burn horticulture societies
- 4 nomadic herding groups
- 3 sedentary, small-scale agriculturalist societies

---

## Summary of Results

1. **The canonical model is not supported in any society studied.**

2. **There is considerably more behavioral variability across groups** than had been found in previous cross-cultural research, and the canonical model fails in a wider variety of ways than in previous experiments.

3. **Group-level differences in economic organization and the degree of market integration explain a substantial portion of the behavioral variation** across societies: the higher the degree of market integration and the higher the payoffs to cooperation, the greater the level of cooperation in experimental games.

4. **Individual-level economic and demographic variables do not explain behavior** either within or across groups.

5. **Behavior in the experiments is generally consistent with economic patterns of everyday life** in these societies.

---

## I. The Evidence

Because the ultimatum game (UG) has been conducted throughout the world with student populations and has generated robust violations of the canonical model, we conducted this game in all of our 17 societies.

### Experimental Design

The "proposer" in this game is provisionally assigned an amount equivalent to a day or two's wages in the society and asked to propose an offer to a second person, the "respondent." The respondent may then either accept the offer, in which case the two players receive the proposed amounts, or reject it, in which case the two receive nothing.

**Canonical Prediction:** If both players conform to the canonical model and if this is common knowledge, the proposer will know that the respondent will accept any positive offer and so will offer the smallest possible amount, which will be accepted.

In most field experiments subjects played anonymously, not knowing the identity of the person or persons with whom they were paired.

### Key Findings from Table 1

**Contrary to the standard model predictions:**

1. Even groups with the smallest offers have mean offers greater than 25% of stake size.

2. **Large variation in mean offers:** While mean offers in industrial societies are typically close to 44%, the mean offers in our sample range from **26% to 58%**.

3. **Modal variation:** While modal offers are consistently 50% in industrialized societies, our sample modes vary from **15% to 50%**.

4. **Rejection variation:** While in industrial societies offers below 20% are rejected with probability 0.40 to 0.60:
   - Among the Achuar, Aché and Tsimané: **zero rejections** after 16, 51, and 70 proposer offers respectively
   - Nearly 50% of Tsimané offers were at or below 30%, yet all were accepted
   - Machiguenga responders rejected only one offer despite >75% of offers being below 30%
   - Hadza responders rejected 24% of all offers and 43% of offers at 20% and below
   - **Au and Gnau of Papua New Guinea rejected both unfair AND hyper-fair (>50%) offers** with nearly equal frequency

5. **Non-income-maximizing behavior:** In the majority of groups the modal behavior of proposers is not consistent with expected income-maximization.

### Public-Goods Games (7 societies)

Data show much greater variation than previously found, with novel deviations from canonical predictions.

**Typical student distribution:** U-shaped, with mode at contributing nothing, secondary mode at full cooperation, mean contribution 40-60%.

**Machiguenga:** Mode at contributing nothing, not a single subject cooperating fully, mean contribution **22%**.

**Aché and Tsimané:** Inverted distributions, with few or no contributions at full free-riding or full cooperation.

### Dictator Games (3 societies)

**Orma:** Mode at 50%, secondary mode at 20% (contrasts with student mode at zero).

**Hadza:** Mode at 10%, with 0% and 50% also popular.

**Tsimané:** No zero offers; mean was 32%, mode was 25%.

## II. What Explains Group-Level Differences?

The large variations across cultural groups suggest that preferences or expectations are affected by group-specific conditions. We ranked societies along two dimensions:

### (i) Payoffs to Cooperation (PC)
How important and how large is a group's payoff from cooperation in economic production?

- **Lowest:** Machiguenga and Tsimané (almost entirely economically independent at family level)
- **Highest:** Lamelara whale-hunters (go to sea in large canoes manned by 12+ individuals)

**Rationale:** With little cooperative production there will be little necessity to share returns; those whose livelihood depends on large-scale cooperation must develop ways of sharing the joint surplus.

### (ii) Market Integration (MI)
How much do people rely on market exchange in their daily lives?

**Rationale:** The more frequently people experience market transactions, the more they will also experience abstract sharing principles concerning behaviors toward strangers.

### Regression Results

In a regression:
- Both PC and MI were **highly significant**
- Normalized regression coefficients were large (~0.3)
- The two measures jointly explained **68% of the variance**

**Individual-level measures** (sex, age, relative wealth, village population size, stake size, experimenter experience) were **not significantly related** to offers.

## Ethnographic Interpretations

### Au and Gnau (Papua New Guinea)
The hyper-fair UG offers (>50%) and frequent rejections of these offers reflect the **culture of gift-giving** found in these societies. Accepting gifts commits one to reciprocate at some future time determined by the giver. Receipt of large gifts establishes one in a subordinate position. Excessively large unsolicited gifts are refused due to anxiety about unspecified strings attached.

### Hadza
Low offers and high rejection rates reflect their reluctant process of sharing (termed **"tolerated theft"**). While Hadza extensively share meat, many hunters look for opportunities to avoid sharing and share only because they fear social consequences (informal sanctions, gossip, ostracism).

### Aché (Paraguay)
Did not reject low offers, yet 94% made offers above 40%. This coincides with ethnographic descriptions of **widespread meat-sharing and cooperation** despite the absence of punishment fear. Aché hunters returning home quietly leave their catch at the edge of camp, often claiming fruitless hunt; the catch is later discovered and meticulously shared among all in camp.

### Machiguenga
Show the **lowest cooperation rates** in public-goods games, reflecting ethnographic descriptions of little cooperation, exchange, or sharing beyond the family unit.

### Orma
Subjects quickly dubbed the public-goods experiment a **"harambee game"** (referring to widespread institution of village-level voluntary contributions for schools or roads). They contributed generously **(58%)**, higher than most U.S. subjects.

## III. Discussion

Our data indicate that the degree of cooperation, sharing, and punishment exhibited by experimental subjects closely corresponds to **templates for these behaviors in the subjects' daily lives**, and that the substantial variability in experimental behaviors across groups is an expression of large between-group differences in social interaction structures and modes of livelihood.

### Alternative Explanations Considered

1. **Implementation variation:** Experiments were run from identical protocols; where we could test for experimenter effects we found none.

2. **Public actions concern:** Subsequent conversations with participants convinced us subjects believed actions were anonymous.

3. **Repeated interaction belief:** Extensive evidence from advanced economies shows subjects understand games well; those who reject positive offers affirm having goals besides maximizing monetary payoff.

### Why Do Subjects Share and Punish?

**Proximate reason:** Situations cue emotional responses which induce measured behaviors.
- Ultimatum-game responders facing low offers experience emotional impulse to hurt the proposer
- New Guinea responders rejecting hyper-fair offers may experience same anxiety as with unsolicited gifts

**Ultimate determinants:** Long-run evolutionary processes governing distribution of genes and cultural practices could result in substantial fraction predisposed to forgo material payoffs to share or punish unfair actions. Recent contributions show prosocial behavior can proliferate under conditions approximating early human social/physical environments.

---

## IV. Conclusion

While our results do not imply that economists should abandon the rational-actor framework, they do suggest **two major revisions:**

1. **The canonical model of the self-interested material payoff-maximizing actor is systematically violated.** In all societies studied, UG offers are strictly positive and often substantially in excess of income-maximizing offers.

2. **Preferences over economic choices are not exogenous** but rather are shaped by economic and social interactions of everyday life.

### Implications

- Judgments in welfare economics assuming exogenous preferences are questionable
- Predictions of changing economic policies that fail to account for behavioral change are problematic
- The connection between experimental behavior and structure of everyday economic life should guide revisions to the canonical model

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **WEIRD Problem:** Standard behavioral economics parameters may be WEIRD-specific
   - α, β from Fehr-Schmidt may vary by culture
   - Punishment propensity varies dramatically

2. **Context Parameters:** Two key group-level predictors:
   - **Payoffs to Cooperation (PC):** Economic structure matters
   - **Market Integration (MI):** Exposure to markets shapes preferences

3. **Individual Variables Fail:** Sex, age, wealth do NOT predict behavior
   - Group-level factors dominate
   - Implications for segmentation: cultural context > demographics

4. **Exogeneity Rejected:** Preferences shaped by daily economic life
   - Policy interventions may change preferences themselves
   - Dynamic models needed
