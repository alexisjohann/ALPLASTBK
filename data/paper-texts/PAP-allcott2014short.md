# The Short-Run and Long-Run Effects of Behavioral Interventions: Experimental Evidence from Energy Conservation

**Authors:** Hunt Allcott, Todd Rogers
**Year:** 2014
**Journal:** American Economic Review, Vol. 104, No. 10, pp. 3003-3037
**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.104.10.3003

## Abstract

This paper documents three remarkable features of the Opower program, in which
social comparison-based home energy reports are repeatedly mailed to more than
six million households nationwide.

First, initial reports cause high-frequency "action and backsliding," but these
cycles attenuate over time. Second, if reports are discontinued after two
years, effects are relatively persistent, decaying at 10-20 percent per year.
Third, consumers are slow to habituate: they continue to respond to repeated
treatment even after two years.

The authors show that previous conservative assumptions about post-intervention
persistence had dramatically understated cost effectiveness and illustrate how
empirical estimates can optimize program design.

(JEL D12, D83, L94, Q41)

## Methodology

- **Design:** Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT), field experiment
- **Intervention:** Opower Home Energy Reports — social comparison-based mailings showing household energy use relative to neighbors
- **Assignment:** Random assignment of households to treatment (reports) vs. control (no reports)
- **Frequency variation:** Monthly vs. quarterly report mailings
- **Duration:** Multi-year panel, with some cohorts receiving reports for 2+ years and then discontinued

## Sample and Data

- **Sample size:** 6+ million households nationwide (US)
- **Data source:** Utility billing data (monthly electricity/gas consumption)
- **Geographic scope:** Multiple US utilities participating in Opower program
- **Time period:** Multiple cohorts starting from different dates, tracked over several years
- **Identification:** Large-scale randomization ensures causal identification

## Key Findings

1. **ACTION AND BACKSLIDING:** Initial reports cause immediate conservation
   followed by decay until the next report arrives. These high-frequency
   cycles attenuate over time as habits form.

2. **PERSISTENT EFFECTS:** After discontinuation of reports, treatment effects
   decay at only 10-20% per year, not immediately. This indicates substantial
   habit formation and capital stock changes.

3. **SLOW HABITUATION:** Consumers continue responding to repeated treatment
   even after two years of reports. Marginal treatment effects remain positive.

4. **SOCIAL COMPARISON POWER:** Comparing household energy use to neighbors
   reduces consumption by 1.4-3.3% on average.

5. **FREQUENCY MATTERS:** Monthly reports reduce energy consumption by 0.5
   percentage points more than quarterly reports.

6. **COST EFFECTIVENESS:** Actual persistence is much higher than previously
   assumed, making interventions more cost-effective than conservative
   engineering estimates suggested.
