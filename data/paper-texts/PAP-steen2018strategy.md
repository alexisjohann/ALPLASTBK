## Abstract

This paper addresses primarily two questions. First, when (and why) should a company's strategy be developed by its CEO versus by some outside analyst or other insider? Second, how does strategy interact with vision (in the sense of a strong belief about the right course of action)? The paper studies these questions using a functional definition of strategy as "the smallest set of choices to optimally guide other choices." Among other things, the paper shows that strategy formulation by the CEO leads to both better strategy and better execution, and that a strategist's vision may improve execution. In the process, the paper also identifies criteria that make a decision strategic and derives explanations why strategies often reflect the background of the strategist.

## 1. Introduction

Whether at the level of a new venture, a product line, or a multidivisional corporation, one of the fundamental questions of strategy is who should develop it. Should the strategy be developed by the CEO or by some outside analyst? Should divisions develop their own strategy?

Developing strategy requires expertise, knowledge, and intelligence. It is often an involved process that requires an unbiased, open-minded, systematic approach, with outside professionals sometimes able to bring better skills, wider experience, and a more neutral perspective.

But who develops a strategy seems to matter beyond, and independently of, these "qualifications." In particular, strategies often seem to reflect the background of the strategist. Wasserman (2003) argues that venture capital firms push out academic founders because founders lack the skills or "human capital" needed to run a growing firm. Kaplan et al. (2012), however, find that at least in the successful VC-backed firms they study, "founders who are replaced tend to be replaced because... [VCs] believe that different skills may be needed at a later stage." In fact, in interviews, both founders and VCs "emphasize a consistent strategic direction" as one of the key rationales for founder-CEOs.

### Research Questions

This paper addresses two questions:
1. When (and why) should a company's strategy be developed by its CEO versus by an outside analyst?
2. How does strategy interact with vision (strong belief about the right course of action)?

## 2. Model

### 2.1 Basic Setup

As in Van den Steen (2017), the model considers a project that depends on K choices {C₁, ..., Cₖ}. The project revenue depends both on whether choices are correct on a standalone basis and on whether they align correctly:

$$\Pi = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k I_k + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{l=1}^{k-1} \gamma_{kl} I_{kl}$$

where:
- I_k = indicator whether choice k is correct
- α_k = standalone importance
- I_kl = indicator whether choices align correctly
- γ_kl = interaction importance

### 2.2 Strategy Formulation

The game proceeds in two stages:

**Stage 1: Strategy Formulation**
- The strategist can investigate states and observe signals
- The strategist announces a strategy (subset of choices)

**Stage 2: Strategy Implementation**
- Participants observe their own signals about states
- Participants make their choices
- Payoffs are realized

### 2.3 Vision

A key concept is **vision**: a (strong) belief about the right course of action.

**Definition (Vision):** A person P has a **vision** about a decision Cₖ if P's belief about the optimal Cₖ is unaffected by signals about the state, i.e., if P's belief satisfies: E[V(Cₖ)|s, P] = E[V(Cₖ)|P] for all signals s.

## 3. Main Results

### 3.1 The Benefits of CEO as Strategist

**Proposition 1:** Strategy formulation by the CEO leads to:
1. Better strategy (higher expected value of strategic choices)
2. Better execution (higher expected value of implementation)

**Key Insight:** When the CEO develops the strategy, implementation is driven by the CEO's beliefs about what is right. This leads to better alignment between strategic intent and execution.

### 3.2 What Makes a Decision Strategic (New Criteria)

The paper identifies three new criteria that make a decision strategic, beyond those in Van den Steen (2017):

**Criterion 1: Need for Alignment with Strategist's Beliefs**
A decision is more strategic when correct implementation requires alignment with the strategist's beliefs.

**Criterion 2: Strategist's Private Information**
A decision is more strategic when the strategist has private information about it.

**Criterion 3: Downstream Implications**
A decision is more strategic when it has more downstream implications that depend on the strategist's beliefs.

### 3.3 Vision and Strategy

**Proposition 2:** A strategist's vision may improve execution by:
1. Providing clearer direction
2. Reducing uncertainty about the strategist's intent
3. Enabling better coordination

**Proposition 3:** Vision is most valuable when:
- There is high uncertainty
- Coordination is important
- The decision has many downstream implications

### 3.4 Why Strategies Reflect the Strategist

The paper derives two explanations:

**Explanation 1: Selection Effect**
Strategists are selected based on their beliefs, which then shape the strategy.

**Explanation 2: Adaptation Effect**
Strategies are adapted to the strategist's strengths and beliefs for better execution.

## 6. Conclusion

The paper shows that:

1. **CEO as strategist is valuable:** Both strategy quality and execution improve when the CEO develops the strategy.

2. **Vision matters:** Strong beliefs can improve execution by providing clear direction.

3. **New strategic criteria:** Beyond interactions, decisions are strategic when they require alignment with the strategist's beliefs or downstream implementation.

4. **Strategist background matters:** Strategies reflect the strategist's background through both selection and adaptation effects.

---

## Formal Definitions

**Definition 1 (Strategy):** A strategy is "the smallest set of choices to optimally guide other choices." (From Van den Steen 2017)

**Definition 2 (Vision):** A person has a vision about a decision if their belief about the optimal choice is unaffected by signals about the state.

**Definition 3 (Strategic):** A decision is strategic to the degree that it should be investigated or announced as part of the optimal strategy.
