# Strategy and the Strategist: How It Matters Who Develops the Strategy

**Author:** Eric Van den Steen
**Year:** 2018
**Journal:** Management Science, Vol. 64, No. 10, pp. 4533-4551
**DOI:** 10.1287/mnsc.2017.2857
**Working Paper Version:** HBS Working Paper 17-002, July 2016
**Archived Date:** 2026-02-24 (updated)

## Abstract

This paper addresses primarily two questions. First, when (and why) should a company's strategy be developed by its CEO versus by some outside analyst or other insider? Second, how does strategy interact with vision (in the sense of a strong belief about the right course of action)? The paper studies these questions using a functional definition of strategy as "the smallest set of choices to optimally guide other choices." Among other things, the paper shows that strategy formulation by the CEO leads to both better strategy and better execution, and that a strategist's vision may improve execution. In the process, the paper also identifies criteria that make a decision strategic and derives explanations why strategies often reflect the background of the strategist.

---

## 1. Introduction

Whether at the level of a new venture, a product line, or a multidivisional corporation, one of the fundamental questions of strategy is who should develop it. Should the strategy be developed by the CEO or by some outside analyst? Should divisions develop their own strategy?

Developing strategy requires expertise, knowledge, and intelligence. It is often an involved process that requires an unbiased, open-minded, systematic approach, with outside professionals sometimes able to bring better skills, wider experience, and a more neutral perspective.

But who develops a strategy seems to matter beyond, and independently of, these "qualifications." In particular, strategies often seem to reflect the background of the strategist. Wasserman (2003) argues that venture capital firms push out academic founders, though Kaplan et al. (2012) find that in successful VC-backed firms, founders are replaced not for lack of skill but because VCs believe different skills are needed at later stages. In both accounts, the identity of the strategist shapes the strategy.

### Research Questions

1. When (and why) should a company's strategy be developed by its CEO versus by an outside analyst or other insider?
2. How does strategy interact with vision — a strong belief about the right course of action?

### Key Contribution

This paper shows that WHO develops the strategy matters for three reasons:
- Strategy quality depends on the strategist's information and beliefs
- Strategy execution depends on alignment between the strategist and implementers
- The strategist's identity affects both content and commitment to the strategy

## 2. Model

### 2.1 Setup

The model builds directly on Van den Steen (2017). A project depends on K choices {C₁, ..., Cₖ} with payoff:

$$\Pi = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k I_k + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{l=1}^{k-1} \gamma_{kl} I_{kl}$$

where αₖ is standalone importance, γₖₗ is interaction importance, Iₖ indicates correct standalone choices, and Iₖₗ indicates correct alignment.

### 2.2 Players

The model introduces two types of players:

**The Strategist** — can be the CEO, an outside consultant, or a divisional manager. The strategist investigates states, forms beliefs, and announces a strategy.

**The Implementers** — participants who observe their own signals and make their choices, guided by the announced strategy.

The key innovation is that the strategist's identity (CEO vs. outsider) affects:
1. **What information the strategist has** (insiders have different knowledge than outsiders)
2. **How implementers respond to the strategy** (they may follow a CEO's strategy more willingly)
3. **Whether the strategist will be present during implementation** (a CEO implements; a consultant leaves)

### 2.3 Vision

**Definition (Vision):** A person P has a vision about a decision Cₖ if P's belief about the optimal Cₖ is unaffected by signals about the state. Formally: E[V(Cₖ)|s, P] = E[V(Cₖ)|P] for all signals s.

Vision is modeled as a strong prior — so strong that new evidence does not change the person's belief about the right course of action. This can be rational (based on deep expertise) or biased (based on overconfidence), but the paper studies the coordination consequences regardless of the source.

## 3. Main Results

### 3.1 The Benefits of CEO as Strategist (Proposition 1)

**Proposition 1:** Strategy formulation by the CEO leads to:
1. **Better strategy** (higher expected value of strategic choices)
2. **Better execution** (higher expected value of implementation)

**Why better strategy?** The CEO has two informational advantages:
- **Insider knowledge:** The CEO knows the organization's capabilities, constraints, and culture from the inside. An outside analyst must infer these from limited data.
- **Ongoing presence:** The CEO will be present during implementation and can update the strategy as new information arrives. An outside analyst hands off the strategy and leaves.

**Why better execution?** The CEO as strategist creates alignment through three channels:
- **Authority:** Implementers are more likely to follow directions from their boss than from an outside consultant
- **Shared context:** The CEO and implementers share organizational context, making communication more efficient
- **Skin in the game:** The CEO's reputation and career depend on the strategy's success, creating credible commitment

**Key qualification:** This result holds when the CEO has at least moderate competence. An incompetent CEO who insists on developing strategy can be worse than an outside analyst. But for CEOs of reasonable quality, there is an additional benefit from being both strategist and implementer.

### 3.2 New Criteria for Strategic Decisions (Propositions 2-4)

Beyond the criteria identified in Van den Steen (2017), this paper identifies three new criteria:

**Proposition 2 (Alignment with Strategist's Beliefs):** A decision is more strategic when correct implementation requires alignment with the strategist's beliefs about the right course of action. This is a new criterion not present in the pure coordination setting of the 2017 paper.

*Intuition:* When the strategist has private information or strong beliefs, the strategy serves not just to coordinate but to communicate the strategist's vision. Decisions that require alignment with this vision become more strategic.

**Proposition 3 (Strategist's Private Information):** A decision is more strategic when the strategist has private information relevant to that decision.

*Intuition:* Strategy is more valuable when it communicates information that implementers do not have. The CEO's unique vantage point (seeing across functions, knowing board priorities, understanding competitive dynamics) makes certain decisions strategic precisely because only the CEO has the relevant knowledge.

**Proposition 4 (Downstream Implications):** A decision is more strategic when it has more downstream implications that depend on the strategist's beliefs.

*Intuition:* A decision that merely affects its own outcome is less strategic than one whose implications cascade through many subsequent decisions. The strategist's beliefs about this decision shape not just this choice but all decisions that depend on it.

### 3.3 Vision and Strategy (Propositions 5-7)

**Proposition 5:** A strategist's vision may improve execution by providing clearer direction, reducing uncertainty about the strategist's intent, and enabling better coordination.

*Mechanism:* When the strategist has a vision, implementers face less uncertainty about what the strategy is trying to achieve. This reduces coordination failures because everyone has a clearer picture of the target.

**Proposition 6:** Vision is most valuable when:
- There is high uncertainty about the right course of action
- Coordination across decisions is important
- The decision has many downstream implications

*Intuition:* Vision is most valuable precisely when the situation is most uncertain, because that is when implementers most need guidance. In clear situations, vision adds little because implementers can figure out the right action themselves.

**Proposition 7:** A visionary strategist may be worse at choosing the right strategy but better at executing it. The net effect is positive when execution matters more than strategy selection.

*This creates a fundamental trade-off:*
- A visionary CEO may choose a suboptimal strategy (because vision implies ignoring some evidence)
- But a visionary CEO executes the chosen strategy better (because vision provides clearer direction)
- The net effect favors vision when (a) uncertainty is high, (b) interactions are strong, and (c) the number of implementers is large

### 3.4 Why Strategies Reflect the Strategist (Propositions 8-9)

**Proposition 8 (Selection Effect):** Strategists are selected based on their beliefs, which then shape the strategy. Firms choose CEOs whose vision matches the firm's needs, and that vision determines the strategy.

*Example:* When Apple hired Tim Cook to succeed Steve Jobs, they selected someone with operational expertise. The subsequent strategy shift toward operational efficiency and services (vs. radical product innovation) reflects Cook's background and beliefs.

**Proposition 9 (Adaptation Effect):** Even holding the strategist fixed, the strategy is adapted to leverage the strategist's strengths. A strategist with deep marketing knowledge will naturally develop strategies that rely on marketing capabilities, while an operations expert will develop operations-heavy strategies.

*Both effects together explain why:*
- CEO turnover often leads to strategy changes
- Firms with similar market positions but different CEOs pursue different strategies
- Industry veterans vs. outsider CEOs adopt systematically different approaches

## 4. Extensions

### 4.1 Multiple Strategists

When strategy formulation is distributed (e.g., division managers each develop divisional strategies):

- **Coordination costs increase:** Different strategists may have different visions, leading to misalignment
- **Information gains:** Local strategists have better knowledge of their domain
- **Optimal structure:** Centralize strategic decisions with strong cross-divisional interactions; decentralize those with primarily local effects

This connects to the classical centralization-decentralization trade-off in organizational design (Dessein 2002; Rantakari 2008).

### 4.2 Strategy and Organizational Design

The paper's framework connects strategy to organizational design:

- **Decision rights:** Strategic decisions should be retained at the level of the strategist; operational decisions should be delegated
- **Communication:** The strategy must be communicated clearly enough that implementers can use it as guidance
- **Hierarchy:** The natural hierarchy created by strategy (Van den Steen 2017) maps onto organizational hierarchy

### 4.3 Strategy and Culture

Strategy and culture interact:
- **Strategy** provides explicit guidance on a few key choices
- **Culture** provides implicit guidance on many routine choices
- They are **complements:** Strategy sets the direction; culture ensures consistent execution

This anticipates Van den Steen's later work on corporate culture dynamics (2024).

## 5. Empirical Implications

The paper generates several testable predictions:

1. **CEO-developed strategies** should produce better performance outcomes than consultant-developed strategies, controlling for CEO and firm characteristics
2. **Visionary CEOs** should be more prevalent in uncertain, interaction-heavy industries
3. **Strategy changes** should correlate with CEO turnover
4. **CEO background** should predict strategy content
5. **Firms with stronger cultures** should benefit more from CEO-developed strategy (culture amplifies execution)

Several of these predictions are supported by existing evidence:
- Bertrand and Schoar (2003): CEO fixed effects matter for corporate policies
- Bandiera et al. (2020): CEO behavioral types affect firm performance
- Kaplan et al. (2012): CEO characteristics predict VC-backed firm outcomes

## 7. Conclusion

This paper extends the formal theory of strategy (Van den Steen 2017) to address the fundamental question of WHO should develop strategy. The main findings are:

1. **CEO advantage:** The CEO as strategist produces both better strategy and better execution, through information advantages, authority, and alignment.

2. **Vision as asset:** A strategist's strong beliefs (vision) can improve execution by providing clearer direction, even at the cost of potentially suboptimal strategy selection.

3. **Identity matters:** Strategies reflect the background of the strategist through both selection effects (firms choose CEOs whose vision fits) and adaptation effects (strategies leverage the strategist's strengths).

4. **New strategic criteria:** Decisions are more strategic when they require alignment with the strategist's beliefs, when the strategist has private information, and when they have many downstream belief-dependent implications.

The central message is that strategy is not merely a technical optimization problem that can be delegated to the most analytically skilled person. Who develops the strategy fundamentally affects both its content and its execution. This has important implications for CEO selection, succession planning, and the role of strategy consultants.
