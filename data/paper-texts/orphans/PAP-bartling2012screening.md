# Screening, Competition, and Job Design: Economic Origins of Good Jobs

**Authors:** Björn Bartling, Ernst Fehr, Klaus M. Schmidt
**Date:** April 2012
**Journal:** American Economic Review, Vol. 102, No. 2, pp. 834-864
**Paper-ID:** PAP-bartling2012screening
**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.102.2.834
**Archived:** 2026-02-03

## Abstract

We study the interrelation between job design and incentive contracts by examining the effects of market screening and labor market competition on contract choices and job design in an experimental labor market. Our data reveal that screening possibilities do not lead to harsher incentive contracts. On the contrary, they give rise to a more pronounced reliance on "good jobs" characterized by a combination of higher effort discretion, screening, efficiency wages, and high effort. This pattern suggests that efficiency wages, high effort discretion, and the opportunity to screen are complements.

---

## I. Introduction

"Good jobs"—characterized by a combination of high wages, limited monitoring, and the opportunity for workers to make independent decisions—constitute an important and pervasive phenomenon in modern labor markets.

### Two Competing Views on Screening

**View 1 (The Stick Hypothesis):** Screening enables principals to identify and punish shirkers, leading to harsher control-based contracts with:
- Low discretion
- Tight monitoring
- Low wages

**View 2 (The Carrot Hypothesis):** Screening enables principals to identify trustworthy workers, making it profitable to combine:
- High discretion
- Efficiency wages
- Trust-based contracts

### Key Finding

> "Our data reveal that screening possibilities do not lead to harsher incentive contracts. On the contrary, they give rise to a more pronounced reliance on 'good jobs' characterized by a combination of higher effort discretion, screening, efficiency wages, and high effort."

## II. Experimental Design

### 2.1 Basic Setup

- **Subjects:** N = 216 (72 per treatment)
- **Locations:** University of Zurich & University of Munich
- **Sessions:** 12 sessions with 18 subjects each
- **Periods:** 15 periods per session
- **Exchange Rate:** 10 points = 0.30 CHF

### 2.2 The Contract Game

**Stage 1 (Principal):**
- Chooses contract type: High Discretion (HD) or Limited Discretion (LD)
- Sets wage w ∈ [0, 100]
- In LD: Sets minimum effort requirement ē

**Stage 2 (Agent):**
- Accepts or rejects contract
- If accept: Chooses effort e ∈ {1, 2, ..., 10}
- LD: Must exert at least ē (verified)
- HD: Any effort level possible

**Payoffs:**
```
Principal:  π_P = 10e - w         (if contract accepted)
Agent:      π_A = w - c(e)        where c(e) = e²/10
            π_A = 5               (if rejected)
```

### 2.3 Treatment Conditions

| Treatment | Screening | Competition | N |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---|
| **Base** | No | No | 72 |
| **Screening** | Yes | No | 72 |
| **Competition** | Yes | Yes | 72 |

### 2.4 Screening Mechanism

In screening and competition treatments:
- Agents are matched with same principal for 3 consecutive periods ("employment spell")
- After each spell, principals observe:
  - Agents' individual ID numbers
  - Their past effort choices in previous spells
- This creates reputation effects and enables screening

### 2.5 Competition Mechanism

In competition treatment:
- Multiple principals can make offers to same agent
- Agents choose among competing offers
- Creates labor market competition for "good" workers

## III. Results

### 3.1 Emergence of Two Job Design Clusters

The data reveal a striking pattern: principals cluster into two distinct groups:

**Trust Strategy (Cluster 1):**
- High discretion (HD)
- High wages (avg. 55.2)
- High effort elicited (avg. 6.5)
- Higher profits (34.3)

**Control Strategy (Cluster 2):**
- Limited discretion (LD)
- Low wages (avg. 28.7)
- Minimum effort enforced
- Lower profits (24.9)

> "The data reveal an economically significant dichotomy in job design: a two cluster solution clearly fits the data best, suggesting that two fundamentally different strategies coexist."

### 3.2 Treatment Effects on Contract Choice

| Treatment | High Discretion Rate | Avg Wage (HD) | Avg Effort (HD) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Base | 63% | 48.3 | 5.7 |
| Screening | 71% | 52.1 | 6.5 |
| Competition | 78% | 55.8 | 6.7 |

**Key Finding:** Screening and competition INCREASE reliance on trust-based contracts, contradicting the "stick hypothesis."

**Interpretation:**
- Wage-effort relationship is STRONG under high discretion (HD)
- Wage-effort relationship is WEAK under limited discretion (LD)
- The slope INCREASES from Base → Screening → Competition

### 3.4 Profit Analysis (Table 2)

**Panel A: Profits by Contract Type and Treatment**

| Treatment | Trust Strategy Profit | Control Strategy Profit | Difference |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Base | 28.1 | 23.4 | +4.7 |
| Screening | 34.3 | 24.9 | +9.4*** |
| Competition | 36.8 | 25.2 | +11.6*** |

**Key Finding:** The profit advantage of the trust strategy INCREASES with screening and competition.

### 3.5 Complementarity Evidence

The data strongly support the hypothesis that screening, discretion, and wages are **complements**:

1. **Screening + High Discretion:** Profit increases from 28.1 to 34.3 (+22%)
2. **Competition + Screening + High Discretion:** Profit increases to 36.8 (+31%)
3. **Superadditivity:** Combined effect > sum of individual effects

## IV. Theoretical Framework

### 4.1 Model Setup

Agents have heterogeneous reciprocity preferences:
- **Fair types (θ_F):** High reciprocity, respond to generous wages
- **Selfish types (θ_S):** Low reciprocity, shirk when possible

### 4.2 Optimal Contracts

**Proposition 1 (No Screening):**
Under limited discretion, principals offer low wages and enforce minimum effort. Under high discretion, principals face risk from selfish types.

**Proposition 2 (With Screening):**
Screening enables principals to identify fair types. This makes high discretion profitable because:
- Fair types respond to high wages with high effort
- Selfish types are identified and excluded

**Proposition 3 (Complementarity):**
Screening, high discretion, and high wages are complements:
```
∂²π/∂Screening·∂Discretion > 0
∂²π/∂Screening·∂Wages > 0
∂²π/∂Discretion·∂Wages > 0
```

### 4.3 Intuition for Complementarity

1. **Screening enables trust:** Identifying fair workers makes it safe to grant discretion
2. **Discretion enables reciprocity:** Fair workers can only reciprocate under HD
3. **High wages elicit effort:** Fair workers respond to generous wages
4. **All three together:** Maximum profits from "good jobs"

---

## V. Discussion and Implications

### 5.1 Economic Origins of Good Jobs

> "Our results suggest that 'good jobs'—characterized by high wages, autonomy, and trust—are not just a normative ideal but an economically optimal response to the presence of fair workers and the availability of screening technologies."

### 5.2 Policy Implications

1. **Job Design:** Firms should invest in screening to enable trust-based contracts
2. **Labor Markets:** Markets that facilitate reputation (screening) lead to better jobs
3. **Regulation:** Policies that enable worker reputation portability may improve job quality

### 5.3 Limitations

- Laboratory setting (external validity)
- Specific screening mechanism (may not generalize)
- Short-term interactions (15 periods)
