# Global Evidence on Economic Preferences

**Authors:** Armin Falk, Anke Becker, Thomas Dohmen, Benjamin Enke, David Huffman, Uwe Sunde
**Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 133, Issue 4, November 2018, pp. 1645-1692
**DOI:** 10.1093/qje/qjy013
**Superkey:** PAP-enke2019globalpref

## Abstract

This article studies the global variation in economic preferences. For this purpose, we present the Global Preference Survey (GPS), an experimentally validated survey data set of time preference, risk preference, positive and negative reciprocity, altruism, and trust from 80,000 people in 76 countries. The data reveal substantial heterogeneity in preferences across countries, but even larger within-country heterogeneity. Across individuals, preferences vary with age, gender, and cognitive ability, yet these relationships appear partly country specific. At the country level, the data reveal correlations between preferences and biogeographic and cultural variables, such as agricultural suitability, language structure, and religion. Variation in preferences is also correlated with economic outcomes and behaviors. Within countries and subnational regions, preferences are linked to individual savings decisions, labor market choices, and prosocial behaviors. Across countries, preferences vary with aggregate outcomes ranging from per capita income, to entrepreneurial activities, to the frequency of armed conflicts.

## I. Introduction

Economic preferences determine how people make decisions about risk, time, and social interactions. Understanding these preferences is fundamental to economic analysis, yet comprehensive evidence on global variation has been lacking. This paper addresses this gap by presenting the Global Preference Survey (GPS), a new data set that measures six key economic preferences across 80,000 individuals in 76 countries.

### The Six Preferences

1. **Patience (Time Preference):** Willingness to delay gratification
2. **Risk Taking:** Willingness to take risks
3. **Positive Reciprocity:** Tendency to reward kind actions
4. **Negative Reciprocity:** Tendency to punish unkind actions
5. **Altruism:** Willingness to give to others without direct return
6. **Trust:** Willingness to trust strangers

### Research Questions

1. How do economic preferences vary across the world's population?
2. What demographic, cultural, and geographic factors predict preference variation?
3. How do preferences relate to economic outcomes at individual and country levels?

## II. Data

### II.A. The Global Preference Survey (GPS)

The GPS was collected as part of the Gallup World Poll 2012, using face-to-face interviews in most countries. The sample comprises:

- **76 countries** representing all inhabited continents
- **80,000 respondents** (approximately 1,000 per country)
- **Representative samples** of resident populations aged 15 and older
- **Standardized protocols** across all countries

### II.B. Preference Measures

Each preference was measured using survey items experimentally validated in a companion paper (Falk et al., 2016).

#### Patience

**Quantitative item:** "Imagine the following situation: you can choose between receiving a payment today or a payment in 12 months. How much would the payment in 12 months need to be for you to prefer this over receiving 100 € today?"
- Responses on 5-point scale from 101€ to 185€

**Qualitative item:** "How willing are you to give up something that is beneficial for you today in order to benefit more from that in the future?"
- 0-10 scale

#### Risk Taking

**Quantitative item:** Staircase risk task based on certainty equivalents
- Series of binary choices between lottery and safe amounts

**Qualitative item:** "How willing are you to take risks, in general?"
- 0-10 scale

#### Positive Reciprocity

**Qualitative item:** "When someone does me a favor I am willing to return it."
- 0-10 scale

**Gift-giving question:** "How willing are you to give a gift to someone after they helped you?"
- 0-10 scale

#### Negative Reciprocity

Three items measuring willingness to punish unfair behavior:
1. "If I am treated very unjustly, I will take revenge at the first occasion"
2. "If somebody puts me in a difficult position, I will do the same to him/her"
3. "If somebody offends me, I will offend him/her back"
- All on 0-10 scales

#### Altruism

**Quantitative item:** "Imagine you won a lottery of 1,000€. How much would you donate to a good cause?"
- Open-ended response

**Qualitative item:** "How willing are you to give to good causes without expecting anything in return?"
- 0-10 scale

#### Trust

"I assume that people have only the best intentions."
- 0-10 scale

### II.C. Experimental Validation

The survey measures were validated against incentivized experiments:
- **Sample:** 409 participants at University of Bonn
- **Method:** Participants completed both survey and incentivized tasks
- **Result:** Survey measures predict experimental behavior with substantial explanatory power

**Validation R² values:**
| Preference | R² (Survey predicts experiment) |
|------------|--------------------------------|
| Patience | 0.52 |
| Risk Taking | 0.47 |
| Altruism | 0.41 |
| Positive Reciprocity | 0.29 |
| Negative Reciprocity | 0.24 |
| Trust | 0.21 |

## III. Global Distribution of Preferences

### III.A. Cross-Country Variation

**Key finding:** All six preferences exhibit substantial cross-country variation.

**Figure I: World Maps of Preferences**

The paper presents world maps showing the geographic distribution of each preference, revealing distinct regional patterns:

- **Patience:** Highest in Western Europe and English-speaking countries; lowest in Africa and parts of South Asia
- **Risk Taking:** Highest in African countries; lowest in Eastern Europe and East Asia
- **Positive Reciprocity:** Relatively uniform globally with some variation
- **Negative Reciprocity:** Highest in Middle East and Africa; lowest in East Asia
- **Altruism:** Highest in Western countries; lowest in South Asia
- **Trust:** Highest in Scandinavia; lowest in Africa and South America

### III.B. Within-Country vs. Between-Country Variation

**Critical finding:** Within-country variation exceeds between-country variation for all preferences.

**Table II: Variance Decomposition**

| Preference | Between-Country (%) | Within-Country (%) |
|------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Patience | 12 | 88 |
| Risk Taking | 6 | 94 |
| Positive Reciprocity | 7 | 93 |
| Negative Reciprocity | 8 | 92 |
| Altruism | 8 | 92 |
| Trust | 9 | 91 |

**Implication:** Individual heterogeneity within countries is more important than country-level differences.

### III.C. Correlations Between Preferences

## IV. Individual-Level Determinants

### IV.A. Age Effects

**Figure II: Age Profiles of Preferences**

- **Patience:** Decreases with age (strongest effect)
- **Risk Taking:** Decreases with age
- **Positive Reciprocity:** Increases with age
- **Negative Reciprocity:** Decreases with age
- **Altruism:** Increases with age
- **Trust:** Increases with age

**Magnitude:** Moving from age 20 to 80 changes preferences by 0.15-0.30 standard deviations.

### IV.B. Gender Differences

**Table V: Gender Differences in Preferences**

| Preference | Male-Female Difference (SD) | p-value |
|------------|----------------------------|---------|
| Patience | -0.06 (women more patient) | < 0.001 |
| Risk Taking | 0.23 (men more risk-taking) | < 0.001 |
| Positive Reciprocity | -0.02 | 0.02 |
| Negative Reciprocity | 0.06 | < 0.001 |
| Altruism | -0.18 (women more altruistic) | < 0.001 |
| Trust | 0.01 | 0.28 |

**Key finding:** Gender differences are largest for risk taking and altruism.

**Country-specific patterns:** The magnitude of gender differences varies across countries. Notably, gender differences in risk and patience are larger in richer and more gender-equal countries.

### IV.C. Cognitive Ability

Higher cognitive ability (math skills) is associated with:
- Greater patience (r = 0.12)
- Greater risk tolerance (r = 0.04)
- Lower negative reciprocity (r = -0.04)
- Higher trust (r = 0.05)

### V.A. Biogeographic Variables

**Table VI: Biogeographic Correlates**

| Variable | Patience | Risk | Neg. Rec. | Altruism | Trust |

| Crop yield (potential) | 0.41*** | -0.15 | -0.31*** | 0.19 | 0.42*** |

**Interpretation:** Countries with historically favorable agricultural conditions have populations with greater patience and trust.

### V.B. Cultural and Linguistic Variables

**Language structure and preferences:**
- Languages with future time reference (FTR) are associated with less patience
- This replicates Chen (2013) using the GPS data

**Religion:**
- Protestant countries show higher patience and trust
- Muslim-majority countries show higher negative reciprocity

## VII. Robustness and Validation

### VII.A. Alternative Measures

Results robust to:
- Using only quantitative survey items
- Using only qualitative survey items
- Different aggregation methods

### VII.B. Selection and Response Biases

Addressed through:
- Gallup World Poll's rigorous sampling methodology
- Country-specific survey modes
- Extensive interviewer training
- Back-translation of all survey items

### VII.C. Experimenter Demand Effects

Mitigated by:
- Hypothetical framing of quantitative questions
- Inclusion in broader Gallup World Poll (not identified as economics study)
- Validation against incentivized experiments

## VIII. Discussion

### VIII.A. Key Contributions

1. **First globally representative preference data:** The GPS provides standardized measures across 76 countries.

2. **Within-country heterogeneity dominates:** Most preference variation is between individuals within countries, not between countries.

3. **Preferences predict behavior:** Both at individual and aggregate levels, preferences correlate with economically relevant outcomes.

4. **Cultural and geographic roots:** Preferences correlate with deep historical and cultural factors.

### VIII.B. Limitations

1. **Cross-sectional data:** Cannot establish causality or track changes over time.

2. **Survey measures:** Despite validation, may not capture all preference dimensions.

3. **Sample coverage:** 76 countries, but still missing some regions (e.g., many African and Asian countries).

4. **Potential confounds:** Country-level correlations may reflect omitted variables.

### VIII.C. Implications

**For economic theory:**
- Heterogeneous preferences should be a standard assumption
- Cultural and geographic factors deserve more attention

**For policy:**
- Interventions may need to account for preference heterogeneity
- One-size-fits-all policies may be suboptimal

---

## IX. Conclusion

The Global Preference Survey provides the first comprehensive picture of economic preferences around the world. Our findings document substantial variation in preferences both across and within countries, with individual-level heterogeneity being quantitatively more important. Preferences are systematically related to individual characteristics, cultural factors, and economic outcomes. These results suggest that accounting for preference heterogeneity is crucial for understanding economic behavior globally.

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **Within-country heterogeneity dominates:** 88-94% of variance is within countries
   - Segmentation matters more than country-level targeting
   - Individual differences outweigh cultural stereotypes

2. **Gender × Development interaction:** Gender differences in preferences are larger in more developed countries
   - Replicates findings on the "gender equality paradox"
   - Ψ_K (culture) × WHO (gender) interaction

3. **Biogeographic roots:** Historical agricultural suitability predicts current preferences
   - Deep historical Ψ_K effects
   - Patience and trust cluster geographically

4. **Experimental validation:** Survey measures validated against incentivized experiments
   - R² = 0.21-0.52 for different preferences
   - Enables large-scale preference measurement

5. **Universal measurement:** Same instruments work across 76 countries
   - Addresses external validity concerns
   - Enables cross-cultural parameter calibration
