## Abstract

Political disagreement is increasingly moral, rather than economic, in nature, raising the question how the fields of political economy and moral psychology relate to each other. While these disciplines were initially deeply intertwined, cross-disciplinary exchange became rare throughout the 20th century. More recently, the tide has shifted again—social scientists of different backgrounds recognized that morality and politico-economic outcomes influence each other in rich bidirectional ways. Because psychologists and economists possess distinct and complementary skill sets, part of this movement consists of productive "economic imperialism"—economists leveraging their empirical toolkit to test and substantiate theories from moral psychology at scale or in the wild. To illustrate this, I present two case studies of recent economics research on prominent ideas in moral psychology.

---

## Background

During the majority of the post–World War II period, politics was dominated by disagreement about economics. In recent years, however, **politics has become highly moralized**. Deep divisions over values and identity have taken center stage, and disagreements over what is "right" and "wrong" occupy much of the public discussion.

### Historical Context

At the origin of the social sciences, economics, politics and morality were deeply intertwined. Worldly philosophers such as Adam Smith, Jeremy Bentham, Karl Marx, or Alexis de Tocqueville routinely discussed the relationship between morality, economics, and the political process.

Then, over time, economics and political science diverged from psychology, and cross-disciplinary exchange about morality became rare. This divergence reflected differences in interests and methodological approaches:

- **Psychologists:** Focused on individual moral reasoning, rather than its interaction with broader social, economic, and political phenomena
- **Economists:** Reluctant to engage with morality partly because they valued the analytical traction that insisting on Homo economicus provided

### Recent Convergence

More recently, the tide has started turning again. Both fields came to recognize that **economics and morality influence each other in a rich bidirectional relationship**, in a way that directly impacts political outcomes.

**Key Insight:**
> "A prime example for the conceptual overlap between moral psychology and economics is the fact—which for outsiders is often surprising—that one of the most foundational ideas in modern moral psychology is partly economic in nature. A rich body of theories posits that morality is ultimately functional and reflects incentives—that it evolved as a form of 'psychological and biological police' to enforce prosocial behavior and cooperation, including in economic production and exchange."

## Complementarities in Social Sciences

### Why Economists Can Contribute

Economists possess distinct and complementary skill sets:

1. **Rich and sophisticated toolkit** for analyzing large amounts of data
2. **Entrepreneurial and technical skills** in navigating complex data structures
3. **Natural experiments** and quasi-experimental methods
4. **Large-scale surveys** and field experiments

### What Psychologists Contribute

Moral psychologists possess unique skills and methods:

1. **Beautiful experimental paradigms**
2. **Eye-opening vignettes**
3. **Rich questionnaires**
4. **Enormous creativity** in formulating hypotheses about human nature

### Productive Division of Labor

> "This, I believe, allows for a productive partial division of labor in the social sciences... The vast majority of the economics papers that I allude to subsequently do not contain truly original ideas or hypotheses—rather, they 'only' deploy the economics toolkit to test psychological ideas at scale, or in the wild."

## Case Study 1: Economic Incentives Shape Morality

### The Universalism-Particularism Cleavage

A **universalist morality** emphasizes:
- Equal treatment
- Relationship-independent values

A **particularist morality** highlights:
- Relationship- or group-specific principles
- Loyalty
- Treating in-group members well

> "The key difference between universalism and particularism is, hence, not who is 'more moral' but, instead, whether one's moral principles are primarily impersonal or tied to certain relationships."

### Three Contexts Where Economic Incentives Shape Morality

#### 1. Kinship Relations

**Enke (2019)** studies the effects of cross-cultural variation in historical kinship networks on moral systems:

- Societies with **tight extended families** structure interactions around family/clan → **particularist morality**
- **Loose kinship systems** go with more anonymous interactions → **universalist morality**

**Findings:**
- Tighter kin networks associated with less universalist values
- Strong emphasis on personal punishment
- High cultural salience of external shame (vs. internalized guilt)

#### 2. Market Exposure

**Key Hypothesis:** A universalist morality is more likely to emerge when market transactions among strangers take place.

**Evidence:**
- **Enke (2024):** Cultural folklore analysis of ~1,000 pre-industrial groups shows high market salience predicts universalist morality
- **Rustagi (2026):** Natural experiment in Ethiopia—villages closer to historical market developed stronger norms of generalized cooperation
- **Agneman & Chevrot-Bianco (2022):** Experimental evidence from Greenland

> "My reading of this body of work is that, while none of the suggested causal identification strategies is airtight, the evidence broadly supports the idea that markets foster a universalist morality."

#### 3. Ecology

**Key Hypothesis:** Some ecologies are conducive to intensive local cooperation—and should hence give rise to particularist morality.

**Evidence:**
- **Raz (2020):** Homogeneous soil → settlers share best practices → strong communal ties → particularist morality
- **Grosjean (2014), Cao et al. (2021):** Pastoralism (herding) → "culture of honor" → violence and conflict

> "Whether or not an ethnic group's distant ancestors practiced herding is strongly predictive of the frequency of conflict, including of civil conflict, today."

## Case Study 2: Morality Shapes Political Behavior

### Universalism and Voting

**Enke (2020):**
- Analyzed moral appeal of presidential candidates based on campaign speeches
- Candidates garner higher vote shares in counties where citizens' moral values match the politician's espoused values
- Moral values explain both across-party AND within-party differences

**Enke et al. (2024):**
- Linked donations data and voting records
- Estimated each Congressional District's universalism based on donation patterns
- Districts that donate more universally tend to be more Democratic
- Explanatory power of universalism for vote shares is **much larger** than economic variables (income, education, unemployment)

### Global Evidence

**Cappelen et al. (2022):**
- Data from 60 countries, 65,000 respondents via Gallup World Poll
- In essentially all "Western" countries, universalism strongly predicts left-wing policy views
- Universalism explains views on:
  - Taxation
  - Redistribution
  - Health care
  - Foreign aid

### Fairness Types

**Almås et al. (2020):**
- Identified three fairness types through incentivized experiments:
  1. **Egalitarians:** Equal outcomes
  2. **Meritocrats:** Outcomes based on effort
  3. **Libertarians:** Market outcomes

**Findings:**
- Scandinavians and Americans equally meritocratic
- More egalitarians in Scandinavia
- Fewer libertarians in Scandinavia
- Fairness types predict political views

### False Positives vs. False Negatives

**Cappelen et al. (2023):**
- Any redistributive policy trades off:
  - **False positives:** Some receive transfers who don't deserve them
  - **False negatives:** Some don't receive transfers who do deserve them

**Findings:**
- Majority prefer avoiding false negatives
- Strong partisan divide: **conservatives prioritize avoiding false positives**

## Toward Greater Integration

### Five Thoughts for Future Research

1. **Complementary strengths:** Psychologists should continue creative hypothesis development; economists test at scale
2. **Unexplored areas:** Respect, honor, social dominance deserve more attention
3. **Theoretical modeling:** Economics can formalize moral psychology ideas
4. **Market design:** Need to understand moral principles constraining transactions
5. **Interdisciplinary exchange:** Both fields gain from closer interaction

### Conclusion

> "I believe that both economists and moral psychologists stand much to gain from more closely interacting. The most compelling case for the broad proposition that politico-economic outcomes and morality influence each other is likely to emerge when interdisciplinary exchange takes place."
