## Abstract

Recent research from economics, psychology, cognitive science, computer science, and marketing is increasingly interested in the idea that people face cognitive costs when making decisions. Reviewing and synthesizing this research, we develop a framework of cognitive costs that organizes concepts along a temporal dimension and maps out when costs occur in the decision-making process and how they impact decisions. Our unifying framework broadens the scope of research on cognitive costs to a wider timeline of cognitive processing. We identify implications and recommendations emerging from our framework for intervening on behavior to tackle some of the most pressing issues of our day, from improving health and saving decisions to mitigating the consequences of climate change.

**Keywords:** Cognitive effort, Decision making, Cognitive resources, Opportunity cost, Algorithmic cost, Representations

## 1. What are Cognitive Costs?

Research from economics, cognitive science, neuroscience, and marketing is increasingly focused on the idea that decision-making is costly. A common thread is the assumption that human cognition is guided by a tradeoff between the quality of people's decisions and the cognitive costs associated with making them.

### Heterogeneity Across Disciplines

Despite commonality, there is heterogeneity across research fields in:
1. What is thought to be costly in cognition
2. How to measure these costs
3. How cognitive costs influence behavior

**Economics:** Cognitive costs as sacrificed opportunities, measured in monetary terms (willingness to pay)

**Cognitive Science/Neuroscience:** Costs operationalized in reaction times or neurophysiological markers; focus on representational costs and algorithmic costs

## 2. The Timeline of Cognitive Costs

### 2.1 Before the Decision: Pre-Decision Costs

#### Entry Costs

**Definition:** Costs of engaging with a decision at all; failure to represent a decision as actionable.

**Examples:**
- Not filing for tax exemptions because paperwork seems too daunting
- Not participating in stock market because it seems too complex
- Not considering retirement savings at all

**Measurement:** Related to task switching costs, but involves voluntary control to engage

#### Representational Costs

**Definition:** Costs of constructing and maintaining a mental representation of the decision problem before engaging with it.

**Key Properties:**
- Dynamic construct: Initial construction + ongoing maintenance
- More complex representations = higher memory cost
- "Functional fixedness" when stuck in particular representation

**Examples:**
- Remembering credit card number (high) vs. postcode (low)
- Complex categorization rules harder to learn than simple ones

#### Meta-Cognitive Costs

**Definition:** Costs of identifying and selecting a suitable algorithm/strategy for the problem.

**Key Properties:**
- Anticipated algorithmic costs before decision commences
- Costs of identifying viable strategies themselves are costly
- May lead to choice deferral if too high

---

### 2.2 During the Decision: In-Process Costs

#### Algorithmic Costs

**Definition:** Computational processing steps needed to implement a decision.

**Rooted in Computer Science:**
- Time complexity (runtime)
- Space complexity (working memory burden)

**Measurement:**
- Response time (longer = more processing)
- Speed-accuracy tradeoff

**Evidence Accumulation:**
- Sample noisy information
- Build up evidence for one option over another
- Select when threshold reached

#### Opportunity Costs

**Definition:** Costs measured relative to best available alternatives that must be sacrificed.

**Traditional Economics View:** Hedonic costs - reduction in personal well-being from engaging in laborious behavior vs. alternatives.

**"Sense of Effort":** People monitor opportunity costs with which they can reallocate mental processing to tasks offering greater return.

**Key Insight:** Both engaging in effortful task AND withholding action (boredom) can be costly.

**Measurement:**
- Money as universal, divisible, commensurate measure
- Willingness to pay to avoid cognitively laborious behavior
- Responsiveness to differences in rewards

---

### 2.3 After the Decision: Post-Decision Costs

#### Remembered Costs

**Definition:** Memory traces of incurred costs; routine and habit formation.

**Process:**
1. Evaluate implemented decision strategy
2. Store memory trace of incurred costs
3. Form routines/habits over repeated use
4. Reduce cognitive costs of repeated activities
5. May experience regret, changing future approach

## 3. Synthesis: The Temporal Framework

### Figure 1: Conceptual Timeline

```
PRE-DECISION                 DURING DECISION              POST-DECISION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Entry Costs → Representational → Meta-Cognitive → Algorithmic → Remembered
              Costs              Costs           Costs        Costs
                                                 ↓
                                           Opportunity
                                              Costs

Timeline: ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────►
          Engaging    Constructing   Selecting    Computing    Learning
          with        mental         strategy     decision     from
          problem     model                                    decision
```

### Key Insights

1. **Different costs have different cognitive consequences**
2. **Unifying principle:** Consider WHEN costs come into play
3. **Person may withdraw at any point** or not enter at all
4. **Interconnections exist** but placing each at primary accrual point facilitates intervention design

### Interconnections

- **Opportunity costs ↔ Algorithmic costs:** Sharing cognitive resources creates opportunity cost
- **Representational costs ↔ Algorithmic costs:** Maintaining representations requires computation
- **Meta-cognitive costs → Algorithmic costs:** Strategy selection involves computation
- **All costs:** Involve elements of sacrificed opportunity and algorithmic implementation

## 5. Intervention Details by Phase

### 5.1 Before the Decision Process Begins

**For Entry Costs:**
- External cues that prompt representation (ads, campaigns, reminders)
- Reduce red tape that prevents engagement
- Text message reminders (e.g., flu shots)

**For Representational Costs:**
- Information control: Remove extraneous information
- Provide cues about effective representation
- Graphic vs. text-based displays (e.g., interest compounding)
- Nutrition labels, carbon footprint labels
- Allow personalized/customizable design

**For Meta-Cognitive Costs:**
- Combat choice deferral by increasing availability of low-cost strategies
- Remove non-favorable options from consideration set
- Provide effective default options
- Design choice environment (e.g., school cafeterias)

### 5.2 During the Decision-Making Process

**For Algorithmic Costs:**
- Cognitive offloading: Tools or environmental adjustments
- AI and LLMs for adaptive, personalized support
- Calculators (pension, carbon footprint)
- Fact boxes communicating risks/benefits
- Summary formats for complex information

**For Opportunity Costs:**
- Direct incentivization (tax incentives, financial rewards)
- Align policy goals with individual goals
- Social nudges (comparison to neighbors)
- Gamification (badges, achievements)

### 5.3 The Post-Decision Phase

**For Remembered Costs:**
- Provide feedback (water/energy consumption)
- Highlight value of repeated practice
- Support routine/habit formation
- Offload routines (automated payments)
- Yearly reminders

## 6. Example Application: Junk Fees

**Problem:** Hidden fees added after purchase is agreed to.

**Temporal Analysis:**
1. **Representational costs:** Junk fees obfuscate important aspects of decision
2. **Algorithmic costs:** Disaggregated prices impose heavy cognitive burden
3. Even if possible to deduce best option, may be too costly to compute

**Intervention Implication:** Framework identifies multiple intervention points:
- Improve representation (transparent pricing)
- Reduce algorithmic costs (total price display)

## 7. Key Distinctions

### Cognitive Costs vs. Cognitive Effort (Box 2)

**Cognitive Effort:** Volitional deployment of cognitive resources towards a task

**Cognitive Costs:** Timing, resource, or neuro-metabolic costs arising from increased effort

**Key Insight:** Increased effort always imposes greater costs, but inverse need not be true (e.g., visual processing is expensive but not "effortful")

**Sense of Effort:** Phenomenological experience during mental tasks; related but distinct from costs

### Limited Cognitive Resources (Box 1)

**Resource Rationality:** Cognition guided by optimal use of limited computational resources

**Ecological Rationality:** Simple, effortless strategies can lead to accurate decisions if they fit environment structure
