# Six beliefs I have about inflation

**Author:** N. Gregory Mankiw (Harvard University)
**Journal:** Journal of Monetary Economics 148 (2024) 103631
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2024.103631
**Conference:** Spring 2024 NBER conference on "Inflation in the Covid era and beyond"
**Archived:** 2026-02-05

## Abstract

This essay discusses six of my beliefs about the inflation process that are not universally shared among economists.

## The Six Beliefs

### Belief 1: The Phillips curve is inexorable
- Phillips curve is best understood as CONDITIONAL relationship (on shocks), not unconditional
- Only alternative is full monetary neutrality (RBC theory) — few remaining adherents
- The short-run Phillips curve is "here to stay"
- Supports Akerlof (2002): "probably the single most important macroeconomic relationship"

### Belief 2: But the Phillips curve is not a very useful practical tool
- Blinder (1997) called it a "clean little secret" — would not say the same today
- Staiger et al. (1997): NAIRU for 1990 = 6.2%, but 95% CI = [5.1%, 7.7%] — huge range
- The search for reliable Phillips curve specifications is "like the search for the Holy Grail"
- Various slack measures tried: detrended GDP, short-term unemployment, labor share, vacancy rate, quit rate, U/V ratio
- Conclusion: don't expect much from Phillips curve as forecasting guide

### Belief 3: Disentangling supply and demand shocks is nearly impossible in real time
- NAIRU imprecision makes supply/demand attribution very difficult
- Expectations channel complicates further — indirect effect through expectations may dominate
- 2022 surge: supply chain disruptions OR excess demand OR loose policy expectations — likely all three with "indeterminate weights"

### Belief 4: Economists should escape the "cult of Calvo"
- Calvo (1983) model: elegant but inconsistent with inflation persistence data
- Doesn't generate enough inflation persistence (Mankiw 2001)
- Fischer (1977) contracting model: past expectations of current inflation (more plausible)
- Mankiw & Reis (2002) sticky-information model: similar timing to Fischer
- Speculative proposal: "inflation norms" rather than expected inflation (wage increases depend on other workers' wages, not just expected inflation)

### Belief 5: The monetary aggregates deserve more attention
- Jeremy Siegel predicted 9% inflation in May 2021 using M2 growth
- Fashionable to ignore monetary aggregates, but arguments for ignoring are weak
- Measuring money is hard — but so is measuring slack
- M2 poor track record pre-pandemic — but Phillips curve also poor track record
- Central bankers don't discuss M2 — but monetary economists shouldn't follow central banker rhetoric

### Belief 6: A target of "2" is better than "2.0"
- Significant digits matter — 2.0% implies false precision
- Fed should not worry about 1.6% inflation (rounds to 2)
- Should declare victory at 2.5%
- Suggests range target (1-3%) like some other central banks
- "More humility among central bankers and monetary economists would seem to be in order"
