# A Scientific Approach to Business Strategy

**Authors:** Mu-Jeung Yang, Raffaella Sadun, Nicholas Bloom

**Year:** 2023

**Journal:** Working Paper (Harvard Business School / NBER)

**DOI:** Not available

**Abstract:**

This paper develops a novel framework to measure the extent to which firms adopt a "scientific approach" to business strategy and decision-making. We conduct 262 extensive structured interviews with senior executives, primarily from the Harvard Business School alumni network, to quantify strategic practices across three dimensions: (i) Formalization of strategy, (ii) scientific Development of strategy, and (iii) scientific Implementation of strategy. We find significant heterogeneity in scientific strategy practices across firms, which is strongly correlated with firm size, profitability, and growth. To address endogeneity, we exploit a quasi-natural experiment arising from the restructuring of the core strategy course at Harvard Business School by Michael Porter in 1983, using a regression discontinuity design. We find that exposure to Porter's course had a significant causal impact on the Formalization of strategy but not on the Development or Implementation dimensions, suggesting that formal education in strategy may affect how executives articulate and conceptualize strategy rather than how they develop or implement it. We link our survey data to the U.S. Census of Manufacturing and the Longitudinal Business Database to validate our measures against objective firm-level outcomes.

## 1. Introduction

Business strategy is one of the most important topics in economics and management, yet it remains one of the least well understood from an empirical perspective. While there is extensive theoretical work on strategy -- from Michael Porter's competitive forces framework to the resource-based view of the firm -- there has been comparatively little systematic empirical measurement of how firms actually practice strategy. This gap between theory and empirical evidence is striking given the central role that strategy plays in determining firm performance and, ultimately, in shaping economic outcomes.

In this paper, we take a step toward closing this gap by developing a novel measurement framework for business strategy practices. Our approach draws on the epistemology of science -- specifically, the idea that the process of knowledge creation can be decomposed into distinct stages that range from theory formulation to hypothesis testing to learning from outcomes. We apply this framework to the domain of business strategy, creating a structured interview protocol that captures the extent to which firms adopt what we call a "scientific approach" to strategy.

Our measurement framework rests on a simple but powerful insight: the process of developing and implementing business strategy can be viewed through the lens of the scientific method. Just as scientists formulate theories, design experiments to test predictions, and update their beliefs based on evidence, business leaders can approach strategy with varying degrees of rigor, formalization, and evidence-based decision-making. The degree to which firms adopt such a scientific approach is what we seek to measure.

We decompose strategy practices into three broad categories. First, **Formalization** captures the extent to which a firm has a clearly articulated strategy -- a well-defined strategy statement, a deliberate scope and advantage, and a distinctive strategic position. Second, **Development** captures the process by which strategic decisions are made -- including how decisions are initiated, justified, peer-reviewed, and how alternatives are explored. Third, **Implementation** captures how strategic decisions are carried out -- including planning, testing, validation of causal mechanisms, communication, and learning from resistance.

To implement this framework, we conduct 262 structured interviews with senior executives, primarily drawn from the Harvard Business School (HBS) alumni network. Our interviews are modeled on the structured management practice surveys pioneered by Bloom and Van Reenen (2007), adapted for the domain of business strategy. Each interview lasts approximately 45 minutes and follows a detailed protocol designed to minimize subjective biases while capturing rich information about actual strategic practices.

Our first set of findings documents substantial heterogeneity in strategy practices across firms. We find that there is considerable variation in the overall strategy practices score, as well as in each of the three sub-dimensions. This variation is not simply noise -- it is systematically related to observable firm characteristics. In particular, we find that strategy practices are strongly positively correlated with firm size, as measured by the number of employees. This relationship is robust to controlling for industry fixed effects, interviewer fixed effects, and a rich set of noise controls.

We also examine the relationship between strategy practices and firm performance. Linking our survey data to the U.S. Census of Manufacturing, we find that firms with higher strategy practices scores have significantly higher markups (our measure of profitability). This relationship is robust to controlling for firm size, industry, and a range of other covariates. Moreover, we find evidence that the relationship between strategy practices and performance is moderated by environmental complexity and uncertainty. Specifically, strategy practices appear to be more strongly associated with profitability in industries characterized by greater complexity (as measured by the diversity of strategic decision types) and less forecast error dispersion.

To address the fundamental challenge of endogeneity -- the concern that the correlation between strategy practices and performance may reflect reverse causality or omitted variables -- we exploit a quasi-natural experiment. In 1983, Michael Porter restructured the core strategy course at Harvard Business School, introducing a more analytically rigorous approach to strategy based on his competitive forces framework. This restructuring represented a significant shift in how strategy was taught to MBA students at HBS, and it created a sharp discontinuity in the exposure of successive cohorts of MBA students to formalized strategic thinking.

Using a regression discontinuity design (RDD), we compare the strategy practices of executives who graduated from HBS just before and just after the 1983 course restructuring. We find that exposure to Porter's course had a statistically significant and economically meaningful impact on the Formalization dimension of strategy practices. Specifically, executives who were exposed to Porter's course score approximately 0.6 standard deviations higher on the Formalization sub-score compared to those who graduated just before the restructuring. However, we find no significant impact on the Development or Implementation dimensions.

This pattern of results is both interesting and informative. It suggests that formal education in strategy can have a lasting impact on how executives conceptualize and articulate their strategic positioning, but that it has less influence on the more process-oriented aspects of strategy development and implementation. This finding is consistent with the view that formalization represents a more "teachable" aspect of strategy, while the development and implementation processes may be more dependent on organizational context, experience, and other factors that are less amenable to classroom instruction.

We also link our survey data to the Longitudinal Business Database (LBD) to examine the relationship between strategy practices and firm growth. We find a positive and significant correlation between strategy practices and both firm size and firm growth, reinforcing the evidence from the Census of Manufacturing that scientific strategy practices are associated with better firm-level outcomes.

Our paper makes several contributions to the literature. First, we develop a novel measurement framework for business strategy practices that is grounded in the epistemology of science and captures multiple dimensions of how firms approach strategy. Second, we document substantial heterogeneity in strategy practices across firms and show that this variation is systematically related to firm size, profitability, and growth. Third, we provide causal evidence on the impact of formal strategy education on strategic practices, exploiting the Porter course restructuring at HBS as a quasi-natural experiment. Fourth, we validate our survey-based measures against objective firm-level outcomes from administrative data sources.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 discusses the conceptual framework and related literature. Section 3 describes our measurement approach and data collection. Section 4 presents our main empirical findings on the correlates of strategy practices. Section 5 presents the causal analysis using the Porter RDD. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. Conceptual Framework and Related Literature

### 2.1 The Scientific Method and Business Strategy

Our conceptual framework draws on the analogy between the scientific method and the process of business strategy. The scientific method, broadly construed, involves several key steps: (1) observation and theory formulation, (2) hypothesis generation, (3) experimental design and data collection, (4) analysis and interpretation, and (5) learning and theory revision. We argue that these steps have natural analogs in the domain of business strategy.

A firm that adopts a scientific approach to strategy would, first, have a clearly articulated theory of its competitive advantage -- what we call the Formalization of strategy. This corresponds to the theory formulation stage in science. Second, such a firm would develop its strategic decisions through a rigorous process of information gathering, peer review, and exploration of alternatives -- what we call the Development of strategy. This corresponds to the hypothesis testing and experimental design stages. Third, the firm would implement its strategic decisions with careful planning, testing, validation of causal mechanisms, and learning from outcomes -- what we call the Implementation of strategy. This corresponds to the analysis, interpretation, and learning stages.

This framework is related to but distinct from several strands of the existing literature. Most directly, it builds on the management practices measurement approach pioneered by Bloom and Van Reenen (2007, 2010), which has been widely used to study the relationship between management practices and firm performance across countries and industries. While the Bloom-Van Reenen framework focuses on operational management practices (such as monitoring, targets, and incentives), our framework focuses specifically on strategic practices -- the higher-level processes by which firms make and implement major strategic decisions.

### 2.2 Related Literature

Our work relates to several streams of academic research. First, it connects to the strategy literature in management, which has long debated the role of formal analysis versus intuition in strategic decision-making (Mintzberg, 1990; Porter, 1996; Gavetti and Rivkin, 2007). Second, it relates to the growing empirical literature on management practices and firm performance (Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al., 2013; Bloom et al., 2019). Third, it connects to the literature on decision-making processes in organizations (Eisenhardt and Zbaracki, 1992; Dean and Sharfman, 1996; Csaszar and Eggers, 2013).

The concept of a "scientific approach" to management has historical roots in the work of Frederick Taylor and the scientific management movement of the early 20th century. However, our focus is quite different from Taylor's. While Taylor was primarily concerned with optimizing operational processes through time-and-motion studies, we are concerned with the higher-level strategic decision-making process. Our framework is more closely aligned with the work of scholars like Herbert Simon, who emphasized the role of bounded rationality and organizational decision-making processes in determining firm behavior (Simon, 1947, 1991).

More recently, several scholars have argued for a more scientific approach to strategy and management. Camuffo et al. (2020) conducted a field experiment showing that entrepreneurs who were taught to use a scientific approach to decision-making achieved better performance outcomes. Felin and Zenger (2009) have argued that theory-driven strategy formulation is a key source of competitive advantage. Our work complements these studies by providing the first large-scale systematic measurement of the extent to which firms actually adopt scientific strategy practices.

Our causal analysis using the Porter RDD relates to a small but growing literature that exploits variation in educational experiences to identify causal effects on subsequent behavior and outcomes. Closest to our work is the study by Bernstein et al. (2016), which examines the impact of MBA education on entrepreneurship. Our use of the Porter course restructuring as a natural experiment provides a particularly clean source of variation because it represents a discrete, well-documented change in the content of a core required course that affected all students in the MBA program.

### 2.3 Strategy as a Scientific Process

To formalize our conceptual framework, we decompose the strategy process into three phases, each of which can be practiced with varying degrees of scientific rigor.

**Phase 1: Formalization.** This phase captures the extent to which a firm has a clearly articulated strategic theory. A firm with high formalization has a well-defined strategy statement that specifies the firm's competitive scope and the basis of its competitive advantage. The key questions are: Does the firm have an explicit strategy statement? Does the firm deliberately define the scope of its activities and the basis of its competitive advantage? Does the firm seek to differentiate its strategy from competitors?

**Phase 2: Development.** This phase captures the process by which strategic decisions are developed and evaluated. A firm with high development practices initiates strategic thinking proactively (rather than simply reacting to performance declines), justifies strategic decisions with formal analysis and evidence, subjects proposals to regular and effective peer review, systematically explores alternatives, and considers risks. The key questions relate to the rigor, breadth, and depth of the strategy development process.

**Phase 3: Implementation.** This phase captures how strategic decisions are carried out in practice. A firm with high implementation practices engages in careful implementation planning, tests predictions before full-scale rollout, validates causal mechanisms, communicates strategy effectively across the organization, and learns from resistance and setbacks. The key questions relate to the discipline and learning orientation of the implementation process.

Each of these phases can be measured along a continuum from highly intuitive and ad hoc to highly systematic and evidence-based. Our measurement approach, described in the next section, quantifies where each firm falls along this continuum for each phase.

## 3. Measurement and Data

### 3.1 Survey Design

Our primary data source is a structured interview survey of senior executives, designed to measure the three dimensions of strategy practices described above. The survey instrument consists of 14 questions, grouped into three categories corresponding to the three phases of our framework.

**Formalization (F1-F3):**
- F1: Strategy Statement -- Does the firm have an explicit strategy statement? How formal and detailed is it?
- F2: Deliberate Scope and Advantage -- Does the firm deliberately define the scope of its activities and the basis of its competitive advantage?
- F3: Strategic Distinctiveness -- Does the firm actively seek to differentiate its strategy from competitors?

**Development (D1-D6):**
- D1: Initialization -- What typically prompts strategic thinking? Is it reactive (responding to performance declines) or proactive (anticipating changes)?
- D2: Justification of Decisions -- How are strategic decisions justified? Through intuition, informal analysis, or formal evidence?
- D3: Regular Peer Review -- Are strategic proposals subject to regular, structured review?
- D4: Effective Peer Review -- How effective and substantive is the peer review process?
- D5: Exploration of Alternatives -- Are alternatives systematically explored before major strategic decisions?
- D6: Peer Review of Risk -- Are risks formally assessed as part of the strategy development process?

**Implementation (I1-I5):**
- I1: Implementation Planning -- How detailed and structured is the planning for strategy implementation?
- I2: Testing and Follow-up -- Does the firm test predictions and follow up on outcomes?
- I3: Validating Causal Mechanisms -- Does the firm seek to validate the causal mechanisms underlying its strategic choices?
- I4: Strategy Communication -- How effectively is strategy communicated across the organization?
- I5: Learning about Resistance -- Does the firm systematically learn from resistance and setbacks during implementation?

Each question is scored on a scale from 1 to 5, where 1 represents the least scientific approach and 5 represents the most scientific approach. The scoring is based on a detailed rubric that provides specific criteria for each score level, ensuring consistency across interviews. We use a "double-blind" approach in which the interviewer asks open-ended questions and then scores the responses according to the rubric, without revealing the scoring criteria to the interviewee. This approach, modeled on Bloom and Van Reenen (2007), is designed to minimize social desirability bias and ensure that scores reflect actual practices rather than aspirational statements.

We distinguish between "core" practices that are most directly related to scientific learning (printed in bold in the tables) and "support" practices that facilitate but are not exclusively associated with scientific decision-making (printed in normal font). Core practices for scientific learning include the initialization of strategic thinking (D1), exploration of alternatives (D5), testing and follow-up (I2), and validating causal mechanisms (I3). Support practices include elements like strategy communication (I4) and learning about resistance (I5), which are important for effective strategy but are not uniquely associated with the scientific approach.

### 3.2 Interview Process

We conducted 262 structured interviews between 2019 and 2021. The majority of our interviewees are drawn from the Harvard Business School alumni network, which provides us with a sample of executives who share a common educational background but lead firms of varying sizes, industries, and stages of development. We supplemented this core sample with additional executives from other sources to ensure adequate coverage of certain industries and firm sizes.

Each interview was conducted by one of a team of trained interviewers following a standardized protocol. Interviews lasted approximately 45 minutes on average and were conducted primarily by phone or video conference. The interview protocol follows a "funneling" approach, where initial open-ended questions are followed by more specific follow-up questions based on the interviewee's responses. This approach allows us to capture both the breadth and depth of strategic practices while maintaining a consistent structure across interviews.

To ensure data quality, we implemented several quality control measures. First, all interviewers underwent extensive training on the survey instrument and scoring rubric. Second, we conducted calibration sessions in which multiple interviewers scored the same interview independently to ensure inter-rater reliability. Third, we included several "noise control" variables in our analysis, including interviewer fixed effects, time of day, interview duration, ratings of interviewee expertise and honesty, and a non-CEO dummy.

### 3.3 Sample Characteristics

Our sample of 262 firms spans a wide range of industries, sizes, and ownership structures. Table 1 presents descriptive statistics for our sample.

**Panel A** reports firm and executive characteristics. The mean firm size in our sample is approximately 2,088 employees, but there is substantial variation, ranging from 1 to 96,500 employees. The mean firm age is 48 years, with a range from 1 to 395 years. Approximately 11% of firms in our sample are publicly traded, and 20% are family-owned. The executives we interviewed have an average age of 57 years, with an average tenure in their current position of about 14 years and an average tenure at their current company of about 17 years. About 9% of our interviewees are female. Approximately 37% have a bachelor's degree in business or economics, 4% have a bachelor's degree in engineering, and 71% have an MBA from HBS.

**Panel B** reports the distribution of strategy practice scores. The overall Strategy Practices score has a mean of 2.72 and a standard deviation of 0.57, indicating substantial variation across firms. Among the three sub-dimensions, Formalization has the highest mean (3.02), followed by Development (2.72) and Implementation (2.54). Within the Development dimension, the Exploration of Alternatives (D5) has the highest mean score (2.98), while within the Implementation dimension, Testing and Follow-up (I2) has the highest mean score (3.02), suggesting that some firms do engage in outcome monitoring. Implementation Planning (I1) has the lowest mean score (2.09), suggesting that detailed implementation planning is relatively rare even among sophisticated firms.

We successfully interviewed alumni from cohorts spanning from the 1950s to the 2010s, with the largest concentrations in the 1985-1999 graduation cohorts. The response rate is relatively stable across cohorts, averaging approximately 32%.

### 3.4 Construction of Strategy Practice Scores

Our main dependent variable, the **Strategy Practices** score, is constructed as a normalized z-score with unit variance, which is the sum of all 14 normalized strategy questions with mean zero and unit variance. We also construct z-scores for each of the three sub-dimensions: **Formalization** (F1-F3), **Development** (D1-D6), and **Implementation** (I1-I5). These sub-scores are calculated as the average of the normalized underlying questions within each category.

Missing observations are imputed at sample means, with imputation dummies included whenever observations are imputed. This approach ensures that we maintain a consistent sample size across specifications while accounting for the possibility that missingness may be non-random.

### 3.5 Econometric Approach

Our econometric approach follows two complementary strategies. First, we examine cross-sectional correlations between strategy practices and firm outcomes using OLS regressions of the form:

Y_i = alpha + beta * StrategyPractices_i + gamma * X_i + delta * NoiseControls_i + eta_j + epsilon_i

where Y_i is a firm outcome (such as log employment, markup, or growth), StrategyPractices_i is our strategy score, X_i is a vector of firm and CEO controls, NoiseControls_i includes interviewer fixed effects, time of day, interview duration, and interviewee ratings, and eta_j represents industry fixed effects.

The noise controls deserve special mention. Following Bloom and Van Reenen (2007), we include a set of variables designed to capture potential biases in the interview process: (1) interviewer fixed effects to control for systematic differences in scoring across interviewers, (2) the time of day of the interview to account for potential circadian effects on interviewee responsiveness, (3) interview duration to control for the depth of the interview, (4) the interviewer's rating of the interviewee's knowledge and reliability, (5) the interviewer's rating of the interviewee's apparent honesty, and (6) a dummy for whether the interviewee is the CEO versus another senior executive. These controls help ensure that our strategy practice scores reflect genuine differences in practices rather than artifacts of the interview process.

Second, we use the regression discontinuity design described in Section 5 to provide causal evidence on the impact of strategy education on strategy practices. This approach addresses the fundamental concern that the correlations estimated in the first approach may be driven by reverse causality (better-performing firms can afford to invest in strategy practices) or omitted variables (some unobserved factor drives both strategy practices and firm performance).

### 3.6 Complementary Data Sources

To validate our survey measures and examine the relationship between strategy practices and firm outcomes, we link our survey data to two administrative data sources from the U.S. Census Bureau.

**Census of Manufacturing (CMF) and Management and Organizational Practices Survey (MOPS).** We match a subset of our surveyed firms to the 2017 Census of Manufacturing and the 2015 MOPS. This matching is done through firm name and industry, and results in a matched sample of approximately 50 firms with roughly 100 establishments. From these data, we obtain information on firm sales, costs, employment, and capital stock, which we use to construct measures of markup (profitability) and other performance indicators. We also use the MOPS data to construct measures of environmental contingencies, including complexity and forecast error dispersion.

**Longitudinal Business Database (LBD).** We also match our surveyed firms to the LBD, which provides longitudinal information on employment and payroll for the universe of U.S. establishments with paid employees. This matching allows us to examine the relationship between strategy practices and both firm size (measured by total employment) and firm growth.

## 4. Empirical Results

### 4.1 Strategy Practices and Firm Size

We begin our empirical analysis by examining the relationship between strategy practices and firm size. Table 2 presents the results of OLS regressions of log employment on strategy practice scores, with various controls.

Column (1) shows a simple regression of log employees on the Strategy Practices score, controlling only for noise variables. The coefficient is 0.980 (s.e. = 0.141), indicating that a one standard deviation increase in Strategy Practices is associated with approximately 166% more employees (exp(0.980) - 1). This relationship remains strong after adding industry fixed effects (column 2: 0.864, s.e. = 0.126) and additional firm and CEO controls (column 3: 0.692, s.e. = 0.132).

Columns (4) through (7) decompose the Strategy Practices score into its three sub-dimensions. We find that all three dimensions -- Formalization (0.340, s.e. = 0.117), Development (0.597, s.e. = 0.131), and Implementation (0.391, s.e. = 0.134) -- are individually associated with firm size, with Development showing the strongest correlation. When all three sub-dimensions are included simultaneously (column 7), the coefficients for Formalization (0.256, s.e. = 0.109) and Development (0.501, s.e. = 0.142) remain statistically significant, while the coefficient for Implementation (0.162, s.e. = 0.145) becomes smaller and statistically insignificant.

The log firm age variable is also strongly significant, with a coefficient of approximately 1.0 across specifications, indicating that older firms tend to be larger. The strong correlation between strategy practices and firm size is consistent with the management practices literature (Bloom and Van Reenen, 2007) and suggests that larger firms tend to adopt more structured and formalized approaches to strategy.

### 4.2 Strategy Practices and Strategic Changes

Table 3 examines the relationship between strategy practices and the nature of strategic changes undertaken by firms. We ask each executive to describe up to three typical strategic decisions and record the number of strategic changes, the decision time (in weeks), and the implementation time (in weeks) for each.

Column (1) shows that firms with higher Strategy Practices scores tend to undertake more strategic changes: a one standard deviation increase in Strategy Practices is associated with approximately 14% more strategic changes (coefficient = 0.132, s.e. = 0.067, significant at the 10% level). Column (2) shows that these firms also take longer to make strategic decisions: a one standard deviation increase is associated with approximately 32% longer decision times (coefficient = 0.281, s.e. = 0.107, significant at the 1% level). Column (3) shows a positive but statistically insignificant relationship with implementation time (coefficient = 0.103, s.e. = 0.079).

When we decompose the Strategy Practices score, we find that the Development dimension drives the correlation with strategic changes (coefficient = 0.269, s.e. = 0.107, significant at the 5% level) and that the Implementation dimension drives the correlation with the number of changes (coefficient = 0.190, s.e. = 0.058, significant at the 1% level). The Formalization dimension shows no significant correlation with any of the three outcomes.

These results are consistent with the interpretation that firms with more developed strategy processes engage in more deliberate and extensive strategic decision-making, taking more time to evaluate options but also pursuing more strategic initiatives.

### 4.3 Strategy Practices and Profitability

Table 4 presents our key results linking strategy practices to firm profitability, measured as markup in the Census of Manufacturing. Markup is defined as sales minus operating costs (intermediate inputs, energy costs, wage bill) divided by operating costs.

Column (1) shows a simple regression of markup on Strategy Practices, controlling for log employment, noise controls, and industry fixed effects. The coefficient is 0.155 (s.e. = 0.069), significant at the 5% level, indicating that a one standard deviation increase in Strategy Practices is associated with a 15.5 percentage point increase in markup. This result is robust to adding log capital stock as an additional control (column 2: 0.180, s.e. = 0.070, significant at the 5% level).

Columns (3) and (4) explore interactions between strategy practices and measures of environmental contingencies. In column (3), we interact Strategy Practices with a measure of **Complexity**, defined as the sum of different strategic decision types (e.g., product innovation, M&A, geographic expansion) among the three typical strategic decisions described by each respondent, aggregated to the industry level. The interaction is positive and highly significant (0.202, s.e. = 0.067), suggesting that strategy practices are more valuable in more complex environments where firms face a broader range of strategic challenges.

In column (4), we interact Strategy Practices with **Forecast Error Dispersion**, measured as the standard deviation of forecast errors for sales across establishments within the same 4-digit NAICS industry, using the 2015 MOPS and 2017 Census of Manufacturing. The interaction is negative and highly significant (-0.351, s.e. = 0.090), suggesting that strategy practices are more strongly associated with profitability in industries with less forecast error dispersion -- that is, in industries where the environment is more predictable.

Column (6) interacts Strategy Practices with **Sales Persistence**, measured as the average AR(1) coefficient across establishments within the same 4-digit NAICS industry for 2006-2017. The interaction is positive and significant (0.189, s.e. = 0.089), suggesting that strategy practices are more valuable in industries where competitive advantages are more persistent.

Column (7) includes all three interactions simultaneously. The Complexity interaction remains positive and significant (0.177, s.e. = 0.048), the Forecast Error Dispersion interaction remains negative (though no longer statistically significant at conventional levels: -0.243, s.e. = 0.162), and the Sales Persistence interaction becomes smaller and insignificant (0.109, s.e. = 0.071). These results suggest that environmental complexity is the most robust moderator of the strategy-performance relationship.

### 4.4 Strategy Practices, Firm Size, and Growth in Census Data

Table 5 presents results from linking our survey data to the Longitudinal Business Database (LBD), which allows us to examine the relationship between strategy practices and both firm size and firm growth using administrative data.

Column (1) confirms the positive relationship between strategy practices and firm size (coefficient = 0.464, s.e. = 0.198, significant at the 5% level) in the Census data, consistent with our survey-based results in Table 2.

Column (2) shows that strategy practices are positively associated with one-year firm growth (coefficient = 0.047, s.e. = 0.012, significant at the 1% level). Column (3) shows a similar positive relationship with five-year firm growth (coefficient = 0.095, s.e. = 0.037, significant at the 5% level).

Column (4) explores whether the relationship between strategy practices and growth varies with CEO turnover. We interact Strategy Practices with a CEO Appointment dummy (equal to one from the first year of the CEO's tenure onward and zero prior to that year). The interaction is positive and significant (0.085, s.e. = 0.041, significant at the 5% level), suggesting that the positive association between strategy practices and firm growth is amplified when a new CEO is appointed. This is consistent with the interpretation that new CEOs who bring stronger strategic practices can have a particularly positive impact on firm performance.

### 4.5 Detailed Scoring Rubric and Question Content

To provide further detail on our measurement approach, we describe the scoring criteria for each of the 14 strategy questions. The scoring follows a 1-to-5 scale where higher scores indicate more scientific practices.

**Formalization Questions:**

*F1: Strategy Statement.* Score 1: No strategy statement exists; decisions are made ad hoc. Score 2: An informal or implicit strategy exists but is not written down. Score 3: A written strategy statement exists but is vague or generic. Score 4: A detailed written strategy statement exists with clear articulation of scope and advantage. Score 5: A comprehensive strategy statement is regularly reviewed and updated, with explicit links to competitive analysis and performance metrics.

*F2: Deliberate Scope and Advantage.* Score 1: The firm has no deliberate choices about scope or advantage; it simply does what it has always done. Score 2: Some awareness of competitive positioning but no formal analysis. Score 3: Informal analysis of competitive positioning with some deliberate choices. Score 4: Formal analysis of industry structure and competitive forces, with deliberate positioning decisions. Score 5: Rigorous, evidence-based analysis of competitive dynamics, with continuous monitoring and adjustment of strategic positioning.

*F3: Strategic Distinctiveness.* Score 1: No awareness of or effort toward strategic differentiation. Score 2: Some awareness of competitors but no deliberate effort to differentiate. Score 3: Active monitoring of competitors with some effort to differentiate. Score 4: Systematic analysis of competitive positioning with deliberate differentiation strategy. Score 5: Deep understanding of the competitive landscape with a clearly articulated and actively maintained distinctive strategic position.

**Development Questions:**

*D1: Initialization.* Score 1: Strategic thinking is purely reactive, triggered only by crises or performance declines. Score 2: Primarily reactive but with some anticipation of obvious changes. Score 3: Mix of reactive and proactive, with regular strategic reviews. Score 4: Primarily proactive, with systematic environmental scanning and early warning systems. Score 5: Highly proactive, continuously scanning the environment for subtle shifts and opportunities, integrating information from diverse quantitative and qualitative sources.

*D2: Justification of Decisions.* Score 1: Decisions are justified by gut feeling or the authority of the decision-maker. Score 2: Informal justification based on personal experience. Score 3: Some formal analysis, but decisions are still largely intuitive. Score 4: Systematic analysis with formal evidence, including financial modeling and market research. Score 5: Rigorous evidence-based justification with multiple analytical perspectives, sensitivity analysis, and explicit consideration of assumptions.

*D3: Regular Peer Review.* Score 1: No regular review of strategic decisions. Score 2: Infrequent, informal discussions about strategy. Score 3: Regular strategy meetings, but with limited rigor. Score 4: Regular, structured strategy reviews with clear agendas and preparation. Score 5: Highly structured, frequent strategy reviews with pre-circulated materials, clear decision processes, and follow-up accountability.

*D4: Effective Peer Review.* Score 1: Strategy discussions are superficial and dominated by a single voice. Score 2: Some discussion, but limited challenge or debate. Score 3: Moderate quality of discussion with some genuine debate. Score 4: High-quality discussions with genuine challenge, diverse perspectives, and constructive debate. Score 5: Exceptional quality of strategic discussions with deep intellectual engagement, systematic consideration of counterarguments, and a culture that values dissent and intellectual honesty.

*D5: Exploration of Alternatives.* Score 1: Alternatives are never considered; the first option is always chosen. Score 2: Alternatives are occasionally considered but not systematically. Score 3: Alternatives are regularly considered but in a limited way. Score 4: Systematic exploration of alternatives with formal comparison of options. Score 5: Comprehensive exploration of alternatives including creative options, with formal evaluation using multiple criteria and explicit consideration of option value and reversibility.

*D6: Peer Review of Risk.* Score 1: Risks are never formally assessed. Score 2: Informal risk awareness without systematic assessment. Score 3: Some formal risk assessment for major decisions. Score 4: Systematic risk assessment integrated into the strategy development process. Score 5: Comprehensive, quantitative risk assessment with scenario planning, stress testing, and explicit consideration of downside risks and mitigation strategies.

**Implementation Questions:**

*I1: Implementation Planning.* Score 1: No formal implementation planning; decisions are announced and expected to be carried out. Score 2: Minimal planning with basic timelines. Score 3: Moderate planning with milestones and responsibilities assigned. Score 4: Detailed implementation planning with clear milestones, responsibilities, resource allocation, and contingency plans. Score 5: Comprehensive implementation planning with detailed work-back schedules, resource requirements, risk mitigation plans, and regular progress reviews.

*I2: Testing and Follow-up.* Score 1: No testing or follow-up; outcomes are never formally assessed. Score 2: Occasional informal follow-up. Score 3: Regular follow-up on major decisions but without systematic testing. Score 4: Systematic testing with pilot programs or limited rollouts before full implementation, with formal outcome tracking. Score 5: Rigorous testing with controlled experiments or A/B tests, formal outcome measurement, and systematic comparison of actual versus predicted results.

*I3: Validating Causal Mechanisms.* Score 1: No attempt to understand why outcomes occurred. Score 2: Informal attribution of outcomes to strategic decisions. Score 3: Some effort to understand causal mechanisms but without rigorous analysis. Score 4: Systematic analysis of causal mechanisms with formal evaluation of alternative explanations. Score 5: Rigorous causal analysis using appropriate methods (e.g., natural experiments, difference-in-differences, careful process tracing) to validate the mechanisms underlying strategic outcomes.

Score 3: Strategy is communicated to middle management with some detail. Score 5: Exceptional strategy communication with multiple channels, feedback loops, and mechanisms to ensure alignment and understanding at all levels.

Score 4: Systematic effort to understand resistance, including root cause analysis and stakeholder engagement. Score 5: Proactive identification of potential resistance, systematic analysis of underlying concerns, and creative approaches to addressing resistance while preserving strategic intent.

### 4.6 Correlation Structure of Strategy Practice Sub-Scores

Appendix Table 2 examines the correlation structure among the three strategy practice sub-scores: Formalization, Development, and Implementation. We find that Development and Implementation are strongly positively correlated (0.486, significant at the 1% level), suggesting that firms that invest in rigorous strategy development processes also tend to invest in disciplined implementation. Formalization is positively correlated with Development (0.227, significant at the 5% level) and weakly negatively (but not significantly) correlated with Implementation (-0.032). There is also a significant positive correlation between Formalization and Development via the Formalization-Development interaction (0.162, significant at the 1% level).

Within the Formalization sub-score, we find that F3 (Strategic Distinctiveness) is the component most strongly correlated with Development (0.195, significant at the 5% level) and Implementation (0.155, significant at the 5% level), suggesting that firms that actively seek to differentiate their strategy are also more likely to have rigorous development and implementation processes.

### 4.6 Complementarity in Strategy Practices

Appendix Table 3 examines whether there is complementarity among the three dimensions of strategy practices -- that is, whether the combination of multiple dimensions is more valuable than the sum of their individual effects. We estimate models that include pairwise interactions and a three-way interaction of Formalization, Development, and Implementation, with markup as the dependent variable.

We find a positive and significant interaction between Development and Implementation (0.302, s.e. = 0.118, significant at the 5% level in column 1; 0.277, s.e. = 0.077, significant at the 1% level in column 2). This suggests that the value of rigorous strategy development is enhanced when it is accompanied by disciplined implementation, and vice versa. The interactions involving Formalization are not statistically significant, and the three-way interaction is negative but not significant. These results suggest that the complementarity in strategy practices is primarily between the Development and Implementation dimensions.

## 5. Causal Analysis: The Porter RDD

### 5.1 Background: Porter's Course Restructuring

In 1983, Michael Porter restructured the core strategy course at Harvard Business School. Prior to 1983, the strategy course at HBS was taught primarily using the case method, with a focus on general management and qualitative analysis of business situations. Porter's restructuring introduced a more analytically rigorous approach based on his competitive forces framework, which had been developed in his influential book "Competitive Strategy" (Porter, 1980) and subsequent academic work. The restructured course emphasized formal tools for analyzing industry structure, competitive positioning, and sustainable competitive advantage.

This restructuring had several key features that make it suitable as a natural experiment. First, it was a discrete change that affected all MBA students in a given cohort. All first-year MBA students at HBS were required to take the core strategy course, so the restructuring represented a universal treatment for each cohort. Second, the timing of the restructuring (1983) was driven by academic and curricular considerations rather than by any systematic change in the types of students enrolling at HBS or the types of firms they would go on to lead. Third, the restructuring represented a substantial change in the content and approach of the course, making it plausible that it could have lasting effects on students' approach to strategy.

### 5.2 Identification Strategy

We use a regression discontinuity design (RDD) to estimate the causal impact of exposure to Porter's restructured strategy course on subsequent strategy practices. The running variable is the MBA graduation year (or equivalently, the year of entry into the MBA program), and the cutoff is 1983 (the first graduating cohort to be fully exposed to the restructured course). This approach follows the standard RDD framework as outlined in Hahn, Todd, and Van der Klaauw (2001), Imbens and Lemieux (2008), and Lee and Lemieux (2010).

The key idea behind the RDD is that executives who graduated just before and just after the 1983 cutoff are nearly identical in all observable and unobservable characteristics, with the sole exception of their exposure to Porter's restructured course. By comparing the strategy practices of these two groups, we can isolate the causal effect of the course restructuring from other confounding factors that might drive the correlation between strategy education and strategy practices.

Formally, we estimate the following model:

Y_i = alpha + tau * D_i + f(X_i) + epsilon_i

where Y_i is the strategy practice score for executive i, D_i is an indicator for whether executive i graduated in or after 1983 (i.e., was exposed to Porter's restructured course), X_i is the graduation year (the running variable), and f(X_i) is a flexible function that captures smooth trends in strategy practices over graduation cohorts. The parameter tau is our coefficient of interest, capturing the causal effect of the Porter treatment.

We estimate this model using both non-parametric and parametric approaches. For the non-parametric approach, we use the local linear regression method with the optimal bandwidth selection procedure of Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012), with both nearest-neighbor (IK-NN) and plug-in (IK-PI) variance estimators. We also use the bandwidth selection procedure of Fuji, Imbens, and Kalyanaraman (2009). For the parametric approach, we estimate polynomial regressions of orders 2 (quadratic) and 3 (cubic) with the polynomial allowed to differ on each side of the cutoff. All standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust, following MacKinnon (2013).

Our identification strategy relies on the assumption that executives who graduated just before and just after the 1983 cutoff are comparable in all relevant respects except for their exposure to Porter's course. We test this assumption in several ways. First, we conduct McCrary tests for manipulation of the running variable, which test whether there is a discontinuity in the density of observations at the cutoff. Figure A4 presents the McCrary test results for both our survey sample and the broader BoardEx sample of HBS alumni. We find no evidence of manipulation -- the density of observations is smooth through the cutoff, both in our survey sample and in the broader population of HBS alumni.

Second, we conduct placebo tests using predetermined covariates. Appendix Table 6 examines whether there are discontinuities at the 1983 cutoff in variables that should not be affected by the course restructuring, such as gender, family CEO status, undergraduate degree type, tenure, and other demographic characteristics. We find no significant discontinuities in any of these placebo outcomes, supporting the validity of our identification strategy.

Third, we conduct placebo cutoff tests using alternative cutoff years (1990, 1991, 1992). Appendix Table 7 shows that there are no significant discontinuities in our strategy practice measures at these placebo cutoff dates, further supporting the validity of the 1983 cutoff.

### 5.3 RDD Results

Table 6 presents our main RDD results. **Panel A** reports results with Formalization as the dependent variable. Across four different specifications -- non-parametric with nearest-neighbor variance estimation (IK-NN), non-parametric with plug-in variance estimation (IK-PI), parametric with cubic control, and parametric with quadratic control -- we find a positive and statistically significant effect of the Porter treatment on Formalization. The estimates range from 0.582 to 0.845 standard deviations, with the baseline IK-NN estimate being 0.845 (s.e. = 0.424, significant at the 5% level). These results indicate that exposure to Porter's restructured course led to a substantial and lasting increase in the formalization of strategy among HBS MBA alumni.

**Panel B** reports results with Implementation as the dependent variable. Here, we find a negative and statistically significant effect across all specifications. The estimates range from -0.601 to -1.270 standard deviations, with the baseline IK-NN estimate being -1.216 (s.e. = 0.312, significant at the 1% level). This negative effect on Implementation is somewhat surprising and suggests that Porter's emphasis on analytical frameworks may have come at the cost of implementation-oriented practices.

Figure 5 presents a regression discontinuity plot for the Formalization score (F2 and F3), showing a clear upward jump at the 1983 cutoff. The sample averages within bins show a clear pattern: pre-1983 cohorts score below the mean on Formalization, while post-1983 cohorts score above the mean.

### 5.4 Disaggregated RDD Results

Appendix Table 5 presents RDD estimates for all 14 individual strategy questions, using five different RDD specifications. Among the Formalization questions, F2 (Strategy Scope) shows the most consistent positive effect, with significant estimates in three of five specifications. F3 (Strategic Differentiation) shows a positive effect that is significant in the parametric quadratic specification. F1 (Strategy Statement) shows no significant effect, consistent with the interpretation that Porter's course affected the analytical substance of strategic thinking rather than the mere existence of a strategy statement.

Among the Development questions, the results are mixed. D1 (Proactivity and External Focus) shows positive but inconsistent effects. D4 (Strategy Meetings: Involvement) and D5 (Consideration of Alternatives) show mostly negative effects.

Among the Implementation questions, I1 (Implementation Planning), I2 (Strategy Review and Follow-ups), I4 (Strategy Communication), and I5 (Resistance to Change) all show negative and often significant effects. This pattern reinforces the finding that Porter's course had a negative impact on implementation-oriented practices.

The overall Formalization sub-score shows a significant positive effect (0.845 in the baseline specification), while the overall Implementation sub-score shows a significant negative effect (-1.165 in the baseline specification). The Development sub-score shows no significant effect.

### 5.5 Robustness and Validity

We conduct several additional tests to assess the robustness of our RDD results.

**Bandwidth sensitivity.** Our baseline results use the optimal bandwidth selection procedure of Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012). We verify that our results are robust to alternative bandwidth choices, including the bandwidth selection procedures of Calonico, Cattaneo, and Titiunuk (2017).

**Functional form.** We estimate both non-parametric and parametric specifications, with the latter using cubic and quadratic polynomial controls for the running variable. Our results are qualitatively similar across all specifications, although the magnitude of the estimates varies somewhat.

**Manipulation tests.** As discussed above, McCrary tests show no evidence of manipulation of the running variable. Additionally, the density of observations is smooth through the cutoff in both our survey sample and the broader BoardEx sample.

**Placebo outcomes.** We find no significant effects on predetermined covariates (Appendix Table 6) or at placebo cutoff dates (Appendix Table 7).

**Sample composition.** We verify that the composition of our sample does not change discontinuously at the 1983 cutoff. There are no significant differences in gender, family CEO status, educational background, or tenure between pre- and post-1983 cohorts within the RDD bandwidth.

### 5.6 Interpretation

The finding that Porter's course increased Formalization but not Development, and actually decreased Implementation, is both surprising and informative. We interpret these results as follows.

First, the positive effect on Formalization is consistent with the view that Porter's competitive forces framework provided MBA students with a set of analytical tools for thinking about strategic positioning. The emphasis on industry analysis, competitive advantage, and strategic differentiation appears to have had a lasting impact on how these executives conceptualize and articulate strategy.

Second, the null effect on Development suggests that the process-oriented aspects of strategy development -- such as peer review, exploration of alternatives, and risk assessment -- are less amenable to classroom instruction. These practices may be more dependent on organizational context, leadership style, and accumulated experience.

Third, the negative effect on Implementation is perhaps the most thought-provoking finding. One possible interpretation is that the emphasis on analytical rigor in Porter's course may have led to a relative de-emphasis on the practical challenges of strategy execution. Alternatively, it is possible that the increased formalization of strategy led to a more top-down approach to strategy that was less effective in engaging the organization in implementation. A third possibility is that the negative effect reflects a cohort effect -- the post-1983 cohorts may have been more likely to lead firms during the 2000s and 2010s, when rapid technological change made traditional implementation approaches less effective.

## 6. Discussion and Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary of Findings

This paper develops a novel framework for measuring scientific strategy practices in firms and provides new evidence on the relationship between strategy practices and firm outcomes. Our framework decomposes strategy practices into three dimensions -- Formalization, Development, and Implementation -- that correspond to the key stages of the scientific method as applied to business strategy.

Using data from 262 structured interviews with senior executives, we document substantial heterogeneity in strategy practices across firms. We find that strategy practices are strongly positively correlated with firm size, profitability (measured as markup in Census data), and firm growth. The relationship between strategy and profitability is moderated by environmental contingencies: strategy practices are more valuable in complex environments and in industries with more predictable competitive dynamics.

To address endogeneity concerns, we exploit the 1983 restructuring of the core strategy course at HBS by Michael Porter as a quasi-natural experiment. Using a regression discontinuity design, we find that exposure to Porter's course had a significant positive effect on the Formalization of strategy but not on Development, and a significant negative effect on Implementation. This pattern suggests that formal strategy education can shape how executives conceptualize strategy but may have more limited effects -- or even unintended consequences -- for the process of strategy development and implementation.

Our findings have several implications. For researchers, they suggest that strategy practices are an important and measurable feature of firm heterogeneity that is associated with economically significant differences in performance. The decomposition into Formalization, Development, and Implementation provides a useful framework for future research on the determinants and consequences of different approaches to strategy. For practitioners and educators, our findings highlight both the value and the limitations of formal strategy education. While analytical frameworks can help executives think more clearly about strategic positioning, the development and implementation of strategy may require different types of knowledge and skills that are less easily transmitted in a classroom setting.

For managers and organizations, our results underscore the importance of investing not only in strategic analysis but also in the organizational processes and capabilities needed to develop and implement strategy effectively. The evidence of complementarity between Development and Implementation suggests that these process-oriented dimensions of strategy may be particularly valuable when pursued together.

### 6.2 Implications for Theory

Our findings have important implications for the theoretical understanding of business strategy. The fact that strategy practices vary substantially across firms and are systematically related to performance suggests that strategy is not simply a matter of making the right choices, but also of having the right processes for making and implementing those choices. This is consistent with the view, advanced by scholars like Gibbons and Henderson (2013), that organizational processes and capabilities are important sources of persistent performance differences among firms.

The decomposition of strategy into Formalization, Development, and Implementation provides a useful analytical framework for understanding different aspects of the strategy process. Our finding that these three dimensions are partially correlated but also partially independent suggests that they capture genuinely distinct aspects of how firms approach strategy. The evidence of complementarity between Development and Implementation further suggests that these dimensions should be viewed as a system rather than as independent components.

The RDD results provide particularly interesting theoretical insights. The finding that formal education affects Formalization but not Development or Implementation suggests that different aspects of strategy may be acquired through different channels. Formalization -- the ability to articulate a coherent strategic position -- may be a form of "analytical knowledge" that is relatively easily taught in a classroom setting. Development and Implementation, on the other hand, may be forms of "tacit knowledge" or "organizational capability" that are more dependent on experience, context, and the specific organizational environment.

### 6.3 Implications for Practice and Education

For business educators, our findings suggest that strategy courses may be most effective at teaching students how to think analytically about strategic positioning -- the Formalization dimension -- but may have less impact on the process skills needed for strategy development and implementation. This suggests that strategy education might benefit from incorporating more experiential learning approaches, such as simulations, fieldwork, and coaching, that can help develop the process skills associated with Development and Implementation.

For managers, our results underscore the importance of attending to all three dimensions of strategy. Having a well-articulated strategy (Formalization) is valuable, but it is not sufficient. Firms also need rigorous processes for developing strategic options (Development) and disciplined approaches to executing strategic decisions (Implementation). The evidence of complementarity between Development and Implementation suggests that investing in both simultaneously may be particularly valuable.

The finding that strategy practices are more valuable in complex environments has practical implications for resource allocation. Firms operating in environments with diverse strategic challenges may benefit disproportionately from investing in scientific strategy practices. Conversely, firms in simple, predictable environments may be able to achieve adequate performance with less formalized strategy processes.

### 6.4 Limitations

Our study has several important limitations that should be acknowledged. First, our sample, while diverse in terms of firm size and industry, is drawn primarily from HBS alumni. This may limit the external validity of our findings, particularly for firms led by executives without elite MBA training. Second, our survey captures strategy practices at a single point in time, which limits our ability to examine the dynamics of strategy practices and their evolution over time. Third, while the RDD provides causal evidence on the impact of strategy education, the correlational evidence linking strategy practices to firm performance should be interpreted with caution given the potential for reverse causality and omitted variable bias. Fourth, our measures of strategy practices are based on self-reports from a single informant (the CEO or senior executive), which may be subject to common method bias and social desirability effects, although our double-blind scoring approach is designed to mitigate these concerns.

### 6.5 Future Directions

Several important questions remain for future research. First, what organizational practices and leadership behaviors are most effective in developing scientific strategy practices? Second, how do strategy practices evolve over time within firms, and what triggers changes in strategy practices? Third, how do strategy practices interact with other organizational practices (such as management practices and innovation practices) in determining firm performance? Fourth, can the framework be extended to capture additional dimensions of strategy, such as the role of experimentation, iteration, and adaptation in dynamic environments?

We hope that our measurement framework and initial empirical findings will stimulate further research on these important questions and contribute to a deeper understanding of how firms make and implement strategic decisions.

**Appendix Table 3: Complementarity in Strategy Practices (Census of Manufacturing).** The interaction of Development and Implementation is positive and significant (0.302** in column 1, 0.277*** in column 2), suggesting complementarity between these two dimensions. Other pairwise interactions and the three-way interaction are not significant.

**Appendix Table 4: Other Firm and CEO Correlates of the Strategy Score.** We find that relative position tenure is negatively associated with strategy practices (-0.272**, s.e. = 0.117), suggesting that executives who have been in their position longer relative to their total company tenure tend to have lower strategy scores. Executive age is strongly negatively associated with strategy practices (-1.440***, s.e. = 0.290 for log executive age; -1.219***, s.e. = 0.387 for log academic CEO age), suggesting that younger executives tend to adopt more scientific strategy practices. Public firms have significantly higher strategy scores (0.742***, s.e. = 0.200) compared to private firms.

**Appendix Table 5: Porter RDD estimates for all questions, with different RDD specifications.** This table presents RDD estimates for each of the 14 individual strategy questions plus the three sub-scores, using five different estimation approaches: IK-NN, IK-PI, FIK, parametric quadratic, and parametric cubic. Key findings: F2 (Strategy Scope) shows consistently positive effects; I1, I2, I4, I5 show consistently negative effects; the overall Formalization score shows a positive effect while the overall Implementation score shows a negative effect.

**Appendix Table 6: Placebo Outcomes.** Panel A examines gender and family CEO status; Panel B examines undergraduate degree type (engineering, business/economics); Panel C examines tenure (company and position). No significant discontinuities are found at the 1983 cutoff for any of these placebo outcomes, supporting the validity of the RDD design.

**Appendix Table 7: 1990-1991 Recession Placebo.** We test for discontinuities at placebo cutoff years of 1990, 1991, and 1992. Panel A (Formalization) shows no significant effects at any placebo cutoff. Panel B (Implementation) shows a marginally significant positive effect at the 1992 cutoff (0.610*, s.e. = 0.357), but this is in the opposite direction from the main finding and likely reflects noise. These results support the interpretation that the discontinuity at 1983 is driven by the Porter course restructuring rather than by other secular trends.
