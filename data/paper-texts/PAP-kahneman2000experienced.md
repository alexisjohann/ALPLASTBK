## Abstract

This chapter develops a moment-based approach to experienced utility, distinguishing it from decision utility. The concept of utility has carried two different meanings: Bentham's original conception of pleasure and pain (experienced utility) versus the modern economic usage based on revealed preferences (decision utility). The chapter presents arguments that experienced utility can be usefully measured through moment-based methods that assess the experience of the present, and develops a conception of "objective happiness" derived from temporal distributions of moment-utility.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Two Concepts of Utility

**Experienced Utility (Bentham, 1789):** Refers to the experiences of pleasure and pain - the "sovereign masters" that "point out what we ought to do, as well as determine what we shall do."

**Decision Utility (Modern Economics):** The utility of outcomes as inferred from observed choices; utility is inferred from preferences and used to explain choices.

### 1.2 Key Thesis

Contrary to the behaviorist position that led to the abandonment of Bentham's notion, experienced utility can be usefully measured through moment-based methods.

### 1.3 Three Main Goals

1. Present a detailed analysis of experienced utility and the relation between pleasure/pain of moments and utility of extended episodes
2. Argue that experienced utility is best measured by moment-based methods assessing present experience
3. Develop a moment-based conception of "objective happiness"

---

## 2. Two Approaches to Experienced Utility

### 2.1 Memory-Based Approach

- Accepts retrospective evaluations as valid data
- **Remembered utility:** Subject's global assessment of past episodes
- Problems: Duration neglect, Peak/End rule

### 2.2 Moment-Based Approach

- Derives experienced utility from real-time measures of pleasure and pain
- **Moment-utility:** Valence (good/bad) and intensity (mild/extreme) of current affective experience
- **Total utility:** Derived exclusively from the record of moment-utilities

---

## 3. Memory-Based Assessment: Problems

### 3.1 The Colonoscopy Study

**Method:** Patients report pain intensity every 60 seconds during colonoscopy on 0-10 scale.

**Figure 1 Data:**
- Patient A: Short duration, ended with high pain
- Patient B: Longer duration, ended with lower pain

### 3.2 Duration Neglect

**Key Finding:** Correlation between duration and subsequent evaluation = 0.03

Duration varies from 4 to 69 minutes, but remembered utility shows no relationship to duration.

**Hypothesis:** "Evaluation by moments" - remembered utility is determined by constructing a composite representative moment.

### 3.3 The Peak/End Rule

**Empirical Rule:** Retrospective evaluation is predicted by:

$$\text{Remembered Utility} \approx \frac{\text{Peak Pain} + \text{End Pain}}{2}$$

Correlation with actual retrospective reports: r = 0.67

**Result:** Patient A retained worse memory despite objectively shorter/better experience.

### 3.4 Violations of Dominance

**Clinical Experiment (N = 682):**
- Half assigned to extended procedure (colonoscope left in place ~1 minute after examination)
- The extra minute is uncomfortable but not very painful
- Lowers Peak/End average

**Result:** Retrospective evaluations were significantly more favorable in the prolonged procedure group.

**Sound Experiment:**
- Option A: 10 seconds at 78 db
- Option B: 10 seconds at 78 db + 4 seconds at 66 db

Most participants chose the longer sound (Option B) to repeat - a violation of dominance explained by Peak/End rule.

## 4. Moment-Based Assessment: Total Utility

### 4.1 Six Conditions for Valid Measurement

**Measurement Requirements:**

1. **Inclusiveness:** Utility profile is sufficient statistic; measure incorporates all relevant aspects (satiation, adaptation, anticipation)

2. **Ordinal measurement across situations:** Experiences of different types (stubbed toe, humiliating rebuke) must be measured on common scale

3. **Distinctive neutral point:** "Neither pleasant nor unpleasant" anchors the scale, permits comparisons across situations and persons

4. **Interpersonal comparability:** Scale permits comparisons of individuals and groups

**Normative Assumptions:**

5. **Separability:** Order of moment-utilities does not affect total utility (order effects already incorporated into moment-utilities themselves)

6. **Time neutrality:** All moments weighted alike in total utility (unlike decision utility which discounts, or remembered utility which overweights endings)

### 4.2 Measures of Total Utility

**Decumulative Representation:** Shows time spent at or above each utility level

**With Cardinal Measurement:**

$$\text{Total Utility} = \int_0^T u(t) \, dt$$

where u(t) is moment-utility at time t.

**Formal Axioms (Kahneman, Wakker & Sarin, 1997):**

1. Global utility unaffected by concatenation with neutral utility profile
2. Increases in moment-utility do not decrease global utility
3. Replacing one profile with higher global utility increases concatenation's global utility

**Theorem:** These axioms hold iff there exists a value transformation such that global utility equals the temporal integral of transformed moment-utility.

## 5. Objective Happiness: Concept and Measure

### 5.1 Definition

**Objective Happiness:** A moment-based construct derived from temporal distributions of moment-utility, characterizing:
- Individuals (George, Helen)
- Groups (Californians, paraplegics)
- Settings (Washington subway, workplace)

The term "objective" refers to assessment by objective rules; ultimate data are subjective experiences.

### 5.2 The Affect Grid

Two-dimensional structure of affect:
- **Valence:** Good to neutral to bad
- **Arousal:** Frenetic to lethargic

**Figure 3:** Affective Space
```
                    Arousal (+)
                        |
    Agitated Distress   |   Joyful Enthusiasm
                        |
Distress (-)  ----------+---------- Pleasure (+)
                        |
    Apathetic Depression|   Serene Pleasure
                        |
                    Arousal (-)
```

### 5.3 Evaluation Against Four Criteria

**1. Inclusiveness:**
- Objective happiness is only one constituent of well-being
- Not incompatible with other criteria (meaning, goals, flow)
- A life meaningful and cheerful > meaningful but sad

**2. Ordinal Measurement:**
- Can compare physical and emotional pain
- Coherence tested by correspondence between rankings and ratings

**3. Absolute Zero Point:**
- "Neither pleasant nor unpleasant"
- Can be used to match experiences across time and individuals

**4. Interpersonal Comparability:**
- High inter-subject agreement on psychophysical functions
- Observer ratings of colonoscopy patients correlated r = 0.70 with patients' own evaluations
- Demonstrates agreement across patients in scale use

### 5.4 Physiological Validation

**Prefrontal Asymmetry (Davidson, 1998):**
- Left prefrontal activity → Positive affect
- Right prefrontal activity → Negative affect
- Stroke in left (happier) hemisphere produces greater misery
- Individual differences highly correlated with personality measures

**Implication:** The function relating self-reports to brain states is quite similar across people.

## 6. The Ambiguity of Treadmill Effects

### 6.1 Treadmill Effects Defined

**Observation:** Life circumstances make only small contribution to happiness variance.

**Examples:**
- Japan 1958-1987: Real income ×5, no increase in happiness
- Lottery winners not much happier than controls
- Paraplegics not much unhappier (Brickman, Coates & Janoff-Bulman, 1978)

### 6.2 The Hedonic Treadmill (Brickman & Campbell, 1971)

**Mechanism:** Adaptation level (Helson, 1964)
- Experience of stimulus drifts toward neutral
- As one adapts, the subjective value changes

### 6.3 The Satisfaction Treadmill (New Hypothesis)

**Mechanism:** Aspiration level (Irwin, 1944)
- People are satisfied when they attain aspiration level
- Aspirations correlate with past attainments

**Helen Example:**
- Graduate student → Lucrative job → Better restaurants
- Satisfaction rises initially, then returns to baseline

**Two Possible Explanations:**

| Treadmill Type | Mechanism | Implication |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Hedonic** | Adaptation level | Pleasure from food returns to baseline; Helen enjoys food equally |
| **Satisfaction** | Aspiration level | Helen still enjoys food more but requires more to report satisfaction |

### 6.4 Critical Distinction

**Hedonic Treadmill:** Californians do NOT actually enjoy life more; objective happiness is the same.

**Satisfaction Treadmill:** Californians DO enjoy life more but have higher standards for declaring themselves happy.

**Resolution:** Can only be determined by measuring objective happiness directly through moment-based methods.

## 7. Four Concepts of Utility

### 7.1 Complete Taxonomy

| Concept | Definition | Based On |
|---------|------------|----------|
| **Decision Utility** | Weight in decisions | Observed preferences |
| **Predicted Utility** | Belief about future experienced utility | Affective forecasting |
| **Total Utility** | Moment-based experienced utility | Aggregated moment-utilities |
| **Remembered Utility** | Memory-based experienced utility | Retrospective assessments |

### 7.2 Research Implications

- Decision utility: Almost exclusive topic in economics
- Remembered utility: Almost exclusive topic in SWB research
- Total utility and predicted utility: Underexplored

### 7.3 Policy Implications

**Questions addressable:**
- Does presence of trees affect mood of pedestrians?
- What is contribution of attractive subway to well-being?
- What are well-being consequences of inflation, unemployment, unreliable health insurance?

**Conventional Economics:** Only willingness to pay

**Proposed:** Include moment-based measures of actual experience in policy assessment

### Critical EBF Implications

1. **Decision ≠ Experienced Utility:**
   - What people choose may not maximize their experienced well-being
   - Duration neglect leads to systematic errors
   - Peak/End rule distorts retrospective evaluation

2. **Moment-Based Measurement:**
   - Experience Sampling Methodology (ESM) as gold standard
   - Physiological correlates (prefrontal asymmetry) provide validation
   - Affect grid: Simple 2D representation of moment-utility

3. **Treadmill Ambiguity:**
   - Cannot distinguish hedonic from satisfaction treadmill without moment-based measures
   - Major implication for well-being research methodology
   - Policy implications depend on which treadmill operates

4. **Objective Happiness Feasible:**
   - Interpersonal comparability demonstrated (r = 0.70)
   - Distinctive zero point ("neither pleasant nor unpleasant")
   - Ordinal measurement across situations possible

5. **Six Conditions for Valid Measurement:**
   - Framework for evaluating well-being measures
   - Separability and time neutrality as normative assumptions
   - Inclusiveness requirement

## Appendix: Formal Theorem

### A.1 Axioms for Temporal Integration

**Given:** Utility profiles U = {u(t) : t ∈ [0,T]}

**Axiom 1:** Concatenation with neutral profile leaves global utility unchanged.

**Axiom 2:** Increases in moment-utility do not decrease global utility.

**Axiom 3:** Replacing profile with higher global utility increases concatenation.

### A.2 Theorem (Kahneman, Wakker & Sarin, 1997)

These axioms hold if and only if there exists a non-decreasing value function v(·), with v(0) = 0, such that:

$$G(U) = \int_0^T v(u(t)) \, dt$$

where G(U) is global utility of profile U.

### A.3 Implication

The transformed moment-utility scale is calibrated by duration:
- If 1 minute at pain=7 ~ 2 minutes at pain=6
- Then v(7) = 2 × v(6)

## Appendix: Memory vs. Moment Comparison

### Key Differences

| Feature | Memory-Based | Moment-Based |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Data source | Retrospective report | Real-time report |
| Duration weighting | Neglected | Equal |
| Peak weighting | Overweighted | Equal |
| End weighting | Overweighted | Equal |
| Dominance | Violated | Preserved |
| Interpersonal comparison | Problematic | Feasible |

### When Each Matters

**Memory-based matters when:**
- Decisions based on remembered utility
- Stories and narratives important
- Identity formation

**Moment-based matters when:**
- Actual well-being is the criterion
- Policy evaluation
- Comparing groups objectively
