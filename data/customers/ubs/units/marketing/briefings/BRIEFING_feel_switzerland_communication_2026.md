# Executive Summary

UBS's Feel Switzerland programme represents a distinctive value proposition connecting clients to Swiss culture, sports, and lifestyle through exclusive access to UBS's extensive sponsorship portfolio. The programme offers 20-50% discounts on tickets across premium cultural and sporting events – from Zurich Film Festival to Art Basel, from Super League football to the Opernhaus Zürich.

The strategic challenge is communication effectiveness: How can Feel Switzerland be communicated in a way that is simple, compelling, and behaviorally activating – while the eligibility conditions (particularly for adults requiring an investment product) are framed positively rather than as barriers?

**The core challenge:** Should UBS communicate the full breadth of Feel Switzerland benefits, or focus on a few compelling advantages? How should eligibility conditions be framed to maximise attractiveness and activation while respecting fairness preferences? And how do optimal communication strategies differ between adult and youth segments?

This engagement will deliver a comprehensive communication briefing with segment-specific recommendations, eligibility framing strategies, and behavioural activation mechanisms grounded in behavioural economics principles.

# Question 1: Communication Strategy Framework

## The Strategic Question

How can Feel Switzerland be communicated in the most simple, attractive, and behaviourally effective way – and should UBS focus communication on a few key benefits or communicate the full breadth of the offering?

## Part A: Focused vs. Broad Communication Analysis

The fundamental strategic tension requires rigorous analysis: UBS currently communicates the full breadth of Feel Switzerland benefits (status quo). The hypothesis to be tested is whether a more focused communication approach might be more effective.

### Behavioural Economics Perspective

The consultant must analyse this question through established behavioural principles:

**Choice Overload and Decision Paralysis**
- At what point does breadth of offering create cognitive overload?
- How does the number of communicated benefits affect processing fluency?
- What is the relationship between perceived complexity and action likelihood?

**Attention and Salience**
- Which benefits are most salient to which segments?
- How does attention allocation affect benefit perception?
- What makes certain benefits "anchor" the overall value proposition?

**Mental Accounting and Categorisation**
- How do customers mentally categorise the Feel Switzerland benefits?
- Does broad communication create a coherent mental category or fragmentation?
- How does categorisation affect perceived value?

### Empirical Questions to Address

| Question | Method | Output |
|:---------|:-------|:-------|
| Does focused communication increase recall? | Literature review, concept testing | Recall metrics by approach |
| Does focused communication increase perceived value? | Conjoint-style analysis | Value perception scores |
| Does focused communication increase action intent? | Behavioural prediction | Conversion likelihood |
| What is the optimal number of benefits to communicate? | Threshold analysis | Recommendation with rationale |

## Part B: Segment-Specific Communication Analysis

Communication effectiveness likely differs significantly between segments. The consultant must analyse:

### Adult Segment Communication

**Contextual Factors (Ψ-Framework)**
- What are the decision contexts in which adults encounter Feel Switzerland messaging?
- What competing demands on attention exist?
- What are the primary motivations for cultural and sporting engagement?

**Value Proposition Resonance**
- Which benefit categories resonate most strongly?
- What is the relative importance of discount magnitude vs. exclusivity vs. experience quality?
- How does Feel Switzerland connect to broader life goals and identity?

### Youth Segment Communication

**Contextual Factors (Ψ-Framework)**
- Where and how do youth encounter financial services messaging?
- What are the barriers to engagement with bank-sponsored programmes?
- What social and identity factors influence programme attractiveness?

**Value Proposition Resonance**
- Which events and experiences are most relevant to youth segments?
- How important is social shareability and peer effects?
- What role does price sensitivity play vs. access and exclusivity?

## Part C: Message Architecture Design

The engagement must develop concrete message architecture recommendations:

### Core Message Development

- What is the single most compelling statement of Feel Switzerland value?
- How should the programme be positioned relative to competitors and alternatives?
- What emotional and rational appeals are most effective?

### Benefit Hierarchy

- If focusing communication, which benefits should be prioritised?
- How should secondary benefits be introduced over time?
- What is the optimal sequencing of benefit communication?

### Channel-Specific Adaptation

- How should messaging be adapted for different channels (digital, branch, direct mail)?
- What are the constraints and opportunities of each channel?
- How does message length and complexity vary by channel?

## Deliverable Requirements

1. Comparative analysis of focused vs. broad communication approaches with behavioural economics rationale
2. Optimal benefit communication strategy with recommended number of focal benefits
3. Segment-specific message architecture for adults and youth
4. Benefit hierarchy with prioritisation rationale
5. Channel-specific messaging guidelines
6. A/B testing framework for validating recommendations

# Question 3: Eligibility Communication Design

## The Strategic Question

How can the eligibility conditions for Feel Switzerland – particularly the investment product requirement for adults – be communicated in a way that is perceived positively, does not violate fairness preferences, maximises programme attractiveness, and activates behaviour?

## Part A: Fairness Perception Analysis

The eligibility structure creates a potential fairness challenge: adults must have an investment product to qualify, while youth have simplified access. The consultant must analyse:

### Fairness Preference Framework

**Equity Considerations**
- How do customers perceive the fairness of tiered eligibility?
- What reference points do customers use to evaluate fairness?
- How does the investment requirement compare to expectations?

**Procedural Fairness**
- Is the eligibility determination process perceived as fair?
- How transparent should eligibility criteria be?
- What appeals or exceptions processes should exist?

**Distributive Fairness**
- Is the distribution of benefits perceived as appropriate?
- How do non-eligible customers perceive the programme?
- What is the risk of negative sentiment from excluded groups?

### Framing Effects on Fairness Perception

Different frames can dramatically affect how eligibility conditions are perceived:

| Frame Type | Example | Likely Perception |
|:-----------|:--------|:------------------|
| **Exclusive Reward** | "For our most valued clients" | Positive (aspiration) |
| **Loyalty Recognition** | "Thank you for your trust in UBS" | Positive (reciprocity) |
| **Qualification Barrier** | "You must have an investment product" | Negative (exclusion) |
| **Membership Tier** | "Gold members enjoy these benefits" | Neutral to positive |
| **Earned Benefit** | "Your relationship unlocks exclusive access" | Positive (achievement) |

## Part B: Eligibility Framing Strategies

### Reframing from Barrier to Benefit

The consultant must develop framing strategies that transform eligibility conditions from perceived barriers into perceived benefits:

**Value-Based Framing**
- How can the investment product requirement be framed as adding value rather than creating restriction?
- What is the narrative that connects investment products to Feel Switzerland benefits?
- How can eligibility be positioned as membership in an exclusive community?

**Reciprocity Framing**
- How can eligibility be framed as UBS reciprocating customer commitment?
- What language emphasises the mutual relationship?
- How does this connect to Swiss banking tradition and values?

**Aspiration Framing**
- How can eligibility motivate non-qualifying customers to take action?
- What is the pathway communication for prospects?
- How can eligibility create goal-directed behaviour?

### Where and How to Communicate Eligibility

**Timing Considerations**
- At what point in the customer journey should eligibility be communicated?
- Should eligibility be front-loaded or revealed progressively?
- How does timing affect conversion and fairness perception?

**Channel Considerations**
- Which channels are most appropriate for eligibility communication?
- How should eligibility messaging differ between mass and personal communication?
- What role should advisors play in eligibility explanation?

**Visual and Linguistic Design**
- What visual hierarchy minimises negative eligibility perception?
- What language choices optimise comprehension and acceptance?
- How can eligibility be communicated simply without legal risk?

## Part C: Activation Despite Eligibility

For customers who do not currently meet eligibility requirements, the engagement must develop strategies to convert eligibility into a motivator:

### Pathway Communication

- How can the steps to eligibility be made clear and attractive?
- What is the optimal framing of the investment product requirement?
- How can UBS help customers see eligibility as achievable?

### Near-Miss Psychology

- How can customers who are "close" to eligibility be identified and targeted?
- What specific interventions work for near-miss segments?
- How can progress toward eligibility be communicated?

### Trial and Taster Approaches

- Should there be limited access for non-eligible customers?
- What role can trial offers play in driving qualification?
- How might Schweiz Tourismus or Ticketcorner partnerships create entry points?

14. Eligibility framing strategy with recommended language and positioning
15. Timing and channel recommendations for eligibility communication
16. Pathway communication strategy for non-eligible customers

**Reference Price Effects**
- What discount levels are perceived as meaningful?
- At what threshold does a discount become "worth acting on"?
- How do discount perceptions differ by event type and price point?

**Quality Signalling**
- Do high discounts signal low quality or desperation?
- How do premium events (Art Basel, Lucerne Festival) differ from mass events?
- What is the optimal discount for maintaining brand positioning?

**Behavioural Activation Thresholds**
- What discount level triggers action vs. passive appreciation?
- How does urgency interact with discount magnitude?
- What role does scarcity play in discount effectiveness?

### Early Access vs. Discount Trade-off

For high-demand events where only early access is offered:

- How is early access valued relative to discounts?
- What segments value early access most?

# Integration Across the Four Questions

The four strategic questions are deeply interconnected:

**Question 1** (communication strategy) establishes whether focused or broad communication is more effective, directly informing how eligibility (Question 3) and offering details (Question 4) should be communicated.

**Question 2** (behavioural impact) identifies the specific mechanisms that drive consideration and conversion, which must be reflected in eligibility framing (Question 3) and promotional strategy (Question 4).

**Question 3** (eligibility communication) is central to adult segment success, as poor eligibility framing can undermine even excellent overall communication (Question 1) and attractive offerings (Question 4).

**Question 4** (offering architecture) provides the substance that communication (Question 1) must convey and that behavioural strategies (Question 2) must activate.

The final communication briefing must synthesise insights from all four questions into an integrated, actionable recommendation.

# BEATRIX Integration

This engagement leverages the BEATRIX framework for systematic behavioural analysis:

## Behavioural Economics Foundations

BEATRIX applies behavioural economics principles central to this engagement:

**Choice Architecture**
- How the Feel Switzerland offering is presented affects decisions
- Defaults, framing, and salience shape customer behaviour
- Eligibility communication is a choice architecture challenge

**Mental Accounting**
- Customers categorise benefits into mental accounts
- Discount perception depends on reference points and categories
- Feel Switzerland must create a coherent mental category

**Social Preferences**
- Fairness preferences affect eligibility perception
- Social proof and peer effects influence programme attractiveness
- Status and belonging motivations vary by segment

**Temporal Discounting**
- Immediate vs. future benefits affect activation
- Urgency and deadlines influence decision timing
- Promotional events leverage temporal effects

## Context Analysis (Ψ-Framework)

The BCM 2.0 Ψ-context dimensions provide systematic analysis of:

- **Ψ_I (Institutional):** Banking relationships, eligibility conditions, programme rules
- **Ψ_S (Social):** Peer effects, social proof, recommendation dynamics
- **Ψ_C (Cognitive):** Attention, processing fluency, decision complexity
- **Ψ_K (Cultural):** Swiss cultural values, segment-specific preferences
- **Ψ_E (Economic):** Price sensitivity, discount perception, value assessment
- **Ψ_T (Temporal):** Decision timing, seasonal effects, life stage

## Complementarity Analysis

The framework analyses interactions between:

- Communication simplicity and benefit comprehensiveness
- Eligibility exclusivity and programme accessibility
- Discount attractiveness and brand premium positioning
- Youth accessibility and adult qualification requirements
