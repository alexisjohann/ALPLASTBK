Key Points 
Discussed
Stream 1 – Positioning & Services
Goal: Market understanding, customer value, differentiation
Key Statements
Describes ALPLA Industrial flexibly depending on customer type:
For smaller customers: emphasizes know-how, guidance, and professional support.
For larger customers: underlines tradition, experience, and ALPLA Group strength (international presence, professional service).
Typical self-description: “We are a bucket/injected packaging producer specialized in buckets, ready to find the right solution for the right product.”
Construction segment (Central Europe) = strongest position, “perfect portfolio.”
Germany = portfolio misfit; adaptation of product sizes underway.
Food market = main growth opportunity, but only possible with major investment (tooling, product range).
External perception: trustworthy supplier but not innovative; sometimes ALPLA staff perceived as “arrogant” or “too corporate.”
Argues ALPLA Industrial should keep its down-to-earth, personal Wolf Plastics style, combined with ALPLA’s professionalism – “but don’t overdo it.”
Interpretation→ Schamberger articulates a dual identity challenge: legacy culture (proximity, pragmatism) vs. ALPLA’s formal corporate image.→ Strength in construction segment, but strategic gap in food packaging.→ Need to reframe Industrial’s market message around trusted expertise + modern professionalism.
Implications
Recalibrate the brand tone (“accessible expertise”) to retain local trust while leveraging ALPLA’s credibility.
Strategically expand into food and German markets with adapted portfolio.
Create customer typologies to tailor communication and service style.
Stream 2 – Automation & Efficiency
Goal: Systems, workflows, data visibility
Key Statements
Reports no CRM system currently in use.
Past experience at RESILUX showed CRM value (documentation discipline, continuity).
CRM would prevent redundant customer interactions and loss of memory.
Key condition: management must use it and role-model reporting.
Current workflows = unstructured, no central repository for visit reports or sample data.
Example: he submits detailed reports with pictures, next steps, etc., but receives no follow-up or reciprocity.
Sampling process currently chaotic: no tracking, no feedback, no rules for cost approval.
Calls for systematic process documentation and structured knowledge management (training materials, catalogues).
Interpretation→ Strong advocacy for digital backbone and disciplined information culture.→ Highlights behavioural barrier: missing management ownership, not technology.
Implications
Prioritise CRM roll-out with leadership accountability (use and review).
Implement sample-tracking and documentation system (digital or structured Excel pilot).
Develop knowledge-sharing platform to retain and scale expertise.
Stream 3 – Organisation & Steering
Goal: Collaboration, governance, communication flow
Key Statements
Identifies communication breakdowns across all levels:
Poor vertical and horizontal information sharing.
Example: pushed for German prospect → sales visited after one year, different agenda, no report back.
Attributes causes to lack of structure and personality factors (“combination of no rules and people not sharing”).
Cooperation ratings:
Hungary: 7–8/10
Kammern: 5–6/10
Romania: 4–5/10
Explains variation through leadership profiles:
Hungary: sales-minded, technically skilled GM.
Romania: sales-driven but not technical → slow sampling, lost opportunities.
Austria: production-oriented GM, low sales trust, tension with commercial team.
Calls for clear strategy, process ownership, and leadership follow-through (“not just talk”).
Advocates for regional management structure to improve coordination.
Suggests having mobile technical experts who support customers during tests and line runs.
Interpretation→ Communication dysfunction is the main structural bottleneck in Industrial’s organisation.→ Success correlates directly with leadership style and technical-commercial integration.→ Central steering mechanisms and technical field support are missing.
Implications
Create standardised communication and reporting protocols (meeting cadence, visit feedback, tracking).
Establish regional management layer for coordination across plants.
Introduce mobile technical service team to link plants, sales, and customers.
Stream 4 – Incentive & Motivation
Goal: Motivation drivers, recognition, feedback culture
Key Statements
Annual bonus model in place – “works fine” but not the main motivator.
Motivation sources: team collaboration, learning, joint creation, not money.
Example: “horseradish project” – cross-team learning, new know-how, and success = personal fulfillment.
Requests honest and constructive feedback to understand performance and contribution; finds it inconsistent.
Emphasises recognition and communication over financial reward.
Interpretation→ Clear preference for intrinsic motivation (purpose, learning, teamwork).→ Recognition gap noted; feedback culture inconsistent.
Implications
Reinforce feedback and recognition loops (peer and leadership).
Link annual bonus criteria to collaboration and learning contributions, not just results.
Introduce regular performance dialogues for transparency.
