================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper discusses a fundamental problem in measuring the growth of knowledge
and comparing the skills of people. New skills emerge that are not just more of
the previously acquired skills. Psychometric convention forces these skills into
arbitrarily constructed scales, which can severely distort measurement.

To formally address this problem, we measure skills using a novel measurement
scheme, estimate a stochastic learning process and reject the common scale
assumption across levels for language and cognitive skills. Furthermore, we
estimate dynamic complementarity without imposing arbitrary scales for skills.

================================================================================
I. INTRODUCTION - THE FUNDAMENTAL PROBLEM
================================================================================

CORE INSIGHT:
Test scores are at best ordinal, and different normalizations generate different
rankings and treatment effects.

NEW CONTRIBUTION:
Skills do not evolve as higher levels of a fixed set of abilities. New skills
EMERGE with development - they are often qualitatively different from skills
measured at earlier ages.

KEY IMPLICATION:
There is NO constant-unit measure of cognition or language that is valid across
ages for the same people. Forcing distinct skills into a common scale distorts
measures of both levels and growth.

PRACTICAL CONSEQUENCE:
This insight can explain key anomalies in the literature, including the apparent
FADEOUT of early treatment effects.

================================================================================
II. THE TECHNOLOGY OF SKILL FORMATION
================================================================================

FUNDAMENTAL EQUATION:

    K(a + 1) = f(a)(K(a), I(a))

Where:
- K(a + 1) = Vector of skills at age a + 1
- K(a) = Vector of skills at age a
- I(a) = Investment at age a (parenting, schools, etc.)
- f(a) = Age-specific production function

CRITICAL NOTE:
K(a + 1) and K(a) might be measured on a standard scale as is common in the
literature, but that is NOT REQUIRED.

DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY:

    ∂²K(a + j + 1)
    ─────────────── > 0
    ∂I(a) ∂I'(a + j)

Meaning: Investment at age a raises the productivity (rate of learning) of
later investment I(a + j), j > 0.

KEY INSIGHT:
This parameter does NOT require any assumption that K at different ages is
measured in the same units.

================================================================================
III. THE STRATEGY - SCALE-FREE TESTING
================================================================================

KEY INNOVATION:
Access to repeated lessons on skills at specific levels administered to people
of the same age.

MEASUREMENT APPROACH:
- Outcomes (learned or not) of each sequence of identical lessons
- Well-defined measures of skill comparable across otherwise identical people
- Measures growth WITHIN levels meaningfully

CRITICAL DISTINCTION:
These measures need not cumulate additively ACROSS levels, as they would if
a constant-unit assumption were appropriate.

NATURAL EXPERIMENT:
Children enter the program at different ages. They face a structured schedule
of repeated instruction and assessments of age-specific skills that applies
to all participants of the same age, irrespective of their previous experience.

TEST:
Whether the learning rate of skills is accelerated by higher levels of skills
present at the start of each stage (level) of instruction.

RESULT:
Dynamic complementarity enables low-ability children to partially close the
gap with high- and normal-ability children.

================================================================================
IV. DATA - CHINA REACH PROGRAM
================================================================================

PROGRAM: China REACH
ORGANIZATION: China Development Research Foundation (CDRF)
DESIGN: Large-scale experimental evaluation
INTERVENTION: Home-visiting for multidimensional skill development

PROTOCOL:
- Trained home visitors (same education level as mothers)
- Weekly visits to each treated household
- One hour of age-specific caregiving guidance
- Children evaluated weekly on their knowledge

SKILLS MEASURED:
1. Language (11 UHP levels)
2. Cognitive (multiple levels)
3. Fine Motor (multiple levels)
4. Social-Emotional

SKILL ORDERING:
Tasks in three skill categories are ordered by difficulty levels following
profiles developed by Palmer (1971) and Uzgiris and Hunt (1975) - "UHP".

================================================================================
V. UHP DIFFICULTY LEVELS FOR LANGUAGE SKILLS
================================================================================

Level 1:  Caregiver and child make sounds to each other to interact
Level 2:  Caregiver tells child the things she does in the house
Level 3:  The child recognizes people's names
Level 4:  The child learns movements (clapping, bye-bye, thank you)
Level 5:  Look at pictures together, child vocalizes and touches pictures
Level 6:  The child recognizes at least one body part
Level 7:  The child identifies and/or names ordinary objects
Level 8:  Child points to named pictures, names one or more, mimics sounds
Level 9:  Child points to named pictures, names two or more, mimics sounds
Level 10: Child points at 7+ pictures and talks about them
Level 11: Child learns descriptive words, names objects, tells usage

CRITICAL INSIGHT:
Within levels, the skills taught and measured are essentially IDENTICAL.
There is no hierarchy of tasks assigned within levels.

EXAMPLE - Level 3 (Teaching child to recognize people's names):
- Month 9, Week 2: Teach names of family members
- Month 11, Week 4: Teach names (same content)
- Month 14, Week 2: Teach names (same content)
- Month 16, Week 3: Teach names (same content)
- Month 18, Week 1: Teach names (same content)

All tasks are essentially the same - no within-level hierarchy.

================================================================================
VI. KEY FINDINGS - LEARNING PATTERNS
================================================================================

WITHIN-LEVEL PATTERN:
Average passing rates increase with number of lessons → evidence of LEARNING

ACROSS-LEVEL PATTERN:
When transitioning to higher difficulty levels, initial passing rates DECLINE
→ evidence that NEW SKILLS are being taught at each level

RECOVERY PATTERN:
After initial declines, passing rates within levels increase as learning of
new skills ensues.

FINE MOTOR EXCEPTION:
At most levels of fine motor skills, there is at best modest learning.

ABILITY GROUP PATTERNS:
- High ability: highest performance across all levels
- Normal ability: second highest, but CATCHES UP at later levels
- Low ability: lowest, but gap NARROWS as children progress

KEY FINDING:
Using dynamic reinforcement learning model (Heckman & Zhou 2026), they REJECT
the hypothesis of a common language skill scale across levels.

Same result for cognitive skills. NOT for fine motor skills.

================================================================================
VII. IMPLICATIONS FOR VALUE-ADDED MEASURES
================================================================================

CRITIQUE:
Value-added measures assume that psychometric scales measure the same skill
at different levels with higher levels being more of the same thing measured
at lower levels.

PROBLEM:
Without a common scale assumption across levels of nominally the same skill,
the empirical literature rests on SHAKY GROUND.

LONG-TERM EVALUATIONS:
Same issue affects long-term evaluations of skill formation programs.

================================================================================
VIII. FADEOUT EXPLAINED
================================================================================

TRADITIONAL VIEW:
Early treatment effects "fade out" over time.

NEW EXPLANATION:
Fadeout is ONE POSSIBLE CONSEQUENCE of the emergence of new skills.
Comparisons of different skills over time are NOT MEANINGFUL.

STOCHASTIC PROCESS:
Heckman and Zhou's (2026) estimates show:
- Pattern of rises and declines WITHIN levels
- Variances of shocks differ ACROSS levels
- This produces pattern of fadeout, recovery, and fadeout

IMPLICATION:
Much-discussed fadeout (Bailey et al., 2020) may be artifact of measurement,
not a real phenomenon.

================================================================================
IX. SCALE-FREE TEST OF DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY
================================================================================

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN:
- Treatment group separated by enrollment status
- One group: full curriculum (starting before level 6)
- Another group: enrolled at level 6 with no prior investment
- Balance tests pass on baseline background variables

MEASURE:
d(s, ℓ) = Number of trials to achieve first success at UHP level ℓ

RESULTS (Table 3 - Language):

                            Level 7  Level 8  Level 9  Level 10  Level 11
Full Exposure:               2.487    2.079    1.496    1.404     1.348
Late Enrollment (Level 6):   2.518    2.367    1.686    1.454     1.698
p-value:                     0.870    0.024    0.017    0.608     0.000
stepdown p-value:            0.864    0.059    0.059    0.827     0.000

INTERPRETATION:
At UHP Language levels 8, 9, and 11, children with full exposure to curriculum
require SIGNIFICANTLY FEWER TRIALS until first success.

CONCLUSION:
Otherwise similar children with more early investment learn more than those
without early investment.

ADDITIONAL FINDING:
Children raised by grandparents are slower to learn.

COGNITIVE SKILLS:
Results are comparable for cognitive skills.

FINE MOTOR:
NO evidence of dynamic complementarity for fine motor skills.

LOW-ABILITY GROUPS:
For initially low-ability groups, the difference in learning rates at later
levels is EVEN MORE PRONOUNCED.

CONCLUSION:
Disadvantaged children benefit the most in early stages through dynamic
complementarity and reduce the gap at later stages.

================================================================================
X. SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. Using unusually fine granular data on learning, documented EMERGENCE of
   new skills at different levels of nominally the same skill.

2. This calls into question current procedures that impose common scales
   on nominally the same skills used to assess growth and compute
   value-added measures.

3. Comparing the incomparable can explain FADEOUT.

4. Lack of common scale does NOT prevent identifying crucial aspects of
   technology of skill formation IF meaningful scales can be found WITHIN levels.

5. Within-level scales identify:
   - Dynamic complementarity
   - Catch-up of the less able

6. Careful design of future studies enables analysts to avoid imposing
   arbitrary scales and computing meaningless value-added measures.

================================================================================
XI. EBF IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

1. PARAMETER ESTIMATION:
   - EBF parameters (λ, β, γ) should not blindly assume cardinal scales
   - Where possible, use scale-free tests

2. LLMMC:
   - Priors should account for ordinal nature of many measures
   - Consider uncertainty about scale transformations

3. COMPLEMENTARITY (γ):
   - Can be estimated without psychometric fiat
   - Use within-level comparisons where available

4. INTERVENTION EVALUATION:
   - Fadeout may be measurement artifact, not real phenomenon
   - Long-term effects should use scale-appropriate methods

5. CONTEXT-DEPENDENCE (Ψ):
   - Skills are age-specific (Ψ_T dimension)
   - Emergence supports context-dependent skill definitions
