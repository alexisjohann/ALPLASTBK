Title: Testing for Altruism and Social Pressure in Charitable Giving
Authors: Stefano DellaVigna, John List, Ulrike Malmendier
Year: 2012
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 127, No. 1, pp. 1-56

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Every year, 90 percent of Americans give money to charities. Is such generosity
necessarily welfare enhancing for the giver? This paper presents a theoretical
framework distinguishing two types of motivation: individuals who like to give
(altruism, warm glow) versus individuals who would rather not give but dislike
saying no (social pressure).

The authors design a door-to-door fund-raiser field experiment where some
households are informed about the exact time of solicitation via a flyer on
their doorknobs. This design allows households to seek or avoid the fund-raiser,
revealing their true preferences.

The flyer reduces households opening the door by 9-25%, and when combined with
a "Do Not Disturb" checkbox, reduces giving by 28-42%. These findings suggest
social pressure is a key determinant of charitable giving, and that door-to-door
campaigns may actually reduce donor welfare.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SOCIAL PRESSURE DOMINATES: Social pressure cost of saying no is $3.50 (local
   charity) to $1.40 (out-of-state charity) - substantial deterrent to refusal

2. AVOIDANCE BEHAVIOR: 9-25% fewer households answer door when warned about
   solicitation - reveals preference to avoid giving opportunity

3. SMALL DONATIONS MOST AFFECTED: Reductions concentrate among donations <$10,
   suggesting these are pressure-driven rather than altruism-driven

4. WELFARE REDUCING: Door-to-door campaigns may on average reduce donor utility
   - giving is often utility-reducing, not welfare-enhancing

5. ALTRUISM VS PRESSURE: Framework distinguishes supply-driven (altruism/warm
   glow) from demand-driven (social pressure) giving motivations

6. FIELD EXPERIMENT DESIGN: Novel flyer methodology allows revealed preference
   identification of social pressure versus altruism
