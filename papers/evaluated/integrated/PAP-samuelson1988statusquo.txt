Title: Status Quo Bias in Decision Making
Authors: William Samuelson, Richard Zeckhauser
Year: 1988
Journal: Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, Vol. 1, No. 1, pp. 7-59

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Most real decisions, unlike those of economics texts, have a status quo
alternative - doing nothing or maintaining one's current or previous decision.
A series of decision-making experiments shows that individuals disproportionately
stick with the status quo.

Data on selections of health plans and retirement programs by faculty members
reveal that status quo bias is substantial in important real decisions. Even
when only small transition costs are involved and the importance of the decision
is great, people show strong preference for current states.

Field data from university health plan enrollments show a large disparity between
new and existing enrollees. One plan with significantly more favorable terms had
growing market share among new employees but significantly lower share among
older enrollees - the lack of switching cannot be explained by unchanging
preferences.

The bias is consistent with loss aversion, and may be explained by commitments,
sunk cost thinking, cognitive dissonance, need for control, and regret avoidance.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. STATUS QUO BIAS: Disproportionate tendency to stick with current state

2. DEFAULT STICKINESS: Defaults are powerful even for important decisions

3. FIELD EVIDENCE: Health plan choices show large bias even with significant
   financial stakes

4. LOSS AVERSION: Status quo bias consistent with loss aversion (departures
   feel like losses)

5. PSYCHOLOGICAL MECHANISMS: Commitments, sunk costs, cognitive dissonance,
   regret avoidance

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Defaults matter enormously for choice architecture
