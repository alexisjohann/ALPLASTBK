Title: The Disposition to Sell Winners Too Early and Ride Losers Too Long: Theory and Evidence
Authors: Hersh Shefrin, Meir Statman
Year: 1985
Journal: Journal of Finance, Vol. 40, No. 3, pp. 777-790

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper introduces the term "disposition effect" to describe the
predisposition of investors to sell their winners too early and to ride their
losers too long. The authors place this behavior into a wider theoretical
framework that includes mental accounting, regret aversion, self-control, and
tax considerations.

The behavior is rooted in Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory - specifically
loss aversion. People dislike realizing losses significantly more than they
enjoy realizing gains. This leads to holding losers (hoping they'll recover)
while selling winners (locking in the pleasure of gains).

Evidence suggests that tax considerations alone cannot explain the observed
patterns - the behavior is consistent with a combined effect of taxes and the
psychological elements. The concentration of loss realizations in December
is not consistent with fully rational behavior (losses should be realized
year-round for tax efficiency) but is consistent with the disposition effect
theory.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. DISPOSITION EFFECT: Investors sell winners too early, hold losers too long

2. LOSS AVERSION BASIS: Realizing losses is psychologically painful

3. MENTAL ACCOUNTING: Separate mental accounts for each stock position

4. REGRET AVERSION: Selling losers confirms the mistake was real

5. DECEMBER CONCENTRATION: Loss realizations cluster in December (tax deadline)

6. TAX INEFFICIENT: Behavior is tax-suboptimal (should realize losses sooner)
