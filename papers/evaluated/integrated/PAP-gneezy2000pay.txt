Title: Pay Enough or Don't Pay at All
Authors: Uri Gneezy, Aldo Rustichini
Year: 2000
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 115, No. 3, pp. 791-810

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Economists usually assume that monetary incentives improve performance, and
psychologists claim that the opposite may happen. This paper presents a set of
experiments designed to test these contrasting claims.

The key finding is that the effect of monetary compensation on performance is
not monotonic. In treatments where money was offered, larger amounts yielded
higher performance. However, offering money did not always improve performance:
subjects who were offered small monetary incentives performed more poorly than
those offered no compensation at all.

This demonstrates "motivation crowding out": small extrinsic rewards can
undermine intrinsic motivation, while sufficiently large rewards can overcome
this effect. The implication is stark: pay enough, or don't pay at all.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NON-MONOTONIC EFFECT: Small payments reduce performance relative to no
   payment; large payments increase it - relationship is U-shaped

2. MOTIVATION CROWDING OUT: Small extrinsic incentives crowd out intrinsic
   motivation, reducing total effort

3. THRESHOLD EFFECT: Incentives must exceed a threshold to improve performance;
   below this, they backfire

4. CHARITY FUNDRAISING: High school students raised less money for charity
   when paid a commission than when unpaid

5. NORM TRANSFORMATION: Small payments transform social norms (do it because
   it's right) into market norms (do it for money)

6. PAY ENOUGH: Policy implication is binary - either pay substantially more
   than the threshold or pay nothing at all
