Title: A Fine is a Price
Authors: Uri Gneezy, Aldo Rustichini
Year: 2000
Journal: Journal of Legal Studies, Vol. 29, No. 1, pp. 1-17

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The deterrence hypothesis predicts that introducing a penalty will reduce the
occurrence of the behavior subject to the fine. This field study in day-care
centers contradicts this prediction. Parents used to arrive late to collect
their children, forcing a teacher to stay after closing time. When a monetary
fine for late-coming parents was introduced, the number of late-coming parents
INCREASED significantly. After the fine was removed, no reduction occurred.

Two explanations are offered: First, the fine provided information about
consequences that had been unspecified - while parents may have feared severe
consequences, the fine made clear this was not a threat. Second, before the
fine existed, a social norm restrained parents. Once the fine was introduced,
it transformed extra care into a commodity. Parents were now free to "buy"
the service at the "price" of the fine, without guilt or shame. The internal,
moral motivations were "crowded out" by the market signal.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. FINE BACKFIRED: Introducing a fine INCREASED late pickups (not decreased)

2. CROWDING OUT: Monetary penalty replaced intrinsic social motivation

3. IRREVERSIBILITY: Removing fine did not restore original low lateness rate

4. COMMODIFICATION: Fine transformed social obligation into market transaction

5. NORM DESTRUCTION: Social norms, once replaced by prices, are hard to restore

6. INFORMATION EFFECT: Fine may have signaled that lateness was not serious
