================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper provides evidence that many people have a tendency to cooperate
and to punish defectors, even at a personal cost. We show that cooperation
flourishes in the presence of opportunities for costly punishment of free
riders. Without punishment opportunities, however, cooperation breaks down.
The results suggest that people have strong reciprocal preferences that are
conditional on the behavior of others.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CONDITIONAL COOPERATION
   - Many subjects are "conditional cooperators"
   - They cooperate if others cooperate, defect if others defect
   - Without punishment, cooperation decays over time

2. COSTLY PUNISHMENT
   - When punishment opportunities exist, cooperation is stable
   - People punish free riders even at personal cost
   - Punishment is targeted: defectors receive more punishment
   - Cost ratio: punisher pays c, target loses 3c (typical)

3. EMPIRICAL PATTERNS
   - Without punishment: contributions decay from ~50% to ~10%
   - With punishment: contributions rise to ~90%+
   - Most punishment directed at below-average contributors

4. STRONG RECIPROCITY
   - People reward cooperation (positive reciprocity)
   - People punish defection (negative reciprocity)
   - Both occur even in one-shot anonymous interactions
