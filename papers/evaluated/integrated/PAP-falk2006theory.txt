Title: A Theory of Reciprocity
Authors: Armin Falk, Urs Fischbacher
Year: 2006
Journal: Games and Economic Behavior, Vol. 54, No. 2, pp. 293-315

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

People are reciprocal if they reward kind actions and punish unkind ones. In
this paper the authors present a formal theory of reciprocity. It takes into
account that people evaluate the kindness of an action not only by its
consequences but also by its underlying intention.

The theory is in line with the relevant stylized facts of a wide range of
experimental games, such as the ultimatum game, the gift-exchange game, a
reduced best-shot game, the dictator game, the prisoner's dilemma, and public
goods games.

Furthermore, it predicts that identical consequences trigger different
reciprocal responses in different environments. Context matters for reciprocity -
the same outcome may be perceived as kind or unkind depending on available
alternatives and perceived intentions.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. INTENTION MATTERS: Kindness evaluated by both consequences AND underlying
   intentions

2. CONTEXT DEPENDENCE: Same consequence triggers different responses in
   different environments

3. UNIFIED FRAMEWORK: Explains behavior across ultimatum, gift-exchange,
   dictator, PD, and public goods games

4. FORMAL MODEL: Mathematical formalization of reciprocity with testable
   predictions

5. PUNISHMENT AND REWARD: People both punish unkindness and reward kindness
   at personal cost

6. ALTERNATIVE-BASED: Kindness perception depends on what other options were
   available
