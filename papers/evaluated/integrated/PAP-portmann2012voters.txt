Title: District Magnitude and Representation of the Majority's Preferences: Evidence from Popular and Parliamentary Votes
Authors: Marco Portmann, David Stadelmann, Reiner Eichenberger
Year: 2012
Journal: Public Choice, Vol. 151, No. 3-4, pp. 585-610
DOI: 10.1007/s11127-010-9760-2

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper analyzes whether the size of electoral districts affects how well
parliamentary representatives reflect their constituents' preferences.

Using REFERENDUM MATCHING as identification strategy, the authors compare:
- How voters in each district voted in popular referenda (direct democracy)
- How their elected representatives voted on the same issues in parliament

KEY FINDING: Smaller districts lead to BETTER REPRESENTATION of majority
preferences. In smaller districts, elected officials' votes more closely
match how their constituents voted in referenda.

MECHANISM: In smaller districts, voters can more easily monitor their
representatives, creating stronger accountability.

QUANTITATIVE RESULT: A standard deviation increase in district magnitude
is associated with a 16.8 percentage point DECREASE in preference alignment
between voters and their representatives.

CONTEXT: Switzerland, 1996-2008, using the unique Swiss system where both
popular referenda and parliamentary votes occur on identical issues.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Smaller electoral districts → better representation
2. 16.8pp increase in preference alignment with smaller districts
3. Referendum matching as identification strategy
4. Switzerland 1996-2008 data
5. Accountability mechanism: easier monitoring in small districts
6. Validates median voter theorem under certain conditions
