Title: Save More Tomorrow: Using Behavioral Economics to Increase Employee Saving
Authors: Richard H. Thaler, Shlomo Benartzi
Year: 2004
Journal: Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 112, No. S1, pp. S164-S187

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The Save More Tomorrow (SMarT) program proposes that employees commit in
advance to allocating a portion of their future salary increases toward
retirement savings. This leverages several behavioral biases: employees
procrastinate increased saving to the future, never experience drops in
take-home pay (synchronized to raises), and inertia keeps them enrolled.

The program addresses loss aversion by tying savings increases to pay raises,
so participants never see their paycheck decrease. It addresses procrastination
by making commitment about future (not present) behavior. It uses inertia to
maintain enrollment rather than to prevent it.

Key findings from first implementation (four annual raises): 78% of those
offered joined; 80% of enrollees remained through fourth raise; average saving
rates increased from 3.5% to 13.6% over 40 months. After two wage increases,
98% remained enrolled.

The program's success led to the Pension Protection Act of 2006 supporting
automatic enrollment and escalation features.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. AUTOMATIC ESCALATION: Tie savings increases to pay raises

2. FUTURE COMMITMENT: Commit now to increase later (addresses procrastination)

3. NO PAY CUT: Never see paycheck decrease (addresses loss aversion)

4. INERTIA FOR GOOD: Use status quo bias to maintain, not prevent, savings

5. MASSIVE UPTAKE: 78% joined when offered; 80-98% retention

6. SAVINGS INCREASE: 3.5% → 13.6% over 40 months
