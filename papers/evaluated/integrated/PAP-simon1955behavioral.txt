Title: A Behavioral Model of Rational Choice
Authors: Herbert A. Simon
Year: 1955
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 69, No. 1, pp. 99-118

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper introduces the concept of "bounded rationality" - the first
formalization of a choice procedure performed by a boundedly rational economic
agent. Rather than assuming perfect rationality, Simon emphasized the limits
to rationality that real-life decision makers face.

Human rationality is bounded by three critical limitations: (1) Limited
information - decision-makers rarely have complete information about all
possibilities; (2) Cognitive constraints - the human mind has limited
computational capacity; (3) Time pressure - most decisions must be made under
time constraints that prevent exhaustive analysis.

Instead of optimizing, Simon proposed that humans "satisfice" (satisfy + suffice)
- they search for solutions that are "good enough" rather than perfect. Decision
makers establish an aspiration level (threshold of acceptability) and choose
the first option that meets or exceeds it. This is more realistic than
assuming people evaluate all possible alternatives.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. BOUNDED RATIONALITY: Human rationality limited by information, cognition,
   and time

2. SATISFICING: People seek "good enough" solutions rather than optimal ones

3. ASPIRATION LEVELS: Decision makers have thresholds of acceptability

4. SEARCH STOPS: First satisfactory option chosen (not best possible)

5. PROCEDURAL RATIONALITY: Focus on decision PROCESS not just outcomes

6. COGNITIVE LIMITS: Human mind has limited processing capacity
