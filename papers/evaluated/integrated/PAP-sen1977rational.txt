Title: Rational Fools: A Critique of the Behavioral Foundations of Economic Theory
Authors: Amartya Sen
Year: 1977
Journal: Philosophy and Public Affairs, Vol. 6, No. 4, pp. 317-344

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper critiques the behavioral foundations of economic theory, questioning
the assumption that every agent is actuated only by self-interest. Sen
distinguishes between two departures from purely self-interested behavior:
sympathy and commitment.

Sympathy corresponds to concern for others that directly affects one's own
welfare. "If the knowledge of torture of others makes you sick, it is a case of
sympathy." Commitment, by contrast, involves placing an ethical stance ahead of
one's own well-being - acting on moral principles even when it does not improve
one's welfare.

Sen argues that behavior based on sympathy is in an important sense egoistic,
while commitment represents a genuine departure from self-interest that standard
economic models cannot accommodate.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SYMPATHY VS COMMITMENT: Two types of other-regarding behavior with
   fundamentally different structures

2. SYMPATHY IS EGOISTIC: Helping others because their welfare affects your
   welfare is still self-interested

3. COMMITMENT TRANSCENDS: Acting on moral principles regardless of welfare
   effect represents true departure from self-interest

4. RATIONAL FOOLS: Economic agents modeled as purely self-interested are
   "rational fools"

5. ETHICS IN ECONOMICS: Commitment-based behavior cannot be reduced to
   preference satisfaction

6. BEHAVIORAL FOUNDATIONS CRITIQUE: Standard economic theory misses important
   human motivations
