Title: The Productivity Argument for Investing in Young Children
Authors: James J. Heckman, Dimitriy V. Masterov
Year: 2007
Journal: Review of Agricultural Economics, Vol. 29, No. 3, pp. 446-493

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents a productivity argument for investing in disadvantaged
young children. For such investment, there is no equity-efficiency tradeoff.

The central argument is that on productivity grounds, it makes sense to invest
in young children from disadvantaged environments. Substantial evidence shows
these children are more likely to commit crime, have out-of-wedlock births,
and drop out of school. Early intervention can address these issues cost-
effectively.

The paper emphasizes that skill formation is a dynamic process: skill begets
skill, and early investments make later investments more productive. The
highest returns come from investing early in life, particularly in the most
disadvantaged children.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SKILL BEGETS SKILL: Early skills make acquisition of later skills easier -
   dynamic complementarity in skill formation

2. HIGHEST ROI EARLY: Returns to investment are highest in early childhood,
   decline with age, and are highest for disadvantaged children

3. NO EQUITY-EFFICIENCY TRADEOFF: Investing in disadvantaged children is both
   efficient (high ROI) and equitable (reduces inequality)

4. CRITICAL PERIODS: Early childhood represents critical period for cognitive
   and non-cognitive skill development

5. NON-COGNITIVE MATTERS: Non-cognitive skills (motivation, self-control) are
   as important as cognitive skills for life outcomes

6. REMEDIATION COSTLY: Later interventions are more expensive and less effective
   than early interventions - prevention over remediation
