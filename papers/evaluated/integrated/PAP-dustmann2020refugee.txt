Title: The Labor Market Integration of Refugee Migrants in High-Income Countries
Authors: Courtney Brell, Christian Dustmann, Ian Preston
Year: 2020
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 34, No. 1, pp. 94-121
DOI: 10.1257/jep.34.1.94

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The paper provides an overview of the integration of refugees into the labor
markets of a number of high-income countries.

Discussing the ways in which refugees and economic migrants are differently
selected and so might be expected to perform differently in a host country's
labor market, the authors examine employment and wages for these groups over
time after arrival.

KEY FINDINGS:
- Significant heterogeneity BETWEEN host countries
- In general, refugees experience PERSISTENTLY WORSE outcomes than other migrants
- Gaps between groups decrease on timescale of a decade or two
- Convergence more pronounced in EMPLOYMENT RATES than in WAGES

The authors also discuss how refugees are distinct in terms of:
- Health
- Language skills
- Social networks

POLICY INSIGHT: Supporting refugees in EARLY labor market attachment is crucial.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Refugees vs economic migrants: Different selection mechanisms
2. Refugees have persistently worse labor market outcomes
3. Employment gaps close faster than wage gaps
4. 10-20 year timescale for significant convergence
5. Host country policies matter (significant cross-country heterogeneity)
6. Early intervention is key for successful integration
