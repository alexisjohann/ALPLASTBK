Title: Loss Aversion in Riskless Choice: A Reference-Dependent Model
Authors: Amos Tversky, Daniel Kahneman
Year: 1991
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 106, No. 4, pp. 1039-1061

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents a reference-dependent theory of consumer choice extending
loss aversion from risky to riskless choices. The central assumption is that
losses and disadvantages have greater impact on preferences than gains and
advantages.

The theory has three essential characteristics: (1) Reference dependence - the
carriers of value are gains and losses defined relative to a reference point;
(2) Loss aversion - the function is steeper for losses than gains; losses loom
larger than corresponding gains; (3) Diminishing sensitivity - marginal value
decreases with distance from reference point.

This explains the ENDOWMENT EFFECT - the observation that people place higher
value on goods they own than on identical goods they don't own. Experimental
subjects endowed with a commodity ask for a selling price that substantially
exceeds the buying price others would pay. The loss aversion ratio is
approximately 2.25 (losses feel 2.25x as intense as equivalent gains).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. LOSS AVERSION IN RISKLESS CHOICE: Extends prospect theory beyond gambling

2. ENDOWMENT EFFECT: WTA (selling price) >> WTP (buying price) for same good

3. REFERENCE DEPENDENCE: Value defined relative to current state/expectation

4. λ ≈ 2.25: Loss aversion coefficient quantified

5. DIMINISHING SENSITIVITY: Marginal impact decreases away from reference

6. STATUS QUO EXPLAINS: Model explains status quo bias, endowment effect
