Title: Affective Forecasting
Authors: Timothy D. Wilson, Daniel T. Gilbert
Year: 2003
Journal: Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, Vol. 35, pp. 345-411

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

People base many decisions on affective forecasts, predictions about their
emotional reactions to future events. They often display an impact bias,
overestimating the intensity and duration of their emotional reactions to
such events.

One cause of the impact bias is focalism, the tendency to underestimate the
extent to which other events will influence our thoughts and feelings. Another
is people's failure to anticipate how quickly they will make sense of things
that happen to them in a way that speeds emotional recovery.

The four components of affective forecasting are valence (positive/negative),
specific emotions, intensity, and duration. People predict valence accurately
but frequently mispredict the intensity and duration of feelings.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. IMPACT BIAS: People overestimate intensity and duration of emotional
   reactions to events

2. FOCALISM: Underestimate how other events will influence thoughts and
   feelings

3. PSYCHOLOGICAL IMMUNE SYSTEM: Unconscious adaptation process that speeds
   recovery from negative events

4. IMMUNE NEGLECT: Failure to anticipate psychological adaptation to
   negative outcomes

5. VALENCE ACCURACY: People correctly predict whether they'll feel good or
   bad, but not how much or how long

6. DECISION QUALITY: Misprediction leads to suboptimal choices based on
   incorrect anticipated utility
