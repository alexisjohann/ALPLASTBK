Title: Are Investors Reluctant to Realize Their Losses?
Authors: Terrance Odean
Year: 1998
Journal: Journal of Finance, Vol. 53, No. 5, pp. 1775-1798

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper tests the disposition effect - the tendency of investors to hold
losing investments too long and sell winning investments too soon - by analyzing
trading records for 10,000 accounts at a large discount brokerage house.

These investors demonstrate a strong preference for realizing winners rather
than losers. This behavior does not appear motivated by portfolio rebalancing
or by avoiding higher trading costs of low-priced stocks. Nor is it justified
by subsequent portfolio performance. For taxable investments, it is sub-optimal
and leads to lower after-tax returns.

Tax-motivated selling is most evident in December. The PGR/PLR ratio (Proportion
of Gains Realized / Proportion of Losses Realized) is 1.5 for US investors -
meaning investors are 50% more likely to sell winners than losers.

The disposition effect extends Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory to
investments. Under prospect theory, the value function is concave for gains
and convex for losses, making investors risk-seeking in the loss domain
(holding losers) and risk-averse in the gain domain (selling winners).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. DISPOSITION EFFECT: Investors sell winners too soon, hold losers too long

2. PGR/PLR RATIO: 1.5 - investors 50% more likely to realize gains than losses

3. NOT RATIONAL: Behavior not explained by rebalancing or trading costs

4. TAX SUBOPTIMAL: Leads to lower after-tax returns (should realize losses)

5. PROSPECT THEORY BASIS: Risk-seeking in losses, risk-averse in gains

6. DECEMBER EFFECT: Tax-motivated selling concentrated in December
