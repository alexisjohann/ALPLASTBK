Title: Exchange Asymmetries Incorrectly Interpreted as Evidence of Endowment Effect Theory and Prospect Theory?
Authors: Charles R. Plott, Kathryn Zeiler
Year: 2007
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 97, No. 4, pp. 1449-1466

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Systematic asymmetries in exchange behavior have been widely interpreted as
support for "endowment effect theory," an application of prospect theory
positing that loss aversion and utility function kinks set by entitlements
explain observed asymmetries.

The authors experimentally test an alternative explanation, namely, that
asymmetries are explained by classical preference theories finding influence
through the experimental procedures typically used.

Contrary to the predictions of endowment effect theory, they observe no
asymmetries when they modify procedures to remove the influence of classical
preference theories. When they return to traditional-type procedures, however,
the asymmetries reappear.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. PROCEDURAL ARTIFACTS: WTA-WTP gap may be due to experimental procedures,
   not loss aversion

2. NO GAP WITH CONTROLS: Asymmetries disappear with proper procedures
   (anonymity, training)

3. GAP REAPPEARS: Traditional procedures recreate the gap even after
   elimination

4. CLASSICAL EXPLANATION: Results consistent with standard preference theory,
   not prospect theory

5. METHODOLOGY MATTERS: Subject familiarity with mechanism and anonymity
   critical

6. CHALLENGES ENDOWMENT: Questions whether endowment effect reflects genuine
   loss aversion
