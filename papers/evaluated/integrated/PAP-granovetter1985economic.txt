Title: Economic Action and Social Structure: The Problem of Embeddedness
Authors: Mark Granovetter
Year: 1985
Journal: American Journal of Sociology, Vol. 91, No. 3, pp. 481-510

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper argues that the behavior and institutions analyzed by economists are
so constrained by ongoing social relations that to construe them as independent
is a grievous misunderstanding. The argument is framed in terms of the concept
of "embeddedness": the argument that economic behavior and institutions become
so embedded in social networks and ongoing social relations that analyzing them
as independent entities is fundamentally flawed.

Granovetter critiques two approaches: the "undersocialized" conception in
classical and neoclassical economics (atomized, rational actors) and the
"oversocialized" conception in some sociology (mechanical internalization of
norms). Both share an atomized actor without ongoing social relations.

The paper applies embeddedness analysis to trust and malfeasance in economic
life, arguing that most economic behavior is embedded in networks of
interpersonal relations. Trust arises from concrete personal relations and
networks rather than generalized morality or institutional arrangements.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EMBEDDEDNESS: Economic action is embedded in structures of social relations

2. UNDERSOCIALIZED CRITIQUE: Neoclassical economics treats actors as atomized,
   ignoring social context

3. OVERSOCIALIZED CRITIQUE: Some sociology treats actors as mechanical norm
   followers, equally atomistic

4. TRUST FROM NETWORKS: Trust in economic relations arises from concrete
   personal relations, not institutions

5. MALFEASANCE: Fraud and misconduct are also enabled by social networks
   (not just prevented)

6. MIDDLE GROUND: Between pure market and hierarchy lies network-based
   economic organization
