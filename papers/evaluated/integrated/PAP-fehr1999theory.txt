Title: A Theory of Fairness, Competition, and Cooperation
Authors: Ernst Fehr, Klaus M. Schmidt
Year: 1999
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 114, No. 3, pp. 817-868
DOI: 10.1162/003355399556151

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This highly influential paper develops a formal model of INEQUITY AVERSION
that explains a wide range of experimental results that are anomalous from
the perspective of standard economic theory with purely selfish preferences.

THE MODEL:

U_i(x) = x_i - α_i * max{x_j - x_i, 0} - β_i * max{x_i - x_j, 0}

Where:
- x_i = player i's material payoff
- α_i = DISADVANTAGEOUS INEQUITY AVERSION (disutility from being behind)
- β_i = ADVANTAGEOUS INEQUITY AVERSION (disutility from being ahead)
- Restriction: α_i ≥ β_i and 0 ≤ β_i < 1

KEY INSIGHT: People dislike inequity in BOTH directions, but dislike being
behind more than being ahead (α > β).

PHENOMENA EXPLAINED:
1. ULTIMATUM GAME: Why responders reject unfair offers
2. PUBLIC GOODS GAMES: Why people contribute and punish free-riders
3. GIFT EXCHANGE: Why workers respond to generous wages with effort
4. MARKET GAMES: Why competition can eliminate fairness concerns
5. DICTATOR GAME: Why dictators give positive amounts

PARAMETER ESTIMATES (from experimental data):
- About 30% of subjects have α = β = 0 (purely selfish)
- About 30% have α ≈ 0.5, β ≈ 0.25
- About 30% have α ≈ 1, β ≈ 0.5
- About 10% have α > 1, β > 0.5 (strongly inequity averse)

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Inequity aversion model: U = x - α*max(x_j-x_i,0) - β*max(x_i-x_j,0)
2. Explains ultimatum, public goods, gift exchange results
3. Key restriction: α ≥ β (behind hurts more than ahead)
4. 30% purely selfish, 70% exhibit some inequity aversion
5. Competition can override fairness preferences
6. Most cited social preference model (19,000+ citations)
