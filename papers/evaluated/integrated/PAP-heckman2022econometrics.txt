================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper provides a comprehensive comparison of three major approaches to
causal inference:

1. THE ECONOMETRIC TRADITION (Frisch-Haavelmo-Cowles)
   - Based on thought experiments and structural equations
   - Can handle simultaneous equations
   - Answers all four policy questions (P1-P4)

2. THE NEYMAN-RUBIN FRAMEWORK (Potential Outcomes)
   - Based on switching treatment regimes
   - Clear identification in experiments
   - Limited to P1, P2 without additional structure

3. PEARL'S DO-CALCULUS (Graphical Models)
   - Based on Directed Acyclic Graphs
   - Algorithmic identification criteria
   - Cannot handle simultaneity (acyclic by definition)

The central argument: Only the econometric approach can handle simultaneous
equations and answer all four fundamental policy questions, making it essential
for structural behavioral economics.

================================================================================
I. THE THREE CAUSAL FRAMEWORKS
================================================================================

ECONOMETRIC TRADITION:

Origins:
- Ragnar Frisch (1926, 1933): Defined "autonomy" of structural relations
- Trygve Haavelmo (1943, 1944): Probability approach to econometrics
- Cowles Commission (1940s-1950s): Simultaneous equations estimation

Key Features:
- Structural equations represent autonomous mechanisms
- Parameters are "policy-invariant" (stable under intervention)
- Thought experiments: "What if we SET variable X to value x?"
- Error terms capture unobserved heterogeneity

Capability:
- Can handle simultaneous equations (feedback loops)
- Distinguishes structural from reduced-form parameters
- Answers P1 (TT), P2 (TUT), P3 (Forecast), P4 (New Policy)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

NEYMAN-RUBIN FRAMEWORK:

Origins:
- Jerzy Neyman (1923): Potential outcomes for randomized experiments
- Donald Rubin (1974, 1978): Extended to observational studies
- Paul Holland (1986): "Statistics and Causal Inference"

Key Features:
- Potential outcomes Y(0), Y(1) for each unit
- SUTVA: Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption
- Identification through randomization or unconfoundedness
- No manipulation, no causation (only well-defined interventions)

Capability:
- Clear identification in RCTs
- Minimal assumptions for internal validity
- Limited for P3, P4 without additional structure

Limitations:
- Cannot handle simultaneous determination
- SUTVA often violated (spillovers, general equilibrium)
- LATE depends on instrument, not policy-relevant

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PEARL'S DO-CALCULUS:

Origins:
- Judea Pearl (1995, 2000, 2009): "Causality" framework
- Spirtes, Glymour, Scheines (1993): PC algorithm

Key Features:
- Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) represent causal structure
- do(X=x) operator: "Set X to x"
- Back-door criterion: Block all confounding paths
- Front-door criterion: Mediation-based identification

Capability:
- Visual representation of causal assumptions
- Algorithmic identification criteria
- Nonparametric (minimal functional form assumptions)

Limitations:
- DAGs are ACYCLIC - cannot represent simultaneity
- Nonparametric: Cannot extrapolate to new contexts
- Limited for P3 (forecast) and P4 (new policy)

================================================================================
II. FIXING VS CONDITIONING - THE FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION
================================================================================

FIXING (Intervention):

Definition:
  Fixing(A=a) = Modify the mechanism M_A that generates A so that it
  produces constant value a regardless of its inputs.

Graphically:
  - Remove all arrows pointing INTO variable A
  - Set A = a (constant)

Probabilistically:
  P(Y | Fixing(A=a)) represents the distribution of Y under intervention

Example:
  "What would blood pressure be if we SET exercise to 30 min/day?"
  → This changes the data generating process

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CONDITIONING (Observation):

Definition:
  Conditioning on A=a = Restrict analysis to the subpopulation where
  variable A happens to equal value a.

Graphically:
  - No change to graph structure
  - Subset data where A = a

Probabilistically:
  P(Y | A=a) = P(Y ∩ A=a) / P(A=a)

Example:
  "What is blood pressure among those who CHOOSE to exercise 30 min/day?"
  → This selects from existing distribution

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY THIS MATTERS:

In general: P(Y | Fixing(A=a)) ≠ P(Y | A=a)

The difference arises from CONFOUNDING:
- If U affects both A and Y (confounder)
- Conditioning on A selects based on U
- Fixing A removes this selection

Example (Confounding):
- U = Health consciousness
- A = Exercise
- Y = Blood pressure

Conditioning: E[Y | A = 30min] reflects health-conscious people
Fixing:      E[Y | Fixing(A = 30min)] reflects everyone forced to exercise

The econometric approach explicitly models the selection mechanism.
The Neyman-Rubin approach assumes away selection (via randomization).
Pearl's approach provides graphical criteria for when Fixing = Conditioning.

================================================================================
III. THE SIMULTANEITY PROBLEM
================================================================================

EXAMPLE: Supply and Demand

Demand: Q = α_D + β_D · P + γ_D · Y + U_D    (β_D < 0)
Supply: Q = α_S + β_S · P + U_S              (β_S > 0)

Market Equilibrium:
  P and Q are jointly determined
  No temporal or causal ordering between P and Q

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY DAGs CANNOT HANDLE THIS:

DAGs are Directed ACYCLIC Graphs:
  - Cannot have cycles: A → B → A is forbidden
  - Simultaneity requires cycles: P ↔ Q

Pearl's Solution Attempt:
  - "Structural equation models can be acyclic"
  - But supply-demand IS genuinely simultaneous

The Reality:
  - Price and quantity are equilibrium outcomes
  - Neither "causes" the other in a sequential sense
  - Both result from the intersection of supply and demand curves

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ECONOMETRIC SOLUTION:

Identification through exclusion restrictions:
  - Demand shifters (income Y) identify supply curve
  - Supply shifters (cost shocks) identify demand curve

Instrumental Variables:
  - Use exogenous shifters as instruments
  - 2SLS, 3SLS estimation

Key Insight:
  - Structural parameters (β_D, β_S) are identified
  - These are policy-invariant under most interventions
  - Can forecast effects of taxes, subsidies, etc.

================================================================================
IV. THE GENERALIZED ROY MODEL
================================================================================

FUNDAMENTAL SELECTION MODEL:

Outcome Equation:
  Y = D · Y₁ + (1-D) · Y₀

Where:
  - Y = Observed outcome
  - D ∈ {0,1} = Treatment indicator
  - Y₁ = Outcome if treated
  - Y₀ = Outcome if untreated

Potential Outcomes:
  Y₁ = μ₁(X) + U₁
  Y₀ = μ₀(X) + U₀

Selection Rule:
  D = 1[S > 0]
  S = μ_D(Z) - V

Where:
  - S = Net benefit (surplus) from treatment
  - Z = Observed determinants of selection
  - V = Unobserved cost/disutility of treatment

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TREATMENT EFFECT PARAMETERS:

Average Treatment Effect (ATE):
  ATE = E[Y₁ - Y₀]
      = E[μ₁(X) - μ₀(X)]

Treatment on Treated (TT):
  TT = E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 1]
     = ATE + E[U₁ - U₀ | S > 0]

Treatment on Untreated (TUT):
  TUT = E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 0]
      = ATE + E[U₁ - U₀ | S ≤ 0]

Marginal Treatment Effect (MTE):
  MTE(v) = E[Y₁ - Y₀ | V = v]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SELECTION ON GAINS:

Key Result:
  When Cov(U₁ - U₀, V) ≠ 0:
    TT ≠ ATE ≠ TUT

Interpretation:
  - People select treatment based on their expected gains
  - Those who benefit most are most likely to select in
  - TT overestimates ATE if high-benefit types select treatment
  - TUT may be negative even if ATE is positive

Implication for Policy:
  - Expanding treatment reaches different population
  - Effect for new participants ≠ effect for current participants
  - Need structural model to predict expansion effects (P3, P4)

================================================================================
V. THE LATE CRITIQUE
================================================================================

WHAT IS LATE?

Local Average Treatment Effect:
  LATE = E[Y₁ - Y₀ | Compliers]

Compliers:
  - Those induced to take treatment by the instrument
  - D(Z=1) = 1 but D(Z=0) = 0

Identification:
  LATE = [E(Y | Z=1) - E(Y | Z=0)] / [E(D | Z=1) - E(D | Z=0)]

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEMS WITH LATE:

1. INSTRUMENT-DEPENDENT:
   Different instruments identify different LATE populations
   LATE(Z₁) identifies effect for Z₁-compliers
   LATE(Z₂) identifies effect for Z₂-compliers
   These populations generally differ

2. NON-COMPARABLE:
   Cannot compare LATE estimates across studies
   "Apples and oranges" - different populations

3. NOT POLICY-RELEVANT:
   Policy changes alter the complier population
   LATE for current policy ≠ LATE for expanded policy

4. REQUIRES MONOTONICITY:
   No "defiers" assumption: D(Z=1) ≥ D(Z=0) for all
   Often unrealistic

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

POLICY QUESTION:

LATE answers:
  "What is the effect for those induced to participate by THIS instrument?"

LATE does NOT answer:
  "What would happen if we expanded the program?" (P3)
  "What would a different intervention achieve?" (P4)

For P3, P4: Need structural parameters, not local effects.

================================================================================
VI. IMPLICATIONS FOR EBF
================================================================================

1. STRUCTURAL PARAMETERS:

EBF behavioral parameters should be STRUCTURAL:
  - λ (loss aversion): Deep preference parameter
  - β (discount factor): Time preference parameter
  - γ (complementarity): Interaction parameter

Why structural?
  - Policy-invariant under Lucas Critique
  - Allow P3 (forecast to new context)
  - Allow P4 (design new interventions)

Treatment effects (ATE, LATE) are insufficient:
  - Context-dependent
  - May not extrapolate
  - Cannot design new policies

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2. CONTEXT AS MECHANISM FIXING:

EBF context dimensions (Ψ) correspond to which mechanisms are held fixed:

  Ψ_I (Institutional): Rules, defaults, legal framework
  Ψ_S (Social): Norms, peer effects, reference groups
  Ψ_C (Cognitive): Attention, processing, mental state
  Ψ_K (Cultural): Values, beliefs, traditions
  Ψ_E (Economic): Resources, constraints, prices
  Ψ_T (Temporal): Timing, deadlines, life stage
  Ψ_M (Material): Tools, technology, infrastructure
  Ψ_F (Physical): Location, environment, space

Different interventions FIX different mechanisms:
  - Nudge fixes Ψ_I (changes default)
  - Social norm intervention fixes Ψ_S (changes reference)
  - Timing intervention fixes Ψ_T (changes when)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3. P1-P4 FRAMEWORK FOR INTERVENTIONS:

P1 (Treatment on Treated):
  "Did the nudge work for those who received it?"
  → Measured by field experiment

P2 (Treatment on Untreated):
  "Would it work for non-participants?"
  → Requires selection model

P3 (Policy Forecast):
  "Will it work in a new context?"
  → Requires structural parameters + context mapping

P4 (New Policy Effects):
  "Can we design better interventions?"
  → Requires full structural model (10C + Ψ)

EBF aims to answer all four questions through:
  - Structural parameters (λ, β, γ)
  - Context specification (Ψ)
  - Mechanism understanding (10C)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4. SIMULTANEITY IN BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS:

Examples where DAGs are insufficient:
  - Present bias ↔ Saving ↔ Wealth ↔ Future self-control
  - Social norms ↔ Behavior ↔ Peer behavior ↔ Norms
  - Attention ↔ Choice ↔ Learning ↔ Attention

EBF handles these through:
  - Structural equations (not just DAGs)
  - Equilibrium concepts (feedback loops)
  - Dynamic models (intertemporal)

================================================================================
VII. SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. THREE FRAMEWORKS:
   - Econometric: Structural equations, handles simultaneity, P1-P4
   - Neyman-Rubin: Potential outcomes, P1-P2, clear in RCTs
   - Pearl: DAGs, graphical identification, no simultaneity

2. FIXING ≠ CONDITIONING:
   - Fixing changes the DGP (intervention)
   - Conditioning selects from distribution (observation)
   - Conflating these → confounding bias

3. SIMULTANEITY:
   - DAGs cannot represent feedback loops
   - Econometric models essential for supply/demand, etc.

4. ROY MODEL:
   - Selection on gains → TT ≠ ATE ≠ TUT
   - Structural model needed for expansion effects

5. LATE LIMITATIONS:
   - Instrument-dependent
   - Not policy-relevant for P3, P4

6. EBF IMPLICATIONS:
   - Use structural parameters (λ, β, γ)
   - Context (Ψ) = mechanism fixing
   - P1-P4 framework for intervention evaluation
   - Structural models required for policy design
