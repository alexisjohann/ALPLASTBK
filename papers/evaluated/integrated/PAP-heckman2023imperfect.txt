================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper considers the problem of making inferences about the effects of a
program on multiple outcomes when the assignment of treatment status is
imperfectly randomized. By imperfect randomization we mean that treatment
status is reassigned after an initial randomization on the basis of
characteristics that may be observed or unobserved by the analyst.

We develop a partial identification approach to this problem that makes use of
information limiting the extent to which randomization is imperfect to show
that it is still possible to make nontrivial inferences about the effects of
the program in such settings.

We consider a family of null hypotheses in which each null hypothesis specifies
that the program has no effect on one of many outcomes of interest. Under weak
assumptions, we construct a procedure for testing this family of null
hypotheses in a way that controls the familywise error rate—the probability of
even one false rejection—in finite samples.

================================================================================
I. THE FUNDAMENTAL PROBLEM
================================================================================

CORE INSIGHT:
Real-world experiments often involve post-randomization reassignment of
treatment status based on characteristics that may be observed or unobserved
by the analyst.

THE CHALLENGE:
Since these characteristics may affect outcomes, differences in outcomes
between treated and untreated groups may be due to:
1. The treatment itself, OR
2. Imperfections in the randomization

SOLUTION APPROACH:
Partial identification using information that LIMITS the extent to which
randomization is imperfect.

================================================================================
II. THE HIGHSCOPE PERRY PRESCHOOL PROGRAM
================================================================================

PROGRAM DETAILS:
- Location: Perry Elementary School, Ypsilanti, Michigan
- Period: Early 1960s
- Treatment: 2.5-hour preschool (weekdays) + weekly home visits
- Duration: 2 years, starting at age 3
- Sample: 123 children from 104 families (disadvantaged African-American)

ELIGIBILITY:
- Stanford-Binet IQ: 70-85
- Low socio-economic status

FOLLOW-UP:
- Yearly interviews ages 3-15
- Major interviews at ages 19, 27, 40
- Over 91% retention through age 40

================================================================================
III. THE RANDOMIZATION PROCEDURE (5 STEPS)
================================================================================

STEP 1: SIBLING ASSIGNMENT
Younger siblings assigned same treatment status as elder siblings.
→ Rationale: Avoid within-family spillovers

STEP 2: INITIAL GROUPING
Participants ranked by Stanford-Binet IQ, ties broken randomly.
Two groups formed: odd-ranked and even-ranked.

STEP 3: BALANCE ADJUSTMENT
Some exchanges between groups to balance:
- Gender
- Socio-economic status
While keeping IQ roughly constant.

STEP 4: RANDOM ASSIGNMENT
Groups labeled "treatment" and "control" with equal probability.

STEP 5: POST-RANDOMIZATION REASSIGNMENT (CRITICAL!)
Some participants with single mothers who were working and UNAVAILABLE
for weekly home visits were moved from treatment to control.

KEY PROBLEM:
Step 5 depends on UNOBSERVED characteristic U (availability for home visits).
This characteristic may be related to outcomes!

================================================================================
IV. FORMAL FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

NOTATION:
- Y_{i,j,k}(d) = Potential outcome k for sibling i in family j under treatment d
- D_j = Treatment status of family j
- Z_j = (Gender, SES, IQ, Wave) - observed characteristics
- U_j = Indicator: mother working AND unavailable (UNOBSERVED)
- MW_j = Indicator: mother working (OBSERVED)

TREATMENT STATUS:
D = δ(D̃, Z, U) = δ₂(δ₁(D̃, Z), U)

Where:
- D̃ = Initial randomization (Steps 1-4)
- δ₁ = Balance adjustment (Step 3)
- δ₂ = Post-randomization reassignment (Step 5)

KEY SETS:
- G_{Z,U} = Permutations preserving (z,u) pairs
- H_{Z,U} = Sign changes respecting U structure
- U(D,MW) = {u: u_j=0 if D_j=1 or MW_j=0}

================================================================================
V. ASSUMPTIONS
================================================================================

ASSUMPTION 3.1 (Conditional Independence):
(Y_k(d): d∈supp(D), k∈K) ⊥⊥ D | Z, U

→ Treatment assignment only uses Z and U

ASSUMPTION 3.2 (Equivariance):
δ₁(gd, gz) = gδ₁(d, z) for any g∈G

→ "Ordering of participants doesn't matter"

ASSUMPTION 3.3 (Symmetry):
hδ₁(d, z) = δ₁(hd, z) for any h∈H_z

→ "Odd/even labels don't matter"

ASSUMPTION 3.4 (Step 5 Rule):
j-th component of δ₂(d,u) equals:
- 0 if d_j=1 AND u_j=1
- d_j otherwise

→ Working mothers unavailable for home visits moved to control

ASSUMPTION 3.5 (Logical Constraint):
U_j = 0 if MW_j = 0

→ Can't be "working and unavailable" if not working

================================================================================
VI. KEY LEMMA - SYMMETRIES IN TREATMENT DISTRIBUTION
================================================================================

LEMMA 4.1:
Let g ∈ G_{Z,U} and h ∈ H_{Z,U}. Then:

(i) Under Assumptions 3.2 and 3.4:
    gδ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U =ᵈ δ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U

(ii) Under Assumptions 3.3 and 3.4:
    hδ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U =ᵈ δ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U

(iii) Under Assumptions 3.2-3.4:
    hgδ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U =ᵈ δ(D̃,Z,U)|Z,U

IMPLICATION:
These symmetries enable construction of valid randomization tests
despite imperfect randomization!

================================================================================
VII. TESTING PROCEDURE - SINGLE HYPOTHESIS
================================================================================

NULL HYPOTHESIS:
H_L: P ∈ ω_L (program has no effect on outcomes in L)

TEST STATISTIC:
T_L = T_L(X_L) where X_L = ((Y_k: k∈L), D, Z)

CRITICAL VALUE:
c̄_L(X_L, 1-α) = max_{u∈U(D,MW)} c_L(X_L, u, 1-α)

Where c_L(X_L, u, 1-α) is the (1-α) quantile of:
{T_L(hgX_L): g∈G_{Z,u}, h∈H_{Z,u}}

THEOREM 4.1:
Under Assumptions 3.1-3.5, rejecting H_L when T_L > c̄_L(1-α)
controls Type I error at level α IN FINITE SAMPLES.

================================================================================
VIII. TESTING PROCEDURE - MULTIPLE HYPOTHESES
================================================================================

PROBLEM:
Testing multiple outcomes leads to spurious discoveries.

SOLUTION:
Romano-Wolf stepdown procedure (Algorithm 4.1)

ALGORITHM:
Step 1: Set L₁ = K. Test max_{k∈L₁} T_k against c̄_{L₁}(1-α).
        Reject H_k if T_k > c̄_{L₁}(1-α). Stop if none rejected.

Step j: Let L_j = remaining hypotheses.
        Test max_{k∈L_j} T_k against c̄_{L_j}(1-α).
        Reject H_k if T_k > c̄_{L_j}(1-α). Continue until none rejected.

THEOREM 4.2:
Under Assumptions 3.1-3.5, Algorithm 4.1 controls FWER at level α:
FWER_P ≤ α for all P ∈ Ω

================================================================================
X. KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. ROBUST EFFECTS PERSIST:
   Program effects on crime, early cognition, and female education
   remain significant even after:
   - Accounting for imperfect randomization
   - Correcting for multiple hypothesis testing
   - Using finite-sample valid inference

2. NAÏVE METHODS MISLEADING:
   - Naïve permutation tests don't account for Step 5
   - Asymptotic approximations may be poor with n=123

3. CONSERVATIVE APPROACH VALID:
   - Max-U critical values provide valid inference
   - Bounds on U from logical constraints (MW_j=0 → U_j=0)

4. METHODOLOGICAL CONTRIBUTION:
   - Framework applies to any experiment with imperfect randomization
   - Requires detailed knowledge of assignment procedure
   - Exploits remaining symmetries for valid tests

================================================================================
XI. EBF IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

1. INTERVENTION EVALUATION:
   - Real-world interventions rarely have perfect randomization
   - Must account for post-randomization adjustments
   - Partial identification provides bounds when point ID fails

2. MULTIPLE OUTCOMES:
   - Behavioral interventions typically affect many outcomes
   - FWER control essential for credibility
   - Romano-Wolf more powerful than Bonferroni

3. P1-P4 FRAMEWORK CONNECTION:
   - P1 (TT): Estimable with bounds under partial ID
   - P2 (TUT): Requires additional assumptions
   - P3-P4: Need structural model for extrapolation

4. PARAMETER ESTIMATION:
   - EBF parameters may be estimated from imperfect experiments
   - Bounds reflect uncertainty from unobserved characteristics
   - Finite-sample validity important for small samples

5. CONTEXT DEPENDENCE (Ψ):
   - U is unobserved context affecting treatment assignment
   - Demonstrates how context enters causal inference
   - Shows importance of documenting assignment procedures

================================================================================
XII. COMPARISON WITH OTHER APPROACHES
================================================================================

VS. HECKMAN ET AL. (2010a):
- They condition on observed characteristics only
- Require parametric assumptions for power
- Our approach allows unobserved characteristics

VS. HECKMAN & KARAPAKULA (2019):
- They model Step 5 as analyst choosing accommodations randomly
- Our approach treats unavailability as characteristic related to outcomes
- Both provide finite-sample valid inference

VS. NAÏVE PERMUTATION:
- Naïve ignores both Step 3 (balance) and Step 5 (reassignment)
- No reason to expect Type I error control
- Included only for comparison
