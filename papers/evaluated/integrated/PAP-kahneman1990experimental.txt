Title: Experimental Tests of the Endowment Effect and the Coase Theorem
Authors: Daniel Kahneman, Jack L. Knetsch, Richard H. Thaler
Year: 1990
Journal: Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 98, No. 6, pp. 1325-1348

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Contrary to theoretical expectations, measures of willingness-to-accept (WTA)
greatly exceed measures of willingness-to-pay (WTP). This paper reports several
experiments that demonstrate that this "endowment effect" persists even in
market settings with opportunities to learn.

Consumption objects (e.g., coffee mugs) are randomly given to half the subjects
in an experiment. Markets for the mugs are then conducted. The Coase theorem
predicts that about half the mugs will trade, but observed volume is always
significantly less.

When markets for "induced-value" tokens are conducted, the predicted volume is
observed, suggesting that transactions costs cannot explain the undertrading
for consumption goods. The effect is seen as a manifestation of "loss aversion,"
the generalization that losses are weighted substantially more than objectively
commensurate gains.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. WTA-WTP GAP: Median selling price ($5.25) nearly twice buying price
   ($2.25-$2.75) for identical mugs

2. UNDERTRADING: Far fewer trades than Coase theorem predicts - people don't
   give up what they own

3. ENDOWMENT EFFECT: Ownership increases valuation beyond any income or
   wealth effect

4. NOT TRANSACTION COSTS: Induced-value tokens trade at predicted rates -
   effect specific to real goods

5. LOSS AVERSION: Giving up owned good is experienced as loss, weighted more
   than equivalent gain

6. MARKET PERSISTENCE: Effect persists with market experience and learning
   opportunities
