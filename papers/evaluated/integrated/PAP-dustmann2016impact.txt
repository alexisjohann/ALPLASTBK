Title: The Impact of Immigration: Why Do Studies Reach Such Different Results?
Authors: Christian Dustmann, Uta Schönberg, Jan Stuhler
Year: 2016
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 30, No. 4, pp. 31-56
DOI: 10.1257/jep.30.4.31

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper classifies the empirical literature on the wage impact of immigration
into three groups, where studies in the first two groups estimate different
relative effects, and studies in the third group estimate the total effect of
immigration on wages. The authors interpret the estimates obtained from the
different approaches through the lens of the canonical model to demonstrate
that they are not comparable.

The authors then relax two key assumptions in this literature, allowing for
inelastic and heterogeneous labor supply elasticities of natives and the
"downgrading" of immigrants.

"Downgrading" occurs when the position of immigrants in the labor market is
systematically lower than the position of natives with the same observed
education and experience levels — meaning immigrants receive lower returns
to the same measured skills than natives when these skills are acquired in
their country of origin.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Three methodological approaches yield non-comparable estimates
2. National skill-cell approach tends to produce more negative wage effects
3. Regional skill-cell approach shows different patterns than national approach
4. Immigrant "downgrading" is a key mechanism explaining wage impacts
5. Labor supply elasticity heterogeneity matters for interpreting results
6. Studies must account for skill transferability across borders
