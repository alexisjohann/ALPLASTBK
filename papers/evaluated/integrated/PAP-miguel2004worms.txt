PAP-miguel2004worms

Title: Worms: Identifying Impacts on Education and Health in the Presence of
       Treatment Externalities
Authors: Edward Miguel, Michael Kremer
Year: 2004
Journal: Econometrica, Vol. 72, No. 1, pp. 159-217

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper evaluates a school-based deworming program in Kenya that provided
mass treatment for intestinal worms at a cost of less than $0.50 per child per
year. The evaluation exploits the randomized phase-in of the program across
schools.

The key methodological contribution is identifying and quantifying treatment
externalities (spillover effects): by reducing disease transmission, deworming
benefits untreated children in treatment schools and children in nearby schools.

School-based deworming reduced serious worm infections by 61 percent and
reduced school absenteeism by 25 percent. Including spillover benefits, the
cost per additional year of school participation was $2.92, making deworming
far more cost-effective than alternative methods like school subsidies.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. LARGE SPILLOVER EFFECTS: Untreated children near treated schools also
   benefited - externalities account for significant share of total benefits

2. HEALTH IMPACTS: School-based deworming reduced moderate-to-heavy worm
   infections by 61% among treated children

3. EDUCATION IMPACTS: Treatment reduced school absenteeism by 25%, with
   additional 7.5% reduction from spillovers to nearby schools

4. COST-EFFECTIVENESS: At $2.92 per additional year of schooling, deworming
   far outperforms alternative interventions

5. RANDOMIZED DESIGN: Phased randomization across schools enables causal
   identification of both direct and spillover effects

6. EXTERNALITIES METHODOLOGY: Demonstrates how to estimate treatment effects
   when externalities are present - prevents underestimation of benefits
