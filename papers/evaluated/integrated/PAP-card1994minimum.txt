Title: Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry
       in New Jersey and Pennsylvania
Authors: David Card, Alan B. Krueger
Year: 1994
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 84, No. 4, pp. 772-793

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

On April 1, 1992, New Jersey's minimum wage rose from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour.
To evaluate the impact of the law, the authors surveyed 410 fast-food
restaurants in New Jersey and eastern Pennsylvania before and after the rise.

Comparisons of employment growth at stores in New Jersey and Pennsylvania
(where the minimum wage remained constant) provide estimates of the effect
of the higher minimum wage. The authors also compare employment changes at
stores in New Jersey that were initially paying high wages (above $5.00) to
changes at lower-wage stores.

Contrary to standard competitive labor market predictions, they find no
indication that the rise in the minimum wage reduced employment. In fact,
relative to stores in Pennsylvania, fast-food restaurants in New Jersey
increased employment by 13 percent.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NO EMPLOYMENT REDUCTION: Minimum wage increase did not reduce employment -
   contradicts standard competitive model predictions

2. EMPLOYMENT INCREASE: NJ fast-food employment rose 13% relative to PA after
   minimum wage increase - positive employment effect

3. DIFFERENCE-IN-DIFFERENCES: PA provides natural control group for NJ,
   enabling causal identification via diff-in-diff design

4. WITHIN-STATE COMPARISON: High-wage vs low-wage stores within NJ show same
   pattern - low-wage stores had larger employment increases

5. MONOPSONY INTERPRETATION: Results consistent with monopsony power in labor
   market where minimum wage increases employment

6. METHODOLOGICAL INNOVATION: Established difference-in-differences with
   survey data as credible quasi-experimental approach
