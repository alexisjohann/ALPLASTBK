Title: Do Workers Work More if Wages Are High? Evidence from a Randomized Field Experiment
Authors: Ernst Fehr, Lorenz Goette
Journal: American Economic Review
Volume: 97, Issue 1, Pages 298-317
Year: 2007
DOI: 10.1257/aer.97.1.298

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper reports results from a field experiment on bicycle messengers.
We test whether an exogenous increase in the piece rate leads to more effort.
Standard economic theory predicts a positive effort response. However, we
find that for those messengers who perceive the higher wage as a gift, effort
increases significantly. This is consistent with gift exchange and reciprocity.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
   - Field experiment with bicycle messengers
   - Random assignment to wage increase
   - Natural work environment

2. MAIN RESULTS
   - Overall: small positive effort response
   - Heterogeneity: response depends on wage perception
   - Gift perceivers: strong positive response

3. RECIPROCITY IN ACTION
   - Workers who view wage increase as "gift" reciprocate
   - Non-gift perceivers show income targeting
   - Field evidence for gift exchange theory

4. IMPLICATIONS
   - Laboratory findings replicate in field
   - Wage increases can increase productivity
   - But: depends on how wages are framed
