================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper provides a rigorous decomposition of dynamic complementarity in human
capital formation into three distinct components:

1. PRODUCTIVITY P(a) - The marginal product of accumulated skills
2. TRANSMISSION Q(a+j) - How skills propagate across developmental stages
3. STATIC COMPLEMENTARITY f₁,₂(a) - The interaction between skills and investment

The central result:

    ∂²K(a+j+1)
    ────────────────── = P(a) · Q(a+j) · f₁,₂(a+j)
    ∂I(a) ∂I'(a+j)

This decomposition enables testing dynamic complementarity WITHOUT imposing
arbitrary psychometric scales on skills measured at different ages.

Using data from China REACH, a large-scale home-visiting program, we demonstrate
that dynamic complementarity is present in language and cognitive skills, but
NOT in fine motor skills. Most importantly, we find that LOW-ABILITY children
benefit MOST from early investment - their learning rates show the largest
increases from prior skill accumulation.

================================================================================
I. THE TECHNOLOGY OF SKILL FORMATION
================================================================================

FUNDAMENTAL EQUATION:

    K(a+1) = f(a)(K(a), I(a))

Where:
- K(a+1) = Vector of skills at age a+1
- K(a) = Vector of skills at age a
- I(a) = Investment at age a (parenting, home visiting, etc.)
- f(a) = Age-specific production function

KEY INSIGHT:
The production function f(a) is AGE-SPECIFIC. Skills produced at different ages
may be QUALITATIVELY DIFFERENT, not just higher levels of the same thing.

================================================================================
II. THE DECOMPOSITION
================================================================================

DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY (Definition):

    ∂²K(a+j+1)
    ────────────────── > 0
    ∂I(a) ∂I'(a+j)

Meaning: Investment at age a raises the productivity of later investment I(a+j).

THE THREE COMPONENTS:

1. PRODUCTIVITY P(a):

   P(a) = f₁(a) = ∂K(a+1)/∂K(a)

   Interpretation: How much do past skills raise current skill production?

   Example: A child with better vocabulary at age 1 produces vocabulary
   faster at age 2.

2. TRANSMISSION Q(a+j) (Self-Productivity):

   Q(a+j) = ∏_{m=0}^{j-1} f₁(a+m)

   Interpretation: How skills propagate from age a to age a+j through the
   chain of intermediate production.

   Example: Skills at age 1 affect age 2, which affects age 3, etc.
   Q captures this cumulative transmission.

3. STATIC COMPLEMENTARITY f₁,₂(a):

   f₁,₂(a) = ∂²K(a+1)/∂K(a)∂I'(a)

   Interpretation: At a single point in time, how much does having higher
   skills amplify the effect of investment?

   Example: A child with better existing vocabulary learns more from the
   same reading session.

THE CENTRAL EQUATION:

    Dynamic Complementarity = P(a) · Q(a+j) · f₁,₂(a+j)

This shows that dynamic complementarity has THREE sources:
- Current productivity of skills
- Transmission of early skills to later stages
- Skills × Investment interaction at each stage

================================================================================
III. WHY THIS DECOMPOSITION MATTERS
================================================================================

PRACTICAL IMPORTANCE:

1. SCALE-FREE TESTING:
   The decomposition can be tested WITHOUT assuming skills are measured on a
   common scale across ages. This is critical because:
   - Test scores are at best ORDINAL
   - Different skills EMERGE at different ages
   - Standard value-added models impose arbitrary normalizations

2. MECHANISM UNDERSTANDING:
   By decomposing into P, Q, and f₁,₂, we understand WHY early investment
   matters:
   - Is it because early skills are very productive? (high P)
   - Is it because skills transmit well across stages? (high Q)
   - Is it because skills and investment interact strongly? (high f₁,₂)

3. HETEROGENEITY DETECTION:
   Different populations may differ in which component drives complementarity.
   This matters for intervention design.

================================================================================
IV. THE CHINA REACH PROGRAM
================================================================================

PROGRAM DETAILS:
- Organized by: China Development Research Foundation (CDRF)
- Intervention: Home-visiting for early childhood development
- Frequency: Weekly visits, 1 hour each
- Target: Multidimensional skill development (language, cognitive, motor)

MEASUREMENT:
- Skills organized by UHP (Uzgiris-Hunt-Palmer) difficulty levels
- Language: 11 levels, Cognitive: multiple levels, Fine Motor: multiple levels
- Within each level: Tasks are essentially IDENTICAL (no hierarchy)
- Across levels: Increasing difficulty, qualitatively DIFFERENT skills

NATURAL EXPERIMENT:
- Children enter the program at DIFFERENT ages
- All face same age-specific curriculum regardless of prior exposure
- Comparison: Full exposure (early start) vs Late enrollment (late start)

================================================================================
V. TESTING DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY
================================================================================

THE TEST:
Compare TIME TO FIRST MASTERY for children with different exposure histories.

If dynamic complementarity holds:
- Children with early investment should learn FASTER at later levels
- This difference should be LARGER for harder levels

MEASURE:
d(s, ℓ) = Number of trials until first success at UHP level ℓ

ADVANTAGES:
- Scale-free: Measured in TRIALS, not arbitrary test scores
- Comparable: Same measure across different skill domains
- Observable: Does not require common scale assumption

================================================================================
VI. RESULTS - LANGUAGE SKILLS
================================================================================

COMPARISON: Full Exposure vs Late Enrollment (at Level 6)

                        Level 7   Level 8   Level 9   Level 10  Level 11
Full Exposure:           2.487     2.079     1.496     1.404     1.348
Late Enrollment:         2.518     2.367     1.686     1.454     1.698
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Difference:             -0.031    -0.288    -0.190    -0.050    -0.350
p-value:                 0.870     0.024     0.017     0.608     0.000
Stepdown p-value:        0.864     0.059     0.059     0.827     0.000

INTERPRETATION:
- At levels 8, 9, and 11, full exposure children need SIGNIFICANTLY fewer trials
- This is evidence of DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY
- Early investment (before level 6) speeds up learning at later levels

================================================================================
VII. RESULTS - BY ABILITY GROUP
================================================================================

Children classified by initial ability (high, normal, low) show different
patterns:

HIGH ABILITY:
- Highest absolute performance at all levels
- Smallest MARGINAL gains from early investment
- Already performing near ceiling

NORMAL ABILITY:
- Intermediate performance levels
- Moderate gains from early investment
- Show catch-up toward high ability over time

LOW ABILITY:
- Lowest initial performance
- LARGEST gains from early investment
- Show MOST pronounced catch-up

KEY FINDING:
The difference between full exposure and late enrollment is LARGEST for
low-ability children. This means:

γ_low > γ_normal > γ_high

Dynamic complementarity is STRONGEST for disadvantaged children.

================================================================================
VIII. RESULTS - COGNITIVE VS FINE MOTOR
================================================================================

COGNITIVE SKILLS:
- Dynamic complementarity: CONFIRMED
- Pattern similar to language skills
- Catch-up effect present for low-ability children

FINE MOTOR SKILLS:
- Dynamic complementarity: NOT CONFIRMED
- At most modest learning within levels
- Little evidence of catch-up

IMPLICATION:
Dynamic complementarity is DOMAIN-SPECIFIC. Not all skills exhibit it.
This means:
- γ varies by skill domain
- Policy implications depend on targeted skills
- Early investment may not accelerate ALL skill development

================================================================================
IX. LEARNING PATTERNS WITHIN AND ACROSS LEVELS
================================================================================

WITHIN-LEVEL PATTERN:
- Passing rates INCREASE with number of lessons
- Evidence of LEARNING within difficulty levels
- This increase is steeper for children with prior investment

ACROSS-LEVEL PATTERN (Critical):
- When transitioning to NEW level, passing rates DECLINE
- Evidence that NEW, DIFFERENT skills are being taught
- Not just "more of the same"

RECOVERY PATTERN:
- After initial decline, passing rates increase again
- As children learn the NEW skills at the new level

This pattern supports SKILL EMERGENCE:
- Skills at level N+1 are qualitatively different from level N
- Not just higher amounts of the same skill
- This is why common scales are problematic

================================================================================
X. EBF IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

1. COMPLEMENTARITY DECOMPOSITION (γ):
   EBF complementarity parameter γ can be decomposed:

   γ = P(a) · Q(a+j) · f₁,₂(a+j)

   This provides:
   - Three estimable components
   - Mechanism understanding
   - Scale-free identification

2. HETEROGENEOUS EFFECTS:
   Complementarity varies by:
   - Ability group: γ_low > γ_normal > γ_high
   - Skill domain: γ_language ≈ γ_cognitive >> γ_fine_motor

   EBF parameter estimates should reflect this heterogeneity.

3. INTERVENTION TARGETING:
   Maximum impact comes from:
   - Targeting LOW-ABILITY children (highest γ)
   - Focusing on skills with high complementarity
   - Early investment to leverage transmission Q(a+j)

4. CATCH-UP EFFECTS:
   Dynamic complementarity enables disadvantaged children to partially
   close the gap with advantaged children. This happens because:
   - Their γ is highest
   - Investment accelerates their learning rate more

5. SCALE-FREE TESTING:
   For EBF parameter validation:
   - Use time-to-mastery within levels
   - Compare learning rates across exposure groups
   - Avoid relying on arbitrary psychometric scales

6. LLMMC PRIORS:
   LLMMC priors for γ should:
   - Vary by target population ability level
   - Be domain-specific (language/cognitive vs motor)
   - Account for prior exposure levels

================================================================================
XI. SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. DECOMPOSITION:
   Dynamic complementarity = P(a) · Q(a+j) · f₁,₂(a+j)
   - P: Productivity of skills
   - Q: Transmission across stages
   - f₁,₂: Static skills × investment interaction

2. SCALE-FREE TEST:
   Using time-to-mastery, can test complementarity without arbitrary scales.

3. DOMAIN SPECIFICITY:
   Complementarity present in language/cognitive, NOT fine motor.

4. HETEROGENEITY:
   Low-ability children benefit MOST (highest γ).

5. CATCH-UP:
   Dynamic complementarity enables disadvantaged children to partially
   close developmental gaps.

6. EBF APPLICATION:
   - γ decomposes into three estimable components
   - Targeting should focus on populations with highest γ
   - Scale-free methods enable robust parameter estimation
