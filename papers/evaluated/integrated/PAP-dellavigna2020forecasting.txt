ABSTRACT

Forecasts of experimental results can clarify the interpretation of research
results, mitigate publication bias, and improve experimental designs. We
collect forecasts of the results of three Registered Reports preliminarily
accepted to the Journal of Development Economics, randomly varying four
features: (1) small versus large reference values; (2) whether predictions
are in raw units or standard deviations; (3) text-entry versus slider
responses; and (4) small versus large slider bounds.

Forecasts are generally robust to elicitation features, though wider slider
bounds are associated with higher forecasts throughout the forecast
distribution. We make preliminary recommendations on how many forecasts
should be gathered.

MOTIVATION: THREE BENEFITS OF FORECASTING

1. INTERPRETATION OF RESULTS
   - Puts results in context of prior expectations
   - Often of independent interest
   - Example: Camerer et al. (2016) - replication probability
   - Example: DellaVigna & Pope (2018) - behavioral motivators

2. MITIGATE PUBLICATION BIAS
   - Null results less likely published (Franco et al. 2014)
   - With priors, compare to average prediction, not just null
   - Changes what constitutes a "surprising" result

3. IMPROVE EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
   - Gauge which treatment arms have higher value of information
   - Helps with power calculations
   - Resource allocation across potential interventions

FORECAST STUDIES (3 Registered Reports at JDE)

Study 1: Yang et al. (2019) - Mozambique
- HIV testing intervention via home visits
- Outcome: Household member received HIV testing in last year

Study 2: Blimpo & Pugatch (2019) - Rwanda
- Teacher training program for entrepreneurship education
- Outcomes: Dropout, business participation, test scores

Study 3: Bouguen & Dillon (2019) - Burkina Faso
- Unconditional cash, asset, and nutrition transfers
- Outcomes: Food consumption, health consumption

RANDOMIZED ELICITATION FEATURES

Feature 1: REFERENCE VALUE
- Small (0.1 SD) vs Large (0.3 SD) in example
- Test for anchoring from examples

Feature 2: UNITS
- Raw units (e.g., percentage points) vs Standard deviations
- Test whether unfamiliar units affect forecasts

Feature 3: RESPONSE FORMAT
- Text entry vs Slider bar
- Test for input method effects

Feature 4: SLIDER BOUNDS
- Small (±0.5 SD) vs Large (±1.0 SD)
- Test for anchoring from bounds

KEY RESULTS

OVERALL FORECASTS
- Average predicted effect: 0.16 SD (SE = 0.01)
- Comparable to 0.12 SD average from 635 development interventions (Vivalt)
- Highly precise with n=106

BY ELICITATION FEATURE:

1. Reference Value (0.1 vs 0.3 SD)
   - Small reference: 0.16 SD
   - Large reference: 0.17 SD
   - p-value: 0.53
   - CONCLUSION: No significant effect

2. Units (Raw vs SD)
   - Raw units: 0.16 SD
   - Standard deviations: 0.17 SD
   - p-value: 0.75
   - CONCLUSION: No significant effect

3. Entry Format (Text vs Slider)
   - Text entry: 0.16 SD
   - Slider: 0.17 SD
   - p-value: 0.93
   - CONCLUSION: No significant effect on average

4. Slider Bounds (±0.5 vs ±1.0 SD)
   - Small bounds: 0.12 SD
   - Large bounds: 0.21 SD
   - p-value: 0.00
   - CONCLUSION: SIGNIFICANT EFFECT - wider bounds → higher forecasts

SLIDER BOUNDS: DEEPER ANALYSIS

The effect is NOT due to censoring at the top:
- Only 1 respondent in narrow condition gave prediction of 0.5 SD
- The ENTIRE distribution shifts rightward with wider bounds

Interpretation:
- Forecasters may make inference from bounds
- Bounds may anchor responses
- Important for cross-study comparability

SAMPLE SIZE RECOMMENDATION

With 73 responses (Yang et al.):
- Average forecast: 0.23 SD
- 95% CI: [0.19, 0.27]
- SE = 0.02

RECOMMENDATION: 15-30 forecasters sufficient for precise estimates

BY FORECASTER TYPE (not pre-registered)
- Faculty: 0.15 SD
- PhD student: 0.17 SD
- Researcher: 0.17 SD
- Practitioner: 0.13 SD
- No significant differences (p > 0.45 for all comparisons)

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS

1. Use consistent slider bounds across studies for comparability
2. 15-30 forecasters are sufficient for most purposes
3. Raw units or SDs both acceptable
4. Text entry or sliders both acceptable
5. Reference examples do not significantly anchor
