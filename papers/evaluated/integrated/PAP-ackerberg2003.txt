Title: Advertising, Learning, and Consumer Choice in Experience Good Markets: An Empirical Examination
Authors: Daniel A. Ackerberg
Year: 2003
Journal: International Economic Review, Vol. 44, No. 3, pp. 1007-1040
DOI: 10.1111/1468-2354.t01-2-00098

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This article empirically analyzes different effects of advertising in a
nondurable, experience good market. A dynamic learning model of consumer
behavior is presented in which the author allows both "informative" effects
of advertising and "prestige" or "image" effects of advertising.

This learning model is estimated using consumer level panel data tracking
grocery purchases and advertising exposures over time. Empirical results
suggest that in this market, advertising's primary effect was that of
informing consumers.

The estimates are used to quantify the value of this information to consumers
and evaluate the welfare implications of an alternative advertising regulatory
regime.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. INFORMATIVE vs PRESTIGE: Distinguishes between informative effects
   (providing product information) and prestige/image effects (brand utility)

2. LEARNING MODEL: Dynamic learning model where consumers update beliefs
   about product quality through experience and advertising

3. INFORMATION DOMINATES: In this market, advertising's primary effect was
   informative rather than image-based

4. EXPERIENCE GOODS: Products whose quality can only be assessed after
   consumption require different information mechanisms

5. CONSUMER WELFARE: Quantifies the value of advertising information to
   consumers

6. REGULATORY IMPLICATIONS: Provides framework for evaluating advertising
   regulation welfare effects
