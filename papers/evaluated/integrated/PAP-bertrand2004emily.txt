Title: Are Emily and Greg More Employable Than Lakisha and Jamal? A Field Experiment on Labor Market Discrimination
Authors: Marianne Bertrand, Sendhil Mullainathan
Year: 2004
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 94, No. 4, pp. 991-1013

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

We perform a field experiment to measure racial discrimination in the labor
market. We respond with fictitious resumes to help-wanted ads in Boston and
Chicago newspapers. To manipulate perception of race, each resume is assigned
either a very African American sounding name or a very White sounding name.

White names receive 50 percent more callbacks for interviews. Callback rates
are 9.65% for White names vs 6.45% for African American names - a gap of
3.2 percentage points or about 50% more callbacks.

Higher quality resumes (better credentials, more experience) increase callback
rates for White names but produce smaller improvements for African American
names. Discrimination appears uniform across occupation, industry, and
employer size.

Racial gaps exist both when employers offer higher wages and when they offer
lower wages. Federal contractors and employers who explicitly state "Equal
Opportunity Employer" discriminate as much as others.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CALLBACK GAP: White names get 50% more callbacks (9.65% vs 6.45%)

2. QUALITY PENALTY: Better credentials help White applicants more than
   African American applicants - discrimination worsens with quality

3. UNIFORM DISCRIMINATION: Similar discrimination across industries,
   occupations, employer sizes

4. EOE IRRELEVANT: "Equal Opportunity Employer" statements don't reduce
   discrimination

5. WAGE INDEPENDENT: Discrimination exists at both high and low wage levels

6. NAME SUFFICIENT: Name alone (without other racial signals) triggers
   discriminatory response
