Title: The Fiscal Effects of Immigration to the UK
Authors: Christian Dustmann, Tommaso Frattini
Year: 2014
Journal: The Economic Journal, Vol. 124, No. 580, pp. F593-F643
DOI: 10.1111/ecoj.12181

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The paper investigates the fiscal impact of immigration on the UK economy,
with a focus on the period since 1995.

KEY FINDINGS:

When considering the resident immigrant population in each year from 1995 to
2011, immigrants from the European Economic Area (EEA) have made a POSITIVE
FISCAL CONTRIBUTION.

EEA immigrants made a positive fiscal contribution even during periods when
the UK was running budget deficits, while Non-EEA immigrants, not dissimilar
to natives, have made a negative contribution.

For immigrants that arrived since 2000, contributions have been positive
throughout, and particularly so for immigrants from EEA countries.

The paper provides estimates for the overall immigrant population for the
period 1995-2012, and for more recent immigrants who arrived since 2000,
distinguishing between different origin groups.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EEA immigrants: POSITIVE fiscal contribution 1995-2011
2. Non-EEA immigrants: Similar to natives (slight negative)
3. Recent immigrants (since 2000): Positive contribution across groups
4. EEA immigrants contribute positively EVEN during budget deficits
5. Human capital "subsidy": £14 billion savings in education costs
6. Origin-based heterogeneity is substantial

================================================================================
METHODOLOGICAL CONTRIBUTION
================================================================================

- Dynamic fiscal accounting framework
- Distinguishes stock (all immigrants) vs flow (recent arrivals)
- Separates EEA vs Non-EEA origin effects
- Controls for demographic structure differences
- Long time series (1995-2012)
