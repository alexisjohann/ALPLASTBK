Title: Paying Not to Go to the Gym
Authors: Stefano DellaVigna, Ulrike Malmendier
Year: 2006
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 96, No. 3
DOI: 10.1257/aer.96.3.694

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

How do consumers choose from a menu of contracts?

DATA: Novel dataset from three U.S. health clubs
- Both contractual choice AND day-to-day attendance
- 7,752 members over three years

KEY FINDING - SYSTEMATIC OVERPAYING:

Members who choose FLAT MONTHLY FEE (>$70):
- Attend on average 4.3 times per month
- Pay PRICE PER EXPECTED VISIT of more than $17
- Could pay $10 per visit using 10-visit pass

MAGNITUDE OF MISTAKE:
On average, users forgo savings of $600 during membership.

ADDITIONAL FINDING - COMMITMENT VALUE:
Consumers who choose MONTHLY contract are 17% MORE LIKELY to stay
enrolled beyond one year than users committing for a year.

INTERPRETATION:
1. OVERCONFIDENCE about future attendance
2. Preference for COMMITMENT devices
3. TIME-INCONSISTENT preferences (want to exercise more than actually do)

This is a CLASSIC behavioral economics finding showing systematic
departure from rational choice theory.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Flat fee members pay $17/visit vs $10 alternative
2. Average foregone savings: $600
3. Monthly users 17% more likely to stay >1 year
4. Evidence of overconfidence and time-inconsistency
5. Classic behavioral economics paper
6. Real-world contract choice anomaly
