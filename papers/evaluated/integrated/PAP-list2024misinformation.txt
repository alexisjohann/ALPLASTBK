ABSTRACT

Misinformation is one of the leading concerns about the internet and social
media. This paper moves beyond the supply-side focus of the literature (fact
checking, labeling, platform interventions) to explore the demand side of
the market for misinformation. We leverage a framed field experiment with
Colombian adults during the 2022 presidential election to test interventions
designed to enhance critical thinking.

We randomly assign participants to: (1) control, (2) personality test showing
cognitive traits and biases, (3) video designed to provoke reflection on
behavioral biases, or (4) both interventions. We then measure vulnerability
to misinformation and willingness to report fake content.

KEY FINDINGS

1. VIDEO EFFECT ON MISINFORMATION VULNERABILITY
   - Estimate: β = -0.040 (SE = 0.019)
   - Interpretation: 30.3% reduction in considering fake news reliable
   - Significance: p < 0.05
   - Strongest for neutral (non-political) fake headlines: -0.074 (32.3%)

2. PERSONALITY TEST ALONE
   - No significant effect on vulnerability
   - Knowing one's own biases is not as effective as relatable videos

3. JOINT INTERVENTION (VIDEO + TEST) ON REPORTING BEHAVIOR
   - Estimate: β = 0.085 (SE = 0.030)
   - Interpretation: 15% increase in willingness to report fake tweet
   - Significance: p < 0.01
   - Only for non-political tweet (Tweet A)

4. INTERVENTION COMPLEMENTARITY
   - Video alone: Reduces misinformation vulnerability
   - Personality test alone: No significant effect
   - Video + Test: Reduces vulnerability AND increases reporting behavior
   - Suggests γ(video, test) > 0 for the reporting outcome

MECHANISM FINDINGS

1. DEHUMANIZATION REDUCTION
   - Video reduces negative characterization of political left: -0.101 (15%)
   - Video reduces negative characterization of political right: -0.061 (9%)
   - Both significant (p < 0.01 and p < 0.05)

2. DISCRIMINATION PERCEPTION
   - Estimate: 0.045 (SE = 0.027)
   - 19.5% increase in not feeling discriminated
   - Marginally significant (p < 0.10)

3. TRUST AND AMBIGUITY AVERSION
   - No significant effects on these mediators

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN

Setting: Online framed field experiment
Context: 2022 Colombian Presidential Election
Sample: 2,235 Colombian adults (after removing duplicates from 3,297 raw)
IRB: University of Chicago IRB22-1217

Treatment Arms:
- Control (n=645): No intervention
- Personality Test Only (n=750): Test showing cognitive traits and biases
- Video Only (n=408): Thought-provoking video on behavioral biases
- Both (n=432): Video + Personality Test

Outcomes:
1. Misinformation vulnerability (19 news headlines rated 1-4)
2. Willingness to report fake tweets (2 tweets, one political, one not)
3. Mediators: trust, dehumanization, discrimination perception, ambiguity

HETEROGENEITY BY POLITICAL ORIENTATION

- Effects strongest for fake news targeting political right
- But control group more likely to trust neutral than political news
- Suggests differential baseline skepticism by political content

KEY INSIGHT FROM AUTHORS

"Videos that provoke viewers to slow down and reconsider their thinking are
successful in increasing skepticism and reducing misinformation vulnerability.
Yet, our assessment that aims at a similar result by providing individuals
with evidence about their cognitive traits has limited effects."

This supports System 2 activation through emotional/relatable content rather
than purely cognitive/informational approaches.

POLICY IMPLICATIONS

1. Demand-side interventions can reduce misinformation vulnerability
2. Videos showing relatable people are more effective than cognitive assessments
3. Combined interventions may be necessary to change behavior (not just beliefs)
4. Demand-side interventions can affect supply side (spillover to reporting)
