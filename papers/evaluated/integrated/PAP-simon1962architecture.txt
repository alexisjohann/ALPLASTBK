Title: The Architecture of Complexity
Authors: Herbert A. Simon
Year: 1962
Journal: Proceedings of the American Philosophical Society, Vol. 106, No. 6, pp. 467-482

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

FOUNDATIONAL PAPER on complex systems and hierarchy.

Simon argues with exceptional clarity for the unavoidability of hierarchies in
complex systems. He shows how, from an evolutionary perspective, it is much
more efficient for complex systems to be composed of sub-systems which are
hierarchically organized.

Hierarchy is not an accidental feature of complex systems, it is an essential
one. Complex systems are not simply hierarchical systems. If they were simply
hierarchical, they would be fully decomposable. Unfortunately they are not
neatly nested like Russian dolls - there are cross-cutting connections.

The paper interweaves notions of complexity, hierarchy, modularity, and
near-decomposability in discussing the evolution and self-organization of
complex systems.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NEAR-DECOMPOSABILITY: Complex systems have weak interactions between
   subsystems, strong interactions within subsystems

2. HIERARCHY NECESSITY: Hierarchical structure is essential for complex
   systems to evolve and persist

3. EVOLUTIONARY EFFICIENCY: Hierarchical systems can evolve much faster
   than non-hierarchical ones (watchmaker parable)

4. MODULARITY: Subsystems can be understood and modified semi-independently

5. CROSS-CUTTING CONNECTIONS: Real systems not perfectly decomposable -
   some connections cross hierarchical boundaries

6. GENERAL SYSTEMS: Provides framework applicable to physical, biological,
   and social systems
