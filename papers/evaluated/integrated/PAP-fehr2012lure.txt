================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Authority and power permeate political, social, and economic life, but empirical
knowledge about the motivational origins and consequences of authority is limited.

We study the motivation and incentive effects of authority experimentally in an
authority-delegation game. Individuals often retain authority even when its
delegation is in their material interest—suggesting that authority has
nonpecuniary consequences for utility.

Authority also leads to overprovision of effort by the controlling parties,
while a large percentage of subordinates underprovide effort despite pecuniary
incentives to the contrary.

Authority thus has important motivational consequences that exacerbate the
inefficiencies arising from suboptimal delegation choices.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

- People RETAIN AUTHORITY even when delegation is materially beneficial
  → Authority has intrinsic (non-pecuniary) value

- Authority holders OVERPROVIDE EFFORT (work too hard)
  → Control itself is motivating

- Subordinates UNDERPROVIDE EFFORT despite monetary incentives
  → Lack of control reduces motivation

- These effects CREATE INEFFICIENCIES in organizations:
  * Suboptimal delegation decisions
  * Misallocated effort (too much from principals, too little from agents)

================================================================================
METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

- Study Design: Laboratory Experiment (Authority-Delegation Game)
- Sample: Experimental subjects
- Identification: Comparison of delegation vs. retention decisions against
  material payoff benchmarks
- Key manipulation: Opportunity to delegate authority with known payoff
  consequences
