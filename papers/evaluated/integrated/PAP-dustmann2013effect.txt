Title: The Fiscal Effects of Immigration to the UK
Authors: Christian Dustmann, Tommaso Frattini
Year: 2014 (CReAM DP 2013)
Journal: The Economic Journal, Vol. 124, Issue 580, pp. F593-F643
DOI: 10.1111/ecoj.12181

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper investigates the fiscal impact of immigration on the UK economy,
with a focus on the period since 1995. The authors provide estimates for the
overall immigrant population for the period between 1995 and 2012, and for
more recent immigrants who arrived since 2000, distinguishing between
immigrants from European versus non-European countries.

The findings indicate that, when considering the resident immigrant population
in each year from 1995 to 2011, immigrants from the European Economic Area (EEA)
have made a positive fiscal contribution, even during periods when the UK was
running budget deficits, while Non-EEA immigrants, not dissimilar to natives,
have made a negative contribution.

For immigrants that arrived since 2000, contributions have been positive
throughout, and particularly so for immigrants from EEA countries. Notable is
the strong positive contribution made by immigrants from countries that joined
the EU in 2004 (A8 countries).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EEA immigrants made positive fiscal contributions even during UK budget deficits
2. Non-EEA immigrants showed fiscal patterns similar to natives (slightly negative)
3. Post-2000 immigrants contributed positively regardless of origin
4. A8 country immigrants (EU 2004 accession) showed particularly strong positive contributions
5. Methodology: Difference between taxes paid and transfers/public goods consumed
