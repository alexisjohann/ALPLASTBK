Title: An Empirical Analysis of Cigarette Addiction
Authors: Gary S. Becker, Michael Grossman, Kevin M. Murphy
Year: 1994
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 84, No. 3, pp. 396-418

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper provides an empirical analysis of cigarette addiction using the
rational addiction framework developed by Becker and Murphy (1988). The
authors test the key predictions of the rational addiction model using
aggregate U.S. time-series data on cigarette consumption.

The rational addiction model predicts that current consumption depends on
both past consumption (habit formation) and future consumption (forward-
looking behavior). The authors test these predictions by examining how
cigarette consumption responds to past, current, and anticipated future
prices.

The empirical findings strongly support the rational addiction model:
consumers respond to future price changes, indicating they are forward-looking
rather than myopic in their addictive behavior.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. FORWARD-LOOKING ADDICTS: Cigarette consumers respond to anticipated future
   price changes, supporting rational addiction

2. ADJACENT COMPLEMENTARITY: Past consumption significantly affects current
   consumption, confirming habit formation

3. PRICE ELASTICITY: Both short-run and long-run price elasticities are
   significant; long-run elasticity is larger

4. NOT MYOPIC: Results reject the hypothesis that addicts are completely
   myopic or ignore future consequences

5. ANTICIPATORY RESPONSE: Consumers begin reducing consumption before price
   increases take effect

6. EMPIRICAL VALIDATION: Provides first large-scale empirical test of
   Becker-Murphy rational addiction theory
