Title: Risk Aversion and Expected-Utility Theory: A Calibration Theorem
Authors: Matthew Rabin
Year: 2000
Journal: Econometrica, Vol. 68, No. 5, pp. 1281-1292
DOI: 10.1111/1468-0262.00158

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This influential paper presents a CALIBRATION THEOREM that demonstrates
expected-utility theory cannot simultaneously explain risk aversion over
modest stakes and reasonable risk attitudes over large stakes.

THE CALIBRATION THEOREM:

If an expected-utility maximizer rejects a 50-50 gamble of losing $100 or
gaining $110 at all wealth levels, then she must also reject a 50-50 gamble
of losing $1,000 or gaining ANY AMOUNT of money.

KEY ARGUMENT:

1. OBSERVATION: People reject small-stakes gambles (like lose $100/gain $110)
2. IMPLICATION under EU: This implies local concavity of utility
3. PROBLEM: Local concavity everywhere implies EXTREME global concavity
4. RESULT: Would reject obviously attractive large-stakes gambles

FORMAL RESULT:
If for all w: u(w+g) - u(w) < (l/g)[u(w) - u(w-l)]
Then for large enough L, regardless of G:
E[u(w + gamble(G,-L))] < u(w)

CALIBRATION EXAMPLES:
- Reject lose $100/gain $110 → Must reject lose $1000/gain ANY amount
- Reject lose $1000/gain $1050 → Must reject lose $20,000/gain $10 million

WHY THIS MATTERS:
- Standard EU theory requires unrealistic utility curvature
- Supports reference-dependent models (Prospect Theory)
- Shows need for loss aversion, not risk aversion, for modest stakes

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Calibration theorem: EU cannot explain small-stakes risk aversion
2. Local concavity implies extreme global risk aversion
3. Rejection of $100/$110 → rejection of $1000/∞
4. Supports loss aversion over diminishing marginal utility
5. Major challenge to expected utility theory
6. 2,000+ citations, highly influential critique
