ABSTRACT

Does media bias affect voting? We address this question by looking at the
entry of Fox News in cable markets and its impact on voting. Between October
1996 and November 2000, the conservative Fox News Channel was introduced in
the cable programming of 20 percent of US towns. Fox News availability in
2000 appears to be largely determined by idiosyncratic factors such as
channel positioning. Using a data set of voting data for 9,256 towns, we
investigate if Republicans gained vote share in towns where Fox News became
available.

We find a significant effect of the introduction of Fox News on the vote
share in Presidential elections between 1996 and 2000. Republicans gained
0.4 to 0.7 percentage points in the towns that broadcast Fox News. This
effect is driven by persuasion rather than mobilization: Fox News did not
affect turnout significantly. We estimate the persuasion rate, the fraction
of Fox News viewers who shifted their vote, to be 3 to 28 percent.

KEY FINDINGS

1. FOX NEWS EFFECT ON REPUBLICAN VOTE SHARE
   - Estimate: 0.4 to 0.7 percentage points
   - Robust across OLS and IV specifications
   - Significant at p < 0.05

2. PERSUASION RATE
   - Range: 3% to 28% (f = 0.03 to 0.28)
   - Preferred IV estimate: 11% to 28%
   - Interpretation: Fraction of viewers persuaded to vote Republican
   - Benchmark: Within range of lab estimates (Gerber et al. 2004)

3. SENATE ELECTIONS
   - Similar effect: 0.6 percentage points
   - Statistically significant

4. TURNOUT
   - No significant effect
   - Interpretation: Fox News persuades, does not mobilize

5. HETEROGENEITY
   - Effects strongest where Republicans were initially weak
   - Suggests persuasion of moderates/independents, not base mobilization
   - Effects scale with local viewership rates (dose-response)

METHODOLOGY

Design: Natural experiment using staggered Fox News introduction

Identification:
- Fox News availability determined by cable system capacity and channel position
- Channel position affects viewership (lower position = more viewers)
- Towns without Fox News serve as control group

Instrumental Variable: Cable channel position
- Lower channel numbers get more viewers
- Channel position assignment largely idiosyncratic

Data Sources:
- Nielsen Media Research (viewership)
- Cable system data from Warren Publishing
- Election returns from Dave Leip's Atlas
- Census data for demographics

Sample: 9,256 towns in 28 states with Nielsen data
Period: 1996-2000 (Fox News expansion)

PERSUASION RATE METHODOLOGY

The paper introduces the persuasion rate formula that becomes standard:

f = (y_T - y_C) / (e_T - e_C × c)

where:
- y_T, y_C = outcome (Republican vote share) in treatment/control
- e_T, e_C = exposure rates (Fox News availability)
- c = compliance rate (fraction who watch when available)

Interpretation: Fraction of exposed audience whose behavior changed

This methodology is subsequently adopted by:
- Enikolopov & Petrova (2015) - Russian media
- Yanagizawa-Drott (2014) - Rwandan radio
- Martin & Yurukoglu (2017) - Cable news
- Social media research (Braghieri et al. 2022)

IDENTIFICATION ASSUMPTIONS

1. Fox News availability uncorrelated with unobserved voting trends
   - Tested via pre-trends analysis (1992-1996)
   - No differential trends before Fox News introduction

2. No spillover effects to non-Fox News towns
   - Geographic clustering tests

3. Parallel trends in absence of treatment
   - Supported by placebo tests

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS

1. BAYESIAN PERSUASION (Becker 1965, Mullainathan & Shleifer 2005)
   - Rational viewers update beliefs based on news slant
   - Extract information even from biased sources

2. PSYCHOLOGICAL PERSUASION (McGuire 1985)
   - Exposure affects attitudes through peripheral processing
   - Repeated exposure, emotional appeals

3. SELECTIVE EXPOSURE (Lazarsfeld 1948)
   - Viewers self-select into like-minded media
   - Confirmation bias, identity-protective cognition

POLICY IMPLICATIONS

1. Media ownership regulation has electoral consequences
2. Biased media can shift aggregate voting outcomes
3. Information environment affects democratic outcomes
4. Persuasion effects are substantial and policy-relevant
