Title: Job Market Signaling
Authors: Michael Spence
Year: 1973
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 87, No. 3, pp. 355-374

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents a model of signaling in the job market, where employers
must make hiring decisions under uncertainty regarding an individual's
productivity. In this environment, workers can use observable characteristics
(particularly education) as signals of their unobservable productivity.

The key insight is that signaling works through self-selection: if the cost
of acquiring education is lower for high-ability workers than for low-ability
workers, then only high-ability workers will find it worthwhile to acquire
the signal. Even if education does not directly enhance productivity, it can
serve as a credible signal precisely because it is differentially costly.

The paper shows that multiple signaling equilibria may exist, and that in
equilibrium, educational credentials allow employers to correctly infer
worker types, even though education itself adds no productive value.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SIGNALING EQUILIBRIUM: Workers with higher ability acquire more education,
   allowing employers to correctly infer productivity from educational attainment

2. DIFFERENTIAL COST ASSUMPTION: Signaling works because education is less
   costly for high-ability workers (e.g., takes less time/effort)

3. EDUCATION AS PURE SIGNAL: Even if education adds zero productivity, it
   can have market value by credibly revealing type information

4. MULTIPLE EQUILIBRIA: Many signaling equilibria exist, with different
   education levels required for the same signaling function

5. HIRING AS LOTTERY: Employers view hiring as investment under uncertainty;
   signals help reduce this uncertainty

6. INDICES VS SIGNALS: Immutable characteristics (indices) and alterable
   characteristics (signals) both affect hiring but through different channels
