Title: Fairness as a Constraint on Profit Seeking: Entitlements in the Market
Authors: Daniel Kahneman, Jack L. Knetsch, Richard H. Thaler
Year: 1986
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 76, No. 4, pp. 728-741

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Traditional economic analysis assumes that excess demand will cause prices to
rise and that prices will fall when there is excess supply. This paper reports
telephone surveys that investigated the rules of fairness that customers apply
to price and wage setting.

The central finding is the principle of dual entitlement: transactors have an
entitlement to the terms of a reference transaction and firms have an
entitlement to their reference profit. It is acceptable for firms to raise
prices (or cut wages) to protect profits, but not to increase them.

Customers perceive it as unfair for firms to exploit their market power to
increase profits at customers' expense, even when the firm has no explicit
obligation not to do so. Firms that violate these fairness norms may face
retaliation from customers who are willing to punish unfair behavior at a
cost to themselves.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. DUAL ENTITLEMENT: Both customers and firms have entitlements to reference
   transaction terms

2. REFERENCE TRANSACTIONS: Current terms set reference points; deviations
   are judged against them

3. ASYMMETRY: Cost increases justify price increases; demand increases do not

4. EXPLOITATION FORBIDDEN: Using market power to exploit customers is unfair
   even if legal

5. PUNISHING UNFAIRNESS: Customers willing to pay to punish firms that
   violate fairness norms

6. STICKY PRICES: Fairness constraints help explain nominal wage and price
   rigidity
