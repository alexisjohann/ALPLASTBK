Title: A Model of Investor Sentiment
Authors: Nicholas Barberis, Andrei Shleifer, Robert W. Vishny
Year: 1998
Journal: Journal of Financial Economics, Vol. 49, No. 3, pp. 307-343
Note: Also NBER Working Paper No. 5926 (1997)

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors present a parsimonious model of investor sentiment, or of how
investors form beliefs, which is consistent with empirical findings. The model
is based on psychological evidence and produces both underreaction and
overreaction for a wide range of parameter values.

Recent empirical research in finance has uncovered two families of pervasive
regularities: underreaction of stock prices to news such as earnings
announcements, and overreaction of stock prices to a series of good or bad news.

The paper relies on earlier work showing why deviations from efficient prices
can persist. An important reason why arbitrage is limited is that movements in
investor sentiment are in part unpredictable, and therefore arbitrageurs betting
against mispricing run the risk that investor sentiment becomes more extreme and
prices move even further away from fundamental value.

As a consequence of such 'noise trader risk,' arbitrage positions can lose money
in the short run. When arbitrageurs are risk-averse, leveraged, or manage other
people's money and run the risk of losing funds under management when performance
is poor, the risk of deepening mispricing reduces the size of the positions they
take.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. UNDERREACTION: Stock prices initially respond too slowly to news like
   earnings announcements

2. OVERREACTION: Stock prices eventually overreact to sequences of similar news

3. PSYCHOLOGICAL BASIS: Model built on documented cognitive biases
   (conservatism, representativeness)

4. LIMITS TO ARBITRAGE: Noise trader risk explains why mispricings persist

5. PARSIMONIOUS MODEL: Single framework explains both underreaction and
   overreaction patterns

6. ARBITRAGEUR CONSTRAINTS: Risk aversion, leverage, career concerns limit
   arbitrage effectiveness
