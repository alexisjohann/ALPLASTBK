Title: The Realization Effect: Risk-Taking after Realized versus Paper Losses
Authors: Alex Imas
Year: 2016
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 106, No. 8, pp. 2086-2109

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Understanding how prior outcomes affect risk attitudes is critical for choice
under uncertainty. A large literature documents significant influence of prior
losses on risk attitudes, but findings appear contradictory: some studies find
greater risk-taking after a loss, others show less risk.

This paper reconciles these inconsistent findings by distinguishing between
realized versus paper losses. Using new and existing data, the author replicates
prior findings and demonstrates that following a REALIZED loss, individuals
avoid risk; if the same loss is NOT realized (a paper loss), individuals take
on GREATER risk. The author defines realization as "an event in which money
or another medium of value is transferred between accounts."

The difference in risk-taking between paper and realized losses is called the
"realization effect." It is explained using cumulative prospect theory and
choice bracketing - unrealized losses remain in an open mental account where
loss aversion drives risk-seeking to "break even."

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. REALIZATION EFFECT: Paper losses increase risk-taking; realized losses
   decrease it

2. MENTAL ACCOUNTS: Realization = closing an account; paper = account still
   open

3. DISPOSITION EFFECT: Explains reluctance to sell losers (would realize loss)

4. BREAK-EVEN SEEKING: Paper losses trigger risk-seeking to recover before
   realization

5. RECONCILES LITERATURE: Explains contradictory findings on losses and
   risk-taking

6. CUMULATIVE PROSPECT THEORY: Theoretical foundation for the asymmetry
