Title: Specious Reward: A Behavioral Theory of Impulsiveness and Impulse Control
Authors: George Ainslie
Year: 1975
Journal: Psychological Bulletin, Vol. 82, No. 4, pp. 463-496

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This foundational paper proposes hyperbolic discounting as a model for
understanding impulsive behavior. The paper argues that delay discounting
follows a hyperbolic rather than exponential function, which explains common
preference reversals and dynamic inconsistencies observed in behavior.

The exponential discounting model of classical economics cannot predict
preference reversals that are evident in daily behavior - for example, people
may prefer $101 in one year and one day over $100 in one year, but a year
later prefer $100 today over $101 tomorrow.

By applying the "matching relationship" from experimental psychology to
individual choices, Ainslie showed that the resulting discount function is
hyperbolic, explaining impulsive choices without abandoning rational choice
theory entirely.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. HYPERBOLIC DISCOUNTING: The subjective value of rewards decreases
   hyperbolically (not exponentially) with delay to receipt

2. PREFERENCE REVERSALS: Hyperbolic discounting explains why preferences
   can reverse as options approach in time

3. IMPULSIVENESS MECHANISM: Impulsive choices result from steeper near-term
   discounting that can override long-term preferences

4. MATCHING RELATIONSHIP: Applied matching law from experimental psychology
   to human temporal choice

5. SELF-CONTROL STRATEGIES: Identified commitment devices and bundling as
   rational responses to predictable preference reversals

6. FOUNDATIONAL IMPACT: First systematic demonstration that common behavior
   patterns (impulsivity, addiction, procrastination) require non-exponential
   discounting models
