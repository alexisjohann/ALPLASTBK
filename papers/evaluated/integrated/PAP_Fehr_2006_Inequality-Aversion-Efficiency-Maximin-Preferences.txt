Inequality Aversion, Efficiency, and Maximin Preferences in Simple Distribution Experiments

Ernst Fehr and Klaus M. Schmidt

American Economic Review, Vol. 96, No. 5 (December 2006), pp. 1912-1917

Abstract

A substantial body of experimental research has documented that many people are motivated by inequality aversion and reciprocity. This paper examines preferences over simple income distributions using experimental methods. We find that subjects consistently prefer more equal distributions, even when this comes at an efficiency cost.

1. Introduction

The standard economic model assumes that individuals are exclusively self-interested. However, evidence from laboratory experiments, field studies, and real-world behavior contradicts this assumption. People often care about fairness, reciprocity, and inequality.

2. Methodology

We conducted a series of dictator game experiments at the University of Zurich in 2005. Our sample consisted of 248 undergraduate students from various departments. Each subject participated in three rounds of distribution decisions.

3. Results

We find that p < 0.01 for the hypothesis that subjects prefer equal distributions. The treatment effect was significant with a coefficient of 0.34 (SE = 0.08). Our regression analysis confirms that inequality aversion is a robust predictor of distribution choices.

4. Conclusion

These findings contribute to the growing literature on social preferences and have implications for the design of institutions and policies.
