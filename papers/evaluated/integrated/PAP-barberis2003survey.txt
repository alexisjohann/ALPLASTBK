Title: A Survey of Behavioral Finance
Authors: Nicholas Barberis, Richard Thaler
Year: 2003
Journal: Handbook of the Economics of Finance, Vol. 1, pp. 1053-1128

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This chapter surveys behavioral finance, which argues that some financial
phenomena can be better understood using models in which some agents are not
fully rational.

The survey covers two main topics: (1) limits to arbitrage, which argues that
it can be difficult for rational traders to undo the dislocations caused by
less rational traders; and (2) psychology, which catalogs the kinds of
deviations from full rationality that we might expect to see.

The survey discusses the application of these ideas to understanding the
aggregate stock market, the cross-section of average returns, and individual
trading behavior.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. LIMITS TO ARBITRAGE: Rational traders face fundamental risk, noise trader
   risk, and implementation costs that prevent them from correcting mispricings

2. INVESTOR PSYCHOLOGY: Overconfidence, optimism, representativeness,
   conservatism, belief perseverance, and anchoring affect financial decisions

3. AGGREGATE MARKET PUZZLES: Equity premium puzzle, volatility puzzle, and
   predictability explained through behavioral lens

4. CROSS-SECTIONAL ANOMALIES: Size effect, value effect, momentum explained by
   investor psychology and limits to arbitrage

5. INDIVIDUAL BEHAVIOR: Underdiversification, excessive trading, selling
   winners/holding losers, and buying attention-grabbing stocks

6. PROSPECT THEORY APPLICATION: Loss aversion and mental accounting explain
   many asset pricing anomalies and trading patterns
