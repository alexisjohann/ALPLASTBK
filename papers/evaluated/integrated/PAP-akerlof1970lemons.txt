Title: The Market for "Lemons": Quality Uncertainty and the Market Mechanism
Authors: George A. Akerlof
Year: 1970
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 84, No. 3, pp. 488-500
DOI: 10.1162/003355395555054

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This Nobel Prize-winning paper demonstrates how ASYMMETRIC INFORMATION about
product quality can cause markets to fail or collapse entirely.

THE LEMONS MODEL:

Consider the used car market:
- Sellers know the true quality of their cars
- Buyers cannot distinguish "lemons" (bad cars) from "peaches" (good cars)
- Buyers offer a price reflecting AVERAGE quality
- At this price, owners of good cars withdraw from the market
- Market "unravels" until only lemons remain (ADVERSE SELECTION)

FORMAL MECHANISM:
1. Quality q distributed uniformly on [0, 2]
2. Seller values car at q, buyer values at (3/2)q
3. Gains from trade exist for all qualities
4. But with asymmetric information, only q < p_offered will sell
5. Buyers anticipate this and lower offers
6. Market can collapse entirely

KEY APPLICATIONS DISCUSSED:
- Used car markets (the "lemons" metaphor)
- Insurance markets (why premiums rise and good risks exit)
- Credit markets in developing countries
- Employment of minorities (statistical discrimination)
- Medical insurance (why the aged cannot get coverage)

SOLUTIONS TO INFORMATION ASYMMETRY:
- Warranties and guarantees
- Brand names and reputation
- Licensing and certification
- Chains and franchising

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. ADVERSE SELECTION: Bad quality drives out good
2. Asymmetric information can cause market failure
3. Average pricing leads to market unraveling
4. Explains insurance market problems
5. Nobel Prize (2001) for information economics
6. Foundation for signaling and screening literature
