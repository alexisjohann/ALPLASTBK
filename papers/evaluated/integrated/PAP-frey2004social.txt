Title: Social Comparisons and Pro-social Behavior: Testing "Conditional Cooperation" in a Field Experiment
Authors: Bruno S. Frey, Stephan Meier
Year: 2004
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 94, No. 5, pp. 1717-1722

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The standard explanation in the literature on public goods provision is that
most individuals are conditional cooperators - they only contribute if they
believe others will do the same. This paper tests conditional cooperation in
a natural field experiment.

The experiment examines whether information about others' contributions affects
people's willingness to cooperate in a real charitable giving context. Students
at the University of Zurich were given the opportunity to donate to two social
funds during course registration.

The results confirm that people's contributions are conditional on beliefs about
others' contributions. When told that average contributions were high, subjects
contributed more; when told contributions were low, they contributed less. This
conditional cooperation effect operates even in natural field settings with
real stakes.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CONDITIONAL COOPERATION: People contribute more when they believe others
   contribute - validated in field setting

2. SOCIAL INFORMATION: Telling people about average contributions affects
   their own giving

3. FIELD VALIDATION: Lab-documented conditional cooperation extends to
   natural field settings

4. UPWARD CONFORMITY: High contribution information increases giving

5. DOWNWARD CONFORMITY: Low contribution information decreases giving

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Social information can be used to increase
   pro-social behavior
