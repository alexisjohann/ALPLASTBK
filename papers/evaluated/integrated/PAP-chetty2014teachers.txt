Title: Measuring the Impacts of Teachers II: Teacher Value-Added and Student
       Outcomes in Adulthood
Authors: Raj Chetty, John N. Friedman, Jonah E. Rockoff
Year: 2014
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 104, No. 9, pp. 2633-2679

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Are teachers' impacts on students' test scores ("value-added") a good measure
of their quality? This question has sparked debate partly due to lack of
evidence on whether high value-added (VA) teachers improve students' long-term
outcomes.

Using school district and tax records for more than one million children, the
authors find that students assigned to high-VA teachers are more likely to
attend college, earn higher salaries, and are less likely to have children as
teenagers.

Replacing a teacher whose VA is in the bottom 5% with an average teacher would
increase the present value of students' lifetime income by approximately
$250,000 per classroom.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. VA PREDICTS ADULT OUTCOMES: High value-added teachers improve college
   attendance, earnings, and reduce teen pregnancy rates

2. LARGE ECONOMIC MAGNITUDE: Replacing bottom 5% teacher with average teacher
   yields ~$250,000 per classroom in lifetime earnings

3. TEST SCORES PREDICT LONG-RUN: Short-run test score impacts translate into
   meaningful long-term improvements in life outcomes

4. QUASI-EXPERIMENTAL VALIDATION: Teacher arrivals/departures create natural
   experiments confirming causal interpretation of VA

5. MINIMAL BIAS: VA estimates exhibit little bias when controlling for prior
   test scores (companion paper finding)

6. HETEROGENEOUS EFFECTS: Teacher quality matters more for disadvantaged
   students - potential for reducing inequality
