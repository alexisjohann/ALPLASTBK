Title: Immigration's Effect on US Wages and Employment Redux
Authors: Alessandro Caiumi, Giovanni Peri
Year: 2024
Journal: NBER Working Paper No. 32389
DOI: 10.3386/w32389

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors revive, extend and improve the approach used in a series of
influential papers written in the 2000s to estimate how changes in the supply
of immigrant workers affected natives' wages in the US.

EXTENSIONS & IMPROVEMENTS:
1. Extended analysis to include more recent years 2000-2022
2. Introduced an IV using a new skill-based shift-share for immigrants and
   demographic evolution for natives (passes validity tests, strong power)
3. Provided estimates of impact on employment-population ratio of natives
   to test for crowding out at the national level
4. Analyzed occupational upgrading of natives in response to immigrants

KEY FINDINGS:

Immigration, thanks to NATIVE-IMMIGRANT COMPLEMENTARITY and college skill
content of immigrants, had a POSITIVE and significant effect:
- +1.7% to +2.6% on wages of LESS EDUCATED native workers (2000-2019)
- No significant wage effect on college educated natives
- Positive employment rate effect for most native workers

Over 2000-2022, immigration raised average US-born worker's wage by 0.8%.
Workers with HS diploma or less saw increases of 2.8% to 3.2%.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Immigration POSITIVE for low-skill native wages (+1.7% to +2.6%)
2. No negative effects on college-educated natives
3. Positive employment effects (no crowding out)
4. Native-immigrant complementarity is key mechanism
5. Occupational upgrading of natives documented
6. New IV with skill-based shift-share
