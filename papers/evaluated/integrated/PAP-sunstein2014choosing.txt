Title: Choosing Not to Choose
Authors: Cass R. Sunstein
Year: 2014
Journal: Duke Law Journal, Vol. 64, No. 1; Harvard Public Law Working Paper No. 14-07

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

"Choice can be an extraordinary benefit or an immense burden." In some contexts,
people choose not to choose, or would do so if asked. Many people prefer not to
make choices about their health or retirement plans, preferring to delegate
those choices to trusted private or public institutions.

This suggests that the line between active choosing and paternalism is often
illusory. When institutions override people's desire not to choose and insist
on active choosing, they may be behaving paternalistically through
"choice-requiring paternalism."

Active choosing can be seen as a form of libertarian paternalism, and a
frequently attractive one, if people are permitted to opt out of choosing in
favor of a default; it is a form of nonlibertarian paternalism insofar as
people are required to choose.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CHOOSING NOT TO CHOOSE: People often prefer to delegate decisions rather
   than choose actively

2. CHOICE-REQUIRING PATERNALISM: Forcing active choice is itself a form of
   paternalism

3. DEFAULTS VS ACTIVE CHOICE: When to use each depends on context, complexity,
   and heterogeneity

4. DELEGATION PREFERENCE: People willing to pay to avoid making certain choices

5. DEFAULT RULES PREFERRED: When context is technical, unfamiliar, or confusing

6. ACTIVE CHOICE PREFERRED: When choice architects lack information or
   heterogeneity is high
