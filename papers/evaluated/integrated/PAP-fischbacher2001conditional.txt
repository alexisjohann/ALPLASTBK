Title: Are People Conditionally Cooperative? Evidence from a Public Goods Experiment
Authors: Urs Fischbacher, Simon Gächter, Ernst Fehr
Year: 2001
Journal: Economics Letters, Vol. 71, No. 3, pp. 397-404

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This study examines the importance of conditional cooperation in a one-shot
public goods game using a variant of the strategy method. The researchers
directly elicited subjects' willingness for conditional cooperation by asking
them to indicate for each average contribution level of other group members
how much they want to contribute to the public good.

The key finding is that approximately 50% of subjects can be classified as
"conditional cooperators" - they contribute more the more others contribute.
About one-third of subjects are classified as free riders who contribute
nothing regardless of others' behavior. Even conditional cooperators display
a self-serving bias, contributing somewhat less than others on average.

The results provide a new interpretation for why contributions decline in
almost all public goods experiments: free riders pull down average contributions,
which in turn causes conditional cooperators to reduce their contributions,
creating a downward spiral.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CONDITIONAL COOPERATION: ~50% of subjects are conditional cooperators who
   match others' contributions

2. FREE RIDERS: ~33% are free riders contributing nothing regardless of others

3. SELF-SERVING BIAS: Even conditional cooperators contribute slightly less
   than others on average

4. DECLINE MECHANISM: Free riders + self-serving bias explains contribution
   decay in repeated games

5. HETEROGENEITY: Clear behavioral types exist - not a single representative
   agent

6. STRATEGY METHOD: Novel elicitation reveals full cooperation function,
   not just single decisions
