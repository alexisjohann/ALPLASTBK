Title: The Age of Reason: Financial Decisions over the Life-Cycle with Implications for Regulation
Authors: Sumit Agarwal, John C. Driscoll, Xavier Gabaix, David Laibson
Year: 2009
Journal: Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, Vol. 2009, No. 2, pp. 51-117

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Many consumers make poor financial choices and older adults are particularly
vulnerable to such errors. About half of the population between ages 80 and 89
either has dementia or a medical diagnosis of "cognitive impairment without
dementia."

The authors study lifecycle patterns in financial mistakes using a proprietary
database that measures ten different types of credit behavior. Financial
mistakes include suboptimal use of credit card balance transfer offers,
misestimation of the value of one's house, and excess interest rate and fee
payments.

In a cross-section of prime borrowers, middle-aged adults make fewer financial
mistakes than younger and older adults. The sophistication of financial
decisions varies with age: middle-aged adults borrow at lower interest rates
and pay fewer fees compared to both younger and older adults.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. U-SHAPED PATTERN: Financial mistakes follow a U-shaped pattern across age,
   with minimum errors around age 53

2. AGE OF REASON: Middle-aged adults (around 53) make optimal financial
   decisions, balancing experience and cognitive capacity

3. COGNITIVE DECLINE: Older adults increasingly make financial errors due
   to cognitive decline, despite greater experience

4. YOUNG INEXPERIENCE: Young adults make errors due to lack of experience
   despite higher cognitive capacity

5. TEN ERROR TYPES: Documented errors include APR spreads, fee payments,
   balance transfer mistakes, and home value estimation

6. REGULATORY IMPLICATIONS: Results suggest need for age-tailored financial
   protections and default options
