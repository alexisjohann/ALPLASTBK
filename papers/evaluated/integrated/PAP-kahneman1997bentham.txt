Title: Back to Bentham? Explorations of Experienced Utility
Authors: Daniel Kahneman, Peter P. Wakker, Rakesh Sarin
Year: 1997
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 112, No. 2, pp. 375-405

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Bentham's view of utility as the hedonic quality of experience was dominant in
economics until its abandonment early in this century. This article reviews
recent developments that make the return to experienced utility possible and
perhaps useful.

We distinguish between two concepts of utility: (1) Decision utility - the
weight of an outcome in a decision, inferred from choices. (2) Experienced
utility - the hedonic experience of an outcome, as Bentham conceived it.

If choice is rational, the two utilities coincide. Systematic violations arise
from: peak-end rule (final and most intense moments dominate), duration neglect
(duration has little effect), and focussing illusion (attention exaggerates
importance of attended features).

The distinction has implications for measurement of well-being, cost-benefit
analysis, and policy evaluation. Experienced utility can be measured in real
time using moment-based techniques.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. TWO UTILITIES: Decision utility (revealed preference) vs Experienced utility
   (hedonic quality) - these can diverge systematically

2. PEAK-END RULE: Retrospective evaluations dominated by peak intensity and
   ending, not duration or average

3. DURATION NEGLECT: Total duration of experience has surprisingly little
   effect on evaluation

4. FOCUSSING ILLUSION: Attention to specific aspects exaggerates their
   perceived importance for well-being

5. MEASUREMENT POSSIBLE: Experienced utility can be measured via moment-based
   sampling (Experience Sampling Method)

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Welfare analysis based on choice may miss what
   actually matters for well-being
