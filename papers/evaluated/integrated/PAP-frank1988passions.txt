Title: Passions Within Reason: The Strategic Role of the Emotions
Authors: Robert H. Frank
Year: 1988
Publisher: W. W. Norton & Company
Type: Book

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This book provides a new interpretation of the empirical research on behaviors
that don't fit economic models of pure self-interest - such as tipping in
restaurants far from home, seeking costly revenge, and staying loyal when
better opportunities arise. Frank argues these behaviors make sense only as
products of moral emotions (love, shame, vengeance, guilt), which themselves
make sense as products of evolution.

The central argument is the "commitment model": evolution made us "strategically
irrational" at times for our own good. A person who gets angry when cheated
and will pursue vengeance regardless of cost earns a reputation that discourages
would-be cheaters. Similarly, genuine love creates commitment that outperforms
calculated loyalty.

Frank presents his commitment model as a refinement of Rational Expectations,
emphasizing the strategic and clarifying role of emotions in facilitating
socially efficient interactions. The emotions solve commitment problems that
purely rational actors cannot solve.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. COMMITMENT MODEL: Emotions solve commitment problems that rational choice
   cannot

2. STRATEGIC IRRATIONALITY: Being "irrational" (e.g., vengeful) can be
   strategically advantageous

3. EMOTIONAL SIGNALING: Emotions signal commitments that others can rely on

4. REPUTATION EFFECTS: Genuine emotions create credible reputations for
   cooperation or punishment

5. EVOLUTIONARY LOGIC: Moral emotions evolved because they solved important
   social problems

6. BEYOND SELF-INTEREST: Material self-interest is insufficient to explain
   observed cooperation
