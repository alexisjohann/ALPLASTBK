Title: Maximizing Versus Satisficing: Happiness Is a Matter of Choice
Authors: Barry Schwartz, Andrew Ward, John Monterosso, Sonja Lyubomirsky, Katherine White, Darrin R. Lehman
Year: 2002
Journal: Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, Vol. 83, No. 5, pp. 1178-1197

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The present studies suggest that some people—maximizers—can feel worse off as
the options they face increase, and the interaction between maximizing and
choice is discussed in terms of regret, adaptation, and self-blame.

Study 1 reported a Maximization Scale, which measures individual differences in
desire to maximize. Seven samples revealed negative correlations between
maximization and happiness, optimism, self-esteem, and life satisfaction, and
positive correlations between maximization and depression, perfectionism, and
regret.

Study 2 found maximizers less satisfied than nonmaximizers (satisficers) with
consumer decisions, and more likely to engage in social comparison. Study 3
found maximizers more adversely affected by upward social comparison. Study 4
found maximizers more sensitive to regret and less satisfied in an ultimatum
bargaining game.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. MAXIMIZATION SCALE: Individual differences in tendency to seek optimal
   vs "good enough" choices

2. MAXIMIZERS LESS HAPPY: Negative correlations with happiness, optimism,
   life satisfaction

3. MORE DEPRESSION/REGRET: Positive correlations with depression, perfectionism,
   and regret

4. PARADOX OF CHOICE: More options can make maximizers feel worse, not better

5. SOCIAL COMPARISON: Maximizers engage in more upward social comparison,
   feel worse after

6. SATISFICING ADAPTIVE: Simon's satisficing strategy associated with
   better well-being outcomes
