Title: The Fair Wage-Effort Hypothesis and Unemployment
Authors: George A. Akerlof, Janet L. Yellen
Year: 1990
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 105, No. 2, pp. 255-283

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper proposes an alternative theory of unemployment based on the fair
wage-effort hypothesis: workers proportionately withdraw effort as their actual
wage falls short of their fair wage. This behavioral assumption, derived from
equity theory in social psychology and social exchange theory in sociology,
explains involuntary unemployment and observed cross-section wage differentials.

The model shows that firms may find it profitable to pay wages above market-
clearing levels because underpaying relative to the fair wage induces workers
to shirk. When the fair wage exceeds the market-clearing wage, unemployment
results. The theory explains wage compression, inter-industry wage differentials,
and why unemployment is concentrated among the less skilled.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. FAIR WAGE-EFFORT HYPOTHESIS: Workers withdraw effort proportionally to the
   gap between actual wage and their perceived fair wage (effort = min(w/w*, 1))

2. EFFICIENCY WAGES: Firms pay above market-clearing wages because paying below
   the fair wage reduces worker productivity through effort withdrawal

3. INVOLUNTARY UNEMPLOYMENT: When fair wage exceeds market-clearing wage, firms
   still pay the fair wage, creating excess labor supply and unemployment

4. WAGE COMPRESSION: The fair wage depends on comparison with similar workers,
   leading to compressed wage distributions within firms

5. INTER-INDUSTRY DIFFERENTIALS: Higher-rent industries pay more because fair
   wage depends partly on firm profitability - explains persistent differentials

6. SKILL-BASED UNEMPLOYMENT: Less skilled workers face higher unemployment
   because their fair wage exceeds their marginal product by a larger margin
