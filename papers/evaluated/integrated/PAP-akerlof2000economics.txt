Title: Economics and Identity
Authors: George A. Akerlof, Rachel E. Kranton
Year: 2000
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 115, No. 3, pp. 715-753

================================================================================
ABSTRACT (VERBATIM)
================================================================================

"This paper considers how identity, a person's sense of self, affects economic
outcomes. We incorporate the psychology and sociology of identity into an
economic model of behavior. In the utility function we propose, identity is
associated with different social categories and how people in these categories
should behave. We then construct a simple game-theoretic model showing how
identity can affect individual interactions. The paper adapts these models to
gender discrimination in the workplace, the economics of poverty and social
exclusion, and the household division of labor. In each case, the inclusion of
identity substantively changes conclusions of previous economic analysis."

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. IDENTITY UTILITY FUNCTION: Identity enters utility as I_j = I_j(a_j, a_{-j};
   c_j, ε_j, P) where c_j = social categories, ε_j = own characteristics,
   P = behavioral prescriptions. Utility U_j = U_j(a_j, a_{-j}, I_j)

2. FOUR CHANNELS OF IDENTITY EFFECTS:
   - Own actions affect own identity (loss I_s from violating prescriptions)
   - Others' actions affect own identity (externality I_o)
   - Choice of identity (c_j may be partially chosen)
   - Manipulation of categories and prescriptions (C and P can change)

3. GENDER IN WORKPLACE: Jobs have "auras of gender" - women lose identity I_s
   in "men's jobs," male coworkers lose I_o, leading to harassment and
   productivity losses. Explains occupational segregation beyond human capital

4. GREEN-RED MODEL OF POVERTY: Excluded groups choose between "Green" (conform
   to dominant culture, suffer exclusion loss r) or "Red" (oppositional identity).
   High r leads to equilibrium with self-destructive behavior that is RATIONAL
   given identity costs

5. HOUSEHOLD DIVISION: Husbands lose identity from housework and from wives
   earning more. Data shows men's share of housework rises to only 37% even
   when they do 0% of outside work - asymmetry unexplained by comparative
   advantage

6. GAME-THEORETIC MODEL: Person One (taste Activity 1) vs Person Two (taste
   Activity 2). With Green prescriptions (Activity 1 = correct), four equilibria
   depending on I_s, I_o, response cost c, and deterrence loss L

================================================================================
FORMAL MODEL SPECIFICATION
================================================================================

Utility Function:
  U_j = U_j(a_j, a_{-j}, I_j)

Identity Function:
  I_j = I_j(a_j, a_{-j}; c_j, ε_j, P)

Where:
  - a_j = own actions
  - a_{-j} = others' actions
  - c_j = assignment to social categories C
  - ε_j = own characteristics (physical, etc.)
  - P = prescriptions (behavior appropriate for each category)

Key Parameters:
  - I_s = identity loss from own prescription violation
  - I_o = identity loss from others' prescription violation
  - r = loss from social exclusion (Green identity in excluded group)
  - c = cost of responding to others' violations
  - L = loss imposed on violator by response

Equilibrium Conditions (Green-Red Model):
  - All-Green: r < I_o^R
  - Mixed: intermediate r
  - All-Red: high r + high I_o^G

================================================================================
EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE CITED
================================================================================

1. PSYCHOLOGICAL EXPERIMENTS: Robbers Cave (group identity formation),
   minimal group paradigm (random labels affect reward allocation)

2. GENDER WORKPLACE: Pierce (1995) law firm study - female lawyers feel
   identity conflict ("squeezed the female part of me into a box")

3. SURVEY DATA: Authors' telephone survey - 84% said male harassment of
   female carpenter was because "he feels less masculine"

4. HOUSEWORK DATA: PSID 1983-1992 - husbands' share of housework only
   10-37% regardless of outside work share. Figure III in paper.

5. ETHNOGRAPHIC: Willis (1977) "lads vs earholes," MacLeod (1987) "Hallway
   Hangers vs Brothers," Whyte (1943) "Corner Boys vs College Boys"

================================================================================
POLICY IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

1. WORKPLACE: Title VII interpretation should include identity-based
   harassment (not just sexual desire). Berkman v. City of New York precedent.

2. POVERTY: Job Corps residential programs succeed by removing identity
   externalities. JOBSTART (non-residential) failed.

3. EDUCATION: Schools like CPESS succeed by reducing I_s^R for minority
   students engaging in "Green" activities (learning Standard English)

4. AFFIRMATIVE ACTION: Affects r (exclusion parameter) through rhetoric
   and symbolism, not just direct admissions effects
