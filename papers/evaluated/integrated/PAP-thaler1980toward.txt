Title: Toward a Positive Theory of Consumer Choice
Authors: Richard Thaler
Year: 1980
Journal: Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, Vol. 1, No. 1, pp. 39-60
DOI: 10.1016/0167-2681(80)90051-7

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This foundational paper introduces several key concepts that challenged
traditional economic theory's assumptions about consumer behavior.

KEY CONCEPTS INTRODUCED:

1. THE ENDOWMENT EFFECT: People value goods more highly when they own them
   than when they don't. Selling prices (WTA) exceed buying prices (WTP)
   beyond transaction costs.

2. SUNK COST FALLACY: Contrary to economic theory, people consider sunk
   costs in their decisions. Resources already spent influence future choices.

3. MENTAL ACCOUNTING: People categorize money into different mental accounts
   (entertainment, food, savings) and treat money differently depending on
   which account it belongs to.

4. LOSS AVERSION IN RISKLESS CHOICE: The pain of losses exceeds the pleasure
   of equivalent gains, even in certain outcomes.

5. ANOMALIES IN CONSUMER CHOICE: Systematic deviations from standard theory
   including:
   - Underweighting opportunity costs
   - Failure to ignore sunk costs
   - Search behavior inconsistencies
   - Framing effects in riskless choice

THIS PAPER LAUNCHED "Mental Accounting" as a research program that Thaler
developed over the following decades.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Endowment effect: WTA > WTP systematically
2. Sunk cost fallacy contradicts normative theory
3. Mental accounting introduces non-fungibility
4. Loss aversion applies beyond risky choice
5. Opportunity costs underweighted vs. out-of-pocket costs
6. Foundation paper for behavioral economics anomalies
