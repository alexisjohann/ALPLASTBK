Title: Explaining Bargaining Impasse: The Role of Self-Serving Biases
Authors: Linda Babcock, George Loewenstein
Year: 1997
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 11, No. 1, pp. 109-126

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors review studies documenting a 'self-serving' bias in judgments of
fairness and demonstrate that the bias is an important cause of impasse in
negotiations. The self-serving bias causes people to conflate what is fair with
what benefits themselves.

The paper presents experimental evidence showing that:
(1) the bias causes impasse in bargaining
(2) it is possible to reduce impasses by debiasing bargainers
(3) the bias results from selective evaluation of information

The authors also review results from a field study of negotiations between
teachers' unions and school boards in Pennsylvania that document the fairness
bias in a naturalistic setting and demonstrate its impact on strikes. The
self-serving bias was clear: plaintiffs' predictions of the judge averaged
$14,527 higher than defendants', and plaintiffs' fair settlement values
averaged $17,709 higher than defendants', with both differences statistically
significant (p<.0001).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SELF-SERVING BIAS: People systematically believe that what is fair is
   what benefits themselves

2. IMPASSE MECHANISM: The bias causes negotiation failure when both parties
   believe their position is objectively fair

3. DEBIASING POSSIBLE: Interventions that reduce bias also reduce impasse
   rates

4. SELECTIVE INFORMATION: Bias arises from selective attention to and
   evaluation of evidence

5. FIELD EVIDENCE: Real-world teacher union negotiations confirm lab findings

6. QUANTIFIED IMPACT: Large discrepancies ($14-17k) between parties' fair
   value assessments
