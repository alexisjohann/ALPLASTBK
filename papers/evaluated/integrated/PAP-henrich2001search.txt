Title: In Search of Homo Economicus: Behavioral Experiments in 15 Small-Scale Societies
Authors: Joseph Henrich, Robert Boyd, Samuel Bowles, Colin Camerer, Ernst Fehr, Herbert Gintis, Richard McElreath
Year: 2001
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 91, No. 2, pp. 73-78

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Existing experimental research couldn't address key questions because virtually
all subjects had been university students, with relatively small cultural
differences compared to the full range of social environments. To address this,
the authors undertook a large cross-cultural study of behavior in ultimatum,
public good, and dictator games. Twelve experienced field researchers, working
in 12 countries on five continents, recruited subjects from 15 small-scale
societies exhibiting a wide variety of economic and cultural conditions.

The results showed: First, the canonical model of Homo Economicus was not
supported in any society studied. Second, there was considerably more behavioral
variability across groups than in previous cross-cultural research, and the
canonical model failed in a wider variety of ways. Third, group-level differences
in economic organization and the degree of market integration explained a
substantial portion of the behavioral variation across societies.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. HOMO ECONOMICUS REJECTED: Self-interested model not supported in ANY of
   15 societies

2. CULTURAL VARIATION: Much greater behavioral variation across groups than
   within WEIRD samples

3. MARKET INTEGRATION: Degree of market exposure predicts fairness behavior

4. ECONOMIC ORGANIZATION: Subsistence mode (foraging, horticulture, herding)
   predicts behavior

5. CONTEXT DEPENDENCE: "Universal" experimental results may be WEIRD-specific

6. ANONYMOUS GAME BEHAVIOR: Even anonymous games show culturally-shaped
   preferences
