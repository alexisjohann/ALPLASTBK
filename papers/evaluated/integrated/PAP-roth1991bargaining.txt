Title: Bargaining and Market Behavior in Jerusalem, Ljubljana, Pittsburgh, and Tokyo: An Experimental Study
Authors: Alvin E. Roth, Vesna Prasnikar, Masahiro Okuno-Fujiwara, Shmuel Zamir
Year: 1991
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 81, No. 5, pp. 1068-1095

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

In an experiment comparing two-person bargaining and multiperson markets in
Israel, Japan, the United States, and Yugoslavia, market outcomes converged to
equilibrium everywhere, with no payoff-relevant differences between countries.

Bargaining outcomes were everywhere different from equilibrium predictions (both
in agreements and in the substantial frequency of disagreements) and differences
were observed between countries.

Because of the experimental design, the fact that the market behavior is the
same in all countries supports the hypothesis that the observed differences are
not due to differences in languages, currencies, or experimenters, but may
tentatively be attributed to cultural differences.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. MARKETS CONVERGE: Market outcomes converged to equilibrium in all four
   countries - no cross-country differences

2. BARGAINING DIVERGES: Bargaining deviated from equilibrium everywhere,
   with cultural variation

3. CULTURAL EFFECTS: Cross-country bargaining differences attributable to
   culture, not methodology

4. CONTROLLED COMPARISON: Market results serve as methodological control
   ruling out experimental artifacts

5. DISAGREEMENT FREQUENCY: Substantial disagreement rates in bargaining
   across all countries

6. INSTITUTION MATTERS: Same people behave differently in markets vs
   bilateral bargaining
