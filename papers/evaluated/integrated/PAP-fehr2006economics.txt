Title: The Economics of Fairness, Reciprocity and Altruism - Experimental Evidence and New Theories
Authors: Ernst Fehr, Klaus M. Schmidt
Year: 2006
Journal: Handbook on the Economics of Giving, Reciprocity and Altruism, Vol. 1, Ch. 8, pp. 615-691

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Most economic models are based on the self-interest hypothesis that assumes
material self-interest exclusively motivates all people. Experimental economists
have gathered overwhelming evidence in recent years that systematically refutes
the self-interest hypothesis, suggesting that concerns for altruism, fairness,
and reciprocity strongly motivate many people.

Several theoretical papers demonstrate that the observed phenomena can be
explained in a rigorous and tractable manner. These theories induced a first
wave of experimental research which offered exciting insights into both the
nature of preferences and the relative performance of competing fairness theories.

The chapter reviews these developments, points out open questions, and suggests
avenues for future research. It also discusses neuroeconomic evidence consistent
with the view that many people have a taste for mutual cooperation and the
punishment of norm violators, and illustrates the powerful impact of fairness
concerns on cooperation, competition, incentives, and contract design.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SELF-INTEREST REFUTED: Experimental evidence systematically contradicts
   pure self-interest assumption

2. INEQUITY AVERSION: People dislike unequal outcomes (Fehr-Schmidt, Bolton-
   Ockenfels models)

3. RECIPROCITY: Conditional cooperation and punishment of norm violators
   are widespread

4. FAIRNESS IN MARKETS: Even competitive markets show fairness constraints
   on behavior

5. CONTRACT DESIGN: Optimal incentive contracts must account for fairness
   preferences

6. NEUROECONOMIC EVIDENCE: Brain imaging supports taste for cooperation
   and punishment
