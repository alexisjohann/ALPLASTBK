Title: Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases
Authors: Amos Tversky, Daniel Kahneman
Year: 1974
Journal: Science, Vol. 185, No. 4157, pp. 1124-1131
DOI: 10.1126/science.185.4157.1124

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This article describes THREE HEURISTICS that are employed in making
judgments under uncertainty:

1. REPRESENTATIVENESS HEURISTIC
   - Used when judging probability that object/event belongs to a class
   - Leads to: Base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, insensitivity to
     sample size, misconceptions of chance

2. AVAILABILITY HEURISTIC
   - Used when assessing frequency or plausibility of events
   - Leads to: Biases due to retrievability, imaginability, illusory
     correlation

3. ANCHORING AND ADJUSTMENT
   - Used in numerical prediction when a relevant value is available
   - Leads to: Insufficient adjustment from initial anchor, biased
     estimates toward anchor value

KEY INSIGHT: These heuristics are HIGHLY ECONOMICAL and USUALLY EFFECTIVE,
but they lead to SYSTEMATIC AND PREDICTABLE errors.

A better understanding of these heuristics and the biases to which they
lead could improve judgments and decisions in situations of uncertainty.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Three core heuristics: Representativeness, Availability, Anchoring
2. Heuristics are cognitive shortcuts (System 1)
3. Errors are systematic and predictable
4. Base rate neglect is pervasive
5. Anchoring effects are robust and large
6. Foundation of "Heuristics and Biases" research program
