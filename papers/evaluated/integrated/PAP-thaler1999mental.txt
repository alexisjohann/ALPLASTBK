Title: Mental Accounting Matters
Authors: Richard H. Thaler
Year: 1999
Journal: Journal of Behavioral Decision Making, Vol. 12, pp. 183-206

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Mental accounting is the set of cognitive operations used by individuals and
households to organize, evaluate, and keep track of financial activities.
This paper reviews the components of mental accounting theory and provides
evidence for mental accounting behavior from various sources.

The key components are: (1) how outcomes are perceived and experienced, and
how decisions are made and subsequently evaluated; (2) the assignment of
activities to specific mental accounts (categories for income and spending);
(3) the frequency with which accounts are evaluated.

Mental accounting violates the economic principle of fungibility - money in
one mental account is not a perfect substitute for money in another. People
treat windfalls differently from regular income, and losses more painfully
than equivalent gains.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. MENTAL ACCOUNTS: People categorize money into non-fungible mental accounts
   (housing, food, entertainment, etc.)

2. FUNGIBILITY VIOLATION: Money is not treated as perfectly substitutable
   across accounts

3. HEDONIC EDITING: People frame outcomes to maximize happiness (segregate
   gains, integrate losses)

4. SUNK COST EFFECTS: Mental accounts create sunk cost fallacies (theater
   tickets, gym memberships)

5. PAYMENT DECOUPLING: Prepayment decouples consumption from pain of paying

6. NARROW BRACKETING: Evaluating outcomes separately vs. together affects
   risk-taking and choices
