================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

When do people IDENTIFY WITH THEIR CLASS?

THEORETICAL BASIS (Social Psychology):
Individuals are more likely to identify with a group if they are
SIMILAR to its members.

SETTING: Early 20th century Britain

INNOVATION: Naming decisions as validated measure of class identity

KEY MECHANISM:
Regional cultural heterogeneity + internal migration → class identity

FINDINGS:

1. MIGRATION EFFECTS
   Migration patterns that increased local share of CULTURALLY-DISTANT
   workers REDUCED working class identification.

2. BEHAVIORAL CONSEQUENCES (where working class distance increased):
   - Workers LESS likely to join unions
   - Voters LESS likely to support nascent Labour Party
   - Parliamentary candidates LESS likely to target working class voters

3. REVERSAL BY 1911:
   - Slower in-migration
   - Rising local population growth
   → Reduced working class distance in urban areas
   → Urban areas became STRONGHOLDS of Labour support

CONCLUSION:
Migration alters SOCIAL IDENTITY and creates POLITICAL CLEAVAGES.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Social identity: people identify with groups they're SIMILAR to
2. Cultural distance reduces class identification
3. Lower class ID → less union membership, less Labour support
4. Naming decisions validated as identity measure
5. By 1911: reduced migration → stronger class identity → Labour rise
6. Migration creates political cleavages through identity
