Title: What Do Laboratory Experiments Measuring Social Preferences Reveal About the Real World?
Authors: Steven D. Levitt, John A. List
Year: 2007
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 21, No. 2, pp. 153-174

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

A critical question facing experimental economists is whether behavior inside
the laboratory is a good indicator of behavior outside the laboratory. To
address this, the authors build a model in which choices depend not just on
financial implications, but also on the nature and extent of scrutiny by others,
the particular context in which a decision is embedded, and the manner in which
participants and tasks are selected.

They present empirical evidence demonstrating the importance of these factors.
To the extent that lab and naturally occurring environments systematically
differ on these dimensions, results obtained inside and outside the lab need
not correspond.

Focusing on experiments designed to measure social preferences, they discuss
whether existing laboratory results generalize to naturally-occurring markets.
Research shows people often tend to be MORE other-regarding in the laboratory
than in the real world - suggesting lab experiments may overstate prosocial
behavior.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SCRUTINY EFFECTS: Being observed increases prosocial behavior in lab

2. SELECTION EFFECTS: Who participates in experiments differs from general
   population

3. CONTEXT EFFECTS: Abstract lab context differs from naturalistic settings

4. STAKES EFFECTS: Lab stakes often low and hypothetical vs. real-world

5. EXTERNAL VALIDITY: Lab social preferences may not transfer to field
   settings

6. MORE PROSOCIAL IN LAB: People appear more other-regarding in lab than
   reality
