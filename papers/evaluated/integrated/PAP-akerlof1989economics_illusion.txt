Title: Can Small Deviations from Rationality Make Significant Differences to Economic Equilibria?
Authors: George A. Akerlof, Janet L. Yellen
Year: 1985
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 75, No. 4, pp. 708-720

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The paper examines how small deviations from full rationality can have large
effects on economic equilibria. The authors show that the losses to those who
are not maximizing are small, in a well-defined sense, but the presence of
nonmaximizing behavior, even in rather small amounts, can significantly alter
the equilibrium obtained.

The paper considers the case of firms with market power that follow a rule of
thumb on pricing: firms do not increase price when demand increases and do not
reduce price when demand falls. They show that such a rule of thumb is "near
rational" - firms do not lose much profit from following this strategy relative
to a strategy of immediately adjusting prices.

However, if many firms follow this strategy, the effect on the overall economy
is substantial. This lack of adjustment of prices means that increases in
money-supply growth increase the growth of real output, and short-run drops in
money-supply growth reduce the growth of real output.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NEAR-RATIONALITY: Small deviations from optimizing behavior ("near-rational"
   behavior) cost individuals very little

2. AGGREGATE EFFECTS: These small individual deviations can have large aggregate
   macroeconomic consequences

3. PRICE STICKINESS: Firms following simple pricing rules of thumb create
   nominal rigidities

4. MONETARY NON-NEUTRALITY: Price stickiness from near-rational behavior means
   money supply changes affect real output

5. NEW KEYNESIAN FOUNDATION: Provides microfoundation for Keynesian sticky
   price models without assuming large costs of adjustment

6. RATIONALITY BOUNDS: Challenges assumption that small irrationalities wash
   out in aggregate
