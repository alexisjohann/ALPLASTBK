================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper demonstrates that humans engage in altruistic punishment - punishing
norm violators at personal cost even when they cannot benefit from the
punishment. Using public goods experiments, we show that altruistic punishment
is widespread and dramatically increases cooperation. This behavior cannot be
explained by self-interest but requires strong reciprocity.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. ALTRUISTIC PUNISHMENT EXISTS
   - Costly punishment of free-riders
   - No material benefit to punisher
   - Widespread across subjects

2. EFFECTS ON COOPERATION
   - Punishment dramatically increases cooperation
   - Near full cooperation with punishment option
   - Stable over time

3. EMOTIONS AND PUNISHMENT
   - Anger drives punishment
   - Proportional to norm violation
   - Not strategic but emotional

4. EVOLUTIONARY IMPLICATIONS
   - Strong reciprocity evolved
   - Supports human cooperation
   - Not explained by kin selection
