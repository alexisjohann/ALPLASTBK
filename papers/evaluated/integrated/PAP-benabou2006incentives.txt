Title: Incentives and Prosocial Behavior
Authors: Roland Bénabou, Jean Tirole
Year: 2006
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 96, No. 5, pp. 1652-1678

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper develops a theory of prosocial behavior that combines altruism,
material self-interest, and social or self-image concerns. The model explains
how explicit incentives can be counterproductive by signaling distrust or
crowding out intrinsic motivation.

The authors analyze three channels through which rewards and punishments can
backfire: they may overjustify the desired behavior (undermining intrinsic
motivation), they may convey negative news about the principal's trust or the
task's nature, and they may create reputational concerns that distort behavior.

The framework accounts for puzzling real-world phenomena including the variable
effectiveness of incentives across cultures and contexts, and the observation
that extrinsic motivation sometimes enhances and sometimes diminishes
prosociality.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. CROWDING OUT: Extrinsic rewards can reduce intrinsic motivation and
   prosocial behavior by undermining self-image or social-image concerns

2. SIGNALING DISTRUST: Offering incentives may signal that the principal
   doesn't trust the agent, which damages the relationship

3. IMAGE CONCERNS: People care about how their actions are perceived -
   incentives muddle the attribution of prosocial behavior

4. OVERJUSTIFICATION: Rewards provide external justification that crowds out
   internal motivation for the activity

5. CONTEXT DEPENDENCE: The same incentive can enhance or reduce prosociality
   depending on cultural norms and baseline expectations

6. OPTIMAL INCENTIVES: Sometimes no reward is better than a small reward;
   large rewards can work but may be costly
