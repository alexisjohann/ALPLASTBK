ABSTRACT

We provide quasi-experimental evidence on the effects of social media on
mental health by leveraging the staggered introduction of Facebook across
US colleges. We find that the social media platform's introduction is
associated with an increase in symptoms of poor mental health, particularly
depression and anxiety. The effects are strongest among students more
susceptible to social comparison.

RESEARCH DESIGN

Natural Experiment: Staggered Facebook rollout across US colleges (2004-2006)

Timeline:
- February 2004: Facebook launched at Harvard
- March-December 2004: Expanded to other Ivy League and elite schools
- 2005: Expanded to most US colleges
- September 2006: Opened to general public

Data Sources:
1. National College Health Assessment (NCHA)
   - Biannual survey of college students
   - Mental health outcomes
   - 430,000+ students at 775 colleges

2. Facebook expansion timeline
   - Exact date of introduction at each college
   - From Facebook internal records and news archives

Identification:
- Compare colleges before/after Facebook introduction
- Control for college fixed effects and time trends
- Difference-in-differences design

KEY FINDINGS

1. MENTAL HEALTH INDEX
   - Facebook introduction increases poor mental health
   - Effect size: β = -0.085 SD (worsening)
   - Highly significant (p < 0.01)

2. DEPRESSION
   - Significant increase in depression symptoms
   - Consistent across multiple measures

3. ANXIETY
   - Significant increase in anxiety symptoms
   - Similar magnitude to depression effects

4. ACADEMIC OUTCOMES
   - No significant effect on GPA or academic performance
   - Mental health effects not driven by time displacement from studying

5. HETEROGENEITY
   - Effects concentrated among students more susceptible to social comparison
   - No significant gender differences in main effects
   - Effects persist over time (not just novelty)

MECHANISMS

1. SOCIAL COMPARISON
   - Primary mechanism supported by data
   - Students see curated positive content from peers
   - Upward social comparison leads to negative self-evaluation
   - Effects strongest for socially susceptible individuals

2. TIME DISPLACEMENT
   - Some evidence of reduced face-to-face interaction
   - May contribute but not primary driver

3. NOT DRIVEN BY:
   - Academic distraction (no GPA effects)
   - General internet use (specific to Facebook)

ROBUSTNESS CHECKS

1. Pre-trends: No differential trends before Facebook introduction
2. Placebo tests: No effects in periods before actual introduction
3. Alternative specifications: Results robust to different controls
4. Attrition: Results not driven by differential survey response

COMPARISON TO OTHER STUDIES

Similar methodology to DellaVigna & Kaplan (2007):
- Both use staggered rollout as natural experiment
- Both exploit variation in timing of media introduction
- Both find significant persuasion/influence effects

Different from McNamee et al. (2021):
- Braghieri: Quasi-experimental (staggered rollout)
- McNamee: Longitudinal with fixed effects
- Both find negative effects of heavy use

POLICY IMPLICATIONS

1. Social media platforms can have significant mental health consequences
2. Effects are causal, not just correlational
3. Particularly concerning for vulnerable populations
4. Supports policies promoting digital well-being
