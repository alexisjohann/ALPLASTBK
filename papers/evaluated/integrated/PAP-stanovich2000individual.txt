Title: Individual Differences in Reasoning: Implications for the Rationality Debate?
Authors: Keith E. Stanovich, Richard F. West
Year: 2000
Journal: Behavioral and Brain Sciences, Vol. 23, No. 5, pp. 645-665

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Much research in the last two decades has demonstrated that human responses
deviate from the performance deemed normative according to various models of
decision making and rational judgment (e.g., the basic axioms of utility theory).
This gap between the normative and the descriptive can be interpreted as
indicating systematic irrationalities in human cognition.

However, four alternative interpretations preserve the assumption that human
behavior and cognition is largely rational. These posit that the gap is due to
(1) performance errors, (2) computational limitations, (3) the wrong norm being
applied by the experimenter, and (4) a different construal of the task by the
subject.

In examining the heuristics and biases literature, the authors examined the
implications of individual differences in performance for each of the four
explanations.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. FOUR EXPLANATIONS: Performance errors, computational limits, wrong norms,
   alternative construals

2. PERFORMANCE ERRORS MINOR: Individual variation not mainly due to random
   errors

3. COMPUTATIONAL LIMITATIONS: Significant factor in deviations requiring
   cognitive decontextualization

4. INDIVIDUAL DIFFERENCES: Cognitive ability correlates with normative
   responding on many tasks

5. ALTERNATIVE CONSTRUALS: Sometimes subjects interpret tasks differently
   than researchers intend

6. RATIONALITY NUANCED: Not simply "rational or irrational" - depends on
   task and individual
