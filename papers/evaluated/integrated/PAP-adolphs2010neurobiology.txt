================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This primer addresses fundamental questions about emotion from a neuroscience
perspective. When asked "what is an emotion?" most people answer by listing
the most salient attributes of emotions.

The psychologist and philosopher William James, in an 1884 essay, causally
linked two commonsense attributes: certain stimuli can trigger emotional
bodily reactions, and our perception of those changes constitutes our
conscious experience of emotions (feelings).

More recent accounts propose neurobiological substrates involved in causing
emotional reactions and perceiving the feelings, laying the foundation for
conceiving of emotions as neural states. The paper discusses the sensory and
motor aspects of emotions - their sensory aspect deriving from being induced
by stimuli, and their motor aspect residing in the fact that emotions motivate
behavior. Emotions have been described as "decoupled reflexes."

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NEURAL SUBSTRATES: Emotions involve specific brain regions including
   amygdala, insula, prefrontal cortex, and subcortical structures

2. DECOUPLED REFLEXES: Like reflexes, emotional reactions are stereotyped,
   but unlike reflexes, we have some control over emotional behavior

3. SENSORY-MOTOR INTEGRATION: Emotions have both sensory (stimulus-induced)
   and motor (behavior-motivating) aspects

4. CONSCIOUS EXPERIENCE: Feelings are the conscious perception of emotional
   bodily states (following James's theory)

5. BIOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE: Amygdala's primary role is coordinating cortical
   network function during evaluation of biological significance

6. EMOTION-COGNITION INTERACTION: Emotions and cognition are deeply
   intertwined in neural processing, not separate systems
