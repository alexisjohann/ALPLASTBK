ABSTRACT
================================================================================

There is strong evidence that people exploit their bargaining power in
competitive markets but not in bilateral bargaining situations. There is also
strong evidence that people exploit free-riding opportunities in voluntary
cooperation games. Yet, when they are given the opportunity to punish free
riders, stable cooperation is maintained, although punishment is costly for
those who punish. This paper asks whether there is a simple common principle
that can explain this puzzling evidence. We show that if some people care
about equity the puzzles can be resolved. It turns out that the economic
environment determines whether the fair types or the selfish types dominate
equilibrium behavior.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. INEQUITY AVERSION MODEL
   - People dislike both disadvantageous inequality (being worse off than others)
   - People also dislike advantageous inequality (being better off than others)
   - Disutility from disadvantageous inequality is larger (α > β)

2. UTILITY FUNCTION
   U_i(x) = x_i - α_i * max(x_j - x_i, 0) - β_i * max(x_i - x_j, 0)
   where α ≥ β and 0 ≤ β < 1

3. KEY PARAMETERS
   - α (alpha): sensitivity to disadvantageous inequality (mean ≈ 0.85)
   - β (beta): sensitivity to advantageous inequality (mean ≈ 0.315)
   - Constraint: α ≥ β, 0 ≤ β < 1

4. BEHAVIORAL PREDICTIONS
   - Ultimatum game: Explains rejection of unfair offers
   - Public goods: Explains conditional cooperation
   - Market competition: Explains why fairness doesn't affect competitive markets
   - Punishment: Explains costly punishment of free riders

5. ENVIRONMENT MATTERS
   - Competitive markets: Selfish types dominate equilibrium
   - Bilateral bargaining: Fair types can influence outcomes
   - Public goods with punishment: Cooperation can be sustained
