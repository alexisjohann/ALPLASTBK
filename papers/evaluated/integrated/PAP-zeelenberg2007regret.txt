Title: A Theory of Regret Regulation 1.0
Authors: Marcel Zeelenberg, Rik Pieters
Year: 2007
Journal: Journal of Consumer Psychology, Vol. 17, No. 1, pp. 3-18

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors propose a theory of regret regulation that distinguishes regret from
related emotions, specifies the conditions under which regret is felt, the
aspects of the decision that are regretted, and the behavioral implications.

Regret is defined as "the emotion that we experience when realizing or imagining
that our current situation would have been better, if only we had decided
differently."

The regret regulation theory proposes that regret is an aversive, cognitive
emotion that people are motivated to regulate in order to maximize outcomes in
the short term and learn to maximize them in the long run. From a
"feeling-is-for-doing" perspective, regret serves a functional purpose in
guiding future behavior and decision making.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. REGRET AS EMOTION: Distinct cognitive emotion arising from counterfactual
   thinking about decisions

2. REGULATION MOTIVE: People are motivated to avoid, minimize, and repair
   regret experiences

3. FUNCTIONAL PURPOSE: Regret signals discrepancy between outcome and better
   alternative

4. BEHAVIORAL IMPLICATIONS: Regret influences future choices through learning
   and avoidance

5. ANTICIPATORY REGRET: People consider future regret when making current
   decisions

6. ADAPTIVE FUNCTION: Regret helps maximize outcomes over time through
   feedback learning
