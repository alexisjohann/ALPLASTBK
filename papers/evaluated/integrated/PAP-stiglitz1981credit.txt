Title: Credit Rationing in Markets with Imperfect Information
Authors: Joseph E. Stiglitz, Andrew Weiss
Year: 1981
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 71, No. 3, pp. 393-410

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper demonstrates that even in equilibrium, credit rationing will exist
in a loan market. The key mechanism is adverse selection: banks cannot directly
observe borrower quality, and the interest rate affects which borrowers apply
for loans.

Credit rationing takes two forms: (a) among loan applicants who appear
identical, some receive loans and some do not, even though rejected applicants
would pay higher interest rates; or (b) entire groups cannot obtain loans at
any rate, even though with larger credit supply they would.

The counterintuitive result is that banks may refuse to raise interest rates
even in the presence of excess demand, because higher rates attract riskier
borrowers (adverse selection) and encourage riskier behavior (moral hazard).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EQUILIBRIUM RATIONING: Credit rationing persists in equilibrium - it is not
   merely a temporary disequilibrium phenomenon

2. ADVERSE SELECTION: Higher interest rates attract riskier borrowers because
   safer projects become unprofitable at high rates

3. MORAL HAZARD: Higher rates incentivize borrowers to shift toward riskier
   projects, increasing default probability

4. BANKS WON'T CLEAR MARKETS: Profit-maximizing banks choose not to raise rates
   to clear excess demand because higher rates reduce expected returns

5. COLLATERAL INEFFECTIVE: Raising collateral requirements may worsen adverse
   selection, not solve it

6. SUPPLY AND DEMAND FAILS: Law of supply and demand is model-specific; it
   fails in credit markets with asymmetric information
