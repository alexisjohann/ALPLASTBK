Title: The Power of Suggestion: Inertia in 401(k) Participation and Savings Behavior
Authors: Brigitte C. Madrian, Dennis F. Shea
Year: 2001
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 116, No. 4, pp. 1149-1187

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper analyzes the impact of automatic enrollment on 401(k) savings
behavior at a large Fortune 500 company. Before the plan change, employees had
to affirmatively elect participation (opt-in). After the change, employees were
automatically enrolled unless they made a negative election (opt-out).

Although none of the economic features of the plan changed, this switch to
automatic enrollment dramatically changed savings behavior. 401(k) participation
jumped from 49% to 86% - a massive increase simply from changing the default.

A substantial fraction of participants hired under automatic enrollment exhibit
"default" behavior - sticking to both the default contribution rate and the
default fund allocation even though very few employees hired before automatic
enrollment picked this particular outcome. This "default" behavior appears to
result both from participant inertia and from many employees taking the default
as implicit advice from the company.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. DEFAULT POWER: Changing default from opt-in to opt-out raised participation
   from 49% to 86%

2. INERTIA: Most employees stick with whatever the default is (participation
   and rate)

3. IMPLICIT ADVICE: Employees interpret defaults as company recommendation

4. STICKY DEFAULTS: Default contribution rate and allocation persist long-term

5. NO ECONOMIC CHANGE: Same plan features, only default changed - huge effect

6. NUDGE FOUNDATION: Foundational evidence for behavioral policy interventions
