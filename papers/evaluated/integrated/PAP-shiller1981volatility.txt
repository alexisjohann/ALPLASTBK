Title: Do Stock Prices Move Too Much to be Justified by Subsequent Changes in Dividends?
Authors: Robert J. Shiller
Year: 1981
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 71, No. 3, pp. 421-436

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The paper develops the efficient markets model to clarify theoretical questions
and derives inequalities that put limits on the standard deviation of the
innovation in price and the standard deviation of the change in price.

The model is restated in innovation form which allows better understanding of
the limits on stock price volatility imposed by the model.

KEY FINDING: Measures of stock price volatility over the past century appear
to be far too high - five to thirteen times too high - to be attributed to new
information about future real dividends.

This paper questions aspects of the efficient market hypothesis and paved the
road for behavioral analysis in financial economics.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EXCESS VOLATILITY: Stock prices are 5-13x more volatile than efficient
   markets theory predicts

2. VARIANCE BOUNDS: Derives theoretical limits on price volatility under
   rational expectations

3. EMH CHALLENGE: First major empirical challenge to efficient market hypothesis

4. BEHAVIORAL OPENING: Paper opened door for psychological explanations of
   market behavior

5. EARNINGS vs DIVIDENDS: Discusses whether earnings data should be used
   instead of dividends

6. DISCOUNT RATES: Explores whether time-varying discount rates can explain
   excess volatility
