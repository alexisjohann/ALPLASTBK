Title: Temporal Construal
Authors: Yaacov Trope, Nira Liberman
Year: 2003
Journal: Psychological Review, Vol. 110, No. 3, pp. 403-421

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Construal level theory proposes that temporal distance changes people's responses
to future events by changing the way people mentally represent those events. The
greater the temporal distance, the more likely are events to be represented in
terms of a few abstract features that convey the perceived essence of the events
(high-level construals) rather than in terms of more concrete and incidental
details of the events (low-level construals).

The informational and evaluative implications of high-level construals, compared
with those of low-level construals, should therefore have more impact on
responses to distant-future events than near-future events.

This article explores the implications of construal level theory for temporal
changes in evaluation, prediction, and choice. Construal level underlies a
broad range of evaluative and behavioral consequences of psychological distance
from events.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. HIGH-LEVEL CONSTRUAL: Distant events represented abstractly, emphasizing
   essential features

2. LOW-LEVEL CONSTRUAL: Near events represented concretely, with incidental
   details

3. TEMPORAL DISTANCE EFFECT: Greater distance → higher abstraction level

4. EVALUATION CHANGES: Same event evaluated differently depending on temporal
   distance

5. PREDICTION DIFFERENCES: Near vs far predictions differ systematically

6. CHOICE IMPLICATIONS: Construal level affects preferences and decisions
   over time
