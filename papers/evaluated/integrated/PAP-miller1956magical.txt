Title: The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two: Some Limits on Our Capacity for Processing Information
Authors: George A. Miller
Year: 1956
Journal: Psychological Review, Vol. 63, No. 2, pp. 81-97

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Miller's paper discusses limits on human information processing capacity,
famously arguing that the number of objects an average human can hold in
short-term memory is 7 ± 2. This has become known as "Miller's Law."

The paper begins with Miller's famous introduction: "My problem is that I have
been persecuted by an integer. For seven years this number has followed me
around, has intruded in my most private data, and has assaulted me from the
pages of our most public journals."

Miller discusses two types of cognitive limitations:
1. ABSOLUTE JUDGMENT: Limited by the amount of information
2. IMMEDIATE MEMORY: Limited by the number of items (chunks)

Miller concluded that memory span is not limited in terms of bits but rather
in terms of "chunks." A chunk is the largest meaningful unit in the presented
material that the person recognizes - what counts as a chunk depends on the
knowledge of the person being tested.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. MAGICAL NUMBER 7±2: Working memory capacity approximately 7 items

2. CHUNKS VS BITS: Memory limited by number of chunks, not information bits

3. CHUNKING: Grouping items into meaningful units extends effective capacity

4. ABSOLUTE JUDGMENT: Channel capacity limits how many categories we can
   distinguish reliably

5. RECODING: Information can be recoded into fewer, more meaningful chunks

6. DOMAIN GENERAL: Limit applies across modalities (visual, auditory, etc.)
