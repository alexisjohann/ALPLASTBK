Title: A Theory of Social Custom, of which Unemployment may be One Consequence
Authors: George A. Akerlof
Year: 1980
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 94, No. 4, pp. 749-775

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper examines adherence to social customs. Models of social customs are
found to be inherently multi-equilibrial. It is found that social customs
which are disadvantageous to the individual may nevertheless persist without
erosion, if individuals are sanctioned by loss of reputation for disobedience
of the custom.

One example of such a social custom is the persistence of a fair (rather than
a market-clearing) wage. In this fashion, involuntary unemployment is
explained as a consequence of wage rigidity maintained by social norms.

The model shows how customs can persist and lead to phenomena like involuntary
unemployment when sanctions for deviation affect reputation and social standing.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. MULTI-EQUILIBRIA: Social custom models are inherently multi-equilibrial
   with customs at various levels of adherence

2. REPUTATION SANCTIONS: Customs persist because violators lose reputation
   and social standing

3. FAIR WAGE NORM: The "fair wage" (not market-clearing) can be a stable
   social custom that causes unemployment

4. INVOLUNTARY UNEMPLOYMENT: Wage rigidity from social norms explains
   unemployment as equilibrium phenomenon

5. CUSTOM PERSISTENCE: Disadvantageous customs can persist without erosion
   due to reputational sanctions

6. NORM ENFORCEMENT: Social norms are enforced through decentralized
   reputation-based punishment
