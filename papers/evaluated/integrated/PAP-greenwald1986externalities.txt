Title: Externalities in Economies with Imperfect Information and Incomplete Markets
Authors: Bruce C. Greenwald, Joseph E. Stiglitz
Year: 1986
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 101, No. 2, pp. 229-264

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents a simple, general framework for analyzing externalities in
economies with incomplete markets and imperfect information. By identifying
the pecuniary effects of these externalities that net out, the paper simplifies
the problem of determining when tax interventions are Pareto improving.

The key insight is that in markets with imperfect information or incomplete
risk markets, pecuniary externalities matter in ways fundamentally different
from the standard model. In perfect markets, such price effects cancel, with
gains of one individual offset by losses of others. But with imperfect
information, this netting out fails, and seemingly innocuous price changes
have real welfare effects.

The approach indicates that tax interventions are almost always Pareto
improving and that equilibria in situations of imperfect information are
rarely constrained Pareto optima.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. PECUNIARY EXTERNALITIES MATTER: Unlike in complete markets, pecuniary
   externalities have real welfare effects under imperfect information

2. MARKET FAILURE IS PERVASIVE: Equilibria with imperfect information are
   rarely constrained Pareto efficient - intervention almost always helps

3. INFORMATION FAILURES: Adverse selection and moral hazard create pecuniary
   externalities that matter for aggregate welfare

4. GENERAL FRAMEWORK: Provides unified approach to analyzing market failures
   from information asymmetries

5. TAX INTERVENTIONS: Identifies when corrective taxes can achieve Pareto
   improvements over laissez-faire outcomes

6. INCOMPLETE MARKETS: Results extend to settings with incomplete risk markets,
   not just informational imperfections
