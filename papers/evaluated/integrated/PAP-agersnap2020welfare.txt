Title: The Welfare Magnet Hypothesis: Evidence from an Immigrant Welfare Scheme in Denmark
Authors: Ole Agersnap, Amalie Sofie Jensen, Henrik Kleven
Year: 2020
Journal: American Economic Review: Insights, Vol. 2, No. 4, pp. 527-542
DOI: 10.1257/aeri.20190510

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper studies the effects of welfare generosity on international migration
using reforms of immigrant welfare benefits in Denmark.

The FIRST REFORM (2002): Lowered benefits for non-EU immigrants by about 50%,
with no changes for natives or EU immigrants.

The policy was later REPEALED and REINTRODUCED.

KEY FINDING (quasi-experimental evidence):
The benefit reduction reduced the net flow of immigrants by about 5,000 people
per year, and the subsequent repeal of the policy REVERSED the effect almost
exactly.

IMPLIED ELASTICITY: Migration elasticity with respect to benefits = 1.3

This represents some of the FIRST CAUSAL EVIDENCE on the welfare magnet
hypothesis.

Denmark provides an ideal setting because it has one of the most generous
welfare systems in the world — benefit rates are even higher than in the
other Nordic welfare states.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. 50% benefit cut → 5,000 fewer immigrants per year
2. Policy repeal REVERSED the effect almost exactly
3. Migration elasticity w.r.t. benefits = 1.3
4. First CAUSAL evidence for welfare magnet hypothesis
5. Quasi-experimental design (reform + repeal)
6. Denmark as ideal laboratory (very generous baseline)

================================================================================
METHODOLOGICAL CONTRIBUTION
================================================================================

- Quasi-experimental design using policy reversals
- Clean identification from 2002 reform + repeal
- Differential treatment: non-EU vs EU/natives
- Natural experiment in highest-welfare setting
- Elasticity estimation with causal interpretation
