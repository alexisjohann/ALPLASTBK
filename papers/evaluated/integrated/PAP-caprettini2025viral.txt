Title: Going Viral: Protests and Polarization in 1932 Hamburg
Authors: Bruno Caprettini, Marcel Caesmann, Hans-Joachim Voth, David Yanagizawa-Drott
Year: 2025
Institution: University of Zurich, University of St. Gallen, CEPR

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Are mass protests merely a sign of increasing cleavages, or do they
POLARIZE societies?

DATA: 622 voting precincts in Hamburg during 6 elections (1932)

KEY FINDINGS:

1. PROPAGANDA CAN PERSUADE
   Nazi marches increased vote share by ~2 percentage points
   (21% above average)

2. BUT IT CAN ALSO BACKFIRE
   Marches repelled voters in some areas
   → POLARIZING EFFECT: Persuasion in upper tail, backlash in lower tail
   → Same stimulus, OPPOSITE REACTIONS

3. SOCIAL NETWORK DIFFUSION
   Effects diffused through social networks
   Measured using Spanish flu outbreak patterns as instrument
   INDIRECT EFFECTS = 50% of total effect (persuasion multiplier)

4. PERSISTENCE
   Electoral effects persist 12+ months

INNOVATION:
Uses 1918 Spanish flu mortality patterns to measure social network
connectivity across neighborhoods.

TRILOGY (with Voigtländer-Voth):
- 2012: STOCK (historical persistence of anti-Semitism)
- 2015: FLOW (Nazi indoctrination mechanisms)
- 2025: MECHANISM (social network diffusion)

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Marches increase Nazi vote share by 2pp (21% above average)
2. POLARIZING: persuasion AND backlash from same stimulus
3. Social network spillovers = 50% of total effect
4. Spanish flu instrument for network connectivity
5. Effects persist 12+ months
6. Shows how propaganda goes "viral"
