Title: Is Addiction Rational? Theory and Evidence
Authors: Jonathan Gruber, Botond Kőszegi
Year: 2001
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 116, No. 4, pp. 1261-1303

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper examines whether addiction is rational in the sense of being
consistent with forward-looking behavior. The authors retain the stock
addiction framework and forward-looking addicts assumption from Becker and
Murphy's rational addiction model, but reject the assumption of time-consistent
preferences.

The paper argues that past literature has merely shown that smokers are not
fully myopic, not that they are truly time-consistent. A rational smoker
considers smoking's current and future costs, taking addiction and health
damage into account. A smoker might know that she could become addicted and
harm her health, but still choose to smoke anyway because she has rationally
determined that smoking is worth the risk.

The authors develop a model of time-inconsistent addictive behavior and test
it using cigarette consumption data, finding support for the hypothesis that
addicts are forward-looking but time-inconsistent.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. TIME INCONSISTENCY: Addicts are forward-looking but have time-inconsistent
   preferences, not simply myopic

2. FORWARD-LOOKING BEHAVIOR: Smokers respond to future price changes,
   demonstrating anticipatory behavior

3. RATIONAL WITH BIASES: Addiction can be rational even if preferences are
   not time-consistent

4. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Government regulation of addictive goods should
   consider internalities, not just externalities

5. TAX INCIDENCE: Optimal cigarette taxation depends on time-inconsistent
   preferences of smokers

6. EXTENDS BECKER-MURPHY: Retains rational framework but adds behavioral
   realism about intertemporal preferences
