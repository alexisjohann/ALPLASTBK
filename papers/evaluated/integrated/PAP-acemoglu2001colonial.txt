Title: The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development: An Empirical Investigation
Authors: Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, James A. Robinson
Year: 2001
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 91, No. 5, pp. 1369-1401

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors exploit differences in European mortality rates to estimate the
effect of institutions on economic performance. Europeans adopted very
different colonization policies in different colonies, with different
associated institutions.

In places where Europeans faced high mortality rates, they could not settle
and were more likely to set up extractive institutions. These institutions
persisted to the present. Exploiting differences in European mortality rates
as an instrument for current institutions, they estimate large effects of
institutions on income per capita.

Once the effect of institutions is controlled for, countries in Africa or
those closer to the equator do not have lower incomes.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SETTLER MORTALITY INSTRUMENT: European settler mortality rates serve as
   valid instrument for institutional quality - exogenous variation

2. INSTITUTIONAL PERSISTENCE: Colonial institutions (extractive vs inclusive)
   persisted from colonial era to present day

3. LARGE INSTITUTIONAL EFFECTS: Institutions have very large effects on
   income per capita - 1 std dev improvement in institutions = 7x GDP

4. GEOGRAPHY NOT CAUSAL: Once institutions are controlled for, geography
   (Africa, equator distance) has no independent effect on income

5. REVERSAL OF FORTUNE: Previously prosperous regions (dense populations)
   became poorer under colonialism due to extractive institutions

6. TWO COLONIZATION STRATEGIES: Europeans either settled (inclusive
   institutions) or extracted (extractive institutions) depending on
   disease environment
