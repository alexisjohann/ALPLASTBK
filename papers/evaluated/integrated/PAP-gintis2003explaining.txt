Title: Explaining Altruistic Behavior in Humans
Authors: Herbert Gintis, Samuel Bowles, Robert Boyd, Ernst Fehr
Year: 2003
Journal: Evolution and Human Behavior, Vol. 24, No. 3, pp. 153-172

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Recent experimental research has revealed forms of human behavior involving
interaction among unrelated individuals that have proven difficult to explain
in terms of kin or reciprocal altruism. One such trait, strong reciprocity, is
a predisposition to cooperate with others and to punish those who violate the
norms of cooperation, at personal cost, even when it is implausible to expect
that these costs will be repaid.

The authors present evidence supporting strong reciprocity as a schema for
predicting and understanding altruism in humans, showing that under conditions
plausibly characteristic of the early stages of human evolution, a small number
of strong reciprocators could invade a population of self-regarding types, and
that strong reciprocity is an evolutionarily stable strategy.

Multilevel selection and gene-culture coevolutionary models support cooperative
behavior among nonkin, and these models are not vulnerable to the classic
critiques of group selection.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. STRONG RECIPROCITY: Predisposition to cooperate AND punish norm violators
   at personal cost without expectation of repayment

2. EVOLUTIONARY STABILITY: Strong reciprocity can invade and persist as ESS

3. GENE-CULTURE COEVOLUTION: Prosocial genes spread in wake of cultural
   group selection

4. PUNISHMENT ASYMMETRY: Altruistic punishment allows cooperation in larger
   groups than altruistic cooperation alone

5. NOT KIN SELECTION: Cannot be explained by kin altruism or direct reciprocity

6. GROUP SELECTION VIABLE: Multilevel selection models avoid classic critiques
