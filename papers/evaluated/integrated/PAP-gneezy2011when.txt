Title: When and Why Incentives (Don't) Work to Modify Behavior
Authors: Uri Gneezy, Stephan Meier, Pedro Rey-Biel
Year: 2011
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 25, No. 4
DOI: 10.1257/jep.25.4.191

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Extrinsic incentives may come into CONFLICT with other motivations.

KEY INSIGHT:
Monetary incentives from principals may CHANGE HOW TASKS ARE PERCEIVED
by agents, with NEGATIVE effects on behavior.

TWO PROBLEMATIC PATTERNS:

1. SHORT-TERM EFFECTIVENESS, LONG-TERM HARM
   Incentives might have desired effects in short term, but still
   WEAKEN INTRINSIC MOTIVATIONS.

2. CROWDING OUT
   External incentives can crowd out internal motivation.

CONCRETE EXAMPLE:
An incentive for a child to learn to read might:
- Achieve that goal in the SHORT TERM
- BUT be COUNTERPRODUCTIVE as an incentive for students to ENJOY
  reading and seek it out over their lifetimes

THREE IMPORTANT DOMAINS EXAMINED:

1. EDUCATION
   - Incentives for grades vs. love of learning

2. CONTRIBUTIONS TO PUBLIC GOODS
   - Extrinsic rewards vs. civic duty

3. LIFESTYLE CHANGES (smoking, exercise)
   - External motivation vs. internal commitment

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Incentives can CROWD OUT intrinsic motivation
2. Short-term effectiveness ≠ long-term effectiveness
3. How task is perceived changes with incentives
4. Education: incentives may harm love of learning
5. Public goods: rewards may reduce civic duty
6. Health behaviors: external motivation fragile
