Title: Prescription for Disaster: The SSDI Rate, Pain, and Prescribing Practices
Authors: William N. Evans, Ethan M.J. Lieber
Year: 2025
Type: NBER Working Paper No. 34265
DOI: 10.3386/w34265

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

KEY FINDING:
A county's fraction of adults in 1990 on Social Security Disability
Insurance (SSDI) is a STRONG PREDICTOR of growth in local drug death
rates after 2000.

WHAT SSDI IS PROXYING:
The part of SSDI rate related to drug deaths is NOT proxying for
well-known contributors to the drug crisis (e.g., OxyContin).
Instead, it captures the fraction of people in CHRONIC PAIN.

THE MECHANISM:
In the late 1990s, physicians began prescribing opioids MORE
AGGRESSIVELY to treat pain.

COUNTERFACTUAL ESTIMATE:
Drug death rates would be 43% LOWER in 2015 had prescribing practices
stayed at 1995 levels.

REFRAMING THE NARRATIVE:
The opioid crisis is not primarily about pharma marketing (OxyContin),
but about a prescribing practice SHIFT affecting a vulnerable population
(chronic pain sufferers identified by 1990 SSDI rates).

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SSDI rate 1990 predicts drug deaths after 2000
2. SSDI proxies for chronic pain, NOT OxyContin exposure
3. Late 1990s: prescribing shift toward more aggressive opioid use
4. Counterfactual: 43% fewer deaths if 1995 prescribing levels
5. Reframes opioid crisis narrative
6. Chronic pain + prescribing change = crisis
