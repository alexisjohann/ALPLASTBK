Title: Social Distance and Other-Regarding Behavior in Dictator Games
Authors: Elizabeth Hoffman, Kevin McCabe, Vernon L. Smith
Year: 1996
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 86, No. 3, pp. 653-660

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors examine whether instructional and procedural manipulation can be
used in a systematic way to understand the social norms that have been said to
be the cause of deviations from game theoretic predictions in dictator and
other games.

They find that such manipulations, intended to affect subjects' degree of
social distance from the experimenter and assumed to affect expectations of
reciprocity, play a key role in determining and understanding behavior.

As anonymity is relaxed, donations increase. This is due to the fact that
anonymity generates "social distance," defined as the degree of reciprocity
that subjects believe exists within a social interaction. Giving behavior in
the Dictator game is influenced by whether the participant is anonymous to
the experimenter.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SOCIAL DISTANCE EFFECT: Anonymity reduces other-regarding behavior -
   more anonymity = less giving

2. EXPERIMENTER DEMAND: Behavior changes when subjects know experimenter
   can identify them

3. IDENTIFIABILITY MATTERS: It's identifiability, not just reciprocity, that
   drives pro-social behavior

4. PROCEDURAL SENSITIVITY: Experimental procedures strongly affect measured
   "preferences"

5. EXTERNAL VALIDITY CONCERN: Lab generosity may overstate real-world altruism

6. METHODOLOGICAL CONTRIBUTION: Demonstrates importance of double-blind
   procedures in social preference experiments
