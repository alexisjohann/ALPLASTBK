================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper develops and estimates a life-cycle model combining the rational
addiction framework (Becker-Murphy 1988) with human capital investment under
borrowing constraints (Keane-Wolpin 1997). The model features:

1. BIDIRECTIONAL CAUSALITY: Education ↔ Addiction
2. ENDOGENOUS DISCOUNT FACTOR: β(q,θ) depends on addiction stock
3. MULTIPLE CHANNELS: Discount factor, flow utility, monetary costs

Key findings using NLSY97 data (1997-2015):
- Education causally reduces smoking (8 pp long-run effect)
- Eliminating smoking increases college attendance by 3 pp
- 40% excise tax achieves similar education effects
- Non-cognitive skills are key driver of quitting behavior

================================================================================
I. MOTIVATION: TWO INTERTWINED QUESTIONS
================================================================================

QUESTION 1: Does addiction to cigarettes reduce educational attainment?
QUESTION 2: Does education impact cigarette addiction?

EMPIRICAL OBSERVATIONS (NLSY97):

Figure 1 - Left Panel:
  Earlier smoking initiation → Less education
  Age 16 initiation: ~11.5 years schooling
  Age 22 initiation: ~14 years schooling

Figure 1 - Right Panel:
  More education → Lower smoking probability
  10 years schooling: ~45% smoke at age 30
  18 years schooling: ~10% smoke at age 30

EMPIRICAL CHALLENGES:
1. SELECTION: Observable and unobservable characteristics jointly determine both
2. DYNAMIC SELECTION: Preferences evolve based on past choices

SOLUTION: Structural life-cycle model with:
- Rational addiction (Becker-Murphy) for addiction decisions
- Human capital investment (Keane-Wolpin) for education decisions
- Endogenous discount factor connecting both

================================================================================
II. MODEL STRUCTURE
================================================================================

TIMING:
- Agents active from age 15 to 64 (decision period)
- Retirement from 65 to 80 (consumption only)
- Schooling choice ends before age 29

CHOICE SET (each period):
- c_t > 0: Composite good consumption
- s_{t+1}: Savings (can be negative)
- d_{q,t} ∈ {0,1}: Smoke or not
- d_{e,t} ∈ {0,1}: Attend school or not
- d_{p,t} ∈ {0,1}: Part-time work while in school

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

RATIONAL ADDICTION (Becker-Murphy 1988):

Addiction Stock Law of Motion:
  q_t = (1 - δ_q)·q_{t-1} + d_{q,t-1}

Where:
  - q_t = addiction stock at time t
  - δ_q ∈ [0,1] = depreciation rate
  - d_{q,t-1} = smoking decision at t-1
  - q_{15} = 0 (initial condition)

KEY IMPLICATION:
  Past smoking affects current utility through addiction stock
  Creates intertemporal non-separability

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ENDOGENOUS DISCOUNT FACTOR:

β(q, e, t, θ) = exp(-φ_{e,t}) · exp(-κ_q · q) · exp(-κ_r + κ_c·θ_c + κ_n·θ_n)
                ─────────────   ─────────────   ───────────────────────────────
                survival rate   addiction       endowed discount factor
                                effect

Components:
1. exp(-φ_{e,t}): Education-age specific survival rate from CDC mortality data
   - Higher education → higher survival → more patient
   - Older age → lower survival → less patient

2. exp(-κ_q · q): Addiction effect on myopia
   - κ_q ≥ 0: Each unit of addiction increases myopia
   - Consistent with Chaloupka (1991): addicts more myopic

3. exp(-κ_r + κ_c·θ_c + κ_n·θ_n): Endowed discount factor
   - κ_r controls level of discount rate
   - κ_c, κ_n: abilities affect patience (Dohmen et al. 2011)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

FLOW UTILITY:

U(c_t, d_t; q_t, e_t, θ, X_t, ε_t) = [c_t^(1-γ) - 1] / (1-γ)
                                   + u_q(smoking utility)
                                   + u_e(education utility)

Smoking Utility:
  u_q = d_{q,t}·(ν_0 + ν_q·q_t + ν_e·e_t + ν_c·θ_c + ν_n·θ_n + ε_{q,t}) + ν_{q2}·q_t²

  - ν_q > 0: Addiction reinforcement (smoking more pleasurable with history)
  - ν_{q2} < 0: Eventually harmful (concavity)
  - ν_e: Education effect on smoking utility
  - ν_c, ν_n: Ability effects on smoking utility

Education Utility:
  u_e = d_{e,t}·(ξ_p·d_{p,t} + ξ_e·X_t + ξ_c·θ_c + ξ_n·θ_n + ε_{e,t})

  - ξ_p: Part-time work disutility while in school
  - X_t: Age, previous enrollment, schooling level, parental education

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

BUDGET CONSTRAINT:

s_{t+1} - s_t·(1 + r(s_t)) = Y_t + T_t + G_t - (c_t + p_t·d_{q,t} + m_{e+1}·d_{e,t})
─────────────────────────   ───────────────   ─────────────────────────────────────
      savings change         income side                 expenditure side

Where:
- r(s): Interest rate (r_b > r_l for borrowing)
- Y_t: Earnings (Mincer function)
- T_t: Parental transfers
- G_t: Government transfers (means-tested)
- p_t: State-year cigarette price
- m_{e+1}: Tuition and fees

BORROWING CONSTRAINT:
  s_{t+1} ≥ S_{t+1}

S_{t+1} = max{-L^g(e_{t+1}, d_{e,t}), -L^s_t(e_{t+1}, θ)}

  - L^g: Maximum government student loan
  - L^s: Natural borrowing limit (PV of future earnings)

================================================================================
III. THREE CHANNELS: SMOKING → EDUCATION
================================================================================

CHANNEL 1: DISCOUNT FACTOR
  Addiction stock q increases → κ_q·q increases → β decreases
  Lower β → Less willing to invest in human capital
  Effect is CUMULATIVE over lifetime

CHANNEL 2: FLOW UTILITY ANTICIPATION
  If education reduces smoking utility (ν_e < 0):
  Rational agent anticipates this decline
  May reduce educational investment to preserve smoking utility

CHANNEL 3: MONETARY COST
  Cigarette expenditure p_t·d_{q,t} reduces available resources
  Less money for tuition, fees, or savings for future education

================================================================================
IV. THREE CHANNELS: EDUCATION → SMOKING
================================================================================

CHANNEL 1: DISCOUNT FACTOR
  More education → Higher survival φ_{e,t} → Higher β
  Higher β → Different smoking decisions (more forward-looking)

CHANNEL 2: FLOW UTILITY
  Higher education reduces smoking utility (ν_e < 0)
  Direct effect on smoking choice through preferences

CHANNEL 3: INCOME EFFECT
  Education increases earnings Y_t
  Income effect on smoking decisions (can go either way)

CHANNEL 4: BORROWING CONSTRAINTS
  Schooling and smoking compete for resources
  Substitution effects between choices

================================================================================
V. DATA AND ESTIMATION
================================================================================

DATA: NLSY97 (1997-2015)

Sample:
- Males born 1982 or later
- 1,605 individuals
- Ages 15-33
- Unbalanced panel

Key Variables:
- Smoking: Daily smoking (regular smoker)
- Education: Years of schooling completed
- Cognitive: ASVAB scores (4 measures)
- Non-cognitive: Early adverse behaviors + YSR items (6 measures)
- Parental education: Average years of schooling by parents

Cigarette Prices:
- State-year specific from CDC
- Median consumption: 14 cigarettes/day
- Price expectation: Δp = 0.042 (4.2% annual growth)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ESTIMATION METHOD: Two-Step

STEP 1: Factor Model (MLE)
  - Extract cognitive and non-cognitive ability factors
  - 4 continuous measures for cognitive (ASVAB)
  - 6 discrete measures for non-cognitive (behaviors + YSR)
  - Allow correlation between factors

STEP 2: Structural Parameters (SMM)
  - Simulated Method of Moments
  - 48,150 simulations (30 × 1,605)
  - 105 targeted moments
  - 44 parameters estimated

IDENTIFICATION:
1. State-year cigarette price variation → smoking parameters
2. Age patterns of initiation → preference shock variance
3. Auxiliary regression (Becker et al. 1994):
   d_{q,t} = b_0 + b_p·p_t + b_{-1}·d_{q,t-1} + b_{+1}·d_{q,t+1} + ...
   - Instrument d_{q,t±1} with price lags/leads
   - b_{+1}/b_{-1} ratio identifies discount factor effects

================================================================================
VI. PARAMETER ESTIMATES
================================================================================

ADDICTION STOCK DEPRECIATION:
  δ_q = 0.433 (SE: 0.0049)
  → Addiction depletes in ~2.3 years
  → Consistent with CDC health improvement timelines

SUBJECTIVE DISCOUNT FACTOR:

  κ_r = 0.0249 (SE: 0.0009)
  → Implied β = 0.9754 for non-addicts with average abilities
  → Consistent with Keane-Wolpin (0.9758-0.9922), Johnson (0.97)

  κ_q = 0.00038 (SE: 0.000046)
  → Each unit addiction reduces β by 0.038%
  → Cumulative lifetime effect: 0.95% utility loss

  κ_c = 0.0097 (SE: 0.0013)
  κ_n = 0.0113 (SE: 0.0019)
  → Higher abilities → more patient (Dohmen et al. 2011)

SMOKING PREFERENCES:

  ν_q = 0.0205 (SE: 0.0014)
  → One unit addiction stock increases smoking utility
  → At q=1: equivalent to 32% more consumption for median 18-year-old

  ν_{q2} = -0.0022 (SE: 0.0009)
  → Weak concavity (harm emerges slowly)
  → Need q > 9 for disutility to dominate

  ν_e = -0.0048 (SE: 0.0004)
  → Education reduces smoking utility
  → 4 years education offsets 1 year smoking (ν_q/ν_e ≈ 4)

  ν_c ≈ 0 (SE: 0.0016)
  → Cognitive ability has NO direct effect on smoking utility

  ν_n = -0.00816 (SE: 0.002)
  → Non-cognitive ability REDUCES smoking utility
  → 1 SD non-cog ≈ 1 year education effect
  → Self-control and discipline matter!

RISK AVERSION:
  γ = 2.129
  → Within standard range for consumption studies

================================================================================
VII. COUNTERFACTUAL 1: EDUCATION → SMOKING
================================================================================

EXPERIMENT: Assign +1 year schooling at age 20

SHORT-RUN EFFECT (Age 20):
  Smoking rate: 18% → 14%
  Reduction: -4 pp (21%)

LONG-RUN EFFECT (Age 30):
  Smoking rate: 27% → 19%
  Reduction: -8 pp (31%)

QUITTERS ANALYSIS:
  Among age-30 smokers in benchmark: 32% quit with extra education

Who Quits? (Probit analysis)
  - Initial education: +14.5 pp per year (p < 0.001)
  - Cognitive ability: +1.4 pp per SD (not significant)
  - Non-cognitive ability: +28.2 pp per SD (p < 0.001)

KEY INSIGHT:
  Non-cognitive skills (self-control, discipline) are PRIMARY driver
  of quitting in response to education. Cognitive skills matter little.

HETEROGENEITY BY ABILITIES:
  - High ability individuals: Less reduction (less likely to smoke anyway)
  - Medium ability individuals: LARGEST treatment effects
  - Policy implication: Target medium-ability youth

================================================================================
VIII. COUNTERFACTUAL 2: SMOKING → EDUCATION
================================================================================

EXPERIMENT: Eliminate smoking as a choice (ν_q → -∞)

EFFECT ON COLLEGE ATTENDANCE:

Among benchmark non-college smokers:
  0% → 9% college attendance (+9 pp)

Among all benchmark smokers:
  30% → 36% college attendance (+6 pp)

Among entire population:
  59% → 62% college attendance (+3 pp)

KEY INSIGHT:
  Without smoking temptation, benefits of non-cognitive ability DECREASE:
  - Benchmark: Non-cog coefficient on college = 0.501
  - No smoking: Non-cog coefficient on college = 0.316

  Non-cog is more important for AVOIDING harm than for education itself.
  Once harm is impossible, cognitive skills become primary driver.

HETEROGENEITY:
  Largest effects for: High cognitive + Low non-cognitive
  These individuals have low psychic cost of schooling but are addicted.
  Without smoking, they choose education.

================================================================================
IX. COUNTERFACTUAL 3: EXCISE TAX POLICY
================================================================================

EXPERIMENT: Additional τ% excise tax, revenue redistributed as tuition subsidy

MECHANISM:
  Price increase → Some would-be smokers don't smoke
  → Reduced addiction stock → Higher β → More patient
  → More resources (no cigarette expense)
  → Higher college attendance

RESULTS BY TAX RATE (among benchmark smokers):

  τ = 0%:   College = 30%, Smoking age 30 = 27%
  τ = 10%:  College = 30%, Smoking age 30 = 26%
  τ = 40%:  College = 36%, Smoking age 30 = 24%
  τ = 100%: College = 41%, Smoking age 30 = 20%

KEY FINDING:
  40% additional excise tax achieves SAME college rate (36%)
  among would-be smokers as eliminating smoking entirely!

  Implied price elasticity of daily smoking: -0.28
  (Consistent with literature range: -0.06 to -1.1)

POPULATION EFFECT (40% tax):
  College attendance: 59% → 63% (+4 pp)
  (Slightly higher than eliminating smoking due to tuition subsidy)

WHO QUITS WITH TAX? (Probit analysis)
  - Initial education: +3 pp per year
  - Cognitive ability: +6.5 pp per SD (significant!)
  - Non-cognitive ability: +12.2 pp per SD

  Unlike education intervention, BOTH abilities matter for tax response.

================================================================================
X. EBF IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

1. ENDOGENOUS DISCOUNT FACTOR:
   EBF should model β as state-dependent, not fixed:
     β(q, θ, context)
   Addiction and context affect time preferences endogenously.

2. NON-COGNITIVE > COGNITIVE FOR HARMFUL BEHAVIORS:
   For interventions targeting harmful behaviors:
   - Non-cognitive skills are primary moderator
   - Cognitive skills matter little directly
   CORE-WHO dimension should distinguish ability types.

3. ABILITY ROLE DEPENDS ON CONTEXT:
   - With harmful behaviors possible: Non-cog prevents harm
   - Without harmful behaviors: Cognitive drives outcomes
   Supports EBF context-dependence (Ψ).

4. NEGATIVE DYNAMIC COMPLEMENTARITY:
   Addiction creates negative feedback loop:
     Smoking → Lower β → Less education → More smoking
   γ can be NEGATIVE in behavioral domains.

5. POLICY SUBSTITUTION:
   Excise taxes can achieve similar outcomes as impossible interventions.
   40% tax ≈ eliminating smoking for education outcomes.
   Structural parameters allow P4 (new policy) analysis.

6. TARGETING BY ABILITY:
   Medium-ability individuals most responsive to interventions.
   Policy should not only target low-ability (less responsive).

7. MULTIPLE CHANNELS MATTER:
   Three channels (β, flow utility, budget) all contribute.
   Cannot reduce to single mechanism.

================================================================================
XII. LIMITATIONS AND EXTENSIONS
================================================================================

LIMITATIONS:
1. Males only (fertility choices excluded)
2. Cohort-specific (born 1982+, historical period 1997-2015)
3. Age range limited (15-33 observed)
4. Cigarettes only (not marijuana, e-cigarettes, alcohol)

SUGGESTED EXTENSIONS:
1. Intergenerational: How do parental addiction habits transmit?
2. Race differences: How do channels differ by race?
3. Other substances: Marijuana, e-cigarettes, opioids
4. General equilibrium: Labor market effects of addiction reduction

================================================================================
XIII. SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. MODEL CONTRIBUTION:
   First structural model of bidirectional addiction ↔ education causality.
   Combines Becker-Murphy rational addiction with Keane-Wolpin human capital.

2. KEY INNOVATION:
   Endogenous discount factor β(q,θ) where addiction increases myopia.
   Creates feedback loop reducing human capital investment.

3. PARAMETER FINDINGS:
   - Addiction depreciates in ~2 years (δ_q = 0.43)
   - β = 0.9754 for non-addicts (consistent with literature)
   - 0.038% β reduction per unit addiction (cumulative: 0.95% lifetime)
   - 4 years education offsets 1 year smoking utility
   - Non-cognitive ≈ 1 year education for smoking reduction
   - Cognitive has NO direct effect on smoking utility

4. CAUSAL EFFECTS:
   Education → Smoking: -8 pp at age 30 (31% reduction) from +1 year
   Smoking → Education: +3 pp college attendance if smoking eliminated
   40% excise tax achieves similar education effects

5. HETEROGENEITY:
   Non-cognitive skills drive quitting in response to education.
   Medium-ability individuals most responsive to policy.
   Ability role depends on whether harm is possible.
