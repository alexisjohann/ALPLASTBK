Title: Beyond Revealed Preference: Choice-Theoretic Foundations for Behavioral Welfare Economics
Authors: B. Douglas Bernheim, Antonio Rangel
Year: 2009
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 124, No. 1, pp. 51-104

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper develops a welfare criterion that permits the evaluation of economic
policies and institutions even when behavior violates classical assumptions
such as transitivity, regularity, and context-independence.

The framework extends standard welfare analysis to accommodate behavioral
findings while retaining the principle that welfare assessments should respect
individual choices. The approach distinguishes between choices made under
standard conditions (which reveal true preferences) and choices made under
suspect conditions (which may reflect cognitive limitations or biases).

The methodology provides foundations for behavioral welfare economics that
can guide paternalistic interventions without abandoning choice-based welfare
evaluation entirely.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. GENERALIZED WELFARE CRITERION: Framework accommodates preference reversals,
   framing effects, and context dependence while respecting choice

2. STANDARD VS SUSPECT CONDITIONS: Distinguishes conditions where choices
   reliably reveal preferences from those where they may not

3. UNAMBIGUOUS WELFARE IMPROVEMENT: Policy improves welfare if it makes
   individual better off under all possible coherent preference orderings

4. REFINEMENTS: Additional criteria can narrow welfare comparisons when
   ancillary conditions reveal more about true preferences

5. PATERNALISM FOUNDATIONS: Provides principled basis for soft paternalism
   and libertarian paternalism without imposing external preferences

6. BEHAVIORAL POLICY ANALYSIS: Enables cost-benefit analysis even when
   behavioral biases distort revealed preferences
