Title: Libertarian Paternalism Is Not an Oxymoron
Authors: Cass R. Sunstein, Richard H. Thaler
Year: 2003
Journal: University of Chicago Law Review, Vol. 70, No. 4, pp. 1159-1202

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The idea of libertarian paternalism might seem to be an oxymoron, but it is
both possible and desirable for private and public institutions to influence
behavior while also respecting freedom of choice.

The core insight: Often people's preferences are unclear and ill-formed, and
their choices will inevitably be influenced by default rules, framing effects,
and starting points. In these circumstances, a form of paternalism CANNOT be
avoided - some default must be chosen. The question is not WHETHER to
influence choices, but HOW.

Equipped with understanding of bounded rationality and bounded self-control,
libertarian paternalists should attempt to steer people's choices in
welfare-promoting directions WITHOUT eliminating freedom of choice. The key
is "asymmetric paternalism" - interventions that help those who err while
imposing minimal costs on fully rational agents.

This paper laid the intellectual foundation for the 2008 book "Nudge" and
modern choice architecture.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. LIBERTARIAN PATERNALISM: Influence behavior while preserving freedom of
   choice

2. UNAVOIDABLE DEFAULTS: Some default must be chosen; neutrality impossible

3. ASYMMETRIC PATERNALISM: Help those who err, minimal cost to rational agents

4. BOUNDED RATIONALITY: People have cognitive limits that policy should address

5. CHOICE ARCHITECTURE: How options are presented affects choices

6. WELFARE-PROMOTING: Steer toward better outcomes without mandates
