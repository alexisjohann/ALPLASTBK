Title: From Efficient Markets Theory to Behavioral Finance
Authors: Robert J. Shiller
Year: 2003
Journal: Journal of Economic Perspectives, Vol. 17, No. 1, pp. 83-104

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The efficient markets theory reached the height of its dominance in academic
circles around the 1970s. Faith in this theory was eroded by a succession of
discoveries of anomalies, many in the 1980s, and of evidence of excess
volatility of returns.

Finance literature in this decade and after suggests a more nuanced view of
the value of the efficient markets theory, and, starting in the 1990s, a
blossoming of research on behavioral finance.

Some important developments since 1990 include feedback theories, models of
the interaction of smart money with ordinary investors, and evidence on
obstacles to smart money.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. EXCESS VOLATILITY: Stock prices more volatile than fundamentals warrant -
   key anomaly efficient markets cannot explain

2. ANOMALIES ACCUMULATE: Succession of anomalies discovered in 1980s eroded
   faith in efficient markets

3. FEEDBACK THEORIES: Positive feedback loops can create bubbles and crashes
   independent of fundamentals

4. SMART MONEY LIMITS: Arbitrage limits prevent sophisticated investors from
   correcting mispricings

5. BEHAVIORAL REVOLUTION: Rise of behavioral finance as serious alternative
   to efficient markets

6. NOT PURE OVERREACTION: Anomalies show both overreaction and underreaction -
   no simple pattern
