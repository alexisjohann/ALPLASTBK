ABSTRACT

This paper compares the effectiveness of fact-checking versus media literacy
interventions in reducing misinformation susceptibility. Using a randomized
survey experiment with 2,953 German residents, we find that fact-checking
primarily affects the specific fake news it addresses, while media literacy
helps distinguish between false and correct information more generally.
Media literacy effects persist at least two weeks after the intervention
and increase vaccination willingness.

RESEARCH DESIGN

Randomized Survey Experiment with German Adult Residents

Timeline:
- Wave 1: Baseline measures + treatment + immediate outcomes
- Wave 2: Follow-up after 2 weeks (persistence check)

Sample: 2,953 participants from online panel
Country: Germany
Context: Corona pandemic misinformation (vaccines, nutrition)

Treatment Arms:
1. Control: No intervention
2. FC-fake: Fact-check correcting a fake claim about vaccines
3. FC-correct: Fact-check confirming a correct claim about nutrition
4. ML: Media literacy tips (6 simple rules)
5. Combined: Fact-check + media literacy

Data Sources:
1. Bilendi online panel
2. Custom survey instrument
3. 2-week follow-up survey

Identification:
- Random assignment to treatment conditions
- Between-subjects design
- Multiple outcome measures

KEY FINDINGS

1. FACT-CHECKING EFFECTS (Targeted)
   - Reduces credibility of targeted fake: β = -0.078 (SE 0.028)
   - Effect size: 15.5% of a standard deviation
   - No spillover to other fake news items
   - No effect on correct information discernment

2. MEDIA LITERACY EFFECTS (General)
   - Reduces credibility of ALL fake news: β = -0.102 (SE 0.019)
   - Effect size: 22.9% of a standard deviation
   - INCREASES credibility of TRUE news: β = +0.067 (SE 0.024)
   - Improves general truth discernment

3. PERSISTENCE
   - Media literacy effects persist after 2 weeks
   - Wave 2 effect: β = -0.09 (not significantly different from Wave 1)
   - No significant decay over time

4. BEHAVIORAL OUTCOME: VACCINATION
   - Media literacy increases vaccination willingness: β = 0.048 (SE 0.022)
   - Combined treatment: β = 0.034 (SE 0.022)
   - 3.4-4.8 percentage point increase

5. HETEROGENEITY
   - AfD supporters (far-right party) show LARGER treatment effects
   - Low-trust individuals more receptive to interventions
   - No significant gender differences

MECHANISMS

1. ATTENTION MECHANISM (Key Finding)
   - Media literacy raises attention to information quality
   - Enables ACTIVE critical evaluation
   - System 2 activation through simple tips

2. PASSIVE VS ACTIVE PROCESSING
   - Fact-checking: Participants become passive recipients
   - Media literacy: Participants become active evaluators
   - "Teaches to fish" vs "gives fish"

3. NOT DRIVEN BY:
   - Prior beliefs (controlled for)
   - Political sophistication (no interaction)
   - General media consumption

MEDIA LITERACY TIPS USED IN INTERVENTION

The 6 tips provided to participants:
1. "Be skeptical of the headline"
2. "Look at the source"
3. "Check when and where an article was published"
4. "Look at whether other outlets are also reporting on the topic"
5. "Look at whether experts also comment on the topic"
6. "Check whether a claim is shared or checked by fact-checking sites"

These tips are:
- Cheap to implement
- Scalable (no per-item cost)
- Easy to understand
- Broadly applicable

COMPARISON TO PREVIOUS RESEARCH

Similar to List et al. (2024):
- Both test demand-side interventions
- Both find critical thinking superior to passive information
- Both find persistence effects

Different from List et al. (2024):
- Berger: Text-based tips; List: Video content
- Berger: German pandemic context; List: Colombian election
- Berger: Intervention comparison; List: Intervention complementarity

Extends Braghieri et al. (2022):
- Different outcome (misinformation vs mental health)
- Different intervention (literacy vs natural experiment)
- Complementary mechanism (attention vs social comparison)

POLICY IMPLICATIONS

1. Media literacy is more cost-effective than fact-checking
   - Scalable: Same tips work for any misinformation
   - Cheap: One-time investment in tip dissemination
   - Persistent: Effects last at least 2 weeks

2. Fact-checking has limited scope
   - Only affects specific targeted content
   - No spillover benefits
   - Requires constant updating

3. Platform design implications
   - Integrate media literacy prompts
   - Not just label individual posts
   - Enable user critical thinking

4. Vaccination policy implications
   - Media literacy can increase vaccine uptake
   - 3-5 percentage point increase is policy-relevant
   - Cost-effective public health intervention
