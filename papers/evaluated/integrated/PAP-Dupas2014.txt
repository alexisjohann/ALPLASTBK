Title: Short-Run Subsidies and Long-Run Adoption of New Health Products: Evidence From a Field Experiment
Authors: Pascaline Dupas
Year: 2014
Journal: Econometrica, Vol. 82, No. 1, pp. 197-228

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Short-run subsidies for health products are common in poor countries. How do
they affect long-run adoption? A common fear among development practitioners
is that one-off subsidies may negatively affect long-run adoption through
reference-dependence: People might anchor around the subsidized price and be
unwilling to pay more for the product later.

But for experience goods, one-off subsidies could also boost long-run adoption
through learning. This paper uses data from a two-stage randomized pricing
experiment in Kenya to estimate the relative importance of these effects for
a new, improved antimalarial bed net.

Reduced form estimates show that a one-time subsidy has a positive impact on
willingness to pay a year later. To separately identify the learning and
anchoring effects, the author estimates a parsimonious experience-good model.
Estimation results show a large, positive learning effect but no anchoring.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. NO ANCHORING EFFECT: People do not anchor around subsidized prices and
   refuse to pay more later

2. POSITIVE LEARNING: Short-run subsidies boost long-run adoption through
   experience and social learning

3. EXPERIENCE GOODS: For products where learning-by-doing matters, subsidies
   are beneficial for adoption

4. RANDOMIZED PRICING: Two-stage experiment with varying discount vouchers
   enables causal identification

5. SOCIAL LEARNING: Observing others using the technology also contributes
   to adoption

6. POLICY IMPLICATION: One-off subsidies are effective for promoting new
   health technologies without creating dependency
