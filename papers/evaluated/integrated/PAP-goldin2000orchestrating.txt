Title: Orchestrating Impartiality: The Impact of "Blind" Auditions on Female Musicians
Authors: Claudia Goldin, Cecilia Rouse
Year: 2000
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 90, No. 4, pp. 715-741

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

A revolution in hiring procedures among major U.S. symphony orchestras began
in the 1970s when most began to use "blind" auditions - auditions with a screen
to conceal the identity of the candidate from the jury. This paper uses
orchestra personnel records from 8 orchestras spanning 1950 to 1995 to test
whether blind auditions affected hiring outcomes.

The analysis finds that the switch to blind auditions significantly increased
the probability that a female would advance from preliminary rounds and increased
the probability that a female would be selected in final rounds. The authors
estimate that blind auditions account for about 25% of the increase in the
proportion of females among new hires and 30% of the increase in the proportion
of females in the orchestras since 1970.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. BLIND AUDITIONS: Screen-based auditions increased female advancement by
   ~50% in preliminary rounds

2. SELECTION BIAS: Without screens, female candidates were disadvantaged at
   all stages of auditions

3. MAGNITUDE: Blind auditions explain 25-30% of increase in female orchestra
   membership

4. NATURAL EXPERIMENT: Staggered adoption across orchestras provides
   identification

5. IMPLICIT BIAS: Results suggest implicit gender bias in evaluation even
   among expert evaluators

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Anonymizing evaluation can reduce discrimination
   without quotas
