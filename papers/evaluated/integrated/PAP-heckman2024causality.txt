================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper examines the econometric approach to causality. Defining and
identifying causal parameters requires thought experiments. We compare two
leading approaches to causality rooted in statistical theory: the Neyman-Rubin
model of experiments and Pearl's Do-Calculus, with the econometric approach to
causality. The econometric approach is centered on thought experiments that
define the class of all possible a priori counterfactuals in terms of
hypothetical worlds generated by fixing some subset of parameters that produce
observed data. This approach has a long tradition in economics. It produces
policy-relevant treatment effects and determines which policy problems are
addressed by any particular thought experiment.

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION - KEY POINTS
================================================================================

The central question: How do we define and identify causal effects?

Three major approaches compared:
1. ECONOMETRIC (Frisch-Haavelmo tradition)
   - Based on thought experiments
   - Uses structural equations
   - Can answer all four policy questions (P1-P4)

2. NEYMAN-RUBIN (Potential Outcomes)
   - Based on randomized experiments
   - Focuses on treatment assignment
   - Limited to P1, P2 without additional structure

3. PEARL (Do-Calculus)
   - Based on Directed Acyclic Graphs
   - Nonparametric
   - Cannot handle simultaneity

================================================================================
2. THE THREE TASKS OF ECONOMETRIC ANALYSIS (Table 1)
================================================================================

TASK 1: DEFINING HYPOTHETICALS
- Precisely specify the thought experiment
- Requires a priori economic theory
- Determines what counterfactuals are meaningful

TASK 2: IDENTIFICATION FROM DATA
- Map hypothetical constructs to observable data
- Requires identification assumptions
- Statistical exercises to recover structural parameters

TASK 3: ESTIMATION
- Use data to estimate identified parameters
- Standard statistical theory applies
- Concerns about inference and uncertainty

KEY INSIGHT: These tasks are logically separate but often conflated in practice.

================================================================================
3. FOUR FUNDAMENTAL POLICY QUESTIONS (P1-P4)
================================================================================

P1: TREATMENT ON THE TREATED (TT)
    Formula: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 1]
    Question: What is the effect for those who received treatment?
    Requirement: Selection mechanism understanding

P2: TREATMENT ON THE UNTREATED (TUT)
    Formula: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 0]
    Question: What would be the effect for non-participants?
    Requirement: Counterfactual extrapolation

P3: POLICY FORECAST
    Question: What would happen if we implement an existing policy in a new setting?
    Requirement: Understanding which mechanisms are invariant
    Challenge: External validity

P4: EFFECTS OF NEW POLICIES
    Question: What would happen under policies never previously implemented?
    Requirement: Full structural model
    Challenge: Most demanding; requires deep parameters

================================================================================
4. FIXING VS. CONDITIONING - CRITICAL DISTINCTION
================================================================================

FIXING (Econometric Approach):
- Modifying or setting a mechanism
- Involves a hypothetical intervention
- Changes the data generating process
- Example: Setting D = 1 for everyone (universal policy)

CONDITIONING (Statistical Approach):
- Restricting sample to a subpopulation
- Does not involve intervention
- Maintains observational distribution
- Example: E[Y | D = 1] (selecting treated observations)

KEY EQUATION:
E[Y | fix(D = 1)] ≠ E[Y | D = 1]

The left side is the effect of a policy; the right side is an observational correlation.

================================================================================
5. THE GENERALIZED ROY MODEL
================================================================================

OUTCOME EQUATION:
Y = D · Y₁ + (1-D) · Y₀

where:
- Y₁ = μ₁(X) + U₁ (potential outcome if treated)
- Y₀ = μ₀(X) + U₀ (potential outcome if untreated)

SELECTION EQUATION:
D = 1[V > 0]

where:
- V = μᵥ(Z) - ε (net benefit of treatment)
- Z includes instruments that affect D but not Y directly

TREATMENT EFFECTS:
- ATE: E[Y₁ - Y₀] = E[μ₁(X) - μ₀(X)]
- TT: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 1] (accounts for selection)
- TUT: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D = 0]
- MTE: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | V = v] (marginal treatment effect)
- LATE: E[Y₁ - Y₀ | Compliers] (for IV estimation)

IDENTIFICATION STRATEGIES:
1. Instrumental Variables: Z affects D but not Y directly
2. Matching: Selection on observables X
3. Control Functions: Model the selection process
4. Difference-in-Differences: Parallel trends assumption
5. Regression Discontinuity: Discontinuous treatment assignment

================================================================================
6. CRITIQUE OF NEYMAN-RUBIN APPROACH
================================================================================

LIMITATIONS IDENTIFIED:
1. Cannot answer P3 and P4 without additional structure
2. SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption) often violated
3. Limited external validity framework
4. Conflates treatment effects with policy effects
5. No framework for general equilibrium effects

SUTVA ASSUMPTION:
Yᵢ(d) = Yᵢ(dᵢ) for all d such that dᵢ = d

This rules out:
- Peer effects
- General equilibrium effects
- Spillovers

================================================================================
7. CRITIQUE OF PEARL'S DO-CALCULUS
================================================================================

LIMITATIONS IDENTIFIED:
1. Nonparametric by design - cannot use functional form
2. Cannot handle simultaneity (DAGs are acyclic)
3. Limited for policy forecasting (P3, P4)
4. Confuses fixing with conditioning in some applications
5. Graph surgery is fixing, but implications not always clear

DO-OPERATOR:
P(Y | do(X = x)) = Σᵤ P(Y | X = x, U = u) P(U)

This is fixing X, but within a restricted (nonparametric) framework.

================================================================================
8. THE ECONOMETRIC TRADITION
================================================================================

HISTORICAL DEVELOPMENT:
1. Frisch (1933): Propagation problems and impulse problems
2. Haavelmo (1944): Probability approach to econometrics
3. Cowles Commission (1950s): Simultaneous equations
4. Heckman (1970s-present): Selection models, treatment effects

KEY PRINCIPLES:
1. Structural parameters are "deep" - invariant to policy
2. Policy analysis requires understanding mechanisms
3. Thought experiments define meaningful counterfactuals
4. Identification requires economic theory + statistical assumptions

LUCAS CRITIQUE CONNECTION:
Reduced-form parameters change with policy.
Structural parameters should be policy-invariant.
This is why P4 requires structural models.

================================================================================
9. IMPLICATIONS FOR EBF
================================================================================

1. PARAMETERS IN EBF:
   - Loss aversion λ is a structural parameter
   - Discount factor β is a structural parameter
   - These should be policy-invariant (deep parameters)

2. INTERVENTIONS IN EBF:
   - Should be evaluated using P1-P4 framework
   - P1: Did the nudge work for those who received it?
   - P2: Would it work for non-participants?
   - P3: Will it work in a different context?
   - P4: Can we design novel interventions?

3. CONTEXT (Ψ) IN EBF:
   - Context = which mechanisms are held fixed
   - Different Ψ = different thought experiments
   - External validity requires mechanism invariance

4. 10C FRAMEWORK MAPPING:
   - CORE-HOW: Causal mechanisms through structural equations
   - CORE-WHAT: Distinguishes treatment effects from parameters
   - CORE-WHEN: Context determines which mechanisms are fixed

10. KEY QUOTES FROM THE PAPER
================================================================================

"The econometric approach to causality is based on thought experiments that
define hypothetical worlds by fixing some subset of the mechanisms that
produce observed data." (p. 3)

"Fixing is an action that involves modifying a mechanism. Conditioning is a
statistical operation that does not involve any action on a mechanism." (p. 15)

"Treatment effects are not policy effects. Policies operate on agents—on
their information sets, constraints, and preferences. Treatment effects
compare potential outcomes." (p. 8)

"The Neyman-Rubin model is designed for randomized experiments. Its direct
application to observational data without additional structure can be
misleading." (p. 22)

"Pearl's Do-Calculus is nonparametric. It cannot utilize functional form
restrictions that are essential for answering questions P3 and P4." (p. 31)
