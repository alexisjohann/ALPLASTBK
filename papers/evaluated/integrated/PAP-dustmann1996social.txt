Title: The Social Assimilation of Immigrants
Authors: Christian Dustmann
Year: 1996
Journal: Journal of Population Economics, Vol. 9, No. 1, pp. 37-54
DOI: 10.1007/PL00003825

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Although there is considerable empirical research on the ECONOMIC assimilation
of migrants to the labor markets of host countries, little or no analysis
exists on the SOCIAL assimilation and integration of migrant workers.

This is surprising since the integration of migrants is not only of political
importance, but it should strongly interact with their economic behavior.

This paper provides an empirical analysis of the determinants of migrants'
integration, using data for Germany.

Ordered probit models are estimated, where the dependent variable is an ordered
response on the FEELING OF NATIONAL IDENTITY.

KEY FINDINGS:
- Personal characteristics affect integration
- Nationality affects integration
- Family context affects integration
- Labor market variables appear to be quite UNIMPORTANT

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. First systematic analysis of SOCIAL (not just economic) assimilation
2. National identity feeling as dependent variable (ordered probit)
3. Personal characteristics matter for integration
4. Family context matters for integration
5. Nationality affects integration patterns
6. Surprisingly: Labor market variables NOT important for social integration

================================================================================
CONTRIBUTION
================================================================================

- Bridges economic and sociological migration literature
- Quantifies determinants of subjective integration
- Shows social and economic assimilation may follow different paths
- Germany as case study (large immigrant population)
