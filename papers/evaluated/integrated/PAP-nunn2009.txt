Title: The Slave Trade and the Origins of Mistrust in Africa
Authors: Nathan Nunn, Leonard Wantchekon
Year: 2011
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 101, No. 7, pp. 3221-3252
DOI: 10.1257/aer.101.7.3221
Note: NBER Working Paper 14783 (2009)

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors show that current differences in trust levels within Africa can
be traced back to the transatlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades.

Combining contemporary individual-level survey data with historical data on
slave shipments by ethnic group, they find that individuals whose ancestors
were heavily raided during the slave trade are less trusting today.

Evidence from a variety of identification strategies suggests that the
relationship is causal. Examining causal mechanisms, they show that most of
the impact of the slave trade is through factors that are internal to the
individual, such as cultural norms, beliefs, and values.

COMPANION TO: Nunn (2008) QJE "Long-term Effects of Africa's Slave Trades"
which shows slave trade → underdevelopment; this paper shows the MECHANISM
is through trust.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SLAVE TRADE → MISTRUST: Historical slave raiding predicts lower trust today

2. CAUSAL IDENTIFICATION: Multiple strategies (IV, ethnic fixed effects)
   support causal interpretation

3. CULTURAL TRANSMISSION: Effect works through cultural norms, beliefs, values
   passed across generations

4. ETHNIC-LEVEL DATA: Uses shipping records and historical documents to
   construct ethnic-group slave export estimates

5. MECHANISM FOR UNDERDEVELOPMENT: Trust is the channel through which
   slave trade affects economic outcomes

6. PERSISTENCE: 400+ year persistence of cultural trait (mistrust)
