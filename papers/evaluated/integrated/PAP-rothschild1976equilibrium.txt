Title: Equilibrium in Competitive Insurance Markets: An Essay on the Economics
       of Imperfect Information
Authors: Michael Rothschild, Joseph E. Stiglitz
Year: 1976
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 90, No. 4, pp. 629-649

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper characterizes competitive equilibrium in insurance markets under
adverse selection. Unlike Akerlof's single-contract, price-competition model,
this framework allows firms to compete on multiple dimensions of the contract
(price and coverage level).

The central result is that equilibrium, if it exists, must be a separating
equilibrium: different risk types purchase different contracts. Low-risk
individuals receive less than full insurance because any contract attractive
to them would also attract high-risk types, creating adverse selection.

Remarkably, the paper demonstrates that equilibrium may fail to exist entirely.
The existence of high-risk individuals imposes a negative externality on low-
risk individuals, potentially preventing any stable market outcome.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SEPARATING EQUILIBRIUM: In equilibrium, high-risk and low-risk types must
   purchase different contracts - pooling equilibria are not stable

2. PARTIAL COVERAGE FOR LOW-RISK: Low-risk types cannot get full insurance
   because full coverage would attract high-risk types (cream-skimming problem)

3. EQUILIBRIUM NON-EXISTENCE: Equilibrium may not exist when the proportion
   of low-risk types is sufficiently high

4. SCREENING VIA SELF-SELECTION: Firms design menus of contracts such that
   each type self-selects into the contract designed for them

5. NEGATIVE EXTERNALITY: High-risk types impose externality on low-risk types,
   degrading the contracts available to low-risk individuals

6. MULTI-DIMENSIONAL COMPETITION: Unlike price-only competition, firms compete
   on both premium and coverage, enabling screening
