# Social media use and emotional and behavioural outcomes in adolescence:
# Evidence from British longitudinal data

ABSTRACT

We analyse the effects of social media use on emotional and behavioural
difficulties in young people using British longitudinal data. Social media use
captures time spent on social networking sites (SNS), chat rooms and messaging,
whether during the week or at weekends. Emotional and behavioural outcomes are
measured using the Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ).

We use data from the Understanding Society (UKHLS) panel survey, following
adolescents who were aged 11-14 when first measured on social media use, and
then again when they were aged 16-20 for outcome measurement. This allows us
to examine longer-term associations while controlling for baseline
characteristics.

Our identification strategy relies on individual fixed effects to control for
time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity. We supplement this with propensity
score matching (PSM) and inverse probability weighted regression adjustment
(IPWRA) as robustness checks.

KEY FINDINGS

1. GOLDILOCKS HYPOTHESIS SUPPORTED
   - High use (>4 hours/day) is associated with negative outcomes
   - Moderate use (<3 hours/day) may have small positive associations
   - Supports "everything in moderation" principle

2. NEGATIVE OUTCOMES FROM PROLONGED USE (>4h/day)
   - Decreased perception of self-value
   - Increased hyperactivity
   - Increased inattention
   - Increased conduct problems

3. MODERATE POSITIVE EFFECTS (<3h/day)
   - Moderate positive association with peer relationships
   - Compared to no use

4. HETEROGENEITY
   - Effects differ by gender
   - Effects differ by age
   - Effects differ by socio-economic background

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS

1. DISPLACEMENT HYPOTHESIS (Lin, 1993)
   Social media displaces health-promoting activities like sleep, physical
   activity, and face-to-face social interaction.

2. USES AND GRATIFICATIONS THEORY (Quan-Haase & Young, 2010)
   Individuals actively choose media to fulfill specific needs. Those with
   poor mental health may use social media as a coping mechanism.

3. GOLDILOCKS HYPOTHESIS (Przybylski & Weinstein, 2017)
   Moderate digital engagement is not harmful and may be advantageous,
   while both over- and under-engagement are associated with negative
   well-being outcomes.

METHODOLOGY

Data Source: Understanding Society (UKHLS) - UK Household Longitudinal Study
Sample: Adolescents aged 11-14 at baseline, followed to ages 16-20
Country: United Kingdom

Identification Strategies:
1. Individual Fixed Effects - controls for time-invariant unobservables
2. Propensity Score Matching (PSM) - robustness check
3. IPWRA Treatment Effects - doubly robust estimation

Outcome Measures (Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire):
- Emotional symptoms
- Conduct problems
- Hyperactivity/inattention
- Peer relationship problems
- Pro-social behavior

DOSE-RESPONSE FINDINGS

HIGH USE (>4 hours/day):
- Statistically significant negative associations across multiple domains
- Largest effects on self-esteem and hyperactivity
- Consistent across FE, PSM, and IPWRA specifications

MODERATE USE (<3 hours/day):
- Small positive associations with peer relationships
- Effects modest in magnitude
- Compared to zero use baseline

POLICY IMPLICATIONS

1. Guidelines limiting prolonged social media use are supported by evidence
2. Moderate use may have some benefits - "digital abstinence" not necessarily optimal
3. Heterogeneity suggests targeting interventions to most vulnerable groups
4. Aligns with recommendations from Royal College of Paediatrics
