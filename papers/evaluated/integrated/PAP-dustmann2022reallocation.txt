Title: Reallocation Effects of the Minimum Wage
Authors: Christian Dustmann, Attila Lindner, Uta Schönberg, Matthias Umkehrer,
         Philipp vom Berge
Year: 2022
Journal: The Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 137, No. 1, pp. 267-328
DOI: 10.1093/qje/qjab028

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The paper investigates the wage, employment, and reallocation effects of the
introduction of a nationwide minimum wage in Germany that affected 15% of all
employees.

Based on identification designs that exploit variation in exposure across
individuals and local areas, the authors find that the minimum wage:
1. RAISED WAGES (as intended)
2. Did NOT lower employment (contrary to some predictions)
3. Led to REALLOCATION of low-wage workers:
   - From smaller to larger establishments
   - From lower- to higher-paying establishments
   - From less to more productive establishments

This worker upgrading accounts for up to 17% of the wage increase induced by
the minimum wage.

Moreover, at the regional level, average establishment quality increased in
more affected areas in the years following the introduction of the minimum wage.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Germany introduced minimum wage (€8.50) in January 2015 - affected 15% of workers
2. Wages increased, employment did NOT fall
3. Worker REALLOCATION is key mechanism (first paper to highlight this)
4. Upgrading accounts for 17% of wage increase
5. Workers moved to: larger, higher-paying, more productive firms
6. Regional establishment quality improved in affected areas

================================================================================
METHODOLOGICAL CONTRIBUTION
================================================================================

- First to document reallocation effects of minimum wage
- Dual identification: individual variation + local area exposure
- Natural experiment: Germany had no prior national minimum wage
- Rich administrative data: full population of workers and establishments
