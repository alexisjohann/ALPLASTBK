Title: Salience Theory of Choice Under Risk
Authors: Pedro Bordalo, Nicola Gennaioli, Andrei Shleifer
Year: 2012
Journal: Quarterly Journal of Economics, Vol. 127, No. 3, pp. 1243-1285
DOI: 10.1093/qje/qjs018

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors present a theory of choice among lotteries in which the decision
maker's attention is drawn to (precisely defined) salient payoffs.

This leads the decision maker to a context-dependent representation of
lotteries in which true probabilities are replaced by decision weights
distorted in favor of salient payoffs.

By specifying decision weights as a function of payoffs, the model provides
a novel and unified account of many empirical phenomena, including frequent
risk-seeking behavior, invariance failures such as the Allais paradox, and
preference reversals.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. SALIENCE-BASED ATTENTION: Decision makers focus on payoffs that "stand out"
   (are salient) relative to alternatives

2. CONTEXT-DEPENDENT: Salience is determined by comparison - same payoff can
   be salient or not depending on context

3. PROBABILITY DISTORTION: True probabilities replaced by decision weights
   favoring salient payoffs

4. ALLAIS PARADOX: Salience theory explains the common consequence effect
   without invoking probability weighting

5. PREFERENCE REVERSALS: Context-dependent salience explains why preferences
   can reverse between equivalent framings

6. RISK-SEEKING: Explains when people seek risk (when upside is salient)
   vs avoid risk (when downside is salient)
