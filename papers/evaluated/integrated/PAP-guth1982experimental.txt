Title: An Experimental Analysis of Ultimatum Bargaining
Authors: Werner Güth, Rolf Schmittberger, Bernd Schwarze
Year: 1982
Journal: Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, Vol. 3, No. 4, pp. 367-388

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper presents the first experimental analysis of the ultimatum game. In
an ultimatum bargaining game, two players have to divide a given positive
amount c of money. First, player 1 demands how much of the 'cake' c he desires
for himself. Player 2 can then accept or reject; if rejected, both get nothing.

The special property of ultimatum bargaining games is that on every stage of
the bargaining process only one player has to decide, and that before the last
stage the set of outcomes is already restricted to only two results.

Results challenged the traditional economic principle that consumers are
rational and utility-maximizing. When carried out between members of a shared
social group, people offer "fair" (i.e., 50:50) splits, and offers of less than
30% are often rejected - even though rejecting means getting zero.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. FAIR OFFERS: Proposers typically offer 40-50% rather than minimal amounts
   predicted by game theory

2. REJECTION OF LOW OFFERS: Offers below 30% are frequently rejected despite
   positive payoff

3. SUBGAME IMPERFECTION: Actual behavior violates subgame perfect equilibrium
   predictions

4. FAIRNESS CONCERNS: Both proposers and responders exhibit fairness preferences

5. SOCIAL NORMS: Behavior consistent with internalized fairness norms

6. FOUNDATIONAL FINDING: Launched entire field of behavioral game theory
