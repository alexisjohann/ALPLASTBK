Title: Evaluating Replicability of Laboratory Experiments in Economics
Authors: Colin F. Camerer, Anna Dreber, Eskil Forsell, Teck-Hua Ho, Jürgen Huber,
         Magnus Johannesson, Michael Kirchler, Johan Almenberg, Adam Altmejd,
         Taizan Chan, Emma Heikensten, Felix Holzmeister, Taisuke Imai,
         Siri Isaksson, Gideon Nave, Thomas Pfeiffer, Michael Razen, Hang Wu
Year: 2016
Journal: Science, Vol. 351, No. 6280, pp. 1433-1436

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The replicability of scientific findings has recently been called into question.
To contribute data about replicability in economics, the authors replicated 18
studies published in the American Economic Review and Quarterly Journal of
Economics between 2011 and 2014.

All replications followed predefined analysis plans made publicly available
beforehand, with statistical power of at least 90% to detect the original
effect size at the 5% significance level.

Results showed a significant effect in the same direction as the original
study for 11 replications (61%). On average, the replicated effect size was
66% of the original. The replicability rate was higher than psychology's 38%
(Open Science Collaboration 2015), attributed to economics' methodological
practices including financial incentives and no-deception norms.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. 61% REPLICATION RATE: 11 of 18 studies replicated with significant effect
   in same direction - higher than psychology's 38%

2. EFFECT SIZE SHRINKAGE: Average replicated effect was 66% of original -
   original studies likely overestimate true effects

3. PREDICTION MARKETS: Peer beliefs (via prediction markets) predicted which
   studies would replicate - markets achieved 71% accuracy

4. METHODOLOGICAL ADVANTAGES: Economics' financial incentives and no-deception
   norms may improve replicability vs other fields

5. PRE-REGISTRATION: All replications used pre-registered analysis plans -
   demonstrates value of pre-commitment

6. POWER REQUIREMENTS: 90% power threshold ensures reliable detection of
   true effects - addresses underpowered original studies
