Title: Doing It Now or Later
Authors: Ted O'Donoghue, Matthew Rabin
Year: 1999
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 89, No. 1, pp. 103-124

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors examine self-control problems - modeled as time-inconsistent,
present-biased preferences - in a model where a person must do an activity
exactly once. They emphasize two key distinctions: whether activities involve
immediate costs or immediate rewards, and whether people are sophisticated
(aware of their bias) or naive (unaware).

Key finding: Naive people procrastinate immediate-cost activities and
"preproperate" (do too soon) immediate-reward activities. Sophistication
mitigates procrastination but exacerbates preproperation.

With immediate costs, a small present bias can severely harm only naive people.
With immediate rewards, it can severely harm only sophisticated people.

The paper uses the (β, δ) model where β captures present bias and δ captures
standard exponential discounting. When β < 1, preferences are "quasi-hyperbolic"
and present-biased. Sophisticates foresee their future time inconsistency and
use self-control devices, while naifs repeatedly postpone unpleasant activities.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. PROCRASTINATION: Naive present-biased people procrastinate immediate-cost
   activities

2. PREPROPERATION: Naive people do immediate-reward activities too soon

3. SOPHISTICATION EFFECTS: Awareness helps with costs but hurts with rewards

4. SMALL BIAS, BIG HARM: Small present bias can cause severe welfare losses

5. NAIVETY VS SOPHISTICATION: Whether awareness helps depends on task type

6. (β,δ) MODEL: Quasi-hyperbolic discounting with present-bias parameter β
