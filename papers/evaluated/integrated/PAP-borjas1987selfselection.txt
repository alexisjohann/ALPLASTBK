Title: Self-Selection and the Earnings of Immigrants
Authors: George J. Borjas
Year: 1987
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 77, No. 4, pp. 531-553
DOI: 10.2307/2546424

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper analyzes the way in which the earnings of the immigrant population
may be expected to differ from the earnings of the native population because
of the ENDOGENEITY OF THE DECISION TO MIGRATE.

The conditions that determine the nature of the self-selection are derived
and depend on ECONOMIC FACTORS.

EMPIRICAL FINDINGS:
Differences in the U.S. earnings of immigrants with the same measured skills,
but from DIFFERENT HOME COUNTRIES, are attributable to variations in political
and economic conditions in the countries of origin at the time of migration.

The empirical work analyzes the U.S. earnings of immigrants from 41 COUNTRIES
using the 1970 and 1980 Censuses.

The variance in (relative) immigrant earnings across these countries is
SUBSTANTIAL.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. Migration decision is ENDOGENOUS (self-selection)
2. Self-selection depends on economic factors in origin country
3. Same measured skills → different earnings by origin country
4. Political and economic conditions at origin matter
5. 41 countries analyzed using 1970 and 1980 Censuses
6. Substantial cross-country variation in immigrant earnings

================================================================================
THEORETICAL CONTRIBUTION
================================================================================

- Self-selection framework for migration
- Roy model application to immigration
- Conditions for positive vs negative selection derived
- Foundation for subsequent immigration economics literature
