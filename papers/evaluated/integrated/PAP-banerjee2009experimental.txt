Title: The Experimental Approach to Development Economics
Authors: Abhijit V. Banerjee, Esther Duflo
Year: 2009
Journal: Annual Review of Economics, Vol. 1, pp. 151-178

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Randomized experiments have become a popular tool in development economics
research and have been the subject of numerous criticisms. This paper reviews
the recent literature and discusses the strengths and limitations of this
approach in theory and in practice.

The authors argue that the main virtue of randomized experiments is that,
owing to close collaboration between researchers and implementers, they allow
estimation of parameters that would not otherwise be possible to evaluate.

The paper discusses concerns that have been raised - including external
validity, ethics, and atheoretical nature - and generally concludes that
while real, these concerns are often not specific to experiments. The paper
concludes by discussing the relationship between theory and experiments.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. RCTs ENABLE CAUSAL IDENTIFICATION: Randomization provides clean
   identification of treatment effects that observational data cannot achieve

2. COLLABORATION MATTERS: Close partnership between researchers and
   implementers allows estimation of otherwise unmeasurable parameters

3. EXTERNAL VALIDITY CONCERN: Results from one context may not generalize -
   but this concern applies to all empirical methods

4. THEORY COMPLEMENTARITY: Well-designed experiments can test theory and
   identify structural parameters, not just "what works"

5. COST-EFFECTIVENESS COMPARISON: RCTs enable rigorous comparison of
   intervention cost-effectiveness across programs

6. ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS: Ethical concerns about randomization exist but
   are often overstated compared to alternative allocation mechanisms
