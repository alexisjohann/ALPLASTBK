Title: Striving for Balance in Economics: Towards a Theory of the Social Determination of Behavior
Authors: Karla Hoff, Joseph E. Stiglitz
Year: 2016
Journal: Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, Vol. 126(PB), pp. 25-57

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper attempts to broaden standard economic discourse by importing insights
into human behavior not just from psychology, but also from sociology and
anthropology. It introduces an evolution in how economists conceptualize
decision-makers.

Whereas in standard economics the concept is the rational actor, and in early
behavioral economics it is the quasi-rational actor influenced by context of
the moment, in recent behavioral economics the decision-maker could be called
the "enculturated actor." This actor's preferences, perception, and cognition
are subject to two deep social influences: (a) the social contexts to which
they have become exposed and accustomed; and (b) the cultural mental models -
including categories, identities, narratives, and worldviews - used to process
information.

The range of possible equilibria and policy tools is much broader than with
rational actors. Large-scale social change may be caused by simultaneous frame
switches by many individuals.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. ENCULTURATED ACTOR: Beyond rational/quasi-rational to socially-shaped
   preferences and cognition

2. MENTAL MODELS: Categories, identities, narratives, worldviews shape
   information processing

3. SOCIAL CONTEXT: Past exposure and acclimatization shape current behavior

4. MULTIPLE EQUILIBRIA: Cultural influences expand range of possible
   equilibria

5. FRAME SWITCHES: Large-scale social change through simultaneous frame
   shifts

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: Interventions can target cultural mental models,
   not just incentives
