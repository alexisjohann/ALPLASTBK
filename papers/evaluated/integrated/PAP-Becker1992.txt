Title: The Economic Way of Looking at Behavior (Nobel Lecture)
Authors: Gary S. Becker
Year: 1992
Journal: Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 101, No. 3, pp. 385-409

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

In this Nobel Prize lecture delivered December 9, 1992, Gary Becker discusses
four topics emblematic of his innovative approach to the economic analysis of
social issues: discrimination against minorities, crime and punishment, the
development and accumulation of human capital, and the structure of families.

The human capital approach considers how the productivity of people in market
and non-market situations is changed by investments in education, skills, and
knowledge. Becker's foremost achievement is to have formulated and formalized
the microeconomic foundations of human capital theory.

The lecture demonstrates how economic reasoning - assuming that individuals
maximize utility subject to constraints - can illuminate understanding of
social phenomena previously thought to be outside the domain of economics.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. HUMAN CAPITAL THEORY: Investments in education and training increase
   productivity and explain wage differentials

2. DISCRIMINATION ECONOMICS: Market forces tend to reduce discrimination;
   discrimination is costly to those who practice it

3. CRIME ECONOMICS: Criminals respond rationally to incentives; punishment
   deters crime by changing expected costs

4. FAMILY ECONOMICS: Household decisions about marriage, fertility, and
   time allocation follow economic principles

5. GENERAL APPROACH: The economic approach applies to all human behavior,
   not just market transactions

6. UTILITY MAXIMIZATION: Individuals maximize utility subject to constraints
   of time, income, and knowledge
