Title: Persistence of Power, Elites, and Institutions
Authors: Daron Acemoglu, James A. Robinson
Year: 2008
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 98, No. 1, pp. 267-293
DOI: 10.1257/aer.98.1.267

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The authors construct a model to study the implications of changes in political
institutions for economic institutions. A change in political institutions
alters the distribution of de jure political power, but creates incentives for
investments in de facto political power to partially or even fully offset
change in de jure power.

The model can imply a pattern of captured democracy, whereby a democratic
regime may survive but choose economic institutions favoring an elite. The
model provides conditions under which economic or policy outcomes will be
invariant to changes in political institutions, and economic institutions
themselves will persist over time.

The main ideas featuring in the model are illustrated using historical examples
from the U.S. South, Latin America and Liberia.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. DE JURE vs DE FACTO POWER: Political institutions determine de jure power,
   but elites can invest in de facto power to offset institutional changes

2. CAPTURED DEMOCRACY: Democratic regimes can persist while still producing
   economic institutions that favor elites

3. INSTITUTIONAL INVARIANCE: Under certain conditions, changes in political
   institutions have no effect on economic outcomes

4. PERSISTENCE MECHANISM: Elites' comparative advantage in collective action
   (smaller numbers, established position) enables persistent influence

5. HISTORICAL VALIDATION: Model explains persistence patterns in U.S. South
   (post-Civil War), Latin America, and Liberia

6. ENDOGENOUS DE FACTO POWER: The amount of elite political power is an
   equilibrium outcome responding to incentives
