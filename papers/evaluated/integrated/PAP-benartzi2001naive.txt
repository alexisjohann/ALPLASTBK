Title: Naive Diversification Strategies in Defined Contribution Saving Plans
Authors: Shlomo Benartzi, Richard H. Thaler
Year: 2001
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 91, No. 1, pp. 79-98

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

There is a worldwide trend toward defined contribution saving plans and growing
interest in privatized Social Security plans. In both environments, individuals
are given some responsibility to make their own asset-allocation decisions,
raising concerns about how well they do at this task.

This paper investigates one aspect of the task, namely diversification. The
authors show that some investors follow the "1/n strategy": they divide their
contributions evenly across the funds offered in the plan. Consistent with this
naive notion of diversification, they find that the proportion invested in
stocks depends strongly on the proportion of stock funds in the plan.

Experimental evidence suggests that some people spread their contributions
evenly across the investment options irrespective of the particular mix of
options. This 1/n heuristic leads to clearly suboptimal portfolio allocations
that depend on the menu of options rather than underlying preferences.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. 1/N HEURISTIC: Investors divide contributions evenly across available funds
   regardless of fund composition

2. MENU DEPENDENCE: Stock allocation depends on proportion of stock funds in
   menu, not investor preferences

3. NAIVE DIVERSIFICATION: Spreading across options ≠ true risk diversification

4. FRAMING EFFECTS: How choices are presented (menu composition) affects
   allocation decisions

5. CHOICE ARCHITECTURE: Plan sponsors influence outcomes through fund selection

6. BOUNDED RATIONALITY: Complex portfolio decisions reduced to simple heuristics
