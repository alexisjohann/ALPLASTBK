Title: Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action
Authors: Elinor Ostrom
Year: 1990
Publisher: Cambridge University Press, 270 pp.

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The governance of natural resources used by many individuals in common is an
issue of increasing concern to policy analysts. Both state control and
privatization of resources have been advocated, but neither the state nor the
market have been uniformly successful in solving common pool resource problems.

Ostrom explores the intricate dynamics of common-pool resource management,
challenging the belief that privatization or external enforcement is the only
solution to avoid over-exploitation. In contrast to the proposition of the
tragedy of the commons argument, common pool problems sometimes are solved by
voluntary organizations rather than by a coercive state.

The book uses institutional analysis to examine different ways—both successful
and unsuccessful—of governing the commons. Eight design principles are proposed
that are associated with sustainable resource governance.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. BEYOND TRAGEDY: Commons problems can be solved through self-organization
   without state or privatization

2. INSTITUTIONAL DESIGN: Eight design principles associated with successful
   commons governance

3. SELF-GOVERNANCE: Communities can develop effective rules without external
   enforcement

4. POLYCENTRIC GOVERNANCE: Multiple overlapping institutions often work better
   than centralized control

5. LOCAL KNOWLEDGE: Users often have better information than external regulators

6. GRADUATED SANCTIONS: Successful systems use proportional penalties for
   violations
