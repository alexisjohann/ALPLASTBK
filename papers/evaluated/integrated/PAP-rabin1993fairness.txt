Title: Incorporating Fairness into Game Theory and Economics
Authors: Matthew Rabin
Year: 1993
Journal: American Economic Review, Vol. 83, No. 5, pp. 1281-1302

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This paper develops a game-theoretic model incorporating the fact that people
like to help those who are helping them and to hurt those who are hurting them.
Outcomes reflecting such motivations are called "fairness equilibria."

The central insight is that reciprocity - the desire to reward kind actions
and punish unkind ones - is a fundamental human motivation that standard game
theory ignores. The model adds fairness-based utility to material-based utility,
where fairness utility depends on both own and others' intentions.

Key results: Outcomes are mutual-max when each person maximizes the other's
material payoffs, and mutual-min when each person minimizes the other's payoffs.
Every mutual-max or mutual-min Nash equilibrium is a fairness equilibrium.
If payoffs are small, fairness equilibria are roughly the set of mutual-max
and mutual-min outcomes; if payoffs are large, fairness equilibria are roughly
the set of Nash equilibria.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. RECIPROCITY: People help those who help them, hurt those who hurt them

2. FAIRNESS EQUILIBRIA: New solution concept incorporating reciprocal
   motivations

3. INTENTIONS MATTER: Fairness judgments depend on perceived intentions,
   not just outcomes

4. STAKE SIZE: Small stakes → fairness dominates; large stakes → Nash dominates

5. MUTUAL-MAX/MIN: Equilibria where both cooperate or both defect are stable

6. KINDNESS FUNCTIONS: Formal model of perceived kindness based on actions
