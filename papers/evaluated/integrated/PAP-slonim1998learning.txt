Title: Learning in High Stakes Ultimatum Games: An Experiment in the Slovak Republic
Authors: Robert L. Slonim, Alvin E. Roth
Year: 1998
Journal: Econometrica, Vol. 66, No. 3, pp. 569-596

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

In an ultimatum game experiment, financial incentives were varied by a factor of
twenty-five. Consistent with prior results, changes in stakes had only a small
effect on play for inexperienced players.

However, rejections were less frequent the higher the stakes and proposals in
the high stakes declined slowly as proposers gained experience. The lower
rejection frequency when stakes were higher can be explained by the added power
of multiple observations per subject in this experiment.

A model of learning suggests that the lower rejection frequency is the reason
proposers in higher stakes learn to make lower offers over time.

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. STAKE EFFECTS: Higher stakes reduce rejection rates in ultimatum games

2. LEARNING OVER TIME: Proposers learn to make lower offers as they gain
   experience in high-stakes conditions

3. EXPERIENCE MATTERS: Inexperienced players show similar behavior across
   stake levels; differences emerge with experience

4. STRATEGIC LEARNING: Lower rejections teach proposers they can offer less

5. STAKES × EXPERIENCE INTERACTION: Stake effects emerge through learning,
   not immediate calculation

6. CROSS-CULTURAL: First study to detect stake effects, conducted in Slovak
   Republic
