ABSTRACT
================================================================================

There is strong evidence that people exploit their bargaining power in
competitive markets but not in bilateral bargaining situations. There is also
strong evidence that people exploit free-riding opportunities in voluntary
cooperation games. Yet, when they are given the opportunity to punish free
riders, stable cooperation is maintained, although punishment is costly for
those who punish. This paper asks whether there is a simple common principle
that can explain this puzzling evidence. We show that if some people care about
equity the puzzling evidence can be explained in a coherent framework. The
model makes several testable predictions.

================================================================================
I. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

The paper begins by documenting puzzling empirical findings:

1. ULTIMATUM GAME PUZZLE: Responders reject unfair offers (e.g., 80-20 splits)
   even though accepting gives positive payoff. Standard theory predicts all
   positive offers should be accepted.

2. PUBLIC GOODS PUZZLE: Initial contributions are positive (40-60% of endowment)
   but decay to near-zero. However, when punishment is allowed, cooperation
   is sustained at 50-90% levels.

3. GIFT EXCHANGE PUZZLE: Employers pay above market-clearing wages and workers
   respond with above-minimum effort, even in one-shot anonymous interactions.

4. MARKET PUZZLE: In competitive markets with many traders, outcomes converge
   to selfish equilibrium - fairness concerns seem to disappear.

The authors propose that INEQUITY AVERSION - people disliking unfair outcomes -
can explain all these patterns within a single, parsimonious model.

================================================================================
II. THE MODEL OF INEQUITY AVERSION
================================================================================

CORE ASSUMPTION: Besides material self-interest, people care about the
distribution of payoffs. Specifically, they suffer disutility from inequity.

UTILITY FUNCTION (Two-Player Case):

    U_i(x) = x_i - α_i · max{x_j - x_i, 0} - β_i · max{x_i - x_j, 0}

Where:
    x_i = player i's material payoff
    x_j = player j's material payoff
    α_i = DISADVANTAGEOUS INEQUITY AVERSION (disutility from being behind)
    β_i = ADVANTAGEOUS INEQUITY AVERSION (disutility from being ahead)

KEY RESTRICTIONS:
    (i)   α_i ≥ β_i  (being behind hurts MORE than being ahead)
    (ii)  β_i ≥ 0    (non-negative advantageous inequity aversion)
    (iii) β_i < 1    (otherwise would destroy own payoff to help others)

INTERPRETATION:
- α_i captures ENVY or resentment when others earn more
- β_i captures GUILT or compassion when earning more than others
- The restriction α ≥ β means envy dominates guilt

N-PLAYER GENERALIZATION:

    U_i(x) = x_i - α_i/(n-1) · Σ_{j≠i} max{x_j - x_i, 0}
                 - β_i/(n-1) · Σ_{j≠i} max{x_i - x_j, 0}

================================================================================
III. APPLICATIONS
================================================================================

A. ULTIMATUM GAMES
------------------
Model prediction: Responder rejects if offer s < α/(1+2α)

With α = 1: Reject any offer below 33%
With α = 4: Reject any offer below 44%

This explains why 20% offers are frequently rejected even though
accepting gives positive payoff.

Proposer prediction: Anticipating rejection, proposers offer more equal splits.
Equilibrium offers depend on distribution of α in population.


B. PUBLIC GOODS GAMES
---------------------
Without punishment:
- Selfish types free-ride
- Inequity-averse types initially contribute but decrease over time
  as they observe others free-riding
- Result: Decay toward zero

With punishment:
- Inequity-averse types punish free-riders (costly punishment)
- Even selfish types contribute to avoid punishment
- Result: Stable high cooperation (50-90%)

Key insight: Only need SOME inequity-averse types to sustain cooperation


C. GIFT EXCHANGE / LABOR MARKETS
--------------------------------
The model predicts:
- Firms offer wages above market-clearing
- Workers respond with effort above minimum
- Higher wages → higher effort (gift exchange)

This explains Akerlof's (1982) efficiency wage observations.


D. COMPETITIVE MARKETS
----------------------
Prediction: When n → ∞, fairness concerns become irrelevant

Intuition: With many traders, each individual's impact on others' payoffs
approaches zero, so inequity aversion doesn't bind.

This explains why competitive markets converge to selfish equilibrium
while bilateral bargaining shows strong fairness effects.

================================================================================
IV. PARAMETER ESTIMATES
================================================================================

From experimental data (ultimatum, dictator, public goods games):

DISTRIBUTION OF TYPES:
    ~30% are SELFISH:        α = 0, β = 0
    ~30% are MODERATE:       α ≈ 0.5, β ≈ 0.25
    ~30% are FAIR:           α ≈ 1.0, β ≈ 0.5
    ~10% are STRONGLY FAIR:  α > 1, β > 0.5

POPULATION AVERAGES:
    α_mean ≈ 0.85
    β_mean ≈ 0.315

KEY FINDING: Heterogeneity is crucial. A population of purely selfish
or purely fair individuals would not generate the observed patterns.

================================================================================
V. TESTABLE PREDICTIONS
================================================================================

1. Rejection rates in ultimatum games depend on relative (not absolute) payoffs

2. Punishment of free-riders is more likely when:
   - The free-rider earned significantly more
   - The punisher has high α

3. Gift exchange should be stronger when:
   - Interaction is more personal (bilateral vs. market)
   - Wage is clearly above market rate

4. Competitive pressure reduces fairness-driven behavior

5. The presence of even a few inequity-averse agents can sustain cooperation

================================================================================
VI. RELATION TO OTHER MODELS
================================================================================

Comparison with:

BOLTON & OCKENFELS (2000) - ERC MODEL:
- Also models fairness preferences
- Uses relative payoff share rather than absolute differences
- Similar predictions in many cases

RABIN (1993) - RECIPROCITY:
- Focuses on intentions (kind/unkind)
- Fehr-Schmidt is purely outcome-based
- Reciprocity can explain some additional phenomena

CHARNESS & RABIN (2002):
- Combines social welfare and reciprocity
- More complex but captures additional patterns

================================================================================
VII. CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

Main contributions:

1. UNIFYING FRAMEWORK: Single model explains ultimatum, public goods,
   gift exchange, and market behavior

2. PARSIMONY: Only two parameters (α, β) with clear interpretation

3. HETEROGENEITY: Mix of selfish and fair types generates rich dynamics

4. TESTABLE: Clear predictions for new experiments

5. POLICY RELEVANCE: Mechanism design must account for fairness preferences

The model has become one of the most influential in behavioral economics,
with over 19,000 citations. It established social preferences as a core
research area and influenced work on:
- Experimental economics
- Labor economics
- Public economics
- Mechanism design
