\section{LIT-R: Richard Thaler Research: Mental Accounting and Choice Architecture}
\label{app:litr}

This appendix integrates papers by Richard Thaler on mental accounting,
behavioral finance, choice architecture, and nudge theory. Thaler's work demonstrates how
psychological considerations fundamentally reshape understanding of economic decision-making,
particularly in consumption, saving, and financial markets.

\subsection{Research Program Overview}

Richard H. Thaler's research program addresses core behavioral economics themes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mental Accounting}: Cognitive framing of financial decisions
  \item \textbf{Choice Architecture}: Default effects and option presentation
  \item \textbf{Behavioral Finance}: Limits to arbitrage and market psychology
  \item \textbf{Nudges & Policy}: Low-cost interventions for behavior change
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{1996: Mental Budgeting and Consumer Decisions (Heath \& Soll)...}

\paragraph{Citation.}
Heath, Chip and Soll, Jack B. (1996). ``Mental Budgeting and Consumer Decisions.''
\textit{Journal of Consumer Research}, 23(1), 40--52.

\paragraph{Core Finding.}
Consumers track expenses against pre-set mental budgets, leading to underconsumption when budgets are exhausted and overconsumption when budgets reset (Effect size: 0.95)

\paragraph{Key Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Extends Thaler's mental accounting to explicit budget tracking
  \item Documents systematic tracking errors in expense categorization
  \item Shows budget violations trigger stronger aversion than equivalent losses
  \item Establishes ``payment depreciation'' effect for pre-paid purchases
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{10C Integration.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Domain}: Finance, Consumer Behavior
  \item \textbf{Dimension}: F (Financial)
  \item \textbf{Context}: Budgeting, Self-Control
  \item \textbf{Complementarity}: γ = 0.55
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{EBF Application.}
Critical for HR benefits design: employees mentally budget benefits usage. When ``wellness budget'' is exhausted, gym usage drops even if objectively valuable. Supports mental identity budgeting intervention (INT-HR-007).

outside the specific domain contexts discussed.

general principles that should be calibrated to specific contexts. Cross-domain
validation remains an open research question.

\subsection{Objection 2: Measurement Challenges}

\textbf{Objection:} Key constructs may be difficult to measure reliably in
practice.

\textbf{Response:} We recommend using validated scales and instruments where
available, and developing domain-specific proxies where necessary. Sensitivity
analysis should assess robustness to measurement uncertainty.
